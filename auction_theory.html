Auction Theory

The mathematics of who wins, what they pay, and why honesty can be optimal

Chapter 18 · Economics & Game Theory · Topic 5 · Difficulty 2

Auctions are among the oldest market mechanisms — and among the richest in mathematical structure. William Vickrey’s 1961 paper revealed a startling truth: in a second-price sealed-bid auction, bidding your true value is always optimal, regardless of what others do. Even more surprising, under standard assumptions all four major auction formats yield the same expected revenue to the seller. Yet real auctions harbour traps like the winner’s curse, and modern mechanism design extends these ideas to allocate goods, spectrum licences, and internet advertising billions of times per second.

How Each Auction Works
English: Price rises from zero; bidders drop out when price exceeds their value. Last bidder wins at the second-highest value.
Dutch: Price falls from high; first bidder to “call” wins at that price. Strategically equivalent to first-price sealed-bid.
First-Price Sealed: Each bidder submits one bid. Highest bid wins and pays that bid. Rational bidders shade below their value.
Vickrey: Each bidder submits one bid. Highest bid wins but pays the second-highest bid. Truthful bidding is dominant.

2 · Vickrey’s Insight

In a second-price auction, bidding your true value is a dominant strategy — it maximises your payoff no matter what others bid. Set your value and bid to see why.

Why truthful bidding is dominant: If you bid above V, you risk winning but paying more than V (negative payoff). If you bid below V, you risk losing auctions you would have profited from. Bidding exactly V ensures you win precisely when it is profitable, and never overpay — because you pay the second price, not your own bid.
Did you know?
William Vickrey proposed the second-price sealed-bid auction in 1961 and proved that truthful bidding is a dominant strategy — a result so elegant it helped earn him the 1996 Nobel Prize in Economics. Tragically, he died just three days after the announcement, before he could deliver his Nobel lecture.

Revenue Equivalence Theorem (Vickrey 1961, Myerson 1981): With independent private values drawn from the same distribution, any auction that (1) always gives the item to the highest-value bidder and (2) gives zero payoff to the lowest-possible type yields the same expected revenue. Expected revenue = E[2nd-highest value] = (n−1)/(n+1) · 100 for U[0,100].
When does revenue equivalence break down?
Revenue equivalence holds only under strong assumptions: (1) Independent private values — each bidder knows their own value but not others’, (2) Risk-neutral bidders, (3) Symmetric information — values drawn from the same distribution, (4) Highest value wins. Relax any of these and the four auction formats can generate very different revenues.
Optimal bid shading (first-price sealed-bid):
With n risk-neutral bidders whose values are uniformly distributed,
b(v) = ((n − 1) / n) · v
With 2 bidders you shade by half; with 10 bidders you barely shade at all. As n → ∞, first-price bids converge to true values.

VCG Payment Rule
Each winner i pays: (total welfare of others without i) − (total welfare of others with i). This equals the harm i’s participation causes to others. Under VCG, reporting true values is a dominant strategy for every bidder — exactly the multi-item analogue of Vickrey’s insight.

Algorithmic Complexity

Why some problems take forever — Big-O notation and the speed of computation
CS, AI & Cryptography · Difficulty 2

How long does a computation take? The answer depends not just on the computer, but on the algorithm. Big-O notation captures how an algorithm's running time grows as its input grows. Some algorithms scale gracefully. Others collapse under the weight of large inputs. Understanding this difference is one of the most important ideas in all of computer science.

Did you know?
If a computer performs 1 billion operations per second, solving a problem of size n = 100: O(n²) takes 0.00001 seconds. O(2n) takes longer than the age of the universe (2100 ≈ 1030 operations — roughly 1013 years). The jump from polynomial to exponential is not gradual; it is a cliff.

4. P vs NP: The Millennium Problem

Finding vs Verifying

P = problems solvable in polynomial time. NP = problems where a solution can be verified in polynomial time. The question: does P = NP? Can every problem that is easy to check also be easy to solve?

At n=5, brute force checks 32 subsets — trivial. At n=20, it must check 1,048,576 subsets. At n=50, 250 ≈ 1015 — even a billion checks per second would take days. Yet verifying any proposed solution takes just n additions. This gap between finding and verifying is the heart of P vs NP.
Why It Matters

Every software engineer uses Big-O daily. Choosing an O(n log n) sort over an O(n²) sort can mean the difference between a responsive application and one that freezes.

The P vs NP question is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems, carrying a $1,000,000 bounty from the Clay Mathematics Institute. Its resolution would reshape our understanding of what computers can and cannot do.

Modern cryptography relies on the assumption that certain problems are hard. RSA encryption depends on the difficulty of factoring large numbers. If P = NP, much of our digital security would collapse overnight.

The Master Theorem provides a general framework for analysing divide-and-conquer algorithms: for recurrences of the form T(n) = aT(n/b) + O(nd), the complexity depends on the relationship between logba and d.

Hyperbolic Functions

●●●● Advanced

The exponential cousins of sine and cosine — built from ex, living on the hyperbola

1. Introduction

Defined via Exponentials

The hyperbolic functions are defined in terms of the exponential function. Where the trigonometric functions sin and cos parametrise the unit circle x² + y² = 1, the hyperbolic functions sinh and cosh parametrise the right branch of the unit hyperbola x² − y² = 1.

sinh(x) = (ex − e−x) / 2
cosh(x) = (ex + e−x) / 2
tanh(x) = sinh(x) / cosh(x) = (ex − e−x) / (ex + e−x)

The “h” stands for hyperbolic. Just as (cos t, sin t) traces the unit circle, (cosh t, sinh t) traces the unit hyperbola. The parameter t represents twice the signed area enclosed between the ray from the origin, the curve, and the x-axis.

Hyperbolic functions appear throughout mathematics and physics: in the shape of a hanging chain (the catenary y = a cosh(x/a)), in special relativity (rapidity), in the solutions of Laplace’s equation, and in the integration of many rational functions.

The circular functions and hyperbolic functions are unified by Euler’s formula: cosh(x) = cos(ix) and sinh(x) = −i sin(ix). What looks like two separate families is really one family seen from two different angles in the complex plane.

The fundamental identity cosh²(x) − sinh²(x) = 1 mirrors the Pythagorean identity cos²(θ) + sin²(θ) = 1, but with a minus sign. This sign change reflects the geometry: a circle has x² + y² = 1, a hyperbola has x² − y² = 1.

Osborn’s Rule: To convert any trigonometric identity into its hyperbolic counterpart, replace cos → cosh and sin → sinh, then negate any term containing a product of two sinh factors.

Trig: cos²(θ) + sin²(θ) = 1
Replace: cosh²(x) + sinh²(x) = 1
Negate sinh² term: cosh²(x) − sinh²(x) = 1   ✓
▶ Why Osborn’s rule works

The connection comes from the complex exponential. Since cos(θ) = cosh(iθ) and sin(θ) = −i sinh(iθ), every trigonometric identity becomes a hyperbolic identity under the substitution θ → ix.

When sin appears once, the factor of −i cancels harmlessly. But when two sin factors multiply, we get (−i)(−i) = i² = −1, introducing an extra minus sign. This is why Osborn’s rule says: negate any product of two sinh terms.

For example, cos(A+B) = cos A cos B − sin A sin B becomes cosh(A+B) = cosh A cosh B − (−sinh A sinh B) = cosh A cosh B + sinh A sinh B. The sign flip on the sinh sinh product is exactly what Osborn predicts.

4. Inverse Hyperbolic Functions

Logarithmic Forms

Since the hyperbolic functions are built from exponentials, their inverses can be expressed as natural logarithms. The standard notation uses the prefix “ar” (for area), reflecting the geometric interpretation as hyperbolic sector area.

arsinh(x) = ln(x + √(x² + 1))  for all x
arcosh(x) = ln(x + √(x² − 1))  for x ≥ 1
artanh(x) = ½ ln((1 + x) / (1 − x))  for |x| < 1
▶ Derivation of arsinh(x)

Let y = sinh(x) = (ex − e−x) / 2. We want x in terms of y.

Step 1: 2y = ex − e−x
Step 2: Multiply by ex:   2y ex = e2x − 1
Step 3: Rearrange:   e2x − 2y ex − 1 = 0
Step 4: Quadratic in ex:   ex = (2y ± √(4y² + 4)) / 2 = y ± √(y² + 1)
Step 5: Since ex > 0, take +:   ex = y + √(y² + 1)
Step 6: x = ln(y + √(y² + 1))
▶ Derivation of arcosh(x)

Let y = cosh(x) = (ex + e−x) / 2 with x ≥ 0.

Step 1: 2y = ex + e−x
Step 2: Multiply by ex:   2y ex = e2x + 1
Step 3: e2x − 2y ex + 1 = 0
Step 4: ex = y ± √(y² − 1)
Step 5: Taking x ≥ 0 gives ex ≥ 1, so:   ex = y + √(y² − 1)
Step 6: x = ln(y + √(y² − 1)) (y ≥ 1)
▶ Derivation of artanh(x)

Let y = tanh(x) = (ex − e−x) / (ex + e−x).

Step 1: y(ex + e−x) = ex − e−x
Step 2: Multiply by ex:   y e2x + y = e2x − 1
Step 3: e2x(y − 1) = −1 − y
Step 4: e2x = (1 + y) / (1 − y)
Step 5: x = ½ ln((1 + y) / (1 − y)) (|y| < 1)

6. Worked Examples

Problem 1: Solve cosh(x) = 3

Find the exact value(s) of x satisfying cosh(x) = 3.

▶ Show solution
Method 1 (logarithmic form):
x = arcosh(3) = ln(3 + √(9 − 1)) = ln(3 + √8) = ln(3 + 2√2)

Method 2 (from definition):
(ex + e−x) / 2 = 3
ex + e−x = 6
e2x − 6ex + 1 = 0  (quadratic in ex)
ex = (6 ± √(36 − 4)) / 2 = 3 ± 2√2

Both roots are positive:
x = ln(3 + 2√2) ≈ 1.7627
x = ln(3 − 2√2) = −ln(3 + 2√2) ≈ −1.7627

Since cosh is an even function, if x is a solution then so is −x. Note that 3 − 2√2 = 1/(3 + 2√2), confirming the symmetry.

Problem 2: Prove sinh(2x) = 2 sinh(x) cosh(x)

Establish the double-angle formula for sinh directly from the exponential definition.

▶ Show proof
LHS:
sinh(2x) = (e2x − e−2x) / 2

RHS:
2 sinh(x) cosh(x)
= 2 × [(ex − e−x) / 2] × [(ex + e−x) / 2]
= 2 × (ex − e−x)(ex + e−x) / 4
= (ex − e−x)(ex + e−x) / 2

Expand using difference of squares:
= (e2x − e−2x) / 2

LHS = RHS   □

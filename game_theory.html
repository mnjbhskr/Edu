Game Theory & Nash Equilibrium

The mathematics of strategic interaction

Chapter 18 · Economics & Game Theory · Topic 2 · Difficulty 2

When your best move depends on what someone else does — and their best move depends on you — you are inside a game. John Nash proved that every finite game has at least one equilibrium: a set of strategies where no player can improve by changing theirs alone. This Nash equilibrium shapes pricing wars, arms races, traffic flows, and evolutionary biology. Below you can build games, find equilibria, and discover why rational individuals sometimes produce irrational outcomes.

The tragedy: Mutual cooperation (3,3) is better for both players, yet rational self-interest drives each to defect. The Nash equilibrium (Defect, Defect) is not Pareto efficient — a recurring theme in game theory.
Formal definition — Nash Equilibrium:
A Nash equilibrium is a strategy profile where each player’s strategy is a best response to the others.
Formally: sᵢ ∈ argmax uᵢ(sᵢ, s₋ᵢ)
No player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.
Dominant strategies: A strategy is dominant if it gives a higher payoff regardless of what the opponent does. In the Prisoner’s Dilemma, ‘Defect’ is dominant for both players — it’s better whether the opponent cooperates or defects. Not all games have dominant strategies.
Did you know? John Nash developed his equilibrium concept in a 27-page PhD dissertation at Princeton in 1950 — he was just 21 years old. The idea was so compact and powerful that it transformed economics, political science, and evolutionary biology. Nash received the Nobel Prize in 1994, after decades of struggle with schizophrenia.

Key insight: At the mixed NE, each player chooses probabilities that make the other player indifferent between their strategies. Neither player can gain by shifting their mix.
Why randomise? Players randomise to make their opponents indifferent. If your opponent can predict your choice, they’ll exploit it. By mixing with the right probabilities, you ensure your opponent gets the same expected payoff regardless of what they do — removing their ability to exploit you.

4 · Best Response Dynamics

Each player’s best response is a function mapping the opponent’s mixed strategy to their own optimal mix. Nash equilibria sit where the two best-response curves intersect. Press Animate to watch iterative best response from a random starting point.

5 · Pareto Efficiency

Each outcome maps to a point in payoff space. The Pareto frontier contains outcomes where you cannot improve one player without hurting the other. Notice how Nash equilibria can fall inside the frontier — room for improvement, but no individual incentive to move.

Theory of Computation

Turing machines, decidability, and the boundaries of what can be computed
Logic & Proof · Difficulty 2

What can a machine compute? This seemingly simple question leads to some of the deepest results in mathematics. In the 1930s, Alan Turing and Alonzo Church independently discovered that there are well-defined problems no algorithm can ever solve. Along the way, they invented the theoretical foundations of every computer that has existed since. Here you will explore these ideas visually — from simple finite automata to the unsolvable halting problem.

1. Finite Automaton Simulator

A deterministic finite automaton (DFA) is the simplest model of computation: a fixed set of states with transitions on each input symbol. Watch strings get accepted or rejected as the machine steps through states.

Choose an Automaton

Why finite automata matter: Regular expressions, lexical analysers in compilers, network protocol validators, and even simple vending machine controllers are all finite automata in disguise. They are limited — they cannot count arbitrarily — but their simplicity makes them fast and verifiable.

The Busy Beaver: The 3-state busy beaver writes six 1s before halting — the maximum any 3-state Turing machine can produce. The 5-state busy beaver writes at least 47,176,870 ones. For 6 states, the number is incomprehensibly large. The busy beaver function grows faster than any computable function — it is itself uncomputable.

3. The Halting Problem

Can we build a programme that decides whether any given programme halts or loops forever? Turing proved the answer is no — using a beautiful argument by contradiction. Step through the proof below.

The Diagonal Argument

Setup: Imagine we have a table of all programmes P₁, P₂, P₃, … and we want to know for each one: does Pi(j) halt? The table has a cell for every programme–input pair.
Cantor's diagonal in disguise: This is exactly the same trick Cantor used to prove the reals are uncountable. In both cases, we construct a new object that differs from every item in a list by flipping the diagonal. The halting problem, the incompleteness theorems, and the uncountability of the reals are all siblings.

4. Complexity Classes

Even among decidable problems, some are vastly harder than others. Complexity theory classifies problems by how much time (or space) they require. The relationship between these classes is one of the greatest open questions in mathematics.

P vs NP — the million-dollar question: If P = NP, then every problem whose solution can be verified quickly can also be solved quickly. Cryptography, optimisation, drug design, and logistics would all be transformed. Most experts believe P ≠ NP, but no one has proved it. The Clay Mathematics Institute offers $1,000,000 for a proof either way.

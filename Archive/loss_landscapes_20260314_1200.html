Loss Landscapes

The geometry of neural network optimisation

When a neural network learns, it searches a vast landscape of possible parameter values, trying to find a valley where the error is lowest. This surface — the loss landscape — determines everything about training: whether the network converges, how fast it learns, and whether it gets stuck. Here we visualise these landscapes and the paths that optimisers carve through them.

Saddle points are where the gradient is zero but the surface curves up in some directions and down in others. In high dimensions, saddle points vastly outnumber local minima. SGD noise and momentum both help escape them — the random perturbations push the optimiser off the saddle ridge.

SGD follows the raw gradient — slow in narrow valleys. Momentum accumulates velocity, overshooting but recovering. RMSProp adapts the learning rate per-parameter using recent gradient magnitudes. Adam combines both ideas and is the default choice for most deep learning today.

5. What Have We Learnt?

  • Loss landscapes are the terrain neural networks must navigate. Their shape — bowls, ridges, saddles, narrow valleys — determines how hard a problem is to optimise.
  • Learning rate is the single most important hyperparameter. Too small and training is glacially slow; too large and it diverges. Schedules that reduce the rate over time often give the best results.
  • Saddle points dominate high-dimensional landscapes. They are not traps for modern optimisers: SGD noise and momentum provide natural escape mechanisms.
  • Adaptive optimisers like Adam adjust the effective learning rate for each parameter individually, making them robust across many landscape geometries.
  • Connections: These ideas link to gradient descent (the foundation), backpropagation (how gradients are computed), and neural network generalisation (flat vs sharp minima).

Interest Rate Volatility: Normal vs Lognormal

●●●● Advanced

When rates went negative, the models had to change — and so did the conventions

1. The Problem That Broke Black’s Model

For decades, interest rate options were priced using Fischer Black’s 1976 model — a variant of Black-Scholes adapted for forwards. The model assumes the forward rate follows a lognormal process: rates are always positive, and volatility is quoted as a percentage of the rate level.

Then came 2014. The European Central Bank cut deposit rates below zero. Swiss franc rates followed. Suddenly, a model that assumed rates cannot be negative was being asked to price options on rates that were deeply negative. The lognormal distribution has no support below zero — log of a negative number is undefined. Screens went blank. Pricing libraries threw errors. The industry’s workhorse model was broken.

The solution was not new mathematics — it was a model from 1900. Louis Bachelier’s original option pricing framework assumed rates follow a normal (Gaussian) process, which naturally accommodates negative values. The industry rapidly adopted normal volatility, quoted in basis points per year, alongside the existing lognormal convention.

Today, every rates desk maintains both conventions. Swaption brokers quote normal vol in basis points. Risk systems convert between them. The SABR model bridges the two with a single parameter β that interpolates between normal (β=0) and lognormal (β=1) dynamics. Understanding both conventions — and how to convert between them — is essential for anyone working with interest rate derivatives.

Today, practitioners routinely quote IR options in both conventions. A swaption broker might say “the 10y10y is 60 normal” (60 bp/year Bachelier vol) or “20% lognormal” — both describing the same option price, just in different units.

2. Two Models, Two Vol Conventions

Black (Lognormal) vs Bachelier (Normal)

The two models differ in a single assumption: whether the forward rate’s absolute changes or its proportional changes have constant volatility. This choice determines the entire distribution of future rates — and the unit in which volatility is expressed.

AttributeBlack (Lognormal)Bachelier (Normal)
Process dF = σln × F × dW dF = σn × dW
Distribution Lognormal (F > 0 always) Normal (F can be negative)
Vol Unit % (percentage of rate) bp (basis points per year)
ATM Call Price F × σln × √(T/2π) σn × √(T/2π)
When to Use Rates clearly positive Rates near or below zero
▶ The stochastic process behind each model

Black (Lognormal) model: The forward rate F(t) satisfies dF = σln F dW, where W is a Brownian motion. Applying Itô’s lemma to ln(F) gives d(ln F) = −½σln² dt + σln dW. Thus ln(FT) is normally distributed, meaning FT is lognormally distributed and strictly positive.

Black call price:
C = D × [F × N(d1) − K × N(d2)]
d1 = [ln(F/K) + ½σln²T] / (σln√T)
d2 = d1 − σln√T

Bachelier (Normal) model: The forward rate satisfies dF = σn dW. This is arithmetic Brownian motion — FT is normally distributed with mean F0 and variance σn²T. The rate can go negative, which is exactly what we need when rates are near zero.

Bachelier call price:
C = D × [(F − K) × N(d) + σn√T × n(d)]
d = (F − K) / (σn√T)
where N(·) is the standard normal CDF and n(·) is the PDF

3. Approximate Conversion

The Quick Rule of Thumb

At-the-money (K = F), and for small volatilities, the two models produce approximately the same option price when:

σn ≈ σln × F    (bp vol ≈ % vol × rate level)

This follows directly from comparing the ATM price approximations: F × σln × √(T/2π) ≈ σn × √(T/2π), which gives σn ≈ σln × F.

Worked Example

Given: F = 3.00%, σln = 30%
Step 1: σn ≈ 30% × 3.00% = 0.009 (in decimal)
Step 2: Convert to bp: 0.009 × 10,000 = 90 bp/year
Rule: A 30% lognormal vol at a 3% rate level is approximately 90 bp normal vol.
▶ The exact correction term

The linear approximation σn ≈ σln × F works well at-the-money when vol is moderate. For a more precise conversion, one can expand to second order:

σn ≈ σln × F × [1 − ½σln²T/24 + ...]

However, the truly exact conversion requires price matching: compute the Black price, then invert the Bachelier formula numerically (via bisection or Newton’s method) to find the normal vol that reproduces exactly the same price. This is what the converter below does.

Away from ATM (K ≠ F), the approximation degrades because the two distributions assign different probabilities to the tails. The exact conversion then depends on both the strike and maturity.

6. The SABR Framework

Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho

The SABR model (Hagan et al., 2002) is the industry standard for interpolating and extrapolating the volatility smile in interest rate markets. It describes the joint dynamics of the forward rate F and its stochastic volatility α:

dF = α Fβ dW1
dα = ν α dW2
dW1 dW2 = ρ dt

The four parameters control distinct features of the smile:

ParameterRoleTypical Range
α (alpha) ATM volatility level Calibrated
β (beta) Backbone — interpolates normal (β=0) and lognormal (β=1) 0 – 1
ρ (rho) Correlation — controls smile skew −1 – 1
ν (nu) Vol-of-vol — controls smile curvature > 0

The β parameter is crucial: at β = 1, SABR reduces to a stochastic-vol version of the Black model. At β = 0, it becomes a stochastic-vol Bachelier model. In practice, β is often fixed (e.g., 0.5 or 0) and the remaining three parameters {α, ρ, ν} are calibrated to market quotes.

▶ Full SABR implied vol formula (Hagan et al. 2002)

For F ≠ K, the SABR implied lognormal volatility approximation is:

σB(K,F) = (α / (FK)(1−β)/2 [1 + (1−β)²/24 × ln²(F/K) + (1−β)⁴/1920 × ln⁴(F/K)])
  × (z / x(z))
  × {1 + [(1−β)²α²/(24(FK)1−β) + ρβνα/(4(FK)(1−β)/2) + (2−3ρ²)ν²/24] × T}

where:

z = (ν/α) × (FK)(1−β)/2 × ln(F/K)
x(z) = ln[(√(1 − 2ρz + z²) + z − ρ) / (1 − ρ)]

For ATM (F = K), the formula simplifies to:

σATM = α/F1−β × {1 + [(1−β)²α²/(24 F2(1−β)) + ρβνα/(4 F1−β) + (2−3ρ²)ν²/24] × T}

8. Swaption Convention Details

Market Conventions for Interest Rate Options

Swaptions — options to enter an interest rate swap — are the most liquid IR option market. Their quoting and settlement conventions carry important subtleties that affect pricing.

Physical vs Cash Settlement

A physically settled swaption delivers an actual swap at expiry. The holder enters the underlying swap if it is in-the-money. A cash settled swaption pays the present value of the swap, discounted using a flat yield at the swap rate. The cash settlement formula introduces a convexity adjustment because the annuity is evaluated at a single rate rather than off the full curve.

PVBP (Present Value of a Basis Point)

The PVBP (or DV01 of the annuity) converts between normal vol in rate space and the option premium in currency terms. For a payer swaption:

Premium = PVBP × Bachelier(σn, F, K, T)

Coterminal vs Constant Maturity

Coterminal swaptions share the same end date (e.g., all expire into swaps ending in 2036). Constant maturity swaptions have the same underlying swap tenor (e.g., always 10-year swaps, regardless of expiry). The standard swaption grid quotes constant-maturity vols.

ConventionDescriptionUsage
Physical settlement Delivers actual swap at expiry Standard in EUR and GBP markets
Cash settlement Pays PV using flat-yield annuity Standard in USD market (pre-2018)
PVBP annuity ∑ DFi × δi over swap fixed leg Scales bp vol to premium
Coterminal Same swap end date, varying expiry Bermudan swaption calibration
Constant maturity Same swap tenor, varying expiry Standard vol grid (1y×10y, 5y×10y, etc.)

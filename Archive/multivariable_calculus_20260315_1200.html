5 · Critical Points and the Hessian

Surface Type

The Hessian matrix of second partial derivatives generalises the second-derivative test. Its eigenvalues reveal whether the surface curves up, down, or both — distinguishing minima, maxima, and saddle points.

6 · What Have We Learnt?

We began with surfaces in three dimensions and learnt to slice them with planes to obtain partial derivatives. Assembling partial derivatives into a single vector gave us the gradient, which always points uphill and is perpendicular to contour lines.

The directional derivative revealed that the rate of change in any direction is simply the dot product of the gradient with that direction — controlled entirely by the angle α between them.

Finally, the Hessian matrix of second derivatives lets us classify critical points as local minima, maxima, or saddle points by examining its eigenvalues.

Key Formulae

∇f = (∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y)
Dᵤf = ∇f · û = |∇f| cos(α)
H = [[fxx, fxy], [fyx, fyy]]
D = fxx fyy − (fxy)²
D > 0 and fxx > 0 ⇒ local minimum
D > 0 and fxx < 0 ⇒ local maximum
D < 0 ⇒ saddle point
Connections: These ideas are the backbone of optimisation. Gradient descent uses ∇f to find minima of loss functions in machine learning. The Hessian appears in Newton's method, physics (potential energy surfaces), and economics (utility maximisation).

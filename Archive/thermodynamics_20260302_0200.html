Rectangular hyperbolas: Each isotherm satisfies PV = const, so it is a rectangular hyperbola. As temperature rises, isotherms shift outward: at any given volume, higher T means higher P.

Internal energy depends only on T: For an ideal gas, U = &frac32;nRT. It does not depend on P or V separately — this is a direct consequence of the equipartition theorem, which assigns ½kT of energy per degree of freedom.

Isothermal (T = const): W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁), ΔU = 0, Q = W
Adiabatic (Q = 0): W = nCv(T₁−T₂), PVγ = const
Isobaric (P = const): W = PΔV, Q = nCpΔT
Isochoric (V = const): W = 0, Q = nCvΔT = ΔU
First Law: ΔU = Q − W
Work is path-dependent: The work done equals the area under the PV curve. Different processes between the same initial and final states yield different amounts of work — this is why W is not a state function.

Adiabatic vs isothermal: An adiabatic curve is always steeper than an isothermal curve through the same point, because γ > 1. Physically: with no heat exchange, the gas cools as it expands, so pressure drops faster.

Adiabatic link: TVγ−1 = const
A universal ceiling: The Carnot efficiency η = 1 − TC/TH depends only on the reservoir temperatures — not on the working gas, the mechanism, or any engineering details. No real engine can exceed it. This is a consequence of the Second Law.

The enclosed area is work: For any cyclic process on a PV diagram, the net work per cycle equals the enclosed area: Wnet = ∮ P dV. The Carnot cycle maximises this area for given TH and TC.

Practical implication: To increase efficiency, raise TH or lower TC. This is why power stations run at the highest possible steam temperature. Even a perfect engine at 600 K / 300 K can only convert 50% of heat into work.

Temperature is a distribution: Temperature is not a single speed — it is a statistical summary of how fast molecules move. The Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution gives the probability density for finding a molecule at speed v.

Universal ratio: The three characteristic speeds are always in the ratio 1 : √(4/π) : √(3/2). This ratio is universal — it does not depend on the gas or the temperature. Only the scale changes.

Lighter = faster: At the same temperature, lighter molecules move faster. This is why hydrogen escapes planetary atmospheres while nitrogen does not. The vrms of H₂ at 300 K is about 1920 m/s — close to Earth’s escape velocity.
The Mathematics of Heat and Energy
Thermodynamics is built on remarkably few equations. The ideal gas law PV = nRT is an equation of state — it connects the macroscopic observables P, V, and T. From there, work becomes a path integral on the PV plane: W = ∫P dV. Different paths (isothermal, adiabatic, isobaric, isochoric) yield different amounts of work for the same endpoints, which is why work is not a state function.

The Carnot cycle combines these paths into the most efficient possible heat engine. Its efficiency η = 1 − TC/TH is a universal ceiling: it depends only on the reservoir temperatures, not on any engineering details. This result foreshadowed the Second Law and the concept of entropy, S = Qrev/T.

The Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution completes the picture by connecting macroscopic temperature to microscopic molecular motion. Boltzmann showed that temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy per degree of freedom: ½kT per degree. The distribution f(v) ∝ v² exp(−mv²/2kT) is the bridge between the statistical mechanics of individual molecules and the thermodynamic laws that govern engines, refrigerators, and stars.

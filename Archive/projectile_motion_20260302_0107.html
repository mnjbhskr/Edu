At 45°, range is maximised. But complementary angles (e.g. 30° and 60°) produce the same range — try it and see. This symmetry comes from sin(2θ) = sin(180° − 2θ).

What happens to velocity during flight

vx = v₀ cos(θ)  (constant)
vy = v₀ sin(θ) − gt (decreasing)
|v| = √(vx² + vy²)
The horizontal velocity never changes — there is no horizontal force. Only gravity acts, pulling downward, steadily stealing vertical speed until it reverses direction.

3. Angle vs Range

Which angle maximises range?

R(θ) = v₀² sin(2θ) / g
The sin(2θ) function reaches its maximum at 2θ = 90°, i.e. θ = 45°. This is why 45° maximises range on flat ground. The curve is perfectly symmetric about 45° — every angle has a “twin” that gives the same range.
The Mathematics of Flight
Projectile motion is one of the first places where trigonometry, algebra, and calculus converge on a single physical problem.

Trigonometry decomposes the initial velocity into horizontal and vertical components using sin and cos. Algebra gives us the quadratic y(t) = v₀sin(θ)t − ½gt², whose roots are the launch and landing times. Calculus connects position, velocity, and acceleration: velocity is the derivative of position, and acceleration (constant gravity) is the derivative of velocity.

The parabolic trajectory is no coincidence — it is the unique curve produced by constant acceleration in one direction and constant velocity in the perpendicular direction. Galileo was the first to recognise this, publishing his analysis in Discorsi (1638).

Assumptions: no air resistance, flat ground, uniform gravitational field (g = 9.81 m/s²). In reality, air drag makes trajectories asymmetric and shorter.

Doubling the mass does not double the period — it increases it by a factor of √2. The square root is what makes SHM special: heavy masses oscillate slowly, but not as slowly as you might expect.

Energy is never lost — it transforms between kinetic and potential twice every cycle. At the extremes, all energy is potential. At the centre, all is kinetic. The total never changes.

The pendulum period depends only on length and gravity, not on mass or amplitude (for small angles). Galileo noticed this watching a chandelier swing in Pisa cathedral — he timed it against his own pulse.

In phase space, every closed orbit means periodic motion. SHM always traces an ellipse. The area of the ellipse is proportional to the total energy — more energy means a bigger orbit.
The Mathematics of Oscillation
Simple harmonic motion is governed by the differential equation x″ + ω²x = 0, whose solutions are sine and cosine. This is why the same mathematics appears in springs, pendulums, electrical circuits, and even the vibrations of molecules.

Trigonometry provides the solution: x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ). Calculus connects position to velocity (dx/dt = −Aω sin(ωt)) and acceleration (d²x/dt² = −ω²x). Energy conservation follows from multiplying the equation of motion by the velocity.

The pendulum adds a twist: the true equation is θ″ + (g/L) sin(θ) = 0. For small angles, sin(θ) ≈ θ gives SHM. For large angles, the motion is still periodic but no longer sinusoidal — the period increases and the wave shape distorts.

Assumptions: no friction, no damping, no driving forces. Real oscillators eventually stop unless energy is continuously supplied.

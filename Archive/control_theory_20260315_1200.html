Control Theory

Feedback, stability, and the mathematics of keeping systems on track

Physics · Advanced

Control theory is the mathematics of making systems behave. From thermostats to spacecraft, the core idea is simple: measure the output, compare it with what you want, and adjust. This page lets you explore the fundamental tools — PID controllers, feedback loops, step responses, stability analysis, and frequency-domain methods — through hands-on interaction.

Key idea: In open-loop mode, the controller blindly applies its output with no correction. In closed-loop mode, the feedback path subtracts the output from the reference, creating a self-correcting system. The closed-loop transfer function is T(s) = C(s)G(s) / (1 + C(s)G(s)).

What to notice: When ζ < 1, the system oscillates (underdamped). At ζ = 1, it reaches steady state as fast as possible without overshooting (critically damped). When ζ > 1, it's sluggish but smooth (overdamped). The ζ = 0.707 case is special — it gives the fastest response with minimal overshoot.

Why it matters: Poles in the left half-plane (Re < 0) → stable. Poles on the imaginary axis → sustained oscillation. Poles in the right half-plane → unstable (exponential growth). This is the fundamental stability criterion for linear time-invariant systems.

5. Bode Plot — Frequency Response

The Bode plot shows how a system responds to sinusoidal inputs at different frequencies. The magnitude plot tells you how much the signal is amplified; the phase plot tells you how much it's delayed. Together they predict stability via gain and phase margins.

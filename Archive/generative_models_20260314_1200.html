Generative Models

GANs, VAEs, diffusion models, and generative systems

Generative models learn to create new data that resembles their training examples. Rather than classifying or predicting, they answer a deeper question: what does the underlying distribution of this data look like, and how can we sample from it? This page explores three landmark architectures—GANs, VAEs, and diffusion models—through interactive 2D visualisations.

1. What Is a Generative Model?

A generative model attempts to learn the probability distribution p(x) of training data. As training progresses, generated samples should converge toward the true distribution. The KL divergence measures how different the generated distribution is from the real one—lower is better.

In a GAN, the generator G learns to transform random noise z into realistic-looking data, whilst the discriminator D learns to distinguish real data from fakes. When training succeeds, D can no longer tell them apart—its accuracy converges to 50%, meaning G has captured the true distribution.

3. VAEs: The Probabilistic Encoder

A Variational Autoencoder encodes data into a probabilistic latent space, then decodes it back. The KL weight β controls the trade-off: higher β encourages a well-structured latent space (smooth interpolation) but may sacrifice reconstruction quality. The ELBO (Evidence Lower Bound) measures the overall quality of the model.

4. Diffusion: Noise to Signal

Forward and Reverse Process

0 / 50
SNR: — dB  |  Step: 0 / 50
Diffusion models work by learning to reverse a gradual noising process. The forward process adds Gaussian noise step by step until the data becomes pure noise. The reverse process learns to denoise, recovering structure from randomness. The signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) quantifies how much signal remains at each step.

5. What Have We Learnt?

All three architectures share a common goal: learn to sample from an unknown data distribution p(x). They differ fundamentally in how they approach this challenge.

GANs use adversarial training—a minimax game between generator and discriminator. They produce sharp samples but can suffer from mode collapse and training instability.

VAEs use variational inference, optimising a tractable lower bound (the ELBO) on the log-likelihood. They offer a principled latent space for interpolation, but samples can appear blurry due to the Gaussian assumptions.

Diffusion models iteratively denoise, learning a sequence of denoising steps. They achieve state-of-the-art sample quality but require many steps to generate each sample.

Modern generative AI systems (such as DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, and GPT) build upon these three foundational ideas. Understanding the mathematical trade-offs between adversarial training, variational inference, and iterative denoising is essential for grasping why different generative approaches excel at different tasks.

Finding the best solution — gradients, constraints, and the art of minimisation

1. Gradient Descent in 2D

Click anywhere on the contour plot to place a starting point, then watch gradient descent navigate to the minimum. The gradient arrow shows the direction of steepest ascent — the algorithm moves opposite to it.

Click on the canvas to begin.

Paraboloid has a single bowl. Rosenbrock has a narrow curved valley. Rastrigin has many local minima — gradient descent can get trapped.

2. Convex vs Non-Convex

On the left, a convex landscape has a single global minimum — every random start converges to the same point. On the right, a non-convex landscape traps different starts at different local minima.

In practice, non-convex problems are solved with random restarts, momentum, or stochastic perturbations.

3. Lagrange Multipliers

We wish to minimise f(x, y) = x² + y² subject to a constraint g(x, y) = c. The optimum is where a contour line of f is tangent to the constraint curve, meaning ∇f = λ∇g.

2.5

The red arrows show ∇f and the blue arrows show ∇g. At the optimum, they are parallel (one is a scalar multiple of the other).

4. Linear Programming

The feasible region (blue polygon) is defined by linear inequality constraints. The objective function appears as parallel dashed lines. Drag the slider to sweep the objective — the optimum is always at a vertex.

5. Newton’s Method vs Gradient Descent

Newton’s method uses second-order curvature information (the Hessian) to take smarter steps. Compare both methods descending the same surface side by side.

0.005

Newton converges in far fewer steps for well-conditioned problems, but each step requires computing and inverting the Hessian matrix.

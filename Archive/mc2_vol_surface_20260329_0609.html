Volatility Quoting: Surface to Model Input

●●●● Advanced

What traders mean by ‘vol’ — and how to turn it into a model input

1. Why Markets Quote Vol

Option prices are not directly comparable. A call on a £10 stock and a call on a £1,000 stock will have vastly different premiums even if the underlying uncertainty is identical. Prices also depend on time to expiry, interest rates, and the strike level — making it impossible to compare options across strikes, maturities, or underlyings by looking at premiums alone.

Implied volatility solves this. By inverting the Black-Scholes formula, the market extracts a single number — the annualised standard deviation of log-returns that would make the model price equal the observed market price. This normalises away directional effects, time effects, and magnitude effects, leaving a pure measure of uncertainty priced by the market.

Implied vol is not a model prediction — it is a market quote expressed in a convenient unit. Just as bond traders quote yields rather than prices, options traders quote vol rather than premiums. The Black-Scholes formula serves as the translation mechanism, even when market participants do not believe the model is correct.

Information content. When a trader says “the 25-delta put is trading at 22 vol”, they are communicating something far richer than a price. They are saying: the market assigns approximately 22% annualised uncertainty to the downside at that particular strike. This convention allows instant comparison across thousands of option contracts, across asset classes, and across time.

2. Strike Representations

Four Ways to Specify a Strike

Different markets and models express the strike in different units. Each representation has advantages depending on the context — trading, calibration, or risk management.

RepresentationDefinitionWhen Used
Absolute Strike (K) Actual price level in currency units Equity options, physically settled contracts
Moneyness (K/F) Strike / Forward, or log(K/F) SABR model, academic literature
Delta (Δ) Black-Scholes delta at that strike FX options, OTC markets
Standard Deviations Distance from ATM in σ√T units Model-consistent but requires vol input
▶ Log-moneyness ln(K/F) and why models prefer it

Simple moneyness K/F has a problem: it is bounded below by zero and asymmetric around at-the-money. A strike 10% above forward (K/F = 1.10) is not “equally far” from ATM as a strike 10% below (K/F = 0.90) in any meaningful probabilistic sense.

Log-moneyness k = ln(K/F) fixes both issues. It ranges from −∞ to +∞, is centred at zero for ATM, and — crucially — corresponds directly to the exponent in the lognormal distribution assumed by Black-Scholes.

k = ln(K/F)
ATM: k = 0   |   OTM put: k < 0   |   OTM call: k > 0

The SABR model, local volatility models, and stochastic volatility models all parameterise the smile as a function of log-moneyness because it produces smooth, well-behaved surfaces that extrapolate sensibly into the wings.

3. Converting Delta to Strike

The Delta-to-Strike Formula

In the Black-Scholes framework, the delta of a call option determines a unique strike. Given a call delta Δcall, the corresponding strike is:

K = F × exp(−N−1(Δcall) × σ√T + ½σ²T)

where F is the forward price, σ is implied volatility, T is time to expiry in years, and N−1 is the inverse of the standard normal CDF.

Worked Example

Given: 25-delta call, F = 100, σ = 20%, T = 1 year
Step 1: N−1(0.25) = −0.6745
Step 2: σ√T = 0.20 × 1.0 = 0.200
Step 3: K = 100 × exp(0.6745 × 0.20 + 0.5 × 0.04)
         = 100 × exp(0.1349 + 0.0200)
         = 100 × exp(0.1549)
         = 116.76
Log-moneyness: ln(116.76/100) = 0.1549
▶ Full derivation from Black-Scholes delta

The Black-Scholes call delta (forward delta, undiscounted) is:

Δcall = N(d1)   where   d1 = [ln(F/K) + ½σ²T] / (σ√T)

Setting Δcall = δ and inverting:

N−1(δ) = d1 = [ln(F/K) + ½σ²T] / (σ√T)

N−1(δ) × σ√T = ln(F/K) + ½σ²T

ln(K/F) = −N−1(δ) × σ√T + ½σ²T

K = F × exp(−N−1(δ) × σ√T + ½σ²T)

Note: this uses the forward delta convention (no discounting). The spot delta convention, common in FX markets, multiplies by e−rT and produces a slightly different mapping. For put delta, use K = F × exp(N−1(−Δput) × σ√T + ½σ²T), since Δput is negative.

5. The Volatility Surface

Black-Scholes assumes that implied volatility is constant across all strikes and maturities. Markets reject this assumption categorically. If you invert the Black-Scholes formula for every traded option on a single underlying, the resulting implied vols vary systematically with both strike and expiry.

Equity skew. Out-of-the-money puts trade at higher implied vol than ATM or OTM calls. This reflects demand for downside protection and the empirical observation that equity returns have negative skewness — crashes are more violent than rallies. The pattern has persisted since the 1987 crash.

FX smile. Currency options often display a more symmetric pattern — both wings trade at elevated vol relative to ATM. This reflects the symmetric uncertainty in exchange rates: a large move in either direction is considered plausible.

Term structure. Near-dated options often exhibit steeper skew and higher ATM vol than longer-dated options, because short-term uncertainty spikes more dramatically around events (earnings, elections, central bank meetings).

The existence of the vol surface is itself a statement about model inadequacy. If Black-Scholes were correct, implied vol would be flat. The surface’s shape encodes the market’s collective view on tail risk, jump risk, and the failure of lognormal assumptions.

7. Where It Matters

Where It Matters

SABR model calibration. The SABR model parameterises the vol smile with four parameters (α, β, ρ, ν). Calibrating it requires converting market quotes — often given in delta space — into strikes, then fitting the model to the resulting vol-vs-moneyness curve at each expiry. Incorrect strike conversion propagates directly into mis-calibration.

Local vol / Dupire’s formula. Dupire showed that a unique local volatility surface can be extracted from European option prices. The input is a smooth, arbitrage-free implied vol surface parameterised by strike and maturity. The quality of the local vol surface depends entirely on the quality of the interpolation and parameterisation of the implied surface.

Risk management and P&L attribution. Vega risk is reported per strike bucket and per tenor. Converting between delta and strike representations is essential for aggregating risk across books that use different conventions. P&L attribution separates the contribution of ATM vol moves, skew changes, and curvature changes — all of which require a well-specified surface parameterisation.

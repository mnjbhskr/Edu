Second-Order Differential Equations

●●●● Advanced

From springs to circuits — the mathematics of oscillation and decay

1. Introduction

Second-order differential equations are the language of oscillation. Whenever something vibrates, bounces, or swings — a mass on a spring, current in an electrical circuit, the deflection of a loaded beam — the governing equation is second-order.

The general form of a second-order linear ODE with constant coefficients is:

ay″ + by′ + cy = f(x)

Here a, b, and c are constants, and f(x) is the forcing function. When f(x) = 0, the equation is homogeneous; otherwise it is non-homogeneous.

The general solution of a non-homogeneous equation has two parts: the complementary function (CF) — the general solution of the homogeneous equation — and a particular integral (PI) — any single solution of the full equation. The complete solution is y = CF + PI.

Newton’s second law F = ma is itself a second-order DE. The acceleration a = y″ is the second derivative of position. Every physical system governed by forces and inertia leads naturally to a second-order equation.

2. The Auxiliary Equation

Finding the Complementary Function

To solve the homogeneous equation ay″ + by′ + cy = 0, we try a solution of the form y = emx. Substituting gives:

am² + bm + c = 0   —  the auxiliary (or characteristic) equation

The discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac determines three distinct cases:

CaseDiscriminantRootsGeneral Solution (CF)
Two distinct real roots Δ > 0 m1, m2 y = Aem1x + Bem2x
Repeated real root Δ = 0 m = m1 = m2 y = (A + Bx)emx
Complex conjugate roots Δ < 0 α ± βi y = eαx(A cos βx + B sin βx)
▶ Derivation of the complex-root solution

When Δ < 0, the quadratic formula gives roots m = α ± βi where α = −b/(2a) and β = √(4ac − b²)/(2a).

The two independent solutions are e(α+βi)x and e(α−βi)x. Using Euler’s formula eiθ = cosθ + i sinθ and taking real linear combinations gives the real-valued form:

y = eαx(A cos βx + B sin βx)

The exponential factor eαx controls growth or decay, while cosβx and sinβx produce oscillation. When α < 0, the solution is a decaying oscillation — precisely what we observe in damped vibrations.

4. Particular Integrals

The Method of Undetermined Coefficients

For the non-homogeneous equation ay″ + by′ + cy = f(x), we need a particular integral yp. The strategy: guess a trial form for yp based on the shape of f(x), substitute back, and solve for the unknown coefficients.

Forcing Function f(x)Trial ypNotes
Polynomial of degree n anxn + … + a1x + a0 Include all terms down to the constant
ekx Cekx If k is a root, multiply by x (or x² if repeated)
sin(kx) or cos(kx) C1cos(kx) + C2sin(kx) Always include both sin and cos terms
ekxsin(kx) ekx(C1cos kx + C2sin kx) Combine exponential and trig trial forms

The Overlap Rule

If any term in the trial solution already appears in the complementary function, the standard guess will fail (it solves the homogeneous equation and yields zero on the right). Multiply the entire trial by x to obtain a linearly independent form. If that also overlaps, multiply by x².

Worked Example

Solve: y″ + 3y′ + 2y = 4e3x
Step 1: Auxiliary eqn: m² + 3m + 2 = 0 ⇒ m = −1, −2
Step 2: CF = Ae−x + Be−2x
Step 3: Try yp = Ce3x (no overlap with CF)
Step 4: yp′ = 3Ce3x, yp″ = 9Ce3x
Step 5: 9C + 9C + 2C = 4 ⇒ 20C = 4 ⇒ C = 1/5
Result: y = Ae−x + Be−2x + ⅕e3x
The overlap rule is not arbitrary — it reflects a fundamental principle of linear algebra. The particular integral must be linearly independent of the complementary function. Multiplying by x guarantees independence because xemx is not a constant multiple of emx.

6. Damped Oscillations

Mass-Spring-Damper System

A mass m on a spring (constant k) with viscous damping (coefficient c) satisfies:

m x″ + c x′ + k x = 0

The discriminant c² − 4mk determines the behaviour:

RegimeConditionBehaviour
Overdamped c² > 4mk Slow, monotonic return to equilibrium — no oscillation
Critically damped c² = 4mk Fastest return without oscillation
Underdamped c² < 4mk Decaying oscillation at the natural frequency

Critical damping is the engineering ideal for shock absorbers and instrument needles — returning to equilibrium as fast as possible without overshooting. Even a tiny reduction in damping below the critical value produces oscillation.

7. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Initial Value Problem

Solve y″ − 5y′ + 6y = 0 subject to y(0) = 1 and y′(0) = 4.

Step 1: Auxiliary equation: m² − 5m + 6 = 0
Step 2: (m − 2)(m − 3) = 0 ⇒ m1 = 2, m2 = 3
Step 3: General solution: y = Ae2x + Be3x
Step 4: Apply y(0) = 1: A + B = 1
Step 5: y′ = 2Ae2x + 3Be3x. Apply y′(0) = 4: 2A + 3B = 4
Step 6: Solving: A = −1, B = 2
Solution: y = −e2x + 2e3x
▶ Verification

y = −e2x + 2e3x

y′ = −2e2x + 6e3x

y″ = −4e2x + 18e3x

y″ − 5y′ + 6y = (−4e2x + 18e3x) − 5(−2e2x + 6e3x) + 6(−e2x + 2e3x)

= (−4 + 10 − 6)e2x + (18 − 30 + 12)e3x = 0 ✓

y(0) = −1 + 2 = 1 ✓    y′(0) = −2 + 6 = 4 ✓

Example 2 — Non-Homogeneous with Resonance

Find the general solution of y″ + 4y = 8sin(2x).

Step 1: Auxiliary equation: m² + 4 = 0 ⇒ m = ±2i
Step 2: CF = A cos 2x + B sin 2x
Step 3: Try yp = C1cos 2x + C2sin 2x — overlap with CF!
Step 4: Multiply by x: yp = x(C1cos 2x + C2sin 2x)
Step 5: Differentiate (product rule twice):
yp′ = C1cos 2x + C2sin 2x + x(−2C1sin 2x + 2C2cos 2x)
yp″ = −4C1sin 2x + 4C2cos 2x + x(−4C1cos 2x − 4C2sin 2x)
Step 6: yp″ + 4yp = −4C1sin 2x + 4C2cos 2x = 8sin 2x
Step 7: Comparing: 4C2 = 0 ⇒ C2 = 0, −4C1 = 8 ⇒ C1 = −2
Solution: y = A cos 2x + B sin 2x − 2x cos 2x
The −2x cos 2x term is crucial: the factor of x means the amplitude grows without bound. This is resonance — the forcing frequency matches the natural frequency. In physical systems, damping prevents the amplitude from truly reaching infinity, but near-resonance can still produce dangerously large oscillations.

Surprising places where π appears in mathematics, probability, and physics

Why is this "the most beautiful equation"?
Euler's identity, eiπ + 1 = 0, ties together the five most fundamental constants of mathematics in a single, elegant equation. The number e (the base of natural logarithms), i (the imaginary unit), and (the ratio of circumference to diameter) each come from entirely different branches of mathematics — yet they combine with the additive identity 0 and the multiplicative identity 1 into one equation.

The deeper result is Euler's formula: eiθ = cos(θ) + i·sin(θ). As θ sweeps from 0 to 2π, the point traces the unit circle in the complex plane. At θ = π, we land at the point −1 on the real axis — and so eiπ = −1, giving eiπ + 1 = 0. Pi appears because it is the angle (in radians) for a half-turn around the circle.

2. The Basel Problem — π²/6

Summing the Reciprocal Squares

Terms: 1
Why does π appear in a sum of fractions?
In 1735, Leonhard Euler stunned the mathematical world by proving that 1/1² + 1/2² + 1/3² + 1/4² + … = π²/6. This was the "Basel Problem," which had defeated mathematicians for nearly a century.

There is nothing obviously circular about adding up reciprocal squares — yet the answer involves π. The deep reason is that the sine function, sin(πx), can be written as an infinite product involving these very terms. Euler factored sin(πx)/(πx) like a polynomial (using its roots at ±1, ±2, ±3, …), expanded, and compared coefficients. The π in the sine function percolates through the algebra into the sum. The circle hides inside trigonometry, and trigonometry hides inside this infinite series.

3. Random Integers & Pi (Probability)

Coprime Pairs — Estimating π from Randomness

Why does π appear in probability?
If you pick two positive integers at random, the probability that they share no common factor (are "coprime") is 6/π² ≈ 60.8%. This astonishing result connects the randomness of number theory to the geometry of circles.

The reasoning: the probability that both numbers are divisible by a prime p is 1/p², so the probability they are not both divisible by p is 1 − 1/p². Since divisibility by different primes is independent, the probability of being coprime is the product over all primes: ∏(1 − 1/p²) = 1/ζ(2) = 6/π². And there it is again — the Basel Problem in disguise, now with π emerging from the distribution of prime numbers.

4. Pi in Physics — The Pendulum

Simple Pendulum — T = 2π√(L/g)

Length L (m): 1.00

Stopwatch — Estimate π from Swings

Why does π appear in a swinging weight?
A simple pendulum swinging through small angles obeys the same differential equation as a point moving around a circle — simple harmonic motion. The period is T = 2π√(L/g), where L is the length and g is gravitational acceleration (~9.81 m/s²).

The π appears because the restoring force is proportional to the sine of the angle, and for small angles sin(θ) ≈ θ. The solution involves sine and cosine — circular functions with period 2π. So the pendulum's swing time is fundamentally tied to the geometry of the circle. By measuring the period of a pendulum of known length, you can estimate π from a purely physical experiment — no geometry needed.
The Mystery of π
Why does the same number keep appearing in contexts that seem to have nothing to do with circles? Euler's identity connects π to complex exponentials. The Basel problem finds it hiding in infinite sums. Number theory reveals it in the statistics of prime numbers. Physics encounters it in any system that oscillates.

The common thread is periodicity and circular symmetry. Wherever something repeats, rotates, oscillates, or cycles, the mathematics of sine and cosine inevitably appears — and with them comes π. The circle is not merely a shape; it is a fundamental pattern woven into the fabric of mathematics itself.

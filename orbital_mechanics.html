2. Kepler’s Second Law — Equal Areas in Equal Times

The planet sweeps equal areas in equal time intervals

Area near perihelion
Area near aphelion
A planet sweeps equal areas in equal times. Near the star it moves fast over a short, wide triangle. Far from the star it moves slowly over a long, thin triangle. The areas match exactly — this is conservation of angular momentum.

3. Kepler’s Third Law — T² ∝ a³

Period squared is proportional to semi-major axis cubed

T² = k · a³ line
Solar system planets
T² = (4π² / GM) a³
T² / a³ = constant (for all orbits around the same star)
Kepler discovered this law empirically in 1619. Newton later proved it from gravity: the constant is 4π²/(GM). Measure the period and distance, and you can weigh the Sun.
The Mathematics of Orbits
Kepler’s laws connect to deep mathematics. The first law (elliptical orbits) follows from the inverse-square gravitational force — any 1/r² central force produces conic section trajectories. The second law (equal areas) is equivalent to conservation of angular momentum: L = mr²dθ/dt = constant. The third law (T² ∝ a³) follows from integrating the equations of motion.

Conic sections from geometry reappear here: eccentricity e = 0 gives a circle, 0 < e < 1 an ellipse, e = 1 a parabola, and e > 1 a hyperbola. Only ellipses produce bound (repeating) orbits.

The key computational challenge is solving Kepler’s equation: M = E − e sin(E), which relates mean anomaly M (proportional to time) to eccentric anomaly E (related to position). This transcendental equation has no closed-form solution and must be solved numerically — Newton–Raphson iteration converges in 3–4 steps.

Assumptions: two-body problem only (no perturbations from other planets), Newtonian gravity (no relativistic corrections).

Repeated Games & Cooperation

Why selfish players learn to cooperate when the game never ends

Chapter 18 · Economics & Game Theory · Topic 4 · Difficulty 2

In a single Prisoner’s Dilemma, betrayal is rational. But when the same players meet again and again, a shadow falls from the future over the present — making cooperation not just possible, but strategically optimal. Robert Axelrod’s famous tournaments revealed that the simplest strategy, Tit-for-Tat, outperforms far more sophisticated ones. The key ingredients: be nice, be retaliatory, be forgiving, and be clear.

3. The Shadow of the Future

Discount factor δ

0.90
δ = probability the game continues to the next round. High δ means the future casts a long shadow — making cooperation sustainable.
δ = 0.90 · Cooperation: sustainable
Folk Theorem threshold: cooperation by TFT is stable when δ ≥ (T − R) / (T − P) = (5 − 3)/(5 − 1) = 0.50
Key insight: The Folk Theorem says that when δ is high enough, any feasible payoff that exceeds the minimax value can be sustained as a Nash equilibrium. The future disciplines the present: if I know you will punish my defection in all future rounds, the short-term gain from betrayal is not worth it.

Cooperation can evolve without central authority.

Key insight: Direct reciprocity requires repeated encounters between the same pair. Indirect reciprocity extends cooperation to strangers: “I help you not because you helped me, but because you helped someone.” Reputation is the currency. As Martin Nowak showed, indirect reciprocity requires cognitive abilities — you must track who did what — and may have driven the evolution of language and social intelligence.
Why Repeated Games Matter
One-shot games predict a bleak world of universal defection. But most real interactions — trade, diplomacy, friendships, ecosystems — are repeated. The possibility of future encounters transforms the strategic landscape. Axelrod’s work showed that cooperation can emerge among purely selfish agents, provided encounters are repeated, players can recognise each other, and the shadow of the future is long enough. This insight bridges game theory, evolutionary biology, and moral philosophy.

The Attention Mechanism

Which words should pay attention to which — the question that transformed AI

Before attention, neural networks processed text sequentially and forgot distant context. In 2017, Vaswani et al. asked a radical question: what if every token could directly attend to every other token? The result — the Transformer — now powers every major language model, from GPT-4 to Claude. At its core is a single elegant mechanism: scaled dot-product attention.

Each word decides how much to “look at” every other word
The classic example: “The animal didn’t cross the street because it was too tired.” To understand “it”, you must attend to “animal.” The attention mechanism learns to do this automatically — no rules, no parsing, just learned weights.

Attention lets every word look at every other word and decide what matters — context on demand.

2. Queries, Keys, and Values

The QKV computation pipeline

Step through the animation to see each matrix operation in sequence.

Queries (Q) — what am I looking for?
Keys (K) — what do I contain?
Values (V) — what do I contribute?
d=4

Without the 1/√dk scaling factor, large embedding dimensions cause dot products to grow proportionally to dk. This pushes softmax inputs into regions where gradients vanish — the distribution becomes nearly one-hot, and the model stops learning. Scaling by 1/√dk keeps the variance of the scores at approximately 1, regardless of dimension.

Press Step to begin the attention computation.
Q = X × WQ   “What am I looking for?”
K = X × WK   “What do I contain?”
V = X × WV   “What do I contribute?”
Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKT / √dk) × V
Attention is literally a search operation. Q is the query (like a Google search). K is the index (page titles). V is the content returned (page body). The dot product Q·K measures relevance; softmax converts scores to probabilities; the weighted sum of V is the answer.
Geometric interpretation. The dot product q·k measures similarity in embedding space — geometrically, it is proportional to the cosine of the angle between the query and key vectors. High dot product = small angle = similar meaning = high attention weight. Attention is, at its core, a learned similarity search.

Q asks the question. K says “I might be relevant.” V says “Here is what I know.” Attention is information retrieval.

Each head learns a different type of relationship
Multiple heads allow the model to attend to different types of relationships simultaneously. One head might track syntax (subject–verb agreement), another tracks coreference (what does “it” refer to?), another tracks adjacency. The final output concatenates all perspectives — richer than any single viewpoint.

One head sees grammar. Another sees meaning. Together they see language.

Why This Matters

The core equation of modern AI:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKT / √dk) × V

This single equation is the beating heart of every major language model. “Attention Is All You Need” (Vaswani et al., 2017) has over 130,000 citations and spawned every major LLM — GPT, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA. Before attention, sequence models struggled with long-range dependencies. Attention solved this by letting every token directly access every other token, at the cost of quadratic compute in sequence length.

Attention complexity is O(n²) in sequence length — every token attends to every other token. For a 128,000-token context window, that is 16 billion attention computations per layer. This quadratic cost is why long-context models are expensive, and why techniques like sparse attention, sliding window attention, and FlashAttention exist.

Probability & Softmax

Raw scores become probabilities — the gateway between algebra and uncertainty

A neural network's final layer outputs raw numbers called logits — they can be any magnitude, positive or negative, and they certainly don't sum to one. Softmax is the elegant function that converts these raw scores into a proper probability distribution: every value between 0 and 1, and all values summing to exactly 1. This is the mathematical bridge between a network's internal algebra and the probabilistic predictions we actually need.

→ exp = [7.39, 2.72, 1.11] → sum = 11.21

A neural network never directly outputs probabilities. It outputs raw scores (logits). Softmax is the function that converts confidence scores into a proper probability distribution — every output positive, every output less than 1, and the whole thing summing to exactly 1.

Softmax turns arbitrary real numbers into a valid probability distribution — the final step in every classifier.

2. Temperature — The Creativity Dial

Softmax at different temperatures

All three panels share the same logits. Temperature controls how peaked or flat the distribution becomes.

T = low (confident)
T = 1.0 (normal)
T = high (creative)
1.0
softmaxT(zi) = exp(zi / T) / Σj exp(zj / T)
T → 0: argmax — all probability on the highest logit (deterministic)
T = 1: standard softmax (balanced)
T → ∞: uniform distribution — every class equally likely (maximum randomness)
When you set ChatGPT's ‘temperature’ to 0, you get deterministic responses — it always picks the most likely next token. At high temperature, it gets creative — and occasionally wrong. Temperature is not a metaphor; it is literally this parameter in the softmax function.

Temperature is the single dial that controls the confidence-creativity tradeoff in every language model.

The exponential function is the secret ingredient: it makes large logits enormously larger, while small or negative logits get squished toward zero. This is why a small change in the top logit can dramatically redistribute probability away from every other class.

Exponentiation amplifies differences — the mathematical engine behind the winner-take-most behaviour of softmax.

3. Cross-Entropy Loss

Prediction vs true label — measuring the cost of being wrong

Drag the prediction bars to change the model's output. Watch the loss change live. The true label is highlighted in green.

True label
Prediction (softmax)
Cross-entropy loss
cat
H(p, q) = −Σi p(i) · log q(i)
where p = true distribution (one-hot), q = model's prediction
For one-hot labels: H = −log q(true class)
Example: true = cat, q(cat) = 0.7 → loss = −log(0.7) = 0.357
Cross-entropy is how we measure the cost of being wrong. The entire training process of an LLM is the minimisation of this single number across billions of examples. When the model is confident and correct, loss is near zero. When confident and wrong, loss explodes toward infinity.

Cross-entropy loss is the objective function of intelligence — minimise it, and the model learns.

Why This Matters

Every prediction a language model makes — every next-token probability, every classification, every decision — passes through softmax. Temperature scaling is the reason ChatGPT can be both precise and creative. Cross-entropy loss is the single number that the entire training process works to minimise. These three ideas — softmax, temperature, and cross-entropy — form the mathematical foundation of how neural networks express and refine uncertainty.

Claude Shannon introduced cross-entropy in his 1948 paper “A Mathematical Theory of Communication”. Seventy-seven years later, it remains the loss function of choice for every large language model on Earth.

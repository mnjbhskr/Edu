Spam Filters and Naive Bayes

How a 250-year-old theorem keeps your inbox clean — and why innocent words can make emails look guilty

Your inbox receives approximately 120 emails per day. Of these, 85% are spam. Yet you barely notice because a clever mathematical filter intercepts most of them before you ever see them. The secret? A theorem published in 1763 by Thomas Bayes. Bayesian classification treats spam filtering as a probability problem: given the words in an email, what is the probability that it is spam? The answer turns out to be remarkably powerful, and surprisingly simple.

Section 1: Bayes' Theorem — A Quick Refresher

At the heart of modern spam filtering lies a single equation. Bayes' theorem inverts conditional probabilities: instead of asking "What is the probability of seeing these words, given that an email is spam?", we ask "What is the probability that an email is spam, given that we see these words?"

P(A|B) = P(B|A) × P(A) / P(B) where: A = the email is spam B = we observe these words P(A|B) = posterior probability (what we want) P(B|A) = likelihood P(A) = prior probability P(B) = evidence (normaliser)

Let us explore this with an interactive example. Imagine a medical test for a rare disease: 1% of the population has it, and the test is 99% accurate (correctly identifies 99% of sick people and 99% of healthy people). If you test positive, what is the probability you actually have the disease?

Why this matters for spam: Most emails are not spam (base rate ~ 85% spam is high, but still). A classifier needs high specificity to avoid false positives, even if it seems very accurate.

Section 2: From Bayes to Spam — The Bag of Words

A spam filter must answer a practical question: "Given this email's words, is it spam or legitimate (ham)?" We use Bayes' theorem, but adapt it for text:

P(spam | words) = P(words | spam) × P(spam) / P(words) The key insight: decompose the email into its words and compute: P(spam | w₁, w₂, …, wₙ) ∝ P(spam) × P(w₁|spam) × P(w₂|spam) × … × P(wₙ|spam) This product over all words is the foundation of Naive Bayes classification.

Training Phase: Learning Word Probabilities

Before we can classify emails, we need training data. A filter learns by observing thousands of emails labelled as spam or legitimate. For each word, it computes:

P(word | spam) = count of word in spam emails / total words in spam P(word | ham) = count of word in legitimate emails / total words in legitimate emails

Words like "buy", "free", "winner" appear frequently in spam. Words like "meeting", "report", "project" appear more often in legitimate mail.

Section 3: The Naive Assumption

The Naive Bayes classifier earns its name from a bold, almost certainly false assumption: given the class (spam or ham), all words are independent.

In reality, words are correlated. If an email contains "buy", it is more likely to also contain "now". If it contains "meeting", it is more likely to contain "tomorrow". Yet Naive Bayes ignores these dependencies and treats each word's probability in isolation:

P(spam | w₁, …, wₙ) ∝ P(spam) × Π P(wᵢ | spam) The ∏ symbol (product) means we multiply all word probabilities together.

Why This Naive Assumption Works

Despite its naivity, Naive Bayes works remarkably well in practice for two reasons:

  1. Calibration: We do not need exact probabilities, only relative ordering. Even if assumptions are violated, the ordering of spam vs ham often remains correct.
  2. Computational simplicity: Independence assumptions allow us to factorise the joint distribution, making the classifier fast and scalable.

Section 4: Laplace Smoothing — Handling Unknown Words

Here lies a subtle trap: what if a word never appeared in the training data? Its probability is zero, which means P(word | spam) = 0. When we multiply probabilities, a single zero kills the entire product, and the email becomes unclassifiable.

Without smoothing: P(word | spam) = count / total_words Problem: if count = 0, then probability = 0 ✗ With add-one smoothing (Laplace): P(word | spam) = (count + 1) / (total_words + |vocabulary|) Result: rare and unseen words get small but non-zero probability ✓

This simple adjustment is called Laplace smoothing or add-one smoothing. It ensures that no word, no matter how rare or unseen, receives zero probability.

Section 5: Evaluating the Filter — Precision, Recall, and ROC Curves

A spam filter must make a hard decision: is this email spam or ham? But underneath, the classifier computes a probability. We can tune where we draw the line (the threshold). This trade-off is crucial, because false positives and false negatives have different costs.

Confusion Matrix

When we apply a classifier to a test set, we get four outcomes:

True Positives (TP): Spam email correctly classified as spam False Positives (FP): Legitimate email wrongly classified as spam ← BAD! True Negatives (TN): Legitimate email correctly classified as ham False Negatives (FN): Spam email wrongly classified as ham ← MISSED!

A false positive (marking a real email as spam) is often worse than a false negative (missing spam). Users notice when their email disappears; they rarely notice spam that slips through.

Key Metrics

Precision = TP / (TP + FP) "Of the emails I flagged as spam, how many actually are?" Recall = TP / (TP + FN) "Of all the spam emails, how many did I catch?" F₁ Score = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall) Harmonic mean of precision and recall.

The Cost Asymmetry: A low threshold catches more spam (high recall) but sends legitimate emails to spam (high false positives). A high threshold protects legitimate email but lets spam through. Most services choose a threshold that keeps false positives very low, accepting some missed spam.

Wrapping Up: Why Naive Bayes Still Dominates

Despite its age (the theorem is from 1763) and its naive independence assumption, Bayesian spam filtering remains state-of-the-art in many contexts. Why?

  1. Interpretability: You can see which words contribute to a spam score. This transparency is invaluable for debugging and trust.
  2. Speed: Bayesian filters are extremely fast. They scale to millions of emails with minimal computational overhead.
  3. Robustness: The model does not overfit. Even though the independence assumption is violated, the classifier generalises well.
  4. Adaptability: You can easily update probabilities as new spam patterns emerge. No need to retrain a complex neural network.

Modern spam filters often combine Naive Bayes with other techniques (machine learning, reputation systems, content analysis), but the Bayesian foundation remains essential. Every time you clear a spam message from your inbox—or thankfully, never see it at all—you are witnessing probability theory at work.

Historical note: Thomas Bayes was a Presbyterian minister who published his theorem posthumously in 1763. He never would have imagined it would one day protect billions of people from unwanted email. Mathematics is full of such surprises.

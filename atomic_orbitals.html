Atomic Orbitals

Where electrons live — probability clouds in three dimensions

Chapter 16 · Chemistry · Topic 6 · Difficulty 2

An electron in an atom does not orbit the nucleus like a planet around a star. Instead, it exists as a probability cloud described by a wavefunction ψ(r, θ, φ). The shape and size of this cloud — the atomic orbital — is governed by three quantum numbers that emerge naturally from solving the Schrödinger equation for hydrogen-like atoms.

ψn,l,m(r,θ,φ) = Rn,l(r) · Yl,m(θ,φ)

The quantum number rules: n = 1, 2, 3, … (shell); l = 0, 1, …, n−1 (subshell: s, p, d, f); ml = −l, …, +l (orientation). Radial nodes = n − l − 1. Angular nodes = l.

2. Radial Wavefunctions

The radial part R(r) determines how the wavefunction varies with distance from the nucleus. R²(r)·r² is the radial probability density — the probability of finding the electron at distance r.

Display

The number of times R(r) crosses zero (excluding r = 0 and r → ∞) equals the number of radial nodes = n − l − 1. Higher n orbitals extend further from the nucleus.

3. Angular Wavefunctions

The angular part Yl,m(θ,φ) gives the directional shape: spheres, dumbbells, cloverleaves. Drag to rotate. Positive lobes are amber, negative lobes are cyan.

s orbitals (l = 0) are spherically symmetric. p orbitals (l = 1) have two lobes. d orbitals (l = 2) have four lobes (cloverleaf pattern). The sign of the wavefunction matters for chemical bonding — lobes of the same sign overlap constructively.

The Aufbau Principle

Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy, roughly following n + l. Within a subshell, electrons spread out with parallel spins first (Hund’s rule) before pairing (Pauli exclusion allows at most two electrons per orbital with opposite spins). The exceptions — Cr ([Ar] 3d&sup5; 4s¹) and Cu ([Ar] 3d¹&sup0; 4s¹) — arise because half-filled and fully-filled d subshells carry extra stability.

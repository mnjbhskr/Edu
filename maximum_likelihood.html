Maximum Likelihood Estimation

●●●● Advanced

Finding the parameters that make observed data most probable

1. Log-Likelihood Surface

For data drawn from N(μ, σ²), the log-likelihood is a function of μ and σ. The MLE sits at the peak. Contour lines show equal log-likelihood values.

2. Newton-Raphson Optimisation

Watch Newton-Raphson climb the log-likelihood for estimating the mean of a normal distribution. Each step uses the score and curvature to jump towards the MLE.

Newton-Raphson converges quadratically near the maximum. Each iteration: θk+1 = θk − [ℓ″(θk)]−1 ℓ′(θk). The curvature (second derivative) determines the step size.
MLE properties and regularity conditions
Under regularity conditions, the MLE is:
Consistent: θ̂MLE converges in probability to θ0 as n → ∞.
Asymptotically normal: √n(θ̂ − θ0) → N(0, I(θ0)−1) where I is the Fisher information.
Asymptotically efficient: No consistent estimator has smaller asymptotic variance — MLE attains the Cramér-Rao lower bound.
Invariant: If θ̂ is the MLE of θ, then g(θ̂) is the MLE of g(θ) for any function g.

The regularity conditions include: the parameter space is open; the support of the density does not depend on θ; and the log-likelihood is sufficiently smooth (three times differentiable).
Where you will see this: MLE underpins most of modern statistics and econometrics. Probit and logit models, ARMA models, GARCH volatility models, and structural equation models are all estimated by MLE. In finance, the Black-Scholes implied volatility is found by maximising the likelihood of observed option prices.

3. Asymptotic Properties — Monte Carlo

Run thousands of MLE estimates at different sample sizes. As n grows, the sampling distribution concentrates around the true parameter. The Cramér-Rao lower bound (CRLB) gives the minimum achievable variance.

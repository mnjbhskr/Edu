Teaching machines to create

Generative models learn the probability distribution of training data and then draw new samples from it. From GANs’ adversarial game to VAEs’ smooth latent spaces and diffusion models’ noise-to-signal trick, these architectures underpin modern AI creativity — image generation, text synthesis, drug discovery, and more. Here we explore the core ideas visually, watching each approach transform noise into structure.

2 · GANs: The Adversarial Game

Two networks compete: the generator creates fakes, the discriminator tries to catch them. At equilibrium, the generator wins.

The adversarial game: GANs pit two networks against each other: the generator tries to fool the discriminator, and the discriminator tries to catch fakes. At equilibrium, the generator produces data indistinguishable from real — and the discriminator is reduced to guessing (50% accuracy).

3 · VAEs: The Probabilistic Encoder

Why the KL penalty? VAEs force the latent space to be smooth and regular by penalising deviation from a normal distribution. This means you can generate new data by simply sampling from a normal distribution and decoding. The β parameter controls the trade-off: high β means a tidier latent space but blurrier reconstructions.

Summary

Generative models transform noise into structured data by learning the underlying probability distribution. GANs use an adversarial game — elegant but sometimes unstable. VAEs impose structure through variational inference, yielding smooth latent spaces ideal for interpolation. Diffusion models, the newest of the three, learn to reverse a noise-corruption process one small step at a time, achieving the best of both worlds: stable training and high-quality samples.

Together, these architectures power the generative AI revolution — from image synthesis and drug discovery to music composition and code generation. Understanding how they work demystifies the “magic” and reveals the elegant mathematics beneath.

Electromagnetism

Forces between charged particles and the fields they create

Electromagnetism unifies two seemingly different phenomena — the static cling of a rubbed balloon and the invisible pull of a magnet — into a single, elegant framework. At its heart lie just a few laws: Coulomb’s inverse-square force between charges, the Lorentz force on a moving charge in electric and magnetic fields, and Faraday’s law of induction. Together these underpin everything from circuit design to radio waves to the structure of atoms.

Like charges repel, opposite charges attract. The force falls off as the square of the distance — double the separation, quarter the force. Notice how the field lines curve from positive to negative charges.

2. Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge

The cross product F = qv × B means the force is always perpendicular to the velocity, producing circular motion.

E only: parabolic path (constant acceleration). B only: circular motion. E + B parallel: helical path. Crossed E × B: cycloidal drift — the particle drifts sideways at velocity E/B, the principle behind velocity selectors.

4. Electromagnetic Induction

Bar magnet + conducting loop — observe induced EMF

Faraday’s law: EMF = −dΦ/dt. Moving the magnet changes the flux through the loop, inducing a current.

20
EMF = −dΦ/dt = 0.00 V
Faraday’s law tells us that a changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an electromotive force. Lenz’s law adds that the induced current opposes the change that produced it — nature resists change. This is the basis of generators, transformers, and wireless charging.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Key Equations
Coulomb’s Law:   F = k q&sub1;q&sub2; / r²
Magnetic Force:   F = q v × B
Lorentz Force:    F = qE + q v × B
Faraday’s Law:   EMF = −dΦ/dt
These four relationships are the building blocks of classical electromagnetism. They are all encoded within Maxwell’s four equations, which unify electricity, magnetism, and light into a single coherent theory:
∇ · E = ρ / ε&sub0;    (Gauss’s law)
∇ · B = 0            (No magnetic monopoles)
∇ × E = −∂B/∂t   (Faraday’s law)
∇ × B = μ&sub0;J + μ&sub0;ε&sub0; ∂E/∂t  (Ampère–Maxwell)
Connections: Coulomb’s law follows from Gauss’s law for static charges. The Lorentz force governs how charges respond to fields. Faraday’s law of induction — the principle behind every electric generator — is Maxwell’s third equation. Together they predict electromagnetic waves travelling at c = 1/√(μ&sub0;ε&sub0;), unifying optics with electromagnetism.
From Coulomb’s static force to Faraday’s changing flux, the thread is the same: charges create fields, fields exert forces, and changing fields create more fields. That self-sustaining cycle is light itself.

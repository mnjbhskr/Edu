When They Lie

Every representation distorts — but some distortions are errors and some are deceptions.

Core idea Representations mislead through mathematical distortion, statistical manipulation, or perceptual exploitation — distinguishing these requires knowing what the representation is supposed to preserve.

1. The Truncated Axis — Amplifying Noise into Narrative

AXIS MANIPULATION DEMO

0 
Axis truncation amplification:

True % change     = (max − min) / min × 100%

Visual % change   = (max − min) / (axis_max − axis_min) × 100%

Amplification ratio = visual% / true% = min / (axis_max − axis_min) × (scale constant)

Example: data from 1000 to 1050 (+5% actual change)
  Full axis (0 to 1200)     → visual 4.2% of chart height
  Truncated (980 to 1060)   → visual 62.5% of chart height
  Amplification ratio       ≈ 15×
💡 A truncated axis is not always dishonest. When showing tiny but important absolute changes — body temperature from 36°C to 39°C, say — a full axis starting at 0°C makes the clinically critical variation invisible. The question is whether visual magnitude corresponds to the relative or absolute significance of the change. An honest chart makes the choice explicit.

💡 Every time series chart shows a window. The choice of start date is itself a representation decision. A stock chart from a market peak will show decline; the same chart from a trough will show spectacular growth. This is not a lie about the data — it is a true statement about the chosen window. The deception lies in implying that the window is the full story.

3. Error Bars — The Missing Information

ERROR BAR EXPLORER

30 
Standard deviation (SD)  = spread of the data

Standard error (SE)      = SD / √n      — uncertainty about the mean

95% Confidence Interval = mean ± 1.96 × SE

Welch's t-test (unequal variances):
    t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)

A chart of means without error bars is incomplete:
  it shows where the mean is, not how certain we are.
Two means that look different without error bars may overlap entirely
  when 95% CIs are drawn — and vice versa.
💡 A confidence interval is not a range of possible values for the data — it is a range of plausible values for the population mean, given the sample. Presenting means without intervals hides the most important information: how much should I trust this estimate? A chart without uncertainty quantification is a representation that has discarded its own reliability.

💡 Each deception exploits a specific weakness: axis truncation exploits linear area encoding; pie slices exploit poor angular discrimination; 3D charts exploit foreshortening; dual axes exploit arbitrary rescaling; smoothing exploits the seductive clarity of a trendline. Knowing the mechanism is the first defence.
What have we learnt?
Representations lie in three ways: inevitably (every projection distorts), accidentally (poor choices that mislead unintentionally), and deliberately (choices designed to mislead). Mathematical literacy is the ability to distinguish between these. An axis starting at zero is not always more honest — it depends on whether absolute or relative magnitude is the meaningful quantity. A truncated axis is not always dishonest — it depends on context. What is always dishonest is hiding uncertainty, hiding the choice of window, and encoding magnitude through a channel (area, angle) that systematically underestimates differences. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

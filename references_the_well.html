Acknowledging the researchers, datasets, and open-source projects that inspire our simulations

The Well — A Large-Scale Collection of Diverse Numerical Simulations

Several of our physics simulations are inspired by The Well, a 15 TB benchmark collection of numerical physics simulations curated by PolymathicAI. Published as a NeurIPS 2024 Datasets and Benchmarks paper, The Well spans 16 physical systems — from acoustic scattering and thermal convection to magnetohydrodynamics and supernova explosions.

PolymathicAI — The Well

NeurIPS 2024 · Datasets and Benchmarks Track · BSD-3-Clause

The Well provides pre-computed, high-resolution simulation data in HDF5 format, designed to accelerate machine-learning research on spatiotemporal physical systems. Our interactive pages solve the same underlying partial differential equations in real time using simplified numerical methods — trading resolution for interactivity so that students can explore these phenomena directly in the browser.

GitHub Repository → Project Page → arXiv Paper →
How we use The Well. We do not embed or redistribute any data from The Well. Instead, we implement the same governing equations (Navier–Stokes, Euler, Maxwell, reaction–diffusion) as interactive Canvas simulations. Where The Well runs these at 512×512 over millions of timesteps on HPC clusters, our browser implementations use coarser grids (64–200 cells) with simplified solvers to achieve real-time interactivity.

Scientific References

Thermal Convection

Henri Bénard (1900) — first systematic experiments on convection cells in thin fluid layers heated from below.
Lord Rayleigh (1916) — linear stability analysis establishing the critical Rayleigh number Rac ≈ 1708.
S. Chandrasekhar, Hydrodynamic and Hydromagnetic Stability (1961) — comprehensive theoretical treatment.

Magnetohydrodynamics

Hannes Alfvén (1942) — discovery of magnetohydrodynamic waves (Nobel Prize in Physics, 1970).
D. Biskamp, Magnetohydrodynamic Turbulence (2003) — modern treatment of MHD cascade theory.

Blast Waves & Astrophysics

L. I. Sedov (1946) & G. I. Taylor (1950) — independent derivation of the self-similar point-explosion solution, R(t) ∝ t2/5.
Ya. B. Zel’dovich & Yu. P. Raizer, Physics of Shock Waves and High-Temperature Hydrodynamic Phenomena (1967).

Reaction–Diffusion & Pattern Formation

Alan Turing (1952) — “The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis” — first proposal that reaction–diffusion systems can generate spatial patterns.
J. E. Pearson (1993) — catalogued the astonishing variety of patterns in the Gray–Scott model.
P. Gray & S. K. Scott (1984) — original autocatalytic model with feed and kill parameters.

Acoustic Scattering

Hermann von Helmholtz (1858) — foundational work on acoustics and the Helmholtz equation.
Lord Rayleigh, The Theory of Sound (1877) — classical treatment of wave scattering and diffraction.

Numerical Methods

Methods Used in Our Browser Simulations

Method Used For Trade-offs vs Research Grade
Finite Differences (explicit) Wave equation, heat equation Simple, stable at small CFL; lower order than spectral methods
Jacobi Iteration Poisson equation solves (streamfunction, pressure) Easy to implement; converges slowly vs multigrid or FFT
Lax–Friedrichs Scheme Compressible Euler equations (supernova) Robust shock-capturing; more diffusive than Godunov-type solvers
Runge–Kutta 4th Order (RK4) ODE integration (Lorenz, Rössler, particle tracing) Accurate for smooth ODEs; overkill for stiff systems
Gray–Scott Explicit Euler Reaction–diffusion pattern formation Fast per step; requires small dt for stability
Resolution vs interactivity. Research simulations in The Well typically run on grids of 256×256 to 1024×1024 with millions of timesteps on high-performance computing clusters. Our browser implementations use grids of 64–200 cells per side with simplified boundary conditions, achieving 30–60 fps on a modern laptop. The qualitative behaviour — convection cells, shock fronts, turbulent cascades — is faithfully reproduced, even if quantitative accuracy is reduced.

About PolymathicAI

PolymathicAI is a research collaboration building foundation models for scientific computing. Their team spans the Flatiron Institute, the University of Colorado Boulder, and Princeton University, among others. Beyond The Well, their work includes AION (large omnimodal models for astronomy), AstroCLIP (multimodal contrastive pretraining), and xVal (numerical encoding research).

Vector Spaces & Dimension

A spider chart is a projection — and every projection discards something

Core idea Every projection of high-dimensional data onto a 2D surface loses information — the question is which information, and whether that loss is acceptable.

When data has many attributes it lives in a high-dimensional vector space. To see it, we must project it onto a 2D page. Different projections preserve different properties. Spider charts project onto a polygon. PCA projects onto directions of maximum variance. The curse of dimensionality tells us that in high dimensions our geometric intuition quietly breaks.

1. Spider Charts — Beautiful Lies

INTERACTIVE SPIDER CHART

DATA TABLE

Spider chart: axis i has unit vector uᵢ at angle 2πi/n
A point with scores (s₁, s₂, …, sₙ) maps to:
    P = Σ sᵢ uᵢ   (linear combination of axis vectors)

Enclosed polygon area depends on the order of axes:
    Area = (1/2) |Σᵢ sᵢ sᵢ₊₁ sin(2π/n)|

Re-ordering axes changes area without changing the data.
💡 A spider chart’s enclosed area is not a meaningful measure of “overall performance” — it depends on the arbitrary order of axes. Two athletes with identical data can produce polygons of different areas simply by rotating which axis comes first. This is a projection artefact, not a feature of the data.

2. PCA — Finding the Best View

2

VARIANCE EXPLAINED

PCA finds directions w that maximise the variance of the projected data:
    maximise  Var(Xw) = wᵀ Σ w   subject to ||w|| = 1

Solution: eigenvectors of the covariance matrix Σ = (1/n) XᵀX
    First  PC: eigenvector with largest eigenvalue (most variance)
    Second PC: eigenvector with next largest, orthogonal to first

Variance explained by k components:
    R² = (λ₁ + … + λₖ) / (λ₁ + … + λₙ)

Closed form for a 2×2 covariance matrix (a b ; b c):
    λ = ((a+c) ± √((a−c)² + 4b²)) / 2
💡 PCA finds the most informative linear projection — the direction that loses the least variance. But it only sees linear structure. If the data lies on a curved manifold, PCA will unfold it incorrectly. That is why t-SNE and UMAP exist: to preserve non-linear neighbourhood structure that PCA cannot see.

3. The Curse of Dimensionality

DIMENSION EXPLORER

2

Vᶯ / 2ᵈ vs DIMENSION

Volume of the unit sphere in d dimensions:
    Vᶯ = π^(d/2) / Γ(d/2 + 1)

Ratio to the bounding cube of edge 2:
    Vᶯ / 2ᵈ → 0   as d → ∞

Implication: in high dimensions, almost all of the cube’s volume lies near
its corners, far from the centre. Almost all pairs of random points become
approximately equidistant. “Nearest neighbour” loses its meaning.
💡 In 1000 dimensions — typical of modern ML models — a randomly placed point is almost certainly near the edge of the data space, roughly equidistant from all others. This is why raw high-dimensional data is nearly unclusterable, and why dimensionality reduction is not optional preprocessing — it is mathematically necessary.
What have we learnt?
High-dimensional data is invisible to human perception. Representation requires projection — a mathematical operation that reduces dimension while attempting to preserve the structure that matters. Spider charts project onto a polygon and accidentally mislead through area encoding. PCA projects onto the directions of maximum variance and loses non-linear structure. Every projection makes a choice: what to preserve and what to sacrifice. The curse of dimensionality tells us that in high dimensions our geometric intuition fails entirely — which is why the mathematics matters so much. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

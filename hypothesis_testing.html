Hypothesis Testing

●●●● Intermediate

Assume, collect, decide — the logic of testing claims with data

1. Introduction

Hypothesis testing is the backbone of statistical reasoning. The core logic is beautifully simple: assume something is true (the null hypothesis, H₀), collect data, then ask “how unlikely is this data if H₀ is true?” If the data turns out to be very unlikely under that assumption, we reject H₀.

Think of it like a courtroom trial. The defendant is presumed innocent (this is our null hypothesis). The prosecution presents evidence. A jury evaluates whether the evidence is so overwhelming that the presumption of innocence can no longer stand. If so, the jury convicts — they “reject the null hypothesis”. If the evidence is insufficient, the defendant is found not guilty — which is not the same as being proven innocent. Similarly, failing to reject H₀ does not prove it is true.

The strength of the evidence is measured by the p-value: the probability of observing data at least as extreme as what we collected, assuming H₀ is true. A small p-value means the data would be very surprising under H₀, giving us grounds to reject it.

A common misconception: the p-value is not the probability that H₀ is true. It is the probability of observing data this extreme given that H₀ is true. This distinction matters enormously in practice.

2. Key Language

Essential Terminology

TermDefinitionExample
Null hypothesis (H₀) The default claim we assume to be true. Usually a statement of “no effect” or a specific parameter value. H₀: p = 0.5 (coin is fair)
Alternative hypothesis (H₁) What we are trying to find evidence for. The claim that contradicts H₀. H₁: p > 0.5 (coin is biased towards heads)
Significance level (α) The threshold probability below which we reject H₀. Chosen before collecting data. α = 0.05 (5%)
Test statistic A value calculated from the sample data that summarises the evidence against H₀. Z = (x̄ − μ₀) / (σ/√n)
p-value The probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one calculated, assuming H₀ is true. p = 0.021
Critical value The boundary value of the test statistic beyond which we reject H₀. Zcrit = 1.645 (one-tailed, 5%)
Critical region The set of test statistic values that lead to rejecting H₀. Also called the rejection region. Z > 1.645
One-tailed vs two-tailed One-tailed tests for a change in one direction only; two-tailed tests for any change (increase or decrease). H₁: p > 0.5 (one-tailed)
H₁: p ≠ 0.5 (two-tailed)

3. Binomial Hypothesis Test

Testing a Proportion

When we are testing a claim about a proportion, and each trial has exactly two outcomes (success or failure), we use the binomial distribution under H₀.

The setup: we hypothesise H₀: p = p₀, where p is the true probability of success. We observe X successes in n independent trials. Under H₀, X follows a Binomial(n, p₀) distribution. We calculate the probability of getting a result at least as extreme as what we observed, and compare it with our significance level α.

Worked Example: Is this coin fair?

A coin is flipped 20 times and lands heads 15 times. Is there evidence at the 5% significance level that the coin is biased towards heads?

H₀: p = 0.5   (coin is fair)
H₁: p > 0.5   (coin is biased towards heads)
One-tailed test, α = 0.05

Under H₀, X ~ Binomial(20, 0.5). We need P(X ≥ 15):

P(X ≥ 15) = P(X=15) + P(X=16) + ... + P(X=20)
= C(20,15)·0.5²₀ + C(20,16)·0.5²₀ + ... + C(20,20)·0.5²₀
= 0.0207

Since 0.0207 < 0.05, we reject H₀. There is sufficient evidence at the 5% level that the coin is biased towards heads.

For a one-tailed upper test, the p-value is P(X ≥ observed). For a one-tailed lower test, it is P(X ≤ observed). For a two-tailed test, double the smaller tail probability.

5. Normal Hypothesis Test

Testing a Mean

When we are testing a claim about a population mean μ, and we know (or can estimate) the population standard deviation σ, we use the normal distribution. Under H₀: μ = μ₀, the test statistic is:

Z = (x̄ − μ₀) / (σ / √n)

If the population is normally distributed (or n is large enough for the Central Limit Theorem to apply), then Z follows a standard normal distribution under H₀. We compare our calculated Z with the critical value from the normal distribution.

Worked Example: Factory weight check

A factory claims its cereal boxes have a mean weight of 500g, with σ = 18g. A random sample of 36 boxes gives a mean of 495g. Test at the 5% significance level whether the mean weight is less than claimed.

H₀: μ = 500   (mean weight is 500g)
H₁: μ < 500   (mean weight is less than 500g)
One-tailed lower test, α = 0.05
Z = (495 − 500) / (18 / √36)
  = −5 / 3
  = −1.667

The critical value for a one-tailed lower test at 5% is Z = −1.645.

Since −1.667 < −1.645, the test statistic falls in the critical region. We reject H₀. There is sufficient evidence at the 5% level that the mean weight is less than 500g.

When σ is unknown and estimated by the sample standard deviation s, we should technically use a t-distribution. However, for large samples (n ≥ 30), the t-distribution is very close to the normal distribution, so the Z-test is a good approximation.

7. Worked Examples

A-Level Practice Problems

Problem 1 — Binomial: Defective items

A manufacturer claims that 90% of items produced are not defective. A quality inspector takes a random sample of 20 items and finds that 16 are not defective. Test at the 5% significance level whether the proportion of non-defective items is less than claimed.

H₀: p = 0.9   (proportion not defective is 90%)
H₁: p < 0.9   (proportion is less than 90%)
One-tailed lower test, α = 0.05

Under H₀, X ~ Binomial(20, 0.9), where X is the number of non-defective items. We observed X = 16.

For a lower-tail test, we need P(X ≤ 16):

P(X ≤ 16) = ∑ P(X = k) for k = 0, 1, ..., 16
= 1 − P(X ≥ 17)
= 1 − [P(X=17) + P(X=18) + P(X=19) + P(X=20)]
= 1 − [0.1901 + 0.2852 + 0.2702 + 0.1216]
= 1 − 0.8670
= 0.1330

Since 0.1330 > 0.05, we do not reject H₀.

Conclusion: There is insufficient evidence at the 5% significance level to suggest the proportion of non-defective items is less than 90%. The sample result (80%) is unusual but not unusual enough to reject the manufacturer’s claim.

Problem 2 — Normal: Journey time

A bus company claims the mean journey time for a particular route is 35 minutes. A commuter believes it takes longer. She records 50 journeys and finds a mean of 37 minutes with a sample standard deviation of 8 minutes. Test at the 1% significance level.

H₀: μ = 35   (mean journey time is 35 minutes)
H₁: μ > 35   (mean journey time is more than 35 minutes)
One-tailed upper test, α = 0.01
Z = (37 − 35) / (8 / √50)
  = 2 / 1.1314
  = 1.768

The critical value for a one-tailed upper test at 1% is Z = 2.326.

Since 1.768 < 2.326, the test statistic does not fall in the critical region.

The p-value is P(Z > 1.768) = 1 − Φ(1.768) ≈ 0.0385.

Conclusion: We do not reject H₀ at the 1% significance level. There is insufficient evidence to conclude that the mean journey time exceeds 35 minutes. (Note: we would reject at the 5% level, since 0.0385 < 0.05 — the choice of α matters.)

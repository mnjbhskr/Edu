Molecular Symmetry & Group Theory

The mathematics that reveals what molecules can and cannot do

Chapter 16 · Chemistry · Topic 1 · Difficulty 2

Every molecule has a shape, and that shape has symmetry. A water molecule looks the same after a 180° rotation; benzene survives six rotations and six reflections unchanged. Group theory — the mathematics of symmetry — turns these geometric observations into precise predictions: which vibrations absorb infrared light, which scatter Raman light, and which molecular orbitals can combine. Symmetry is the deepest shortcut in chemistry.

Why chemists care: Symmetry tells chemists what they can measure without doing the experiment: (1) Which vibrational modes are IR-active or Raman-active, (2) Which electronic transitions are allowed or forbidden, (3) Which atomic orbitals can combine to form molecular orbitals. Group theory replaces trial-and-error with mathematical certainty.

Key insight: A symmetry operation transforms a molecule into a configuration indistinguishable from the original. The identity E always works; rotations Cn turn by 360°/n; reflections σ mirror through a plane; inversion i maps every point through the centre. These are the building blocks of molecular symmetry.

2. Point Groups

Select molecule

Point group: C₂ᵥ
Key insight: The complete set of symmetry operations for a molecule forms a mathematical group — it is closed under composition, associative, has an identity, and every element has an inverse. This group is called the molecule's point group because at least one point stays fixed under every operation.

Key insight: The multiplication table encodes the entire structure of the group. Every row and column is a permutation of the group elements (this is the rearrangement theorem). If you know the table, you can predict the result of any sequence of symmetry operations without ever drawing the molecule.

4. Character Tables

Point group

Each row is an irreducible representation — the fundamental building blocks of the group's action.
Key insight: The character table distils each irreducible representation into a single number (the character) per class of operations. These characters are all you need to decompose any reducible representation — like the 3N-dimensional representation of molecular vibrations — into its irreducible components.
Orbitals and symmetry: The p orbitals (px, py, pz) transform exactly like the x, y, z coordinates under symmetry operations. In the C2v point group of water, px belongs to the B1 representation, py to B2, and pz to A1. This is why character tables list ‘x, y, z’ — they tell you how orbitals transform.
Degenerate orbitals: When a representation has dimension > 1 (like E in C3v), the corresponding orbitals are degenerate — they have the same energy. The two E orbitals in ammonia (dx²−y² and dxy, or px and py) cannot be separated by any perturbation that preserves the molecular symmetry.

5. Why Symmetry Matters

Vibrational modes of H₂O

Mode: Symmetric stretch · IR: active · Raman: active
Selection rules:
IR active: mode transforms as x, y, or z (same symmetry as a translation)
Raman active: mode transforms as x², y², z², xy, xz, or yz (same symmetry as a polarisability component)
Key insight: In molecules with a centre of inversion, no vibration can be both IR-active and Raman-active — this is the rule of mutual exclusion. Water (no inversion centre) has all three modes active in both; CO2 (with inversion) neatly separates them. Symmetry alone, without any calculation, tells you what spectroscopy can see.
Did you know? The buckminsterfullerene molecule (C60) has the same symmetry as a football — the icosahedral point group Ih with 120 symmetry operations. Despite having 60 atoms and 174 vibrational modes, symmetry predicts that only 4 are IR-active and 10 are Raman-active. This was confirmed experimentally, spectacularly validating group theory.

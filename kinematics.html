Kinematics — The Mathematics of Motion

●●●● Beginner

Describing motion without asking why — displacement, velocity, acceleration and time

1. Introduction

Kinematics is the branch of mechanics that describes how objects move, without concerning itself with why they move. We leave forces to dynamics; here, we focus purely on the geometry of motion.

Four quantities fully describe one-dimensional motion:

QuantitySymbolSI UnitScalar / Vector
Displacement s m (metres) Vector
Velocity v m/s (metres per second) Vector
Acceleration a m/s² (metres per second squared) Vector
Time t s (seconds) Scalar
Scalar vs vector: A scalar has magnitude only (e.g. speed = 5 m/s). A vector has both magnitude and direction (e.g. velocity = 5 m/s eastward). Displacement is the vector equivalent of distance; velocity is the vector equivalent of speed.

We also distinguish between initial velocity (u) at the start of motion and final velocity (v) at a later time. This notation is standard in British mathematics and physics.

2. The SUVAT Equations

Constant Acceleration Formulae

When acceleration is constant, the five SUVAT equations relate the four kinematic variables. Each equation omits one variable — this is the key to choosing the right equation for a given problem.

EquationVariablesMissing
v = u + at v, u, a, t s
s = ut + ½at² s, u, a, t v
s = vt − ½at² s, v, a, t u
v² = u² + 2as v, u, a, s t
s = ½(u + v)t s, u, v, t a
▶ Deriving the suvat equations from v = u + at

We start from the definition of constant acceleration:

(1) v = u + at   — directly from a = (v − u) / t

Average velocity for constant acceleration is (u + v) / 2. Displacement = average velocity × time:

(5) s = ½(u + v)t

Substitute v = u + at into equation (5):

s = ½(u + u + at)t = ½(2u + at)t
(2) s = ut + ½at²

Rearrange (1) to get t = (v − u) / a and substitute into (5):

s = ½(u + v) × (v − u) / a = (v² − u²) / 2a
(4) v² = u² + 2as

Rearrange (1) to get u = v − at and substitute into (5):

s = ½(v − at + v)t = ½(2v − at)t
(3) s = vt − ½at²

5. Variable Acceleration

When Acceleration is not Constant

The SUVAT equations assume constant acceleration. When acceleration varies with time — that is, a = f(t) — we must use calculus instead.

Differentiation:
  v = ds/dt     a = dv/dt = d²s/dt²

Integration:
  v = ∫ a  dt     s = ∫ v  dt

Worked Example

Given: a(t) = 6t − 2 m/s²,   u = 3 m/s at t = 0,   s = 0 at t = 0
Step 1: Find v(t) by integrating a(t):
  v = ∫(6t − 2) dt = 3t² − 2t + C
  At t = 0: v = 3, so C = 3
  v(t) = 3t² − 2t + 3
Step 2: Find s(t) by integrating v(t):
  s = ∫(3t² − 2t + 3) dt = t³ − t² + 3t + C
  At t = 0: s = 0, so C = 0
  s(t) = t³ − t² + 3t
The constant of integration C is determined by initial conditions. Without them, the solution is a family of curves rather than a unique trajectory.

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Ball Thrown Upwards

A ball is thrown vertically upwards at 20 m/s. Taking g = 9.8 m/s² downwards, find (a) the maximum height and (b) the total time to return to the starting point.

▶ Show solution

Setup: Take upwards as positive. u = 20, a = −9.8, v = 0 at max height.

(a) Maximum height:
v² = u² + 2as
0 = 20² + 2(−9.8)s
19.6s = 400
s = 20.41 m
(b) Time to return:
At the starting point, s = 0:
s = ut + ½at²
0 = 20t + ½(−9.8)t²
0 = t(20 − 4.9t)
t = 0 (launch)  or  t = 4.08 s (return)

Example 2 — Car Accelerating

A car is travelling at 10 m/s and accelerates uniformly at 2 m/s². Find the distance travelled in 5 seconds.

▶ Show solution

Given: u = 10, a = 2, t = 5. Find s.

s = ut + ½at²
s = (10)(5) + ½(2)(5)²
s = 50 + 25
s = 75 m

Example 3 — Variable Acceleration

A particle moves along a straight line with velocity v = 3t² − 4t + 1 m/s. Find (a) the acceleration and (b) the displacement at t = 3 s, given s = 0 at t = 0.

▶ Show solution
(a) Acceleration:
a = dv/dt = d/dt(3t² − 4t + 1)
a(t) = 6t − 4
At t = 3: a = 6(3) − 4 = 14 m/s²
(b) Displacement:
s = ∫v dt = ∫(3t² − 4t + 1) dt
s = t³ − 2t² + t + C
At t = 0, s = 0 ⇒ C = 0
s(3) = 27 − 18 + 3 = 12 m

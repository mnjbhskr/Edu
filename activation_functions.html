Activation Functions

Non-linearity that enables neural network expressiveness

Intermediate

Without activation functions, a neural network — no matter how deep — collapses into a single linear transformation. Activation functions inject non-linearity, letting networks carve complex decision boundaries, approximate any continuous function, and learn representations that linear models never could. Here we explore why they matter, how each one behaves, and the practical pitfalls that shaped modern architecture design.

Why it matters: Sigmoid’s maximum derivative is 0.25. After n layers, the gradient can shrink by a factor of 0.25n. With 10 layers that’s roughly 10−6 — the early layers barely learn at all.

4 · Dead Neurons

ReLU neurons can “die” — stuck at zero output for all inputs. Higher learning rates kill more neurons. Leaky ReLU offers a fix.

Activation

0.10
Dead neurons: 0 / 64
Dead neuron: When a ReLU neuron’s bias becomes very negative, all inputs produce zero output. Since ReLU’s gradient at zero is also zero, the neuron can never recover. Leaky ReLU allows a small gradient (typically 0.01) for negative inputs, keeping neurons alive.

Summary

Activation functions are the heartbeat of neural networks. Without them, depth is an illusion — every deep linear network collapses to a single layer. The sigmoid era gave way to ReLU for speed and gradient health, while modern architectures increasingly favour smooth alternatives like GELU and Swish that balance expressiveness with optimisation stability.

Choosing an activation is rarely about raw accuracy alone. Consider gradient flow (vanishing and exploding), computational cost, whether zero-centred outputs matter, and robustness to aggressive learning rates. When in doubt, start with ReLU; reach for GELU or Swish when fine-tuning transformers or seeking that last percentage point.

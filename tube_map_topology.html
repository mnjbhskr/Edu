The Tube Map

How Harry Beck threw away geography — and made mathematics more honest.

Core idea Topology is the mathematics of connection without distance — and choosing topology over geometry was one of the most useful design decisions of the 20th century.

2. Euler's Formula — Topology's Fingerprint

EULER CHARACTERISTIC EXPLORER

For any connected planar graph:

    V − E + F = 2

This number 2 is the Euler characteristic of the sphere —
a topological invariant unchanged by any continuous deformation.
A torus has Euler characteristic 0. The Tube map and the real
Underground share the same Euler characteristic because one is a
homeomorphism of the other: a continuous, invertible deformation
that preserves every vertex, every edge, and every enclosed region.
💡 The Euler characteristic is a topological fingerprint. Stretch, compress, or rearrange the Underground however you like — as long as you neither add nor remove connections and keep the drawing planar, V − E + F = 2. Break planarity (force a crossing) and the formula is no longer guaranteed: the graph no longer embeds on the sphere.

3. Topology in Other Transit Systems — Three Choices

THREE MAPS, THREE MATHEMATICAL CHOICES

London Underground (topological)

Preserves: connectivity, order of stops, interchanges. Sacrifices: real distance, angle, area. Beck, 1931.

City bus network (geographic)

Preserves: geographic position, walking distance to a stop. Sacrifices: legibility — dense, tangled, bent to follow real streets.

Regional rail (hybrid)

Preserves: rough geography plus clean schematic within the core. Sacrifices: exact distances on straightened trunk lines. A compromise — as all hybrids are.
💡 Every transit map is the answer to a different question. "Where am I?" favours geography. "Which train do I take?" favours topology. There is no map that answers both questions well — and pretending otherwise is how bad maps are made.
What have we learnt?
Topology is the mathematics of connectivity without measurement. When Beck redesigned the Tube map in 1931, he implicitly chose a mathematical framework — graphs — that captured exactly what travellers needed, and discarded what they did not. This is the fundamental act of representation: choosing which mathematical invariants to preserve. The Euler characteristic tells us that some properties — the number of enclosed regions, the pattern of connections — survive any continuous deformation; others, like distance and angle, do not. Every representation makes this trade. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

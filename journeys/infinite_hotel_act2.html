The Infinite Hotel · Act II
Can we count the fractions?
🔢

In Act I, the hotel absorbed an infinite bus. We matched whole numbers with even numbers — a perfect pairing. Both sets are the same "size."

Now a harder question. Between the numbers 0 and 1, there are infinitely many fractions: ½, ⅓, ¼, ⅕, 1/100, 1/1000...

And between ½ and ⅓, there are more fractions. And between those, more still.

Can we count them? Can we list them all — 1st fraction, 2nd fraction, 3rd fraction...?

Try This

Think about it before you continue. Are there more fractions than whole numbers? Or the same number? Or is the question even meaningful? Sit with your answer for a moment.

What To Notice

Between any two fractions, there is always another fraction. The fractions seem impossibly dense — packed infinitely close together. Surely that means there are "more" of them?

Why It Matters

Cantor's answer to this question shocked the mathematical world. Whether a set is "bigger" than another is not about how densely packed it feels — it is about whether you can build a bijection between them.

Cantor's grid
Arranging all fractions

Here's Cantor's ingenious idea. Arrange all fractions p/q in a grid — numerators along the top, denominators down the side.

Every fraction appears somewhere in this infinite grid. Now — can we find a path that visits every cell?

Try This

Watch the path again and pick any fraction — say 3/4. Can you figure out when it would be visited? Follow the diagonals: it sits on the diagonal where numerator + denominator = 7.

What To Notice

By zigzagging diagonally through the grid, Cantor created a list of every fraction. First 1/1, then 1/2, then 2/1, then 3/1, then 2/2, then 1/3... Every fraction appears exactly once. No fraction is missed.

Why It Matters

Because we can list the fractions 1st, 2nd, 3rd..., we have a bijection between the fractions and the whole numbers. They are the same size — even though fractions are infinitely dense. Density and size are completely different things for infinite sets.

A new word
Countably infinite

We can list the fractions: 1st, 2nd, 3rd... Every fraction eventually appears on the list. We say the fractions are countably infinite.

The whole numbers are countably infinite. The even numbers are countably infinite. The fractions are countably infinite. They're all the same size — and that size has a name.

ℵ₀
Aleph-null (ℵ₀)
The size of the counting numbers.
The smallest infinity.
Any set that can be listed has this size.

Try This

Think of as many different types of infinite sets as you can — odd numbers, negative numbers, multiples of 7. Could you build a listing for each? (Hint: yes, you can.) Now ask: is everything infinite countable?

What To Notice

Whole numbers, even numbers, fractions, prime numbers — they can all be listed. They all have size ℵ₀. It starts to feel like every infinite set might be the same size.

Why It Matters

What about the decimal numbers — 0.123456..., π, √2? Can we list those too? Think carefully. The answer in Act III will shatter this pattern completely. Not all infinities are the same.

The countable infinite
🔢
You listed the fractions
There are infinitely many fractions — but they can all be counted.
ℵ₀: the size of any set you can list.
💡 What you learned

A set is countably infinite if you can match its elements with the counting numbers — 1st, 2nd, 3rd... The whole numbers, even numbers, and all fractions are countably infinite. They all have size ℵ₀. This feels like it should mean everything infinite is the same size. Act III is about to demolish that assumption.

Coming in Act III — The Diagonal Argument

The most beautiful proof in mathematics. You will try to list every decimal number between 0 and 1. Then — in real time — we will construct a decimal that is not on your list. No matter what list you build. The proof is airtight. And it will shatter your intuition about infinity completely.

Try This

Can you list all the fractions between 0 and 1? Try writing them in a grid where row n has denominator n. Then trace a diagonal zigzag path through the grid.

What To Notice

Every fraction appears somewhere on your zigzag path, giving each one a unique counting number. This means the rationals are countable — they can be matched one-to-one with the natural numbers, even though they seem far more numerous.

Why It Matters

This is Cantor's first great surprise: adding fractions to the number line doesn't increase the "size" of infinity. The rationals have the same cardinality as the naturals. But the reals are a different story entirely — that's next.

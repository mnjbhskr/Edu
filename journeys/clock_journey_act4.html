The Code
🔐

Around 50 BCE, Julius Caesar needed to send military orders across the Roman empire without enemies reading them.

His solution: shift every letter by 3. A becomes D. B becomes E. Z wraps around to C.

It's modular arithmetic. On an alphabet of 26 letters — mod 26.

What you'll do in this act

You'll encrypt a real message using Caesar's method. Then you'll be given someone else's ciphertext and shown how to crack it — quickly, using only letter frequencies. Then we'll see why Caesar's cipher fails completely against modern needs.

Try This

Set the shift to 3 (Caesar's original key) and type a short message. Then change the shift to 13 and watch every letter change. Try typing the alphabet itself to see the full mapping.

What To Notice

Each letter shifts by the same amount. A shift of 13 (ROT13) is special: applying it twice returns the original message, because 13 + 13 = 26, and 26 mod 26 = 0. Encryption and decryption use the same key.

Why It Matters

Caesar's cipher is modular arithmetic on the alphabet — mod 26. It shows how encryption works at its simplest: a reversible mathematical operation. But with only 25 possible keys, it is trivially breakable. Real cryptography needs astronomically larger key spaces.

The weakness
Only 25 keys

The Caesar cipher has exactly 25 possible shifts. A computer can try all 25 in less than a millisecond.

Real encryption needs a key space so vast that trying every possibility would take longer than the age of the universe — even for the fastest computer ever built.

That's where prime numbers come back.

Why primes make RSA secure
RSA uses two enormous prime numbers — each hundreds of digits long. Their product N is your public key. To crack RSA, an attacker must factor N back into those two primes.

In Act III, you saw that prime-modulus clocks have complete multiplication cycles — every operation is perfectly reversible. That reversibility is exactly what makes RSA encryption and decryption work cleanly.

And because factoring enormous numbers is computationally impossible, the private key stays private.

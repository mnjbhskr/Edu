The Art of Uncertainty · Act IV
The Prosecutor
⚖️

A crime has been committed. DNA evidence is collected from the scene.

The defendant's DNA matches. The forensic expert testifies: "The chance of a random person matching this DNA profile is 1 in 1,000,000."

The prosecutor stands up and says: "There is a 1 in 1,000,000 chance this defendant is innocent."

Is the prosecutor right?

Think before you answer

The forensic expert's number and the prosecutor's conclusion sound identical. But they are measuring completely different things. Do you see the difference?

💡 The prosecutor's fallacy

The expert testified about P(match | innocent) — the chance of a match given innocence. The prosecutor claimed this equals P(innocent | match) — the chance of innocence given a match. These are completely different quantities. Confusing them is the Prosecutor's Fallacy. And it has sent innocent people to prison.

The consequence
Real people, wrong maths

This is not a theoretical problem. The Prosecutor's Fallacy — presenting P(evidence | innocent) as P(innocent | evidence) — has been used in real courts, with catastrophic results.

💡 The same structure as Act II

A DNA match is just like a positive medical test. The "false match rate" is the false positive rate. The "prior probability of guilt" is the base rate. Without knowing how many people in the population would also match — the base rate — the match tells you almost nothing on its own. Context is everything.

Try This

Go back to the population screen and tap "Reveal the matches" again. Imagine the city had 10 million people instead of 1 million. How many innocent people would match the DNA profile? Now imagine rolling a die 100 times and plotting the sum -- the histogram would form a bell curve, a normal distribution emerging from randomness.

What To Notice

The population grid shows that in a city of 1 million, roughly 1 innocent person matches the DNA. That makes the suspect one of about 2 matches -- a 50% chance, not 99.9999%. This is the same pattern as the Central Limit Theorem: individual outcomes are random, but when aggregated over a population, the distribution of matches follows a predictable pattern centred on the expected value.

Why It Matters

The normal distribution and the Central Limit Theorem explain why averages are predictable even when individuals are not. In a courtroom, forgetting the population size -- the denominator -- leads to the Prosecutor's Fallacy. In statistics, forgetting that individual dice rolls aggregate into a bell curve leads to misunderstanding randomness itself. The shape of uncertainty is always a distribution, and distributions need context.

💡 What you learned

The probability of seeing evidence given innocence is not the same as the probability of innocence given evidence. Confusing them ignores the base rate — how many people in the population would produce the same evidence. A DNA match in a city of millions is not certain guilt. It is a starting point for Bayesian reasoning.

The Pattern
🔄

So far we've added and subtracted on a clock. But what happens when you multiply?

If you multiply numbers on a clock face and keep wrapping around — do you visit every position? Or do some get skipped?

The answer depends on something you already know: whether the clock size is prime.

The question you'll answer

On a clock with N positions, start at 1. Multiply by the same number again and again. Do you visit all positions before returning to 1? The answer reveals a hidden connection between clock arithmetic and prime numbers.

What To Notice

Every non-zero row visits all values from 0 to 4. No gaps. No matter which multiplier you choose, the cycle is complete. Compare this with mod 6 on the next screen.

Why It Matters

This happens because 5 is prime. No non-zero number shares a factor with 5, so every multiplier generates a complete cycle. This completeness property is what gives prime-sized clocks their special algebraic structure.

💡 Something's different

Some multipliers skip values entirely — they cycle through only part of the clock face. Rows 1, 5 work fine. But rows 2, 3, 4 get stuck in a loop, never reaching everything. Why?

The reveal
Primes make complete clocks

The difference between mod 5 and mod 6 is simple: 5 is prime. 6 is not.

When the modulus is prime, every multiplier (except 0) visits all positions before returning. No gaps. Complete cycles.

When the modulus is composite, some multipliers share a common factor with it — and that shared factor creates a shortcut that skips values.

Try different clocks — prime vs composite
Why the gaps happen
💡 The deeper truth

When N is prime, every non-zero number has a multiplicative inverse mod N — a partner that multiplies with it to give 1. This is what makes prime-modulus clocks "complete". It's also exactly why RSA encryption works: operations on a prime-sized clock are perfectly reversible.

You see the prime connection
Prime-sized clocks make complete multiplication cycles.

💡 What you learned

Multiplication mod N works perfectly — no gaps — when N is prime. This property is called having a multiplicative group. When N is composite, some multipliers get trapped in loops. The shared factor between the multiplier and N creates the shortcut that causes the gap.

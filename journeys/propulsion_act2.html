Mathematics of Propulsion · Act II
Newton's Third Law in the Vacuum
⚖️

In space, there's nothing to push against.

On Earth, you push backward against the ground, and the ground pushes you forward. But in the void between stars, there's no ground. No air. Nothing.

So how does a rocket move?

The answer is elegant: conservation of momentum. A rocket throws mass backwards at enormous velocity. By Newton's third law, if the rocket ejects mass backward, that ejected mass pushes the rocket forward.

The rocket doesn't push on anything outside itself — it pushes on its own exhaust.

Momentum Exchange
Action and Reaction

Here's the thrust equation: F = ṁ · vₑ

Force equals mass flow rate (ṁ) times exhaust velocity (vₑ). To increase thrust, either burn more fuel per second, or eject it faster.

vₑ = 4.0 km/s
Why exhaust velocity matters

To get the same thrust with a slower exhaust, you'd need to burn vastly more fuel per second. Modern rockets burn fuel at roughly 500–1000 kg/s. Exhaust velocities range from 2.5 km/s (solid rockets) to 4.5 km/s (liquid rockets).

Efficiency
Why Some Rockets Are Better

Rocket scientists measure efficiency using specific impulse (Isp). It's the exhaust velocity measured in a special way: how many seconds of thrust you get per kilogram of fuel.

Higher Isp means less fuel needed to reach the same velocity.

Solid rocket motor
~265 s
Liquid chemical (LOX/LH₂)
~450 s
Ion thruster
~3000 s
Nuclear thermal
~900 s
The trade-off

Ion thrusters have ten times the Isp of chemical rockets — they could theoretically reach Mars on a fraction of the fuel. But they produce tiny thrust (millinewtons). Chemical rockets are crude, inefficient, and spectacular. That's why we still use them for launch.

Nozzle Physics
Why Rockets Are Shaped That Way

If you simply open a hole in a rocket and let hot gas escape, you waste thrust. The gas expands and loses speed.

A Swedish engineer named Gustaf de Laval discovered a counter-intuitive shape: narrow in the middle, flaring at the throat.

This shape accelerates the exhaust to supersonic speeds, far faster than you'd get from an open hole. It's one of the most elegant discoveries in physics.

The Mach number effect

In the narrow throat, hot gas approaches the speed of sound (Mach 1). Pressure drops dramatically. In the expanding bell, the gas accelerates further — reaching Mach 2, 3, or even 4 in modern rocket nozzles. This doubled or tripled exhaust velocity translates directly to doubled or tripled efficiency.

Feigenbaum's discovery

In 1975, Mitchell Feigenbaum calculated the ratio between successive bifurcation points on a pocket calculator. He expected the ratios to vary. They didn't. Every bifurcation is 4.6692... times closer together than the last one. He ran it on different equations. Same number.

The Edge of Chaos · Act III
The Butterfly Effect
🌪️

1961. Edward Lorenz, a meteorologist at MIT, restarted a weather simulation mid-run -- typing 0.506 instead of 0.506127. A rounding error of 0.0001%.

The forecast was completely different.

Sensitive dependence on initial conditions

In a chaotic system, two trajectories starting arbitrarily close together will eventually diverge completely. The separation grows exponentially -- characterised by the Lyapunov exponent lambda. The future is fully determined. But practically unknowable.

The Lorenz strange attractor

Despite the sensitivity, Lorenz's trajectories never wander off to infinity -- they stay bound to a butterfly-shaped structure in 3D. The strange attractor: chaotic orbit on a bounded fractal surface. Infinite paths that never repeat, never leave.

Why It Matters

This explains why weather forecasts are limited to about 10 days, why you cannot predict turbulence in detail, and why exact long-term prediction of many natural systems is mathematically impossible -- not just technologically difficult. Determinism does not imply predictability.

The Edge of Chaos · Act IV
The Mandelbrot Set
🌀

Two lines of arithmetic. Infinite complexity.

zn+1 = zn^2 + c

z and c are complex numbers. For each point c: does the iteration blow up to infinity, or stay bounded? The Mandelbrot set is all c for which it stays bounded. Its boundary is infinitely complex -- zoom in anywhere and new structure appears, forever.

The connection to Act II

The bifurcation diagram from Act II is literally a cross-section of the Mandelbrot set. Slice the Mandelbrot set along the real axis and you get exactly the period-doubling cascade. The same mathematics, seen from a different angle.

💡 The base rate

The disease is rare — only 1 in 1000 people have it. Even a 99% accurate test produces about 10 false positives for every 1 true positive. The base rate — how common the disease is — dominates the answer. The doctor forgot to tell you that part.

💡 The key relationship

Move the slider to see how prevalence drives everything. When the disease is very rare, most positives are false. When it's common, most positives are real. Base rate is everything.

Try This

Move the prevalence slider to extremes. Set it to 1 in 2 (very common) and then to 1 in 200 (very rare). Watch how the "real chance if positive" changes dramatically even though the test accuracy stays at 99%.

What To Notice

When the disease is rare (1 in 200), a positive result gives you only about a 33% real chance of being sick. When it is common (1 in 2), a positive result is almost certainly real at 99%. The test did not change -- only the base rate did. Expected value depends entirely on context.

Why It Matters

Every decision under uncertainty involves weighing risk against reward. A 99% accurate test sounds decisive, but the expected value of a positive result depends on how common the condition is. This is why screening programmes for rare diseases produce mostly false positives -- and why understanding base rates is essential for rational decision making in medicine, finance, and everyday life.

💡 What you learned

Test accuracy alone tells you nothing. You must know the base rate — how common the condition is in the tested population. In screening programmes for rare diseases, most positive results are false positives. This is not a flaw in the test — it is mathematics, inevitably.

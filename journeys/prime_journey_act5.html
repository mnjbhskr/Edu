Why do primes fall exactly where they do?

What you already know

Primes thin out. Gauss found their approximate density. The Ulam spiral showed hidden diagonal structure. And in 1859, Bernhard Riemann found a connection so deep it seemed impossible — a link between primes and a completely different corner of mathematics. His hypothesis about that link remains unproved to this day.

What Riemann saw
The critical line

Riemann found that buried inside a mathematical object called the zeta function, there are special points where the function equals zero.

These zeros are connected to the primes in a precise, extraordinary way. Where the zeros sit determines exactly how primes are distributed.

And Riemann noticed something about where those zeros appear to sit.

Riemann's zeta function is a kind of mathematical instrument. When you "play" it at certain points, it produces zero — the way a guitar string can produce silence at exact positions along its length.

Those points — called zeros — appear to cluster along a single vertical line. Slide the control below to reveal them one by one.

💡 The critical line

Every zero found so far sits at real part exactly ½ — on the vertical line in the middle of the diagram. The Riemann Hypothesis says they all do. All infinitely many of them. Forever.

Try This

Slowly drag the slider from 0 to 10 and watch each zero appear on the critical line. Pause at each new zero and look at its imaginary coordinate — notice how the spacing between zeros is itself irregular. Try sliding back to 0 and watching again more carefully.

What To Notice

Every single zero lands at real part exactly 1/2, forming a vertical line. Over 10 trillion zeros have been computed, and none has ever deviated. The Prime Number Theorem — that primes near N occur with density roughly 1/ln(N) — follows from the broad location of these zeros. If the Riemann Hypothesis is true, we get the sharpest possible estimate of prime distribution.

Why It Matters

The Riemann Hypothesis is the most important unsolved problem in mathematics. It carries a $1 million Millennium Prize. Proving it would perfect our understanding of prime distribution, confirm the security assumptions behind RSA cryptography, and resolve hundreds of theorems that currently assume it is true. Disproving it would shake the foundations of number theory.

Why It Matters

The Prime Number Theorem tells us primes thin out at a precise, predictable rate. The Riemann Hypothesis, if true, tells us exactly how close the approximation is. This is the connection between the zeros you just explored and the primes themselves.

Two possible futures
True or false — it matters

The Riemann Hypothesis is not merely an abstract curiosity. Its truth or falsehood would change what we know about the deep structure of mathematics.

✓If the Hypothesis is TRUE
Primes are distributed as regularly as they possibly can be, given their constraints. There's a kind of hidden order — a music — underneath their irregular distribution. Mathematics would have a deep, beautiful coherence.
Over 1,000 theorems currently say "assuming the Riemann Hypothesis..."
If proved true — all 1,000 become proven facts simultaneously.
✗If the Hypothesis is FALSE
Somewhere out there — at some incomprehensibly large imaginary height — a zero exists off the critical line. Primes would be more irregular than we thought. Our entire picture of prime distribution would need rebuilding.
All 1,000 theorems built on the hypothesis would collapse.
It would be the most dramatic result in mathematics in centuries.
💡 The strange middle ground

We have checked over 10 trillion zeros. Not one is off the line. The computational evidence is overwhelming. Yet the mathematics stubbornly refuses to yield a proof. This is what makes it extraordinary — it feels obviously true, and yet nobody can prove it.

💡 Why it's so hard

The zeta function is defined everywhere in the complex plane, which means there are infinitely many places a zero could hide. Checking trillions of zeros proves nothing about the infinity we haven't checked. A proof must work for all zeros, everywhere, for all time. That is a fundamentally different kind of argument — and nobody has found it.

Primes protect the internet
Every time you send a password or buy something online, RSA encryption keeps it safe. The idea is simple:
Pick two large primes: p and q
Multiply them: n = p × q  ← easy
Factor n back into p and q  ← nearly impossible
Multiplying two 300-digit primes takes milliseconds. Finding those primes from their product would take every computer on Earth longer than the age of the universe. Primes are not just beautiful — they guard your secrets.

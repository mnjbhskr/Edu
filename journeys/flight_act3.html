Stability and Centre of Gravity

An aircraft is always trying to fall out of the sky. Gravity pulls it down relentlessly. The mathematics of stability determine whether it survives this constant assault.

Lift and weight must balance. But that's not enough. A pencil balanced on its point is technically in equilibrium—until the slightest breeze disturbs it. Then it tumbles.

An aircraft must be stable. Not just momentarily balanced, but naturally resistant to disturbances. When a gust of wind pitches the nose up, the aircraft must automatically correct itself. When banking into a turn, it must want to return to level flight.

This stability comes from careful positioning of two invisible points: the centre of gravity (where all the aircraft's weight acts) and the centre of pressure (where all the aerodynamic forces act).

The relationship between these two points determines whether an aircraft is stable, neutral, or dangerously unstable.

Key Principle: Stability isn't optional. An unstable aircraft cannot be flown—it requires constant pilot intervention and ultimately crashes.

Centre of Gravity vs Centre of Pressure

The distance between these two points is the margin of safety. Move them the wrong way, and the aircraft becomes uncontrollable.

Centre of Gravity Position 50%
Move CG forward → more stable but harder to turn; Backward → more agile but less stable

The diagram shows an aircraft from the side. The coloured circles mark:

  • ● Centre of Pressure (CP): Where the wing's lift acts. Fixed for a given angle of attack.
  • ● Centre of Gravity (CG): Where the aircraft's weight acts. Engineers position this carefully.

Stability Rule: If CG is ahead of CP (towards the nose), the aircraft is stable. If CG is behind CP (towards the tail), it's unstable.

Why? If the nose pitches up by accident, speed drops, lift drops, and the moment (torque) tips the nose back down. Stable equilibrium—the aircraft self-corrects.

Pitch, Roll, and Yaw

An aircraft doesn't just fall straight. It can rotate about three different axes. Each requires its own control surface.

Pitch: Nose up/down rotation. Controlled by the elevator (a hinged surface on the horizontal tail). Moving the control column forward pitches the nose down; back pitches it up.

Control Surface: Elevator on the tail. The area and moment arm must be precisely calculated to counteract pitch instability.

The Stability Paradox

Fighter jets deliberately violate the stability rules. They're unstable—yet somehow safer than ever. Mathematics explains the paradox.

An inherently stable aircraft is easy to fly but sluggish. Moving the control stick produces small, slow changes. For a commercial airliner, this is perfect—you want a stable platform that naturally wants to maintain level flight.

But a fighter pilot needs instantaneous response. They want to bank hard, pitch aggressively, and execute manoeuvres that would tear apart a civilian aircraft. An inherently stable design is a handicap.

So military engineers make jets deliberately unstable. The centre of gravity is positioned behind the centre of pressure—exactly the configuration that should be uncontrollable.

Yet modern fighter jets like the F-16 are incredibly agile and safe to fly. How?

The answer: Computers.

A computer onboard the aircraft measures acceleration, rotation rate, altitude, and airspeed thousands of times per second. Using control theory (which we'll explore in Act 4), the computer constantly adjusts the control surfaces to maintain flight. The pilot doesn't directly control the surfaces; they tell the computer what manoeuvre they want, and the computer makes thousands of tiny corrections to make it happen.

This is called fly-by-wire control. The mathematics of feedback and stability move from the physics of the wing to the realm of electronic control systems.

Insight: By delegating stability to a computer, engineers gained enormous performance. Mathematics made extreme behaviour both safe and possible.

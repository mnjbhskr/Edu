💡 The formula is just counting

Bayes' theorem doesn't introduce any new ideas. It just combines what you already know — prior belief, how likely the evidence would be if your hypothesis is true, and how common the evidence is overall — into a single, precise calculation. Everything you did in Act II was Bayes' theorem.

What To Notice

With few flips, the proportion swings wildly. With many flips, it converges to 0.5. The dashed line at 50% acts like a magnet — the more you flip, the stronger its pull.

Why It Matters

This is the Law of Large Numbers — the foundational theorem of probability. It says that as you repeat an experiment, the observed frequency converges to the true probability. This is why casinos always win, why insurance works, and why polls become more accurate with larger samples.

The Law of Large Numbers

As the number of flips grows, the proportion of heads converges to 0.5. Individual flips remain unpredictable, but the aggregate becomes certain. This is why insurance companies, casinos, and polling firms exist — they bet on the aggregate, not the individual.

A formula for thinking

You have Bayes' theorem
Prior belief × likelihood of evidence ÷ probability of evidence.
A formula for changing your mind correctly.
💡 What you learned

Bayes' theorem is the mathematically correct way to update a belief when new evidence arrives. The prior is your starting belief. The likelihood is how expected the evidence is given your hypothesis. The posterior is your updated belief. Everything in Acts I and II was Bayesian reasoning — now you have the formula.

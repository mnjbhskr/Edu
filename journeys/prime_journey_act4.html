The elegant problem

Multiplying two numbers is easy. Reversing it — finding which two numbers were multiplied — can be almost impossibly hard. This asymmetry is the foundation of modern cryptography. In this act, you'll feel that asymmetry yourself — then build a lock with it.

What To Notice

Multiplying two primes takes a fraction of a second, no matter how large. But factoring the product gets dramatically harder as the numbers grow. Even going from 2-digit to 3-digit primes makes factoring noticeably slower. This asymmetry is not a limitation of your skill — it is a fundamental computational barrier.

Why It Matters

This asymmetry is the foundation of RSA encryption. Your bank card, every HTTPS connection, and every encrypted message relies on the fact that multiplying primes is easy but factoring their product is computationally infeasible for large enough primes. The prime counting function and Riemann zeta function govern how many primes exist in any range — which directly determines the security of these systems.

Your lock
Choose your primes

In RSA, you choose two secret primes — call them p and q.

You multiply them to get N = p × q. That product is your public key — you share it with the world.

Your private key is knowing which two primes you chose. Nobody else can find them without factoring N.

💡 Public vs private

You share N openly — it's your public key. Anyone can use it to send you a secret message. But only you know p and q, so only you can decrypt it. The asymmetry of factoring is what makes this work.

⏱ Time to factor RSA-2048 by brute force

The fastest known algorithms on the most powerful supercomputers would take longer than the age of the universe to factor a 2048-bit RSA key. The universe is approximately 13.8 billion years old. This is why it works.

Lens: Projection

In Act I you discovered that beauty follows proportional rules. Now: how did artists create the illusion of three dimensions on a flat canvas?

Before 1413, paintings were flat. Figures floated without depth, buildings had no recession. Then Filippo Brunelleschi stood in front of the Florence Baptistry with a mirror and a painting — and changed art for ever.

He had discovered that mathematics could create the perfect illusion of three dimensions on a flat surface. The trick? A single, invisible point where all parallel lines converge.

Vanishing Point
One-Point Perspective

Every parallel line in the real world converges to a single point on the canvas. This is the vanishing point — the mathematical heart of perspective drawing.

Click and drag the glowing vanishing point. Watch how every line in the scene adjusts — the floor tiles, the pillars, and the ceiling all obey the same point. Then try the horizon tilt slider to create dramatic compositions.

What To Notice

Tilting the horizon creates dramatic tension — filmmakers call this the ‘Dutch angle’. Same mathematics, different artistic effect. The horizontal lines of the tiled floor grow closer together as they approach the horizon. This is foreshortening — equal distances in the real world compress as they recede.

Why It Matters

Our eyes do this naturally. A long corridor appears to narrow in the distance. Brunelleschi's genius was realising that this optical effect follows precise mathematical rules.

Corners and Angles
Two-Point Perspective

Two vanishing points give us corners — the way we see buildings standing at an angle. Each set of parallel edges converges to its own point on the horizon.

What To Notice

Moving the vanishing points further apart creates a natural, relaxed view. Pushing them closer together produces dramatic distortion — like a wide-angle lens effect. Architects and comic artists use this deliberately.

Why It Matters

Two-point perspective is how we see most buildings in everyday life. Your brain unconsciously computes these converging lines to judge distance and scale.

Projection
Division by Depth

Perspective is division by depth. The further away an object is, the smaller it appears — and the mathematics is beautifully simple.

x′ = f × (X / Z)      y′ = f × (Y / Z)
f = 200
Try This

Adjust the focal length slider. A short focal length (wide angle) exaggerates depth; a long one (telephoto) flattens it. Photographers and painters both exploit this effect. Toggle between the three shapes to see how projection works on different forms.

Why It Matters

This single formula underpins every 3D game, every CGI film, and every architectural rendering. It is the mathematical bridge between the three-dimensional world and the two-dimensional screen.

This same mathematics — dividing by depth — is used everywhere today: in every 3D video game, in the cameras on your phone, in augmented reality headsets, and in self-driving cars. When a computer renders a 3D scene, it applies exactly this projection: x′ = f · X / Z.

Where projection lives today
  • Computer Graphics — every 3D game engine
  • Camera calibration — how your phone’s camera works
  • Augmented Reality / Virtual Reality
  • Robotics and autonomous vehicles
  • Medical imaging — CT/MRI reconstruction
Florence, 1413
Brunelleschi's Experiment

Brunelleschi proved that mathematics could perfectly replicate what the eye sees. He painted the Florence Baptistry on a small panel, drilled a hole through it, and held it up to a mirror. Looking through the hole at the reflection, viewer and reality merged into one.

The proof

When the painting's perspective lines matched the real building exactly, it demonstrated that the mathematical rules of projection were not just useful — they were true. Art and geometry became inseparable.

Leonardo's Addition
Atmospheric Perspective

Leonardo da Vinci added another dimension: aerial perspective. Distance doesn't just shrink things — it softens them. Distant mountains turn blue and hazy, their edges dissolving into the sky.

Why It Matters

Light scattering in the atmosphere creates this natural depth cue. Short-wavelength blue light scatters more, so distant objects appear bluer. Leonardo was the first artist to systematically use this optical fact, adding a layer of realism that geometry alone cannot provide.

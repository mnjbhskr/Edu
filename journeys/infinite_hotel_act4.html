The Infinite Hotel · Act IV
The Sizes of Infinity
♾️

We have two infinities now. ℵ₀ — the countable kind. And 𝔠 — the uncountable kind, strictly larger.

Is 𝔠 the next step up? Or are there infinities between ℵ₀ and 𝔠? And after 𝔠 — are there bigger ones still?

Cantor had a beautiful answer to the last question. And a devastating non-answer to the first.

Try This

Before continuing, think: if ℵ₀ and 𝔠 are different sizes, could there be an infinity between them? And could there be something bigger than 𝔠? Hold your intuitions — you are about to see the answers.

What To Notice

We have two concrete infinities so far. ℵ₀ (countable — whole numbers, fractions) and 𝔠 (uncountable — decimals). The question is whether these are the only two, or whether infinity has an even richer structure.

Why It Matters

There is no largest infinity. From any infinite set, you can always construct a strictly bigger one — using something called the power set. The infinities go on forever, each larger than the last.

The power set
Always a bigger infinity

The power set of a set S is the collection of all possible subsets of S. If S has n elements, its power set has 2ⁿ elements. Always strictly bigger.

This works for infinite sets too — and produces a strictly larger infinity every time.

The infinite ladder of infinities
💡 No largest infinity

Take any infinite set. Its power set is strictly bigger. Take the power set of the power set — bigger still. You can climb this ladder forever. There is no top rung. The infinities themselves form an infinite collection — and even that collection has a power set.

The unanswerable question
The Continuum Hypothesis

We know ℵ₀ < 𝔠. But is there an infinity between them? Or does 𝔠 come immediately after ℵ₀ — no infinity in the gap?

Cantor believed the answer was no — there is nothing between ℵ₀ and 𝔠. He called this the Continuum Hypothesis.

He spent the rest of his life trying to prove it. He never could.

The resolution — 1963
In 1940, Kurt Gödel proved you cannot disprove the Continuum Hypothesis from the standard axioms of mathematics.

In 1963, Paul Cohen proved you cannot prove it either.

Together: the Continuum Hypothesis is independent of mathematics — it can neither be proved nor refuted. Both "yes, there's a gap" and "no, there isn't" are internally consistent. Mathematics genuinely cannot decide.

This was the most unsettling discovery in the history of mathematics.

Try This

Imagine you could add one axiom to mathematics: either "there is an infinity between ℵ₀ and 𝔠" or "there is not." Both choices are consistent. Which would you pick, and why?

What To Notice

This is not a question mathematics has failed to answer yet. It is a question mathematics provably cannot answer from its standard axioms. The Continuum Hypothesis is independent — it can be added or denied without contradiction.

Why It Matters

The foundations of mathematics have genuine gaps. Cantor found the first one. Gödel mapped it. This connects directly to Act V — where the same diagonal logic reveals the limits of proof, computation, and mathematical knowledge itself.

💡 What you learned

The power set of any infinite set is strictly larger — so infinities form an endless ladder. The Continuum Hypothesis — whether anything sits between ℵ₀ and 𝔠 — is independent of standard mathematics. You can add it as an axiom either way and remain consistent. Some questions escape the reach of proof.

Coming in Act V — The Open Door

Everything connects. Cantor's undecidable question echoes through Gödel's incompleteness theorems and Turing's halting problem. In Act V, we draw the complete map — from Hilbert's Hotel to the edges of what mathematics can know — and look through the open door at what lies ahead.

Try This

Take the set {a, b, c}. Write down all its subsets, including the empty set and the set itself. How many subsets are there?

What To Notice

A set with 3 elements has 2^3 = 8 subsets. In general, the power set of any set S always has strictly more elements than S itself — even when S is infinite. This is Cantor's theorem.

Why It Matters

There is no largest infinity. From any infinite set, the power set creates a larger one: aleph-0, aleph-1, aleph-2, and beyond. The hierarchy of infinities is itself infinite.

Act II of V
The Shape of Space · Act II
Measuring Curvature
📐

1820. Carl Friedrich Gauss — perhaps the greatest mathematician who ever lived — is hired to survey the Kingdom of Hanover. He spends years measuring triangles across mountains.

He already knew: the angles of a triangle sum to 180° — on a flat surface. But what if the surface isn't flat?

He realised something profound: the deviation from 180° is not an error. It is the curvature.

The Theorema Egregium — "Remarkable Theorem"

Gauss proved that curvature is intrinsic — a property of the surface itself, measurable from within, without ever stepping outside it. A creature living on a curved surface can discover that curvature by measuring triangles. We might be such a creature.

Real-world connection — GPS satellites

GPS satellites orbit 20,200 km above Earth. Their signals travel through curved spacetime — and the curvature matters. Without correcting for spacetime curvature, GPS would drift by ~10 km per day. Every time your phone finds your location, it is accounting for the curvature of space. Gauss's idea, made practical.

Angle sum
180° — or not quite

Gauss's brilliant insight

You don't need to leave a surface to measure its curvature. Just draw a triangle and measure the angles. The excess above 180° divided by the area gives you the Gaussian curvature K. On a sphere of radius R: K = 1/R². The smaller the sphere, the more curved it is.

What To Notice
On a sphere, angles bulge outward (sum > 180°). On a saddle, they pinch inward (sum < 180°). The amount of deviation is proportional to the area of the triangle relative to the curvature. Tiny triangles on any surface look flat.
Why It Matters
This is exactly how cosmologists measure the curvature of the universe. They use the cosmic microwave background as a giant triangle spanning the observable universe. The angle sum tells them the global geometry. We are the creatures on the surface, using Gauss's method to measure our own space.
Try This

Count the vertices, edges, and faces of a cube. Compute V - E + F. Now try it with a tetrahedron, or any other polyhedron. What do you always get?

What To Notice

For any convex polyhedron, V - E + F = 2. This is Euler's formula — one of the most beautiful results in mathematics. It holds not because of angles or lengths, but because of the shape's topology.

Why It Matters

The Euler characteristic is a topological invariant — it doesn't change under continuous deformation. It classifies surfaces: a sphere has chi=2, a torus has chi=0, and each additional handle reduces chi by 2.

What you learned

Gaussian curvature is intrinsic — it can be measured from within a surface without any external reference. The angle sum of a triangle reveals the curvature. On a unit sphere: triangle angles sum to 180° + area. On a saddle: 180° - area. This is how we could, in principle, measure the curvature of the universe we live in.

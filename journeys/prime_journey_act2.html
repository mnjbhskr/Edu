Now a new question: can you find all primes at once, systematically?

Eratosthenes could. His method is called the sieve — and you're about to run it yourself.

Step by step
You run the sieve

The rule is simple: cross out every multiple of each prime you find. What survives is prime.

Why does this work? If a number is a multiple of 2 — say 6, or 8, or 10 — then 2 divides it evenly. That means it can form a rectangle. So it's not prime. We can cross it out immediately.

Start with 2. Tap 4, 6, 8, 10... to cross them out. Then we'll move to the next prime.

💡 The pattern

Try This

Start with 2: tap every multiple of 2 in the grid (4, 6, 8, 10...) to cross them out. Then advance to 3, and cross out its remaining multiples (9, 15, 21...). Keep going through 5 and 7.

What To Notice

Each new prime has fewer multiples left to cross out — because earlier primes already eliminated most composites. By the time you reach 7, almost everything is already resolved.

Why It Matters

This is the Sieve of Eratosthenes — invented around 240 BCE and still the most elegant way to find all primes up to any limit. It works because every composite number has a prime factor no larger than its square root. That is why you only need to sieve up to sqrt(N).

The full picture
All primes to 100

You've seen the logic. Now watch the sieve run at full speed — hunting every prime from 2 to 100.

Notice how the primes thin out as numbers grow larger. That thinning is one of mathematics' great mysteries.

Try This

Run the sieve at slow speed and watch which numbers survive each round of elimination. Then reset and run it at full speed — notice how the pattern of survivors changes as you go higher. Try stepping through one prime at a time to see exactly which composites each prime removes.

What To Notice

As the sieve runs, the gaps between surviving primes grow wider and more irregular. Twin primes (pairs like 11,13 or 29,31 separated by just 2) become rarer but never seem to stop appearing entirely. The pattern of prime gaps looks almost random, yet hides deep structure.

Why It Matters

The distribution of prime gaps is one of the deepest unsolved problems in mathematics. The Twin Prime Conjecture — that there are infinitely many pairs of primes separated by 2 — remains unproven after centuries of effort. In 2013, Yitang Zhang made a breakthrough by proving gaps are bounded, but the full conjecture is still open.

The deepest question
Do they ever end?

After the sieve, a natural question emerges:

"If I kept going forever, would the primes eventually run out?"

Euclid answered this 2,300 years ago with an argument so elegant it still appears in university courses today.

And you can understand every single step.

Euclid's Strategy
Proof by Contradiction
"Assume the opposite of what you want to prove. Then show that assumption leads to something impossible. Therefore the opposite must be true."

The Revelation
✨

Three acts. Two ideas built entirely separately.

Derivatives: the slope of the tangent. How fast something changes at an instant.

Integrals: the area under a curve. How much something accumulates over an interval.

One measures rates. The other measures totals. They were developed to answer completely different questions.

Are they connected?

The question Newton asked

In 1666, Isaac Newton — working alone in plague-year isolation — noticed something extraordinary. The derivative and the integral were not just related. They were inverse operations of each other. Like multiplication and division. Like squaring and square roots. This is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

The connection
Area → rate → area, revealed

Position, velocity, acceleration. Three quantities. Each is the derivative of the one before — and the integral of the one after. Watch them connect.

💡 The pattern

Differentiate position → get velocity. Differentiate velocity → get acceleration. But also: integrate acceleration → get velocity. Integrate velocity → get position. Differentiation and integration undo each other. They are inverse operations.

The Fundamental Theorem
The most beautiful result

Two separate ideas. One stunning connection. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus — stated in two parts.

Part I — Differentiation undoes integration
d/dx [∫ₐˣ f(t) dt] = f(x)
The rate of change of the accumulated area function
is the original function itself.
Part II — Integration undoes differentiation
∫ₐᵇ f'(x) dx = f(b) − f(a)
The total accumulated change equals
the final value minus the initial value.
💡 Why this is extraordinary

Computing an integral via Riemann sums (infinitely many rectangles) is hard. But Part II says: just find an anti-derivative, evaluate it at the two limits, and subtract. ∫₀⁴ t dt = [t²/2]₀⁴ = 8 − 0 = 8. One line. No rectangles. Exact.

Two men, one theorem
Newton and Leibniz

The greatest theorem in calculus was discovered twice — independently, by two geniuses who never collaborated and spent years in bitter dispute over priority.

1666 — Cambridge, England
Isaac Newton
Develops "the method of fluxions" during the Great Plague, when Cambridge University closed. He does not publish for 20 years. Uses dot notation: ẋ for derivative. Thinks in terms of motion and flow.
1675 — Hanover, Germany
Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz
Independently develops calculus and publishes in 1684. Invents the notation we still use today: dy/dx for derivative, ∫ for integral. Thinks in terms of infinitesimals — infinitely small quantities.
1699–1716 — The priority dispute
Newton vs Leibniz
The Royal Society (stacked with Newton's allies) declares Leibniz a plagiarist. Today, historians agree both discovered calculus independently. Leibniz's notation won — it is clearer and more powerful. We still use it.
Today — everywhere
The language of change
Every differential equation in physics, every optimisation algorithm in machine learning, every option pricing formula in finance — all built on the foundation laid by Newton and Leibniz in the 1670s.

💡 What you learned

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: the derivative of the integral function returns the original function; the integral of a derivative equals the net change. These two operations — built to answer completely different questions — are exact inverses of each other. This is the central theorem of calculus.

What To Notice

Differential equations describe how quantities evolve over time. The simplest — dy/dt = ky — gives exponential growth (k > 0) or decay (k < 0). This single equation models population growth, radioactive decay, and compound interest.

Why It Matters

Most laws of physics are differential equations: Newton's F=ma, Maxwell's equations, the Schrödinger equation. Learning to solve them means learning to predict the future from the present.

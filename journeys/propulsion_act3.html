Mathematics of Propulsion · Act III
Escaping Earth
🌍

Earth holds you in a gravity well.

Gravity doesn't pull on you with constant force. It's the geometry of spacetime itself. Every kilogramme of mass warps spacetime around it, creating a potential energy landscape.

To escape Earth entirely — to reach a place where Earth's gravity is zero — you must gain enough kinetic energy to climb out of this well. The required speed is escape velocity.

For Earth, it's about 11.2 kilometres per second. Nothing can go faster than light. And yet objects can achieve escape velocity. That's what makes spaceflight possible.

Energy Conservation
The Escape Speed

Escape velocity: v_esc = √(2GM/r)

Where G is the gravitational constant, M is the body's mass, and r is the radius. For Earth: 11.2 km/s. For the Moon: 2.4 km/s. For Jupiter: 60 km/s.

Why escape velocity matters

Any object moving at or faster than escape velocity will eventually reach infinity with zero velocity (or faster). Anything slower will fall back down. This is the boundary between ballistic trajectories (falling) and escape trajectories.

Orbital Mechanics
The Hohmann Transfer

To move between circular orbits efficiently, we use a Hohmann transfer — an elliptical path that touches both orbits.

Two burns are needed: Burn 1 speeds you up to enter the transfer ellipse, and Burn 2 circularises you at the target orbit. The total Δv determines how much fuel you need.

Why Hohmann transfers matter

The Hohmann transfer is the most fuel-efficient two-burn manoeuvre between coplanar circular orbits. Real missions from low Earth orbit to geostationary orbit (6.6× LEO radius) use exactly this technique. The total Δv ≈ 3.9 km/s for that journey.

Conservation of Energy
Speed and Altitude

Here's a counter-intuitive truth: to reach a higher orbit, you speed up.

But then, once you're in the higher orbit, you move slower than before. You gain altitude by speeding up. You maintain altitude by slowing down.

This is why orbital manoeuvres are so elegant. A small burn at the right time creates a huge change in orbit. This is the vis-viva equation at work.

Why this matters

Total mechanical energy (KE + PE) is conserved. At higher altitudes, PE increases but KE must decrease to keep the total constant. So you move slower in higher orbits. To get there, you need a Hohmann transfer — the subject of Act IV.

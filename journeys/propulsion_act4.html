The Hohmann Transfer
🛰️

You can't just point a rocket at the Moon and fly there in a straight line.

If you try, you'll overshoot. Or you'll need to slow down, which burns fuel. Or you'll crash.

Instead, space missions use elegant orbital transfers. The most famous is the Hohmann transfer — a fuel-efficient path between two circular orbits. You burn twice: once to enter an elliptical orbit, and again to circularise at your destination.

This is celestial mechanics made practical. It's how we reached the Moon, and how we'll reach Mars.

Orbital Transfers
The Efficient Path

A Hohmann transfer burns at periapsis (closest point) to raise your apoapsis (furthest point), creating an elliptical transfer orbit. When you reach apoapsis, you burn again to enter the higher circular orbit.

Altitude: 1.5 Earth radii
Burn 1 (Hohmann raise)
3.2 km/s
Burn 2 (Circularise)
2.7 km/s
Total Δv
5.9 km/s
Why Hohmann is optimal

The Hohmann transfer uses the minimum Δv to reach another orbit. Any other path requires more fuel. This is why nearly every satellite, spacecraft, and space station uses Hohmann transfers. It's mathematically proven to be the most efficient.

Advanced Manoeuvres
Gravity Assists

The Oberth effect says: it's more fuel-efficient to burn at periapsis (closest point to the planet) than at apoapsis. A burn near a planet does more work.

This led to gravity assists — using a planet's gravity to slingshot a spacecraft to a new trajectory without burning fuel. Voyager 2 used four gravity assists (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune) to explore the outer planets using barely any fuel.

The trajectory bend

A spacecraft approaching a planet from one direction will exit going a different direction. The faster the close approach, the sharper the bend. By timing these assists carefully, you can reach distant planets without carrying enough fuel for direct trajectories.

Real Missions
Delta-v Budgets

Here are the fuel requirements (total Δv) for actual space missions. These values determine the rocket, launch cost, and feasibility of the mission.

Earth to Low Earth Orbit (LEO)
9.7 km/s
LEO to Lunar Orbit
3.3 km/s
Earth to Mars (via Hohmann)
11.6 km/s
Earth to Jupiter (via Hohmann)
19.3 km/s
Earth escape (to solar system)
16.7 km/s
Why the Moon is achievable

Getting to the Moon requires less Δv than reaching Mars because it's closer. Apollo 11 launched with enough Δv budget for the entire mission. Reaching Mars requires significantly more energy — roughly the same as launching multiple Moon missions. This is why Mars remains a frontier.

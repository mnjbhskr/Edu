The mathematics of motion

Every river, every ocean current, every wisp of smoke rising from a candle — they all follow the same mathematics. The equations of fluid dynamics are among the most beautiful and most stubborn in all of physics.

What you see

Eighty particles flowing in parallel lines — laminar flow. Smooth, predictable, orderly. Each layer glides past the next without mixing.

Order to chaos

When fluid moves slowly, it flows in orderly layers — laminar flow. But increase the speed, and something extraordinary happens...

The critical threshold
The Reynolds Number

The transition from calm to chaos depends on just one number — the Reynolds number.

Re = ρvL / μ
density × velocity × length / dynamic viscosity
The threshold

When Re < ~2000, viscous forces dominate and flow stays laminar. Above this threshold, inertial forces win, and the tiniest disturbance can trigger turbulence.

Historical note

Osborne Reynolds first demonstrated this in 1883 by injecting dye into water flowing through a glass tube. Below the critical speed, the dye flowed in a neat line. Above it, the dye dispersed instantly into chaotic swirls.

The unsolvable equation
Navier-Stokes

All of this — every swirl, every eddy, every hurricane — is encoded in a single set of equations that nobody can fully solve.

∂u/∂t + (u·∇)u = −∇p/ρ + ν∇²u
The Navier-Stokes momentum equation
A million-dollar mystery

The Navier-Stokes equations are one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems. Proving whether smooth solutions always exist would win you one million dollars. After nearly two centuries, nobody has managed it.

What each term means

∂u/∂t — how velocity changes over time. (u·∇)u — the fluid carrying itself along. −∇p/ρ — pressure pushing fluid around. ν∇²u — viscosity smoothing out differences.

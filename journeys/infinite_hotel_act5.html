You started with a hotel. A thought experiment. A parlour trick about infinity.

But the logic of that hotel led you here: to the limits of what mathematics can prove. To questions that are genuinely undecidable. To the discovery that reason itself has boundaries.

Cantor opened a door. He didn't know what was behind it. Neither did the mathematicians who followed him.

The door is still open.

What To Notice

Each act introduced a new technique for reasoning about infinity — but the diagonal argument from Act III keeps reappearing. It is the master key that unlocks every door in this journey.

Why It Matters

The story of infinity is not finished. The questions Cantor opened are still being explored today. Set theorists, logicians, and computer scientists continue to map the landscape that begins with a hotel that never fills.

One thread
From hotel to Gödel

Every idea in this journey connects to the next. Here is the single thread that runs through all of them — from Hilbert's Hotel to the limits of mathematical knowledge.

Try This

Look at the connection map above. For each step, can you explain to yourself (or to someone else) what the diagonal move is? In Cantor's proof, it is changing each diagonal digit. In Turing's, it is a program that does the opposite of what another program predicts.

What To Notice

These are not separate discoveries. They are the same discovery, seen from different angles. Cantor showed mathematics has questions it cannot answer. Gödel showed every system has unprovable truths. Turing showed some computations cannot be completed.

Why It Matters

All three used the same diagonal move. A single idea — self-reference combined with contradiction — reveals the limits of counting, proof, and computation. Understanding this one technique gives you insight into some of the deepest results in human thought.

Try This

Consider: is there an infinity between the countable naturals and the uncountable reals? Try to think of a set that might be 'in between' these two sizes.

What To Notice

Cantor conjectured there is no such in-between infinity — this is the Continuum Hypothesis. Remarkably, Gödel and Cohen proved it can be neither proved nor disproved from the standard axioms of mathematics.

Why It Matters

The Continuum Hypothesis shows that some mathematical questions are genuinely undecidable — not because we lack ingenuity, but because the axioms themselves leave room for different mathematical universes.

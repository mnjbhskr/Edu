Stars and Shockwaves
The mathematics of stellar death — and the birth of everything

A massive star has burned through its fuel. In its core, iron accumulates — the ash of nuclear fusion. And then, in less than a second, the core collapses. The resulting explosion is a supernova — briefly outshining an entire galaxy.

The explosion sends a shockwave outward at thousands of kilometres per second. Behind the shock, matter is compressed and heated to millions of degrees.

In 1941, Geoffrey Taylor derived the mathematics of this expansion — using nothing but dimensional analysis. The shock radius follows a beautifully simple power law.

R(t) = C · (E/ρ₀)1/5 · t2/5
Insight

Taylor used this equation to estimate the yield of the first atomic bomb from declassified photographs — calculating the energy to within 10% of the actual value.

Notice

The exponent 2/5 comes purely from dimensional analysis. Energy, density, time, and distance — there is only one way to combine them with the correct dimensions.

But supernovae do more than destroy. The shockwave compresses surrounding gas, triggering the collapse of new molecular clouds — the birth of new stars. And the explosion scatters heavy elements forged in the star’s core: carbon, oxygen, iron, gold…

Insight

Every atom of iron in your blood was forged in a star that exploded billions of years ago. You are, quite literally, made of stardust.

In four acts, we have journeyed from a kitchen sink to a supernova. And the remarkable truth is: the same mathematics connects them all.

  • Act II: Heat + gravity → Rayleigh number → convection cells
  • Act III: Plasma + magnetism → MHD → reconnection

These are not separate subjects. They are different movements in the same symphony — the mathematics of continuous media.

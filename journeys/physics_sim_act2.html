Conduction

Imagine a thin layer of fluid — perhaps oil in a pan — heated gently from below. At first, nothing visible happens. The heat simply conducts upward, molecule by molecule.

What you see

A static temperature gradient — hot at the bottom, cool at the top. No motion. Heat moves by conduction alone, the slow hand-off of energy between neighbouring molecules.

The birth of convection

But increase the temperature difference, and suddenly — convection cells appear. The fluid organises itself into beautiful rolling patterns.

The critical number
The Rayleigh Number

Whether convection occurs depends on the Rayleigh number — a single dimensionless quantity that balances buoyancy against viscous resistance.

Ra = g·α·ΔT·L³ / (ν·κ)
gravity × thermal expansion × temp difference × length³ / (viscosity × diffusivity)
The threshold

The critical value is Ra ≈ 1708. Below this, heat conducts passively. Above it, convection begins — one of the most celebrated stability thresholds in physics.

Notice

This is exactly the same kind of threshold we saw with the Reynolds number in Act I. Physics loves critical numbers — dimensionless ratios where qualitatively different behaviours emerge on either side.

Convection everywhere
Shaping Worlds

Rayleigh-Bénard convection isn't just a laboratory curiosity. It shapes worlds.

  • 🌍
    Earth's Mantle
    Convection currents in the mantle drive plate tectonics, moving continents and triggering earthquakes over millions of years.
  • 🌦️
    Weather Systems
    Atmospheric convection cells — Hadley, Ferrel, and Polar — create the global wind patterns that shape our climate.
  • ⭐
    Stellar Interiors
    Convective zones in stars transport energy from fusion cores to the surface. The Sun's granulation is convection made visible.
  • 🍳
    Your Kitchen
    The rolling motion in a pot of boiling water is Rayleigh-Bénard convection. Same physics, same equations, smaller scale.
The unifying idea

From a kitchen pan to a star — the mathematics is identical. Only the numbers change. This is the extraordinary power of dimensionless analysis.

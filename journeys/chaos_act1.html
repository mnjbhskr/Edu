The Edge of Chaos · Act I
Simple Rules, Surprising Behaviour
🦋

In 1845, Pierre Verhulst wrote down a simple equation to model how animal populations grow. One line. One parameter.

xₙ₊₁ = r · xₙ · (1 − xₙ)

x is the population (as a fraction of maximum). r is the growth rate. Each year, you plug in this year's x, and get next year's.

For low r, the population settles. For medium r, it oscillates. For high r — something nobody expected.

The logistic map

This equation — the logistic map — became one of the most studied objects in mathematics. Not because it's complicated. Because a single parameter r generates behaviour ranging from boring stability to genuine mathematical chaos. The same equation. Just a different number.

What To Notice

As r crosses 3, the single steady line splits into two — the population bounces between two values forever. As r increases further, two becomes four, four becomes eight. The splits come faster and faster until the pattern shatters into chaos.

💡 You found period doubling

At r ≈ 3, the stable fixed point becomes unstable and the orbit splits into 2. At r ≈ 3.44, it splits into 4. Each split is a bifurcation. The gaps between bifurcations shrink by a constant ratio. This ratio — 4.669... — is called the Feigenbaum constant.

🔥 You found chaos

Beyond r ≈ 3.57, the orbit never settles. It wanders unpredictably through (0,1), never repeating. This is deterministic chaos — fully determined by the equation, yet impossible to predict long-term. The same equation, just r a little higher.

Try This

What To Notice

At r ≈ 3 the orbit splits from 1 value to 2. At r ≈ 3.45 it splits to 4. The splits accelerate — each gap is roughly 4.669× shorter than the last.

Why It Matters

This ratio — the Feigenbaum constant — appears in every system that transitions to chaos: dripping taps, electronic circuits, population models. It is a universal constant of nature, discovered from this one equation.

Why It Matters

This is the deepest lesson of chaos theory: complexity does not require complicated rules. One equation with one parameter can generate behaviour so intricate that it took mathematicians until the 1970s to understand it. Nature uses simple rules iterated millions of times — producing weather, turbulence, heartbeat rhythms, and ecosystems.

💡 What you learned

The logistic map generates three distinct behaviours depending on r: stable convergence (r<3), period-doubling oscillation (3<r<3.57), and chaos (r>3.57). Chaos doesn't require a complicated system. It requires iteration of a simple rule.

Coming in Act II — The Bifurcation Diagram

If you plot, for every value of r, all the values where the system eventually settles — you get one of the most beautiful and strange objects in all of mathematics. It reveals the structure hiding inside the chaos, and a universal constant that appears in systems as different as dripping taps and electronic circuits.

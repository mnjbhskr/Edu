What To Notice

When you reach 13, the clock shows 1. At 25, it shows 1 again. The pattern repeats every 12 hours — the clock wraps. The displayed value is always the remainder after dividing by 12.

Why It Matters

This wrapping behaviour is modular arithmetic — one of the most important ideas in mathematics. It appears in cryptography, computer science, music theory, and the calendar. Every time a value cycles back to the start, you are seeing arithmetic mod N.

Clock addition

On a 12-position clock (0-11), addition wraps around: 7 + 8 = 3 because 15 mod 12 = 3. This is modular arithmetic in action — the numbers form a closed cycle.

A name for the pattern
Modular arithmetic

What the clock is doing has a name: modular arithmetic.

When we say "13 o'clock on a 12-hour clock", we mean: 13 mod 12 = 1

The word "mod" means: divide by the clock size, and keep only the remainder.

How it works — three examples

The "clock size" is called the modulus. For a 12-hour clock, the modulus is 12. For a 7-day week, the modulus is 7. For hours in a day, it's 24.

The modulus determines where the counting resets.

💡 The key idea

Modular arithmetic is just regular arithmetic — but instead of letting numbers grow forever, we wrap them around at a fixed point. The clock wraps at 12. The week wraps at 7. A byte in a computer wraps at 256.

A real question
What day will it be?

Today is Monday. What day of the week will it be in 100 days?

You could count 100 days on a calendar. Or you could use the clock idea.

The week has 7 days. It wraps at 7. So this is just: 100 mod 7.

💡 What you learned

Modular arithmetic is just counting with a wrap. The modulus is the wrap point. A mod B gives the remainder when A is divided by B. This simple idea — which a clock does every hour — turns out to be one of the most powerful ideas in mathematics.

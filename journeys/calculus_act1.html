The Shape of Change · Act I
The Speed
🚗

A car sets off. Its position at any moment is given by a curve — t² metres after t seconds.

At t = 3 seconds, the car is at position 9. At t = 4 seconds, it's at position 16.

Average speed over that second: 7 metres per second. Fine.

But what is the speed at exactly t = 3? Not over an interval. Not an average. Instantaneously.

The question that created calculus

This question troubled mathematicians for centuries. The problem: speed requires a distance divided by a time. But an instant has no duration. 0 ÷ 0. Meaningless — until Newton and Leibniz, independently, found the answer in 1666 and 1675.

The limit
Getting closer

We can't measure speed at a single instant. But we can measure it over a very small interval. Let's see what happens as the interval shrinks.

💡 The numbers are converging

As h → 0, the average speed approaches exactly 6. Not approximately 6 — the limit is precisely 6. This is the instantaneous speed at t = 3. This is the derivative. We write: d(t²)/dt = 2t, so at t = 3, the derivative = 6.

The geometry
Secant becomes tangent

The average speed is the slope of a line joining two points on the curve — called a secant line.

As the second point moves toward the first, the secant line rotates — until at the limit, it just touches the curve. That line is the tangent. Its slope is the derivative.

💡 The tangent line

When h → 0, the secant line becomes the tangent — touching the curve at exactly one point. Its slope is exactly 6 at t = 3. Not an approximation. The derivative is defined as this limit: the exact slope of the tangent. f'(3) = lim[h→0] (f(3+h) − f(3))/h = 6.

The derivative
The instantaneous rate of change

You've just invented the derivative — the same way Newton did in a plague year, 1666, when Cambridge closed and he went home to think.

The derivative of f at x is the slope of the tangent line at that point. It measures how fast f is changing at exactly that moment.

f'(x) = limh→0 [f(x+h) − f(x)] / h

💡 What you learned

The derivative f'(x) is the limit as h→0 of [f(x+h)−f(x)]/h. It measures the instantaneous rate of change. Geometrically: the slope of the tangent line. Every time we shrink the interval to zero, we go from approximate to exact. That limit is calculus.

Coming in Act II — The Rules

Do we have to go through the limit process every single time? Newton and Leibniz found shortcuts — patterns that let you differentiate any function instantly. You'll discover the power rule yourself, before being told it exists.

What To Notice

The tangent line's slope at each point IS the derivative. Where the slope is zero, the function has a maximum or minimum. The derivative captures the instantaneous rate of change — something Newton and Leibniz formalised independently in the 1680s.

Why It Matters

Derivatives are the mathematical language of change. Every time you check a speedometer, optimise a portfolio, or model population growth, you are reading a derivative.

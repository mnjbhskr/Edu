Act III of V
The Infinite Hotel · Act III
The Diagonal Argument
↗️

You can list the whole numbers. You can list the fractions. It starts to feel like you can list anything infinite.

Now: can you list every decimal number between 0 and 1?

Numbers like 0.5, 0.333..., 0.14159..., 0.72638495...
Numbers with infinitely many digits after the decimal point.

In 1891, Cantor proved — with a single, devastating argument — that you cannot. No list can contain them all. Not even an infinite list.

Try This

Suppose someone claimed they had listed every decimal between 0 and 1. Before seeing the proof, try to imagine: could you somehow construct a number that is not on their list? How?

What To Notice

In Act II, listing the fractions was possible because we had a systematic grid pattern. Decimals have infinitely many digits — there is no obvious grid. The structure of the problem is fundamentally different.

Why It Matters

This proof — the diagonal argument — is considered one of the most beautiful and important in all of mathematics. It shows that not all infinities are the same, and the technique appears throughout logic, computer science, and philosophy.

The proof
Build a list. Any list.

Here is someone's attempt at a complete list of decimals between 0 and 1. Each number has 8 digits shown.

We are going to construct a new number — digit by digit — that is not on this list.

The diagonal number — building it now
We take the 1st digit of number 1, the 2nd digit of number 2, and so on — the diagonal. Then we change each digit by +1 (wrapping 9→0).
0. _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _
Tap "Next digit" to reveal the diagonal number step by step.
The number we built is not on the list
Try This

Try changing the rule — instead of +1, what if you added +2 to each diagonal digit? You would get a different missing number, but the logic is identical. Any systematic change that avoids matching produces a number not on the list.

What To Notice

Our new number differs from row 1 in its 1st digit. Differs from row 2 in its 2nd digit. Differs from row n in its nth digit. So it cannot be anywhere on the list — it disagrees with every single entry at the diagonal position.

Why It Matters

This is not about one particular list being incomplete. The argument works for any list whatsoever. No matter how cleverly you arrange the decimals, the diagonal always produces a missing number. The decimals are fundamentally uncountable.

The consequence
A bigger infinity

The fractions are countably infinite — size ℵ₀.

The decimals between 0 and 1 cannot be listed. They are uncountably infinite. A genuinely larger infinity.

These two infinite sets are not the same size.

ℵ₀ = size of whole numbers, fractions
‹
𝔠 = size of decimals (the continuum)
𝔠 is strictly larger than ℵ₀. Some infinities are genuinely bigger than others.

Think of it this way. The whole numbers are like a long queue — however long it is, there's always a "next person." You can always point to the 1st, the 2nd, the 47th.

The decimals are like a cloud of dust — so densely packed, in every direction, that you can't even begin to put them in a queue. There's no "first" decimal after 0.

Try This

Try to think of a way around the diagonal argument. Could you somehow include the diagonal number on your list in advance? (Hint: if you did, the diagonal would just produce a different missing number. You can never escape.)

What To Notice

Before 1891, mathematicians assumed infinity was just... infinity. One thing. Cantor proved it comes in different sizes — and that there are actually infinitely many different sizes of infinity.

Why It Matters

Cantor did not discover this — he proved it. Rigorously. With no assumptions beyond logic. The same diagonal technique reappears in Gödel's incompleteness theorems and Turing's halting problem. You have just learned one of the most powerful ideas in all of mathematics.

💡 What you learned

To show a list is incomplete, construct a number that differs from row n in position n — the diagonal. This number cannot be anywhere on the list. Therefore no list of decimals is complete. The decimals are uncountably infinite — strictly larger than ℵ₀.

Coming in Act IV — The Sizes of Infinity

If ℵ₀ and 𝔠 are two different sizes of infinity — are there more? Can we always find a bigger infinity? And is there anything between ℵ₀ and 𝔠? Cantor spent years trying to answer that last question. The answer turned out to be more shocking than any of his earlier discoveries.

Try This

Write down any list of decimal numbers between 0 and 1. Now construct a new number by changing the first digit of the first number, the second digit of the second, and so on. Is your new number on the list?

What To Notice

No matter how you arrange your list, the diagonal construction always produces a number that differs from every entry. This means the real numbers cannot be listed — they are uncountably infinite.

Why It Matters

Cantor's diagonal argument proves there are different sizes of infinity. The reals are strictly 'larger' than the naturals. This single proof changed mathematics forever and opened the door to set theory.

The Broken Machine · Act I
Hilbert's Dream
🏛
David Hilbert
Konigsberg, 1900

David Hilbert. The greatest mathematician of his age. Standing before the world's mathematicians in Paris. About to make a promise.

"Every mathematical problem can be solved."

Not might be. Not probably will be. Can be.

He meant it. And for a moment — for a beautiful, optimistic moment — almost everyone believed him.

Try This

Before you continue, ask yourself: do you believe every mathematical question has a definite answer? Every puzzle a solution? Every conjecture a proof or a disproof? Hold your answer in mind as you enter the dream.

What To Notice

Hilbert's claim is not about intelligence or effort. It is a claim about the nature of mathematics itself — that truth and provability are the same thing.

Why It Matters

If Hilbert was right, mathematics would be a machine. Feed in axioms, apply rules, and every truth would eventually emerge. No mystery. No ambiguity. Just certainty, forever.

This is what Hilbert saw. A machine of pure logic. Feed in true axioms, apply correct rules, and the truth comes out the other end. Every time. No ambiguity. No doubt.

It was, he believed, just a matter of finding the right axioms and being patient enough.

Discovery
The Completeness Question

The machine is still running. But now we feed it harder problems. Watch what happens when we queue up questions that mathematicians have wondered about for centuries.

The gears keep spinning. The last two problems never resolve. Why?

Hilbert's programme had three parts. First: prove that mathematics is complete — every true statement is provable. Second: prove it is consistent — it never proves a contradiction. Third: prove it is decidable — there is a mechanical procedure to determine whether any statement is true.

He called the decidability question "the main problem of mathematical logic."

He was right about its importance. He was wrong about the answer.

Notice something. These three hopes — completeness, consistency, decidability — are not independent. If you had all three, mathematics would be a machine. You would never need intuition, creativity, or genius. You would just follow the procedure.

Hilbert thought that was a good thing. Not everyone agreed.

Proof
Inside a Formal System

Now you build proofs yourself. Here is a simplified formal system — "Peano arithmetic lite" — with three axioms and two inference rules.

Axioms
A1:  0 is a natural number
A2:  If n is a natural number, then S(n) is a natural number
A3:  For any natural number n: S(n) ≠ 0
Inference Rules
Substitution:  Replace a variable with any natural number
Modus Ponens:  From "If A then B" and "A", conclude "B"
The Key Insight

You just proved theorems from scratch. You never needed to "think" — only to follow rules. This is what a formal system is. The axioms are the starting assumptions. The rules are the only moves allowed. Every theorem is just a path from axioms to conclusion, following rules.

The machine can, in principle, explore all possible paths. Try all possible rule applications. Check every sequence. This is what Hilbert imagined: a systematic, exhaustive, mechanical proof of everything.

Connection
The Stakes

Hilbert published his programme in 1900. For thirty years, it dominated mathematics. The greatest minds in the world worked to fulfil it. Consistency proofs were constructed for simpler systems. Progress was made.

Then, in September 1930, Hilbert gave a famous speech.

"Wir mussen wissen. Wir werden wissen."
"We must know. We will know."
The Proof Machine
⚙ ⚙ ⚙
The machine hums. Gold stamps cascade. Everything is working.

The next day, at a conference in the same city, a quiet 24-year-old presented a paper that made "we will know" permanently impossible.

His name was Kurt Godel.

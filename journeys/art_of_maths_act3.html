The Mathematics of Beauty · Act III
Tiling the Infinite
Lens: Symmetry
◇

You've seen how proportion creates beauty and projection creates depth. Now: what happens when a pattern repeats — not once, not twice, but infinitely?

What shapes can tile a floor with no gaps and no overlaps? The question sounds simple. The answer spans thousands of years — from ancient mosaics in the Alhambra to a 2023 discovery that stunned mathematicians.

We shall journey from the humblest square tile to shapes that fill the plane yet never repeat.

A question of infinity

Tiling a floor is finite. Tiling the entire infinite plane — that is where the mathematics becomes extraordinary.

Regular tessellations
The Only Three

Only three regular polygons can tile the plane alone. The key constraint: angles at each vertex must sum to exactly 360°.

Interior angle: 60° × 6 = 360°
Try This

Switch between the three shapes. Watch how each fills the plane differently, yet all share the same 360° rule at every vertex.

What to Notice

Only three regular polygons work: triangles (60°), squares (90°), and hexagons (120°). Pentagons (108°) fail — 108 does not divide 360 evenly.

Why It Matters

The 360° constraint is absolute. For a regular n-gon, the interior angle is (n−2)×180°/n. Only when copies of this angle sum to exactly 360° can the polygon tile the plane.

Islamic geometric art
The Geometry of the Alhambra

Long before Escher, Islamic mathematicians and artists created the most sophisticated geometric art in history. The Alhambra palace in Granada, built in the 13th century, contains examples of all 17 wallpaper symmetry groups — 600 years before mathematicians proved there were exactly 17.

6-fold star — compass-and-straightedge construction from a circle

In 2007, physicists Peter Lu and Paul Steinhardt showed that medieval Girih tiles at the Darb-i Imam shrine in Isfahan create quasi-crystalline patterns — the same mathematical structure Penrose discovered in 1974. Islamic artists found aperiodic order 500 years before Western mathematics.

Try This

Toggle between the three patterns. Watch how the 6-fold star emerges from simple compass construction, the 8-fold star creates interlocking squares, and the Girih pattern uses five tile shapes to create quasi-crystalline order.

What to Notice

The Girih pattern uses five shapes: decagon, pentagon, rhombus, bowtie, and hexagon. Together they produce 10-fold rotational symmetry — something impossible with periodic tilings.

Why It Matters

This is one of the greatest mathematical achievements in art history — and it was hiding in plain sight on mosque walls.

The four symmetry operations
Building the Seventeen

Every repeating pattern is built from exactly four symmetry operations. These four moves — and their combinations — produce all 17 wallpaper groups.

Translation — shifting the pattern along a direction
Try This

Toggle between the four operations. Watch how each transforms the motif in a different way. Notice that glide reflection is the most subtle — a translation combined with a reflection.

What to Notice

Rotation can be 2-fold (180°), 3-fold (120°), 4-fold (90°), or 6-fold (60°). No other rotation orders are possible in a periodic tiling — 5-fold symmetry breaks periodicity.

Now combine these four operations. Mathematicians proved in 1891 that there are exactly 17 distinct ways to combine them in a repeating 2D pattern. Each combination is called a wallpaper group.

p1 — Translation only (simplest wallpaper group)
What to Notice

Each group has different symmetry operations: rotation points (small dots), mirror lines (dashed), and glide reflections. The faint overlay lines show the symmetry elements at work.

Why It Matters

These 17 groups are a complete classification — there is no 18th wallpaper group waiting to be discovered. This is one of the great finiteness theorems of mathematics.

Escher's method
Cut, Translate, Tessellate

M.C. Escher didn't just draw tessellations — he invented a visual algebra of transformations: translate, rotate, reflect. Take a square tile, cut a shape from one side, paste it to the opposite side. The modified tile still tessellates.

Step 1 of 5: A simple square tile
Try This

Watch Escher's process auto-animate. A plain square transforms into an interlocking shape that still tiles the plane perfectly.

What to Notice

The area never changes. What is cut from one side is added to the opposite side, so the tile still fills space without gaps.

Why It Works

Escher's method preserves the tessellation property because what you remove from one side, you add to the other. The tile's area is unchanged, and opposing edges still interlock perfectly.

Aperiodic order
Penrose Tilings

In 1974, Roger Penrose created a tiling with just two shapes that fills the plane — but never repeats. The ratio of the two tile types? The golden ratio, phi.

Fat tiles: 0, Thin tiles: 0, Ratio: —
Try This

Watch the pattern subdivide automatically, growing more intricate with each generation — yet it never settles into a repeating motif.

What to Notice

As subdivisions increase, the ratio of fat to thin tiles converges on phi (1.6180...). The golden ratio appears here because the subdivision rules are built from it.

Why It Matters

Aperiodic tilings have order without periodicity. This isn't merely a curiosity — in 1984, Dan Shechtman discovered quasicrystals with exactly this kind of structure, earning a Nobel Prize.

The 2023 breakthrough
The Einstein Tile

The Penrose tiling (1974) needed two shapes. For 50 years, mathematicians asked: can one shape do it? In 2023, David Smith — a retired printing technician — found the answer. He called it ‘the hat’.

The hat tile — a single aperiodic monotile

The ratio of reflected to unreflected tiles? It involves phi — the golden ratio returns again. From Penrose's two shapes to Smith's single tile, the golden ratio threads through the deepest results in tiling theory.

Try This

Toggle between the aperiodic and periodic views. In the periodic attempt, gaps appear — the hat simply refuses to tile periodically.

What to Notice

Some hat tiles are reflected (mirror images). The combination of reflected and unreflected tiles is what prevents periodicity.

Why This Matters

The ‘einstein’ (German for ‘one stone’) solved one of the oldest open problems in combinatorial geometry. A single shape that tiles the infinite plane, yet never admits a periodic pattern — the holy grail of tiling theory.

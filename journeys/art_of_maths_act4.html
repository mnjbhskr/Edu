You've discovered that proportion creates beauty, projection creates depth, and symmetry creates infinite patterns. Now: what happens when a simple rule repeats itself — over and over, at every scale?

This question led Benoit Mandelbrot in 1967 to ask: how long is the coast of Britain? The answer depends on the length of your ruler — and it revealed a geometry hidden between dimensions.

Mandelbrot called these shapes fractals — from the Latin fractus, meaning broken. They are the geometry of coastlines, mountains, lightning, trees, and the very blood vessels in your body.

The fractal revolution

Classical geometry gives us smooth lines and perfect circles. But nature is rough, jagged, and self-similar — the same patterns repeating at every scale. Fractal geometry is the mathematics of the real world.

In 1904, the Swedish mathematician Helge von Koch constructed a curve that was continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere. Start with a triangle. Replace the middle third of every side with an equilateral bump. Repeat — forever.

The Koch snowflake has infinite perimeter — yet finite area. It converges to exactly 8/5 of the original triangle. A shape that goes on forever, yet fits in your hand.

The Koch snowflake isn't just a mathematical curiosity. Nature is full of shapes that branch, split, and repeat at every scale. Rivers split like trees. Trees branch like lungs. Lightning forks like blood vessels. This isn't coincidence — it's recursion.

Watch the snowflake auto-animate through iterations. Pause to step through one at a time, and see each line segment smoothly split and grow its bump. By iteration 6, the snowflake has 12,288 sides.

What to Notice

The perimeter grows without bound (multiplied by 4/3 each step), but the area converges. The snowflake is adding ever-finer detail at the boundary without expanding outward.

Why It Matters

This is the fractal paradox: infinite complexity contained within finite bounds. It's why coastlines have no definitive length — measurement depends on scale. And it's why nature uses recursive growth: a simple branching rule, repeated, creates extraordinary complexity.

In 1915, Wacław Sierpiński described a triangle that is neither one-dimensional nor two-dimensional. Start with a solid triangle. Remove the central triangle. Repeat for every remaining triangle — forever.

The Sierpinski triangle is not 1-dimensional like a line, nor 2-dimensional like a plane. Its fractal dimension is log(3)/log(2) ≈ 1.585 — it lives between dimensions. Like the Koch snowflake, it emerges from one simple recursive rule applied infinitely.

What to Notice

Each iteration removes the centre third of every triangle. The number of triangles triples, but each is one-quarter the size. Watch the depth-based colours reveal the recursive structure.

Why It Matters

The Sierpinski triangle has dimension log(3)/log(2) ≈ 1.585 — more than a line (1D) but less than a filled shape (2D). This is what "fractal dimension" means: complexity between integer dimensions.

In 1979, Benoit Mandelbrot asked a simple question: for which complex numbers c does the iteration z → z² + c stay bounded? The answer is the most complex object in mathematics.

zn+1 = zn² + c,   z0 = 0

The formula is simple: z → z² + c. Start at zero, square and add, repeat. If the number escapes to infinity, colour it by how fast it escaped. That's it — that's the entire Mandelbrot set.

How do you measure the dimension of a fractal? Mandelbrot used a beautifully simple method: box counting. Cover the shape with a grid of boxes. Count how many boxes contain part of the shape. Make the grid finer, and count again.

Mandelbrot measured the fractal dimension of Britain's coastline at approximately 1.25 — more than a line, but less than a plane. The rougher the coast, the higher the dimension. Nature's recursive growth leaves a measurable signature.

The log-log plot below the coastline shows points falling on a remarkably straight line. The slope of that line is the fractal dimension. For this simplified Britain coastline, it's around 1.25.

Why It Matters

Plot log(count) against log(grid divisions). If the shape is fractal, the points fall on a straight line — and the slope is the fractal dimension. A line gives slope 1. A filled square gives slope 2. Britain's coast gives ~1.25. This single number captures the recursive complexity of a shape.

A tree is the clearest example of nature's recursive growth: every branch splits into smaller branches, which split into smaller branches still. The same rule, applied at every scale — from a 30-metre trunk to a 3-millimetre twig.

Richard Taylor measured Jackson Pollock's drip paintings in 1999 and found fractal dimensions between 1.3 and 1.7 — increasing over his career as his technique evolved. But the deeper insight is this: nature itself grows through recursive processes. Every tree, every river delta, every mountain range is a fractal.

Why It Matters

Nature grows through recursive processes: a single rule — "branch and repeat" — applied at every scale. From Pollock's paintings (D ≈ 1.5) to Romanesco broccoli (D ≈ 1.9), fractal dimension measures how thoroughly a recursive process fills space. Artists and nature alike use recursion to create complexity from simplicity.

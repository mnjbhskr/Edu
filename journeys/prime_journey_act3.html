Try This

Build the Ulam spiral slowly and watch the gold dots appear. Then toggle the diagonal overlay on and off. Try resetting and building again — each time, pay attention to a different region of the spiral. Do the diagonals hold everywhere, or do they fade at the edges?

What To Notice

Primes cluster visibly along certain diagonal lines in the spiral. These diagonals correspond to quadratic polynomials — simple formulas like n2 + n + 41 that produce an unusually high density of primes. The pattern is not perfect, but it is far stronger than chance would predict.

Why It Matters

Stanislaw Ulam discovered this pattern by accident in 1963 while doodling during a lecture. The fact that primes form visible structure in a spiral arrangement is still not fully explained. It connects to deep questions about which polynomial expressions generate primes — a frontier that links number theory to algebra and geometry.

The formula
Gauss at fifteen
🧮
Carl Friedrich Gauss
Mathematician · 1777–1855
"At fifteen, he studied prime-counting tables late into the night. He noticed something — a rough formula that predicted prime density with uncanny accuracy. It would take another century to prove he was right."

Gauss asked: among the numbers 1 to N, approximately how many are prime?

He noticed the answer seemed to follow a pattern connected to something called the natural logarithm.

π(N) ≈ N / ln(N)
π(N) = count of primes up to N  ·  ln = natural logarithm

The remarkable thing isn't just that this works — it's how well it works. Watch:

💡 What is ln(N)?

ln(N) is the natural logarithm — it measures how many times you multiply by e ≈ 2.718 to reach N. Think of it like this: if numbers were rungs on an ever-stretching ladder, ln(N) counts how high the ladder has grown. When N = 100, ln(N) ≈ 4.6. When N = 1,000,000, ln(N) ≈ 13.8. It grows — but agonisingly slowly. That slow growth is why primes thin out: as numbers get larger, each one is less likely to be prime, because there are more potential divisors below it. But the thinning is gentle — primes never vanish entirely.

The unsolved
The deepest mystery

Gauss's formula is an approximation. It tells you roughly how many primes lie up to N.

But mathematicians wanted to know the exact distribution — the precise music behind the primes.

In 1859, Bernhard Riemann found a formula that seemed to capture that music perfectly.

The formula required a hypothesis. A guess.

The guess has never been proved.

The Riemann Hypothesis
All non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function have real part equal to ½.
One of seven Millennium Prize Problems. Prize: $1,000,000.
Status: Unproved since 1859.
What this means simply

Riemann found a connection between prime numbers and a completely different area of mathematics — complex analysis. His hypothesis says that primes are distributed as regularly as possible given their constraints. If it's true, we understand primes deeply. If it's false — our entire picture of prime distribution is wrong.

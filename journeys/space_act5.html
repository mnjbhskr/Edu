The Shape of Space · Act V
The Universe Has Shape
🔭

You have seen three geometries. Flat, spherical, hyperbolic.

You have seen that curvature is measurable from within. You have seen that mass warps spacetime, and that gravity is simply the shape of that warping.

Now the biggest question of all: What is the global shape of the universe itself?

Not a philosophical question. An empirical one. We can measure it.

But first — what do we even mean by "shape" in higher dimensions? Let's build our intuition, one dimension at a time.

Try This
Click through each dimension from 0D to 4D. Watch the shape build up: each new dimension is created by extruding the previous shape in a new perpendicular direction. At 4D, watch the tesseract rotate — it is a projection of a 4D cube onto your 2D screen.
What To Notice
Each dimension doubles the number of vertices: a point has 1, a line has 2, a square has 4, a cube has 8, and a tesseract has 16 vertices. The rotation you see at 4D is a shadow — just as a 3D cube casts a 2D shadow on paper, a 4D hypercube casts a 3D shadow in our space.
Why It Matters
Our universe has at least 4 dimensions (3 of space + 1 of time). String theory proposes 10 or 11. Understanding higher dimensions is not abstract — it is essential for cosmology, for understanding general relativity, and potentially for a theory of everything.
Cosmology
Three possible universes

The global shape of the universe depends on its total energy density. Cosmologists call this the density parameter Omega. Tap each to explore what it means.

What the evidence says

Observations of the cosmic microwave background — the afterglow of the Big Bang — show the universe is flat to within 0.4% precision (Omega = 1.000 +/- 0.004, Planck 2018). We appear to live in a flat universe. Parallel lines really do stay parallel forever — at least as far as we can tell. The question remains open at the very largest scales.

Real-world connection — Cosmology

The Planck satellite measured the cosmic microwave background (CMB) — light from 380,000 years after the Big Bang. By measuring the angular size of temperature fluctuations in the CMB, cosmologists determined the curvature of the universe. The same principle Gauss used with mountain triangles, applied to the largest triangle possible — spanning the observable universe.

Try This

Imagine you're an ant living on a surface. You walk in a big loop and bring the loop back to a point. If every possible loop can shrink to a point, what does that tell you about your surface?

What To Notice

If every loop on a surface can be continuously shrunk to a point, the surface must be a sphere (in the appropriate dimension). This is the Poincaré Conjecture, proved by Perelman in 2003 after a century of attempts.

Why It Matters

The Poincaré Conjecture was one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems. Its proof used Ricci flow — a technique from differential geometry that 'smooths out' shapes. Topology continues to drive some of the deepest mathematics of our time.

The full journey
From postulate to cosmos

Every act was one layer of the same idea. Space has shape. That shape can be measured. That shape is gravity. That shape is the universe.

The thread

Euclid's 5th postulate was an assumption about the shape of space. Remove it, and space can be curved. Gauss showed curvature is intrinsic. Riemann generalised to any dimension. Einstein identified curvature with gravity. The universe turned out to be the largest geometry problem ever posed. It began with a postulate that troubled mathematicians for 2000 years.

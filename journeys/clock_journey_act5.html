The Group
✦

Look back at everything you've discovered.

Clock numbers that wrap. Days that cycle. Multiplication tables with complete cycles. Caesar shifts that chain together. All of them share a hidden shape — a mathematical structure called a group.

A group is not a thing. It is a pattern of behaviour. Four simple rules — and when something obeys them, it unlocks all the theorems of group theory at once.

Why this matters

Group theory is the mathematics of symmetry. It was used to classify every possible crystal structure. To prove which equations can be solved. To understand why the electron has spin ½. It begins here — with a clock.

Four rules
The axioms of a group

Our clock: numbers 0–4, addition mod 5. Tap each rule to verify it holds — and to see why it matters.

Try This

Tap each of the four axiom cards below — Closure, Associativity, Identity, and Inverse. Read the example calculation in each, then verify it mentally on the mod-5 clock.

What To Notice

Every axiom holds. Closure: 3+4 mod 5 = 2, still in the set. Identity: adding 0 changes nothing. Inverses: 3+2 = 0 mod 5, so 2 and 3 are partners. All four rules check out.

Why It Matters

These four axioms define a group — the most fundamental algebraic structure in mathematics. Once you verify them, every theorem of group theory automatically applies. Clock arithmetic mod 5 is not just arithmetic — it is an algebraic structure with deep consequences.

✓ All four axioms verified

Clock arithmetic mod 5 is a group. We just proved it — not by authority, but by checking. Every consequence of group theory now applies to our little clock.

Universality

💡 The remarkable thing

These look completely different — clocks, symmetry, numbers, letters. But they're all groups. A theorem proved about any group applies to all of them simultaneously. That's the power of abstraction: one proof, infinite applications.

The story
Évariste Galois

In 1832, a 20-year-old French mathematician was killed in a duel.

The night before, knowing he might die, Évariste Galois wrote furiously — filling pages with mathematics he feared would be lost. He asked his friend to send the work to the great mathematicians of the day.

Those pages contained the seed of group theory.

The question he answered
For centuries, mathematicians had formulas to solve equations of degree 2, 3, and 4.

Everyone assumed a formula for degree 5 (like x⁵ + x + 1 = 0) existed but hadn't been found yet.

Galois proved — using groups — that no such formula exists. Some equations are genuinely unsolvable by any formula, no matter how clever.

He didn't just answer the question. He invented the language needed to ask it properly.

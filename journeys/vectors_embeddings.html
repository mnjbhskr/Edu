Vectors & Embeddings

How mathematics turns meaning into geometry — and why "King - Man + Woman = Queen" is not magic, but linear algebra.

The Geometry of Meaning

A vector is an arrow in space — a magnitude and a direction. But in the mathematics of modern AI, vectors carry something far more surprising: they carry meaning.

The central insight: if you train a neural network to predict surrounding words in a large corpus of text, it will — as a side effect — learn to represent every word as a point in a high-dimensional space where geometry reflects semantics. Words with similar meanings cluster together. Analogies become vector arithmetic.

The Word2Vec Discovery · Mikolov et al., 2013

Training a shallow neural network on 100 billion words of Google News text, researchers found that the resulting vectors obeyed remarkable geometric relationships. vec("King") - vec("Man") + vec("Woman") ~ vec("Queen"). The geometry of analogy had emerged spontaneously from the task of prediction.

King - Man + Woman = Queen: A Demonstration

This is not magic — it is vector arithmetic. Each word occupies a point in space. The vector from "Man" to "King" encodes the concept of royalty. Apply that same vector starting from "Woman," and you land near "Queen."

Why It Matters
  • Analogy arithmetic shows that embeddings encode relationships, not just categories.
  • This is why AI can reason about novel combinations: the geometry generalises beyond training examples.
  • Every modern language model begins by converting tokens to vectors in this kind of space.

Why Geometry Encodes Meaning

Suppose you describe every word by listing its properties: royalty-ness, gender, geography, living-ness, and so on. Two words with similar profiles will have vectors pointing in similar directions. The angle between them — cosine similarity — measures how alike they are.

Word embeddings discover this description space automatically. The network finds the dimensions useful for prediction. After training, dimension 173 might correspond to "royalty," dimension 512 to "gender." Nobody specified this. It emerges from patterns in language.

cosine_similarity(a, b) = (a . b) / (||a|| . ||b||) Range: -1 (opposite meaning) to +1 (identical meaning) Value: 0 means orthogonal — unrelated vec("Paris") - vec("France") + vec("Germany") ~ vec("Berlin") vec("walked") - vec("walk") ~ vec("ran") - vec("run")

Word Clustering in Vector Space

Words with related meanings naturally cluster together in embedding space. Animals group near animals, cities near cities, emotions near emotions. This clustering emerges entirely from the statistics of language — no labels or categories are provided.

Cosine Similarity

The fundamental measure of relatedness in embedding space is cosine similarity: the cosine of the angle between two vectors. When two vectors point in the same direction, cos(0) = 1 — maximum similarity. When perpendicular, cos(90) = 0 — unrelated. When opposite, cos(180) = -1.

  • Move to 90 degrees — the vectors are perpendicular and similarity drops to 0.
  • Go past 90 toward 180 — similarity becomes negative, meaning the concepts are opposed.
What To Notice
  • Similarity drops quickly at first (0 to 60 degrees), then more slowly — cosine is nonlinear.
  • Most word pairs in real embeddings have cosine similarity near 0 (nearly orthogonal). Only truly related words have high similarity.
Why It Matters
  • Cosine similarity is how search engines find "similar" documents, how recommendation systems find "similar" users, and how transformers compute attention weights.
  • It measures direction, not magnitude — a long vector and a short vector pointing the same way have similarity 1.

High-Dimensional Spaces

Real embedding models use 768, 1024, or 4096 dimensions. Higher dimensions allow more information to be packed into the geometry. But our brains cannot visualise 768-dimensional space — we use 2D and 3D projections (PCA, t-SNE) to inspect what the model has learned.

Counterintuitive fact about high dimensions

In high-dimensional spaces, almost all pairs of random vectors are nearly orthogonal (cosine similarity ~ 0). This is useful: a 768-dimensional embedding can store ~768 independent concepts without interference. The curse of dimensionality becomes a blessing when representing a rich world.

From Words to Everything

The embedding idea extends beyond words. Any discrete object — a product, a user, a protein sequence, a musical chord — can be embedded by training a model to predict something about it. The geometry that emerges reflects the structure of the domain.

Text embeddings encode sentences as single vectors, enabling semantic search: "find documents similar in meaning, not just keyword." This is how RAG systems find relevant context.

Multimodal embeddings (CLIP, 2021) jointly embed images and text into the same space, so a photo of a dog and the text "dog" end up near each other — enabling DALL-E style image generation from text descriptions.

Why this matters for intelligence

The embedding layer is where raw data becomes thought. Before a transformer can reason, it converts input tokens into vectors. The quality of these vectors determines the ceiling of everything the model can do. Embeddings are not preprocessing — they are the model's first act of understanding.

Embedding lookup: E in R^(V x d) V = vocabulary size (e.g. 50,257 tokens in GPT-2) d = embedding dimension (e.g. 768) For token index i: embed(i) = E[i, :] -- the i-th row Analogy arithmetic: a is to b as c is to ? answer = argmax_w cosine( embed(w), embed(b) - embed(a) + embed(c) ) Learning: E is updated by gradient descent — initially random, updated millions of times until geometry reflects semantics.

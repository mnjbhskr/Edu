The calendar wraps too — but at different numbers.

Weeks wrap at 7. Months at 12. Years have rules about leap days that involve mod 4 and mod 100 and mod 400.

What To Notice

Adding 7 days always gives remainder 0 — the same day of the week. Adding 52 weeks (364 days) gives 364 mod 7 = 0. That is why birthdays shift by only 1 or 2 days each year: a year is 365 or 366 days, not an exact multiple of 7.

Why It Matters

The calendar is modular arithmetic with multiple moduli running simultaneously — mod 7 for weeks, mod 12 for months, mod 4, mod 100, and mod 400 for leap years. Understanding this structure lets you calculate any date without a calendar.

💡 The pattern

The day of week depends only on the remainder mod 7 — not on how many full weeks you've passed. 52 weeks (364 days) → 364 mod 7 = 0 → same day of week. That's why birthdays shift by 1 or 2 days each year.

The complication
Leap years and mod

A year has 365 days. But the Earth actually takes 365.2422 days to orbit the sun.

That extra 0.2422 days builds up. After 4 years: ≈ 1 extra day. So we add a leap day every 4 years.

But not exactly. After 100 years: 25 leap days → slightly too many. So we skip the leap year every 100 years. But after 400 years that overshoots again. So we add it back every 400 years.

💡 Why this matters

1900 is divisible by 100 but not 400 — so it's not a leap year. 2000 is divisible by 400 — so it is. 2100 will not be. These rules mean a year is 365.2425 days on average — close enough to the real value that we won't need to adjust for thousands of years.

The Power Rule
d/dx [xⁿ] = n · xⁿ⁻¹
Multiply by the exponent, reduce the exponent by 1.
Works for any real number n.
💡 You discovered calculus's most useful shortcut

The power rule lets you differentiate any polynomial instantly. x⁵ → 5x⁴. 3x² → 6x. x → 1. A constant → 0. What took Newton a year of limit calculations, you can now do in seconds.

The power rule applies term by term. Choose a polynomial and watch each term get differentiated — step by step.

Differentiating term by term
Result
💡 Constants vanish, variables shrink

A constant term (like −5) has derivative 0 — it doesn't change, so its rate of change is zero. A linear term (like 4x) has derivative 4 — a constant rate of change, exactly as you'd expect. The rule works for everything.

The application
Finding the peak

At a maximum or minimum of a curve, it is — for just a moment — neither rising nor falling. The rate of change is exactly zero.

Which means: set the derivative equal to zero and solve. That's how you find the peak or trough — of a profit curve, a physical trajectory, an optimal design.

💡 What businesses actually do

Profit = Revenue − Cost. To maximise profit: differentiate with respect to quantity, set equal to zero, solve. This is the derivative in daily use — every optimisation problem in economics, engineering, and machine learning uses exactly this principle.

💡 What you learned

The power rule: multiply by the exponent, reduce it by one. Applied term by term to any polynomial. At a maximum or minimum, the derivative equals zero — giving us the tool to find optimal values of any smooth function. This is the most-used result in all of applied mathematics.

Coming in Act III — The Area

The derivative answers "how fast?" But a different question is lurking: a car is travelling at varying speed. How far has it gone in total? The answer is the area under the speed curve — and finding it exactly requires the same limiting idea, run in reverse.

What To Notice

The power rule (d/dx of x^n = n*x^(n-1)) works for every polynomial term. Combined with the chain rule, product rule, and quotient rule, you can differentiate almost any function you encounter.

Why It Matters

These rules transform calculus from a philosophical concept into a practical tool. Engineers, economists, and scientists differentiate functions thousands of times a day — these shortcuts make it possible.

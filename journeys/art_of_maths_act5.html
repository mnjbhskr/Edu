Algorithmic Beauty
Lens: Computation

You’ve gained four lenses: proportion, projection, symmetry, and recursion. Each revealed mathematics hidden inside beauty. Now the story reverses: mathematics doesn’t just describe beauty — it creates it.

Remember the question we began with: why do certain shapes, patterns, and proportions feel beautiful? You’re about to discover the answer.

In this final act, you will not merely observe. You will create. Lissajous figures, spirograph curves, cellular automata, fractal plants, and generative compositions — all born from pure mathematics.

Parametric Curves
Lissajous Figures

Lissajous figures were first seen on oscilloscopes — the beautiful signatures of frequency ratios. Two perpendicular oscillations combine into a single, graceful curve.

1 : 2
Frequency a 1
Frequency b 2
Phase δ π/2
Try This

Adjust the frequency sliders to different integer ratios. Try 1:1, then 2:3, then 3:5. Watch how the complexity grows with each combination.

What to Notice

Integer ratios create closed curves that retrace their paths perfectly. If the ratio were irrational, the curve would eventually fill the entire canvas — never quite closing.

Why It Matters

These patterns appear everywhere: in music (harmonic intervals), physics (coupled oscillations), and engineering (signal analysis). Beauty and utility are the same thing.

Rolling Circles
Spirograph Curves

The spirograph is pure mathematics — two circles and a fixed ratio produce curves of astonishing complexity. A small circle rolls against a larger one, and a pen traces patterns that seem impossibly intricate for such simple rules.

Outer R 80
Inner r 30
Pen d 25
Try This

Switch between Inside and Outside modes. Try setting R = 60, r = 20, d = 20 for a classic three-petalled flower, then increase the inner radius to see the petals multiply.

What to Notice

When R/r is a rational number, the curve closes on itself. When it is irrational, the pen never retraces its path — it would draw forever, filling an annular ring without ever repeating.

Why It Matters

Spirograph curves are hypotrochoids and epitrochoids — the same mathematics that describes planetary orbits (Ptolemaic epicycles), Wankel rotary engines, and the security patterns on banknotes. Simple circular motion creates extraordinary complexity.

Cellular Automata
Conway’s Game of Life

In 1970, mathematician John Conway created four simple rules. From those rules, an entire universe emerged — capable of computation, self-replication, and infinite complexity. This is perhaps the most profound example of mathematical beauty: simplicity generating complexity.

Gen: 0 | Pop: 0
  1. A live cell with 2 or 3 neighbours survives.
  2. A dead cell with exactly 3 neighbours comes alive.
  3. All other cells die.
Try This

Load the Glider preset, then press Play. Watch the small pattern travel across the grid. Now try Gosper Gun — it fires a stream of gliders endlessly. Click on the grid to toggle cells and create your own patterns.

What to Notice

Watch for gliders — small patterns that move across the grid. They were the first proof that the Game of Life could transmit information. Look also for still lifes (stable patterns) and oscillators (patterns that repeat cyclically).

Why It Matters

Conway’s Game of Life proves that extraordinary complexity can emerge from absurdly simple rules. This same principle — emergence — appears in economics, biology, and consciousness itself. The Game of Life is actually Turing-complete: it can compute anything a real computer can.

Lindenmayer Systems
Grammars of Growth

In 1968, biologist Aristid Lindenmayer invented a grammar for plant growth. The rules are simple — the results are alive.

Iterations 3
Try This

Select the Plant preset, then slowly increase the iterations from 1 to 5. Watch a twig become a bush, and a bush become a tree. Each iteration applies the same rule — the complexity is an illusion of repetition.

What to Notice

Every branch is a scaled-down copy of the whole. This is self-similarity — the same property you saw in fractals. L-systems are the bridge between fractal geometry and biological growth.

Why It Matters

L-systems show that simple rewriting rules generate enormous visual complexity. Nature’s growth follows similar recursive patterns — a fern frond, a tree branch, a blood vessel network. The code of life is shorter than you think.

Generative Composition
Mathematical Art Gallery

Today, artists like Vera Molnár and Casey Reas use code as their medium. Every pixel is placed by an equation. Every composition is a theorem made visible.

What to Notice

Despite the apparent randomness, every composition has structure. The shapes cluster in mathematically distorted grids. Sine and cosine functions warp the positions, creating organic flow from rigid geometry.

Why It Matters

Generative art proves that seeded randomness + mathematical distortion creates beauty. The same principle powers procedural generation in video games, visual effects in cinema, and the latest generative AI models. Controlled chaos is an art form.

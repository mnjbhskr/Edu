The Infinite Hotel · Act I
The Hotel That Never Fills
🏨

Imagine a hotel with infinitely many rooms.

Every room is occupied. Every single one. The sign outside reads: "No Vacancies."

Then a new guest arrives.

An ordinary hotel would turn them away. But this is Hilbert's Hotel — and the manager smiles and says:

"No problem. I can always make room."

David Hilbert, 1924

The German mathematician David Hilbert used this thought experiment to reveal how infinity breaks every rule we think we know about numbers. You are about to feel exactly the same confusion — and then the same delight — that his students felt a century ago.

Try This

Before you continue, try to answer: can a completely full hotel make room for one more guest? What about infinitely many more? Hold your answer in mind as you enter the hotel.

What To Notice

The key word is infinitely many rooms. In a finite hotel with 100 rooms, full means full. But infinity behaves differently from any number you have ever encountered.

Why It Matters

This is not a riddle or a trick. The logic behind Hilbert's Hotel is the same logic mathematicians use to compare infinite sets and build the foundations of modern mathematics.

The first puzzle
One new guest

Every room is taken. Room 1, room 2, room 3... all the way to infinity. A lone traveller arrives and needs a room.

The manager's solution is simple — and strange. Move every guest one room forward. Guest in room 1 → room 2. Guest in room 2 → room 3. And so on, forever.

Room 1 is now empty. The traveller checks in.

Hilbert's Hotel — rooms 1 to ∞
The hotel is fully occupied. Every room has a guest.
💡 What just happened

By shifting everyone one room forward, we created room 1 — without anyone losing their room. The hotel is still full (every room occupied), but it has one more guest than before. ∞ + 1 = ∞.

The second puzzle
An infinite bus

That was just one guest. Now something more extraordinary happens.

A bus pulls up outside — with infinitely many passengers. Passenger 1, passenger 2, passenger 3... forever.

The hotel is full. The bus is infinite. Can the manager still find rooms for everyone?

Try This

Pick any guest number or passenger number in your head. Can you work out which room they end up in? Guest n goes to room 2n-1. Passenger n goes to room 2n. Verify it for a few examples.

What To Notice

The trick works because infinity has no end. We can interleave two infinite sequences (odd rooms, even rooms) and still fill every room exactly once. No room is double-booked, no room is empty.

Why It Matters

This interleaving is a bijection — a perfect one-to-one matching. It is the formal tool mathematicians use to prove two sets are the same size, even when both are infinite.

The crack in intuition
As many even numbers as whole numbers?

The bus solution did something astonishing. It matched every whole number (the guests) with an even number (their new room).

1 → 2, 2 → 4, 3 → 6, 4 → 8 ...

Every whole number has exactly one even number partner. Every even number has exactly one whole number partner. A perfect, exhaustive matching.

What To Notice

Every whole number has exactly one even number partner — none are left out. The even numbers are a proper subset of the whole numbers, yet the two sets are the same size. This only works because both sets are infinite.

Why It Matters

This perfect matching is called a bijection. It is how mathematicians formally define "same size" for infinite sets. Two sets have the same cardinality if and only if you can pair every element of one with exactly one element of the other, with nothing left over.

The matching — tap each pair to check

But wait. The even numbers are a part of the whole numbers. Surely there are "fewer" of them?

For finite sets, yes. For infinite sets — this logic completely breaks down.

{1, 2, 3, 4, 5 ...} ↔ {2, 4, 6, 8, 10 ...}
These two sets can be matched perfectly — one to one, none left over.
In the language Cantor invented: they have the same size.
ℵ₀ = ℵ₀
The new definition of "same size"

Two sets have the same size — Cantor called this the same cardinality — if you can match every element of one with exactly one element of the other, with nothing left over on either side. For infinite sets, this produces results that feel impossible. But they are provably, rigorously true.

What To Notice

Every whole number has exactly one even number partner (n → 2n). None are left over on either side. This is a bijection — a perfect, exhaustive matching.

Why It Matters

A bijection is how mathematicians define "same size" for infinite sets. The whole numbers and even numbers have the same cardinality (ℵ₀) — even though one is a proper subset of the other. This is the essence of infinity.

∞ + 1 = ∞. ∞ + ∞ = ∞. This is not a trick — it is mathematics.
💡 What you learned

Infinity is not a very large number. It is a different kind of thing entirely. Two infinite sets have the same size if you can match them perfectly, one to one. The whole numbers and even numbers match perfectly — so they're the same size. This defies intuition. That's exactly why it's worth knowing.

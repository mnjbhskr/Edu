Control Theory and Feedback

A modern aircraft makes thousands of corrections per second. It's not the pilot—it's mathematics watching, thinking, and acting faster than human reflexes could ever manage.

In the 1960s, aircraft autopilots were simple mechanical devices. A gyroscope sensed rotation, and springs pulled the control surfaces back to level. Primitive, but it worked—for straight, level flight.

Modern aircraft use computers running sophisticated control algorithms based on feedback control theory. These systems maintain altitude in turbulence, execute approaches in fog where pilots can't see the runway, and manage inherently unstable platforms like fighter jets.

The key insight is this: instead of trying to predict exactly what will happen, a control system measures the current state (altitude, heading, pitch, roll, speed) and adjusts the control surfaces to move towards the desired state.

This is feedback control. The system compares where it is to where it should be, measures the error, and corrects it. The correction algorithm is mathematics.

The Power of Feedback: Even if the aircraft model is imperfect or conditions change unexpectedly, a properly tuned feedback system adapts and maintains control.

The PID Controller

Three terms, three tuning knobs, one universal principle. The PID controller is the most important algorithm in flight control.

The PID controller computes a control signal based on three components:

u = KP·e + KI·∫e·dt + KD·de/dt
  • P (Proportional): Responds to current error. If altitude is 10 m below target, adjust. Proportional gain KP determines response strength.
  • I (Integral): Responds to accumulated error. If the system is consistently slightly low over time, add extra correction. Removes steady-state bias.
  • D (Derivative): Responds to rate of error change. If altitude is falling rapidly, predict it will get worse and correct aggressively. Prevents overshoot.

Tuning Trade-offs: More P = faster but oscillatory. More D = smoother but sluggish. Optimal tuning balances all three.

Fly-by-Wire: Humans vs Computers

Watch what happens when a pilot tries to land manually versus when a computer does it. The difference is dramatic.

The left side shows a human pilot's input—slightly jerky, over-correcting, creating oscillations. The right side shows a computer executing an optimal PID trajectory—smooth, efficient, precise.

This is why modern large aircraft are "fly-by-wire"—the pilots don't directly move the control surfaces. Instead, they tell the computer what manoeuvre they want, and the computer executes it with mathematics.

Benefits include:

  • Smoothness: Passengers experience less turbulence stress
  • Efficiency: Optimal control saves fuel
  • Safety: The computer prevents dangerous manoeuvres (overspeed, stall protection)
  • Stability: Inherently unstable aircraft (like fighters) become flyable

In an Airbus A320 or Boeing 787, if you try to command an impossible manoeuvre, the flight control computer won't let you. It uses mathematics to protect you from yourself.

The Mathematics of Safety: Control theory transformed aviation from dangerous guesswork into a precise, predictable science.

Same car, different question. The speedometer shows the speed at every moment. You want to know the total distance travelled.

Speed × time = distance — but only if the speed is constant. If it's changing every second, how do you add it all up?

You slice the journey into tiny pieces. In each tiny piece, the speed is nearly constant. Multiply speed × time for each slice. Add them all. As the slices get thinner, the sum becomes exact.

The same idea as the derivative

In Act I, we shrank a time interval to find the instantaneous rate of change. Here, we shrink many intervals — and sum them — to find the total accumulation. Two sides of the same coin.

💡 The integral is the limit of a sum

With 200 rectangles the error is less than 1%. With infinitely many — the limit — it becomes exactly 8. This limit is written: ∫₀⁴ t dt = 8. The integral sign ∫ is a stretched S — for "Sum". It is the sum of infinitely many infinitely thin slices.

💡 One formula, endless uses

Area under a speed curve = distance. Area under a force curve = work. Area under a probability curve = likelihood. The integral computes any accumulation — total quantity, total effect, total probability. One idea, infinite applications.

💡 What you learned

The integral ∫f(x)dx is the limit of a Riemann sum — adding up f(x)·Δx over narrower and narrower rectangles. As Δx→0, the sum becomes exact. The ∫ symbol is a stretched S for "Sum". dx is the infinitesimal width. Slicing infinitely thin gives infinite precision.

Coming in Act IV — The Revelation

Two ideas, built separately over three acts. The derivative: how fast something changes. The integral: how much it accumulates. Are these connected? The answer is the most beautiful theorem in all of calculus — and perhaps in all of mathematics.

What To Notice

As rectangles get narrower, the Riemann sum converges to the exact area. This is the definite integral. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects this area to the antiderivative — differentiation in reverse.

Why It Matters

Integration lets us compute total quantities from rates: distance from speed, work from force, probability from density. It is the inverse of differentiation, and together they form the two pillars of calculus.

The Language of Symmetry · Act II
The Group
🔗

We have six symmetry operations of the triangle. What happens when you perform two in sequence?

Rotate 120°, then reflect. What do you get? Is it always another one of our six? Try it and see.

The key question

If combining any two operations always yields another in the set, the set is closed under composition. Closure, plus three more properties, defines a group. The Cayley table captures every possible composition.

The Language of Symmetry · Act III
Groups Everywhere
🔢

Four axioms. One abstract structure. The same four axioms appear in the integers, clock arithmetic, rotations of a circle, and molecular symmetries.

The power: proving something once about the axioms proves it for every group simultaneously.

The abstraction payoff

Lagrange proved his theorem in 1771 — once, for any group. Every group of prime order is automatically cyclic. Every subgroup's size divides the group's size. One proof. Infinite consequences.

💡 Abelian vs non-abelian

In ℤ and ℤ₅, order doesn't matter: a+b = b+a. These are abelian groups. In D₃, order matters: rotate then reflect ≠ reflect then rotate. D₃ is non-abelian. The distinction matters deeply — non-abelian groups describe the weak and strong nuclear forces.

💡 Lagrange's Theorem (1771)

The size of any subgroup divides the order of the group. D₃ has order 6; its subgroups have orders 1, 2, 3, 6. Holds for every finite group. If a group has prime order p, it has no proper subgroups.

The Language of Symmetry · Act IV
Noether's Theorem
⚡

1915. Emmy Noether — working without pay, barred from German universities for being a woman — proved what many call the most important theorem in theoretical physics.

Every continuous symmetry of the laws of physics corresponds to a conserved quantity.

Emmy Noether, 1882–1935

Barred from teaching, she lectured under a male colleague's name. Einstein called her "the most significant creative mathematical genius thus far produced." She proved this theorem the same year Einstein completed General Relativity. The two results are deeply linked.

The correspondence
Symmetry is conservation

Three great symmetries. Three great conservation laws. Not separate facts — the same fact. Tap each to reveal the conservation law it implies.

💡 Symmetry is deeper than conservation

Energy conservation doesn't follow from "energy is fundamental." It follows because the laws of physics are the same today as yesterday. If time-translation symmetry broke — if physics differed on Tuesdays — energy would not be conserved. Symmetry is the reason conservation exists.

Noether's Theorem (1915)
"Every differentiable symmetry of the action of a physical system has a corresponding conservation law."

The Language of Symmetry · Act V
The Standard Model
⚛️

The fundamental forces of nature are not described by equations alone. They are described by groups.

We began with a snowflake. We end with the structure of reality.

Pure 19th-century algebra, 20th-century physics

Galois invented group theory in 1830 to understand polynomial equations — with no idea it would become the language of quantum field theory. Abstract structures invented for entirely different purposes turn out to be exactly what the universe needs.

The Standard Model
U(1) × SU(2) × SU(3)

Three groups. Three forces. One structure describing all known particle interactions.

💡 Group theory predicts particles

In 1961, Gell-Mann organised known hadrons using SU(3) — the "Eightfold Way." The patterns had gaps. He predicted the Omega-minus (Ω⁻) and its mass. Found in 1964, exactly as predicted. The Higgs boson was predicted by symmetry-breaking of SU(2)×U(1). Found at CERN in 2012, 48 years after prediction.

Try This

Rotate the square by 90 degrees. Keep rotating. How many rotations until you return to where you started?

What To Notice

Four 90-degree rotations compose to the identity — doing nothing. This set of rotations forms a group: it is closed (combining any two gives another), has an identity, and every operation has an inverse.

Why It Matters

You have just discovered a cyclic group of order 4 without knowing its name. Group theory was born from exactly this kind of pattern recognition — Galois, at age 20, used it to prove why some polynomial equations cannot be solved by radicals.

Bayesian thinking in the world
Every field, one idea

Bayesian reasoning is not an academic curiosity. It is the engine behind some of the most important technology and methodology of the modern world.

💡 The question to always ask

Every time you encounter a statistic, a test result, a news headline, a claim of certainty — ask: What was the prior? What is the base rate? How common is this in the population? The answer transforms information from raw numbers into genuine understanding.

Try This

Think of a random walk: flip a coin and step right for heads, left for tails. After 100 steps, you could be anywhere -- but most likely you are close to where you started. Now imagine a grain of pollen on water, jostled by millions of molecular collisions. That is Brownian motion -- a continuous random walk discovered by Robert Brown in 1827 and explained by Einstein in 1905.

What To Notice

Each act in this journey was a random walk through ideas. You started with a gut feeling (the prior), took a step when new evidence arrived, and ended up somewhere unexpected. The Bayesian loop -- prior, evidence, posterior -- is itself a walk: each update moves your belief, and after many updates, you converge on reality. Just as a random walk's average displacement grows with the square root of time, your certainty grows with the square root of evidence.

Why It Matters

Random walks and Brownian motion are not just mathematical curiosities. Stock prices follow random walks (the basis of modern finance). Molecules diffuse through random motion (the basis of chemistry and biology). Search algorithms explore solution spaces through randomised steps. Even your brain's neural activity resembles a random walk through possible states. The mathematics of uncertainty is not about eliminating randomness -- it is about understanding its structure and using it wisely.

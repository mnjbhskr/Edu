Spectroscopy

Reading the fingerprints of light and matter

Chapter 16 · Chemistry · Topic 7 · Difficulty 2

Every molecule absorbs, emits, or scatters light at characteristic frequencies determined by its structure. Spectroscopy exploits this fact to identify substances, measure concentrations, and probe molecular geometry. From the radio waves that flip nuclear spins to the gamma rays that probe atomic nuclei, each region of the electromagnetic spectrum reveals a different layer of molecular architecture.

Beer–Lambert Law: A = ε · c · l
A = absorbance  ·  ε = molar absorptivity (L mol−1 cm−1)  ·  c = concentration (mol L−1)  ·  l = path length (cm)
Selection rules: Not all transitions are allowed. Selection rules arise from the mathematics of how light interacts with matter. For electronic transitions: Δl = ±1. For vibrational transitions: the dipole moment must change. For rotational transitions: ΔJ = ±1. These rules explain why some peaks are missing from spectra.

Key insight: In NMR, the chemical shift δ reveals the electronic environment of each proton. Neighbouring protons split peaks into multiplets following the n+1 rule: n equivalent neighbours produce n+1 lines. The integration (area) of each peak tells you how many protons are in that environment.
Mass spectrometry — the fourth pillar: Alongside IR, NMR, and UV-Vis, mass spectrometry measures molecular weight by ionising molecules and sorting them by mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). The fragmentation pattern acts as a molecular fingerprint, revealing the molecule’s structure piece by piece.
Why Spectroscopy Matters
Spectroscopy is the primary tool chemists use to determine molecular structure. UV-Vis tells you about conjugation and colour. IR identifies functional groups. NMR reveals the carbon-hydrogen framework. Mass spectrometry (not covered here) gives the molecular weight. Together, these techniques let chemists identify unknown compounds, monitor reaction progress, and study molecular interactions — from drug design to environmental monitoring to forensic analysis.
Did you know? Astronomers use spectroscopy to determine the composition of stars billions of light-years away. Every element produces a unique set of spectral lines — a cosmic barcode. Helium was discovered on the Sun (helios) through its spectral lines in 1868, 27 years before it was found on Earth.

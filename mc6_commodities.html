Commodity Options & Black-76

●●●● Intermediate

Pricing options on futures — where cost-of-carry changes everything

1. Why Commodities Are Different

In equity and FX markets, the cost-of-carry relationship ties the forward price to the spot price through a clean arbitrage argument: buy the asset, finance it at the risk-free rate, and deliver it forward. The forward price is simply S·erT.

Commodities break this. You cannot costlessly store crude oil, natural gas, or electricity. Storage incurs real costs — tank rental, insurance, spoilage. More importantly, there is a convenience yield: the benefit of holding the physical commodity rather than a paper contract. A refinery with oil in its tanks can keep running; one holding only futures contracts cannot.

Because of these frictions, commodity options are almost always written on futures prices, not spot prices. The futures price already embeds all storage costs and convenience yields. Fischer Black recognised this in 1976 and published a variant of the Black-Scholes formula that prices options directly on forwards and futures — the Black-76 model.

This module covers Black-76 pricing, the quoting conventions of major commodity markets, Asian (average price) options used extensively in energy hedging, and the cost-of-carry relationship that determines whether a market is in contango or backwardation.

The convenience yield — the benefit of holding the physical commodity — breaks the textbook cost-of-carry relationship.

2. Black-76 vs Black-Scholes

Side-by-Side Comparison

Black-76 is not a different model — it is Black-Scholes with the futures price substituted for the spot price. The key insight is that futures prices are already risk-neutral expectations, so there is no drift term.

AttributeBlack-Scholes (1973)Black-76 (1976)
Underlying Spot price S Futures price F
Drift Risk-free rate r None (futures already risk-neutral)
Call Formula S·N(d1) − Ke−rT·N(d2) e−rT[F·N(d1) − K·N(d2)]
d1 [ln(S/K) + (r+½σ²)T] / σ√T [ln(F/K) + ½σ²T] / σ√T
Key Point Requires cost-of-carry assumption No cost-of-carry needed
▶ Derivation of Black-76 from Black-Scholes

Start with the Black-Scholes call formula:

C = S·N(d1) − K·e−rT·N(d2)

In Black-Scholes, d1 contains the term (r + ½σ²)T because the spot price drifts at rate r under the risk-neutral measure. For a futures contract, the no-arbitrage relationship gives:

F = S·erT  ⇒  S = F·e−rT

Substituting S = F·e−rT into the call formula:

C = F·e−rT·N(d1) − K·e−rT·N(d2)
  = e−rT[F·N(d1) − K·N(d2)]

And for d1:

d1 = [ln(S/K) + (r + ½σ²)T] / σ√T
   = [ln(F·e−rT/K) + (r + ½σ²)T] / σ√T
   = [ln(F/K) − rT + rT + ½σ²T] / σ√T
   = [ln(F/K) + ½σ²T] / σ√T

The r term cancels in d1, confirming that futures prices have zero drift under the risk-neutral measure. The discount factor e−rT applies to the entire payoff because the option premium is paid today but the futures settlement occurs at expiry.

3. Commodity Quoting Conventions

Major Commodity Markets

Each commodity market has its own quoting unit and contract size. Understanding these conventions is essential for converting between per-unit option prices and total contract values.

CommodityQuote BasisLot Size
Crude Oil (WTI/Brent) $/barrel 1,000 bbl
Natural Gas (Henry Hub) $/MMBtu 10,000 MMBtu
Gold $/troy oz 100 oz
Agricultural (Wheat, Corn) Cents/bushel 5,000 bu
Power $/MWh Variable
A WTI crude oil option priced at $2.50/barrel has a total contract value of $2,500 (= $2.50 × 1,000 bbl). Tick sizes, margin requirements, and settlement procedures vary by exchange — always check the contract specification.

5. Asian Options (Average Price Options)

An airline hedging its fuel cost does not care about the oil price on one specific day. It cares about the average price over a month, a quarter, or an entire year. Asian options — also called average price options — pay out based on the arithmetic average of the underlying price over a series of fixing dates.

Averaging reduces variance. The volatility of an average is lower than the volatility of the underlying, which makes Asian options cheaper than vanilla options with the same strike and maturity. This discount is the primary reason energy consumers and producers use them so extensively.

There is no closed-form solution for arithmetic average options because the sum of lognormal random variables is not lognormal. The standard approximation uses moment matching: compute the first two moments of the average, fit a lognormal distribution to those moments, and then apply Black-76 with an effective volatility.

The average:   A = (1/n) ∑ F(ti)

Second moment:   E[A²] = (1/n²) ∑i ∑j F² · exp(σ² · min(ti, tj))

Effective vol:   σeff = √(ln(E[A²]/E[A]²) / T)

Once σeff is known, the Asian option price is simply the Black-76 price with σeff in place of σ.

▶ Derivation of the moment-matching approximation

Under the risk-neutral measure, each fixing F(ti) is a martingale with E[F(ti)] = F (the current futures price). The first moment of the average is therefore:

E[A] = (1/n) ∑ E[F(ti)] = F

For the second moment, we need E[F(ti)·F(tj)]. Since ln F follows a Brownian motion with volatility σ:

E[F(ti)·F(tj)] = F² · exp(σ² · min(ti, tj))

This uses the fact that for lognormal variables X(t) = F·exp(σW(t) − ½σ²t), the covariance structure gives E[X(s)·X(t)] = F²·exp(σ²·min(s,t)).

The variance of ln A under the lognormal approximation is:

σeff² · T = ln(E[A²] / E[A]²)
= ln[(1/n²) ∑i,j exp(σ² · min(ti, tj))]

This σeff is then substituted into Black-76. The approximation is excellent for typical commodity option parameters and is the industry standard for energy and agricultural average-price options.

The more fixing dates in the averaging period, the lower the effective volatility and the cheaper the Asian option. Daily fixings produce a larger discount than monthly fixings.

How shocks in one variable propagate through a system over time

1. Impulse Response Functions

A bivariate VAR(1) captures dynamic interactions between two variables. The Cholesky ordering determines the contemporaneous causal structure. Swap the ordering to see how responses change.

Cholesky Ordering

The Cholesky decomposition assumes the first variable can affect the second contemporaneously, but not vice versa. Swapping the order can change the impulse responses dramatically — this is the identification problem in structural VARs.
The identification problem explained
A reduced-form VAR estimates the relationship between variables without imposing economic structure. The error covariance matrix Σ captures contemporaneous correlations, but we need to decompose Σ = PP' to identify “structural” shocks. The Cholesky decomposition is one choice, but it imposes a recursive causal ordering: the first variable can affect all others contemporaneously, the second can affect all but the first, and so on. Other identification strategies include:
Sign restrictions: Impose that certain impulse responses must be positive or negative.
Long-run restrictions: Blanchard-Quah (1989) — demand shocks have no long-run effect on output.
External instruments: Use instrumental variables to identify specific structural shocks.
Where you will see this: Central banks use VARs extensively. The seminal Sims (1980) paper argued that VARs provide a credible alternative to large-scale structural models. Modern monetary policy analysis relies on structural VARs to trace how interest rate shocks affect output and inflation over time.

Predator-Prey Dynamics

Wolves and rabbits — the Lotka-Volterra equations and the dance of survival

Chapter 15 — Biology & Life Sciences  ·  Topic 3  ·  Difficulty ★★★☆☆

In 1910, Alfred Lotka proposed a mathematical model for oscillating chemical reactions. Sixteen years later, Vito Volterra independently derived the same equations to explain why the proportion of predatory fish in the Adriatic Sea had risen during World War I, when fishing was reduced. The result is one of the most elegant models in mathematical biology: coupled differential equations that produce perpetual, phase-shifted oscillations between predator and prey populations. The Hudson's Bay Company's lynx-hare fur trapping records from 1845 to 1935 remain one of the most famous empirical confirmations of these dynamics.

The predator curve always lags the prey curve by roughly a quarter period — the classic 90-degree phase shift. When prey are abundant, predators thrive and multiply; but their growing numbers then deplete the prey, causing the predator population to crash in turn.
Closed loops vs. damped spirals. The classical Lotka-Volterra system is conservative: it has no friction-like term, so every trajectory in the phase plane is a perfectly closed loop that repeats forever. This is elegant but unrealistic. In real ecosystems, density-dependent effects — competition for territory, disease at high densities, limited nesting sites — act like damping forces. They cause orbits to spiral inward toward a stable equilibrium, producing oscillations that gradually fade rather than persist indefinitely. Distinguishing conservative from dissipative dynamics is one of the central questions in mathematical ecology.
Predator-prey cycles in the wild. • Lynx and snowshoe hare — the Hudson's Bay Company fur-trade records (1845–1935) show strikingly regular ~10-year population cycles, perhaps the most famous ecological time series ever recorded.
• Wolves and moose, Isle Royale — a 60-year study on a remote Lake Superior island documented coupled oscillations between wolves and their moose prey, complicated by disease, inbreeding, and climate.
• Fish and plankton — in lakes and oceans, planktivorous fish and their zooplankton prey display rapid boom-bust cycles on seasonal timescales, often studied in the context of eutrophication and water quality.

3 · Ecological Scenarios

Different ecosystems exhibit predator-prey dynamics at vastly different scales and rates. Adding a prey carrying capacity (logistic growth) transforms the conservative system into a damped one: orbits spiral inward to a stable equilibrium rather than cycling forever.

When logistic growth is enabled, the prey growth term becomes αx(1 − x/K) − βxy. This breaks the conservation law: orbits spiral toward the equilibrium point, reflecting the stabilising effect of density-dependent growth. Most real ecosystems are damped rather than truly conservative.
Did you know?  The Paradox of Enrichment: increasing the food supply can actually destabilise an ecosystem, causing predator-prey oscillations to grow until one species goes extinct. In the logistic Lotka-Volterra model, raising the carrying capacity K can push a stable equilibrium through a Hopf bifurcation into limit-cycle oscillations of ever-larger amplitude. More food doesn't always mean more stability — a counter-intuitive result first described by Michael Rosenzweig in 1971.

4 · The Competitive Exclusion Principle

When two species compete for the same niche, the outcome depends on the competition coefficients. The Lotka-Volterra competition model extends logistic growth with interspecific terms. Three regimes emerge: species 1 wins, species 2 wins, or stable coexistence.

Gause's competitive exclusion principle (1934) states that two species competing for exactly the same resources cannot stably coexist. Coexistence requires that each species limits itself more than it limits the other (α₁₂ < 1 and α₂₁ < 1). This principle underpins much of modern community ecology and biodiversity theory.
Why It Matters
The Lotka-Volterra equations, independently discovered by Alfred Lotka (1910) and Vito Volterra (1926), are the foundation of mathematical ecology. Volterra was motivated by a practical puzzle: why did the proportion of predatory fish in Adriatic fish catches increase during World War I, when fishing was reduced? His answer — that reduced harvesting disproportionately benefits predators — became Volterra's principle, now central to fisheries management.

The Hudson's Bay Company lynx-hare dataset (1845–1935) remains one of the most famous time series in all of science, showing beautifully phase-shifted population cycles over nearly a century. These dynamics connect directly to Population Dynamics (B1), Differential Equations (Chapter 6), and Evolutionary Game Theory (B7). Today, predator-prey models inform conservation biology, pest control, epidemiology, and even economics — wherever coupled feedback loops drive cyclical behaviour.

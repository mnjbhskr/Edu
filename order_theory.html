Order Theory

Partial orders, lattices, and the hidden structure of comparison
Analysis & Topology · Difficulty 3 (Advanced)

Order theory studies how objects compare to one another. Not everything can be ranked in a neat line — some things are simply incomparable. From the divisibility of numbers to the subsets of a set, partial orders reveal a rich branching structure that underpins much of modern mathematics.

Hasse diagrams strip away redundancy: if a ≤ b ≤ c, we draw edges a–b and b–c but not a–c. The diagram captures the full order via transitivity.

2. Types of Orders

Select an Order Type

Every total order is a partial order, but not every partial order is total. In a partial order, some pairs of elements may simply be incomparable — neither a ≤ b nor b ≤ a.

3. Lattices — Meet and Join

Choose a Lattice

Click two nodes to find their meet (∧) and join (∨).

A lattice is a partial order where every pair of elements has a unique greatest lower bound (meet) and a unique least upper bound (join). Distributive lattices satisfy a ∧ (b ∨ c) = (a ∧ b) ∨ (a ∧ c). The lattices M3 and N5 are the smallest counterexamples.

4. Zorn’s Lemma Visualised

Building a Partial Order

Press Add Element to build a partial order step by step.
Zorn’s Lemma: If every chain in a partial order has an upper bound, then the order has at least one maximal element. Equivalent to the Axiom of Choice, it is used across algebra (every vector space has a basis), analysis (Hahn–Banach theorem), and topology (Tychonoff’s theorem).

5. Fixed Points & Knaster–Tarski

Monotone Function on a Lattice

A monotone function f on a complete lattice always has at least one fixed point.
Knaster–Tarski Theorem: Let L be a complete lattice and f : L → L be order-preserving (monotone). Then the set of fixed points of f forms a complete lattice — and in particular, f has a least fixed point and a greatest fixed point.

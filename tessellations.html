There are exactly 8 semi-regular (Archimedean) tessellations of the plane. Each uses two or more types of regular polygon, with the same arrangement of polygons at every vertex.

4. Penrose Tiling

Aperiodic tiling

4
Penrose tilings are aperiodic — they never repeat, yet they fill the plane completely. They exhibit five-fold rotational symmetry and are related to quasicrystals, discovered by Dan Shechtman in 1984. Similar patterns appear in Islamic architecture, notably in the Darb-i Imam shrine in Isfahan (1453), predating Penrose’s discovery by over five centuries.

6. What Have We Learnt?

A tessellation (or tiling) covers the plane with shapes, leaving no gaps and no overlaps. The mathematics of tessellations connects geometry, algebra, and number theory in surprising ways.

Interior angle of a regular n-gon = (n − 2) × 180° / n

Tessellation condition: 360° / angle must be a positive integer

Crystallography

The 17 wallpaper groups classify all possible symmetries of periodic tilings in the plane. These same symmetries govern the structure of crystals.

Islamic Art

Mediaeval Islamic artisans discovered complex geometric tilings centuries before Western mathematics formalised them, including patterns with local five-fold symmetry.

M.C. Escher

Escher transformed regular tessellations into figurative art — interlocking birds, fish, and lizards — by systematically deforming the tile boundaries whilst preserving the tiling property.

Quasicrystals

Penrose tilings predicted the existence of quasicrystals — materials with long-range order but no translational periodicity. Shechtman’s 1984 discovery earned the 2011 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

How Science Speaks

Every scientific field invented its own mathematical representation — and the representation shapes what questions can be asked

Core idea Every scientific field developed representations that make its characteristic questions tractable — and the representation shapes which questions can even be asked.

1. Physics — Phase Space

PENDULUM IN TWO VIEWS

Real space — angle(t)
Phase space — (θ, ω)

💡 For small angles the phase curve is a closed ellipse — energy is conserved and the orbit closes exactly. For large angles the ellipse distorts into a lens. Add damping and the closed orbit becomes a spiral drawn inward toward the origin: the system is losing energy. Phase space turns the abstract idea of “stability” into a picture. 
Equation of motion (simple pendulum with damping):
    θ̈ = −(g/L) sin(θ) − γ·θ̇

Phase-space coordinates:   (θ, ω) = (angle, angular velocity)
Energy (undamped):         E = ½L²ω² + gL(1 − cosθ)
Closed orbits ↔ conservation. Spirals ↔ dissipation.

2. Biology — Phylogenetic Trees

TEN MAMMALS, ONE ANCESTRY

💡 Hover any internal node to reveal the estimated common ancestor in millions of years ago. The phylogram makes evolutionary time into a ruler; the cladogram throws the ruler away and keeps only the pattern of branching. Same ancestry, two representations — each answers a different question.

💡 A yield surface is a function ƒ(maturity, rating) rendered as a pseudo-3D mesh. Shift the level and every point rises together; twist the slope and long maturities rise faster than short. The shape carries information a table of numbers would bury — and traders read that shape the way a cartographer reads relief. 
Pseudo-3D oblique projection:
    x_screen = x + 0.5·y·cos(θ)
    y_screen = z − 0.5·y·sin(θ)

Parametric yield (illustrative):
    y(m, r) = level + slope·log(1 + m) + credit·r²

4. A Gallery of Scientific Representations

SIX FIELDS, SIX GEOMETRIES

Quantum — |ψ|²
The wavefunction is complex; only its squared modulus is observable — a probability density over space.
Epidemiology — SIR
Three coupled ODEs draw the epidemic: susceptible falls, infected peaks, recovered climbs.
Networks — P(k)
Degree distribution on a log-log plot: a straight line reveals a scale-free power law.
Medicine — Kaplan-Meier
A staircase of survival probability, falling at each observed event; censored data marked with ticks.
Climate — Anomaly
Temperature deviations from a reference baseline; blue below, red above — spatial structure appears.
Economics — Supply & Demand
Equilibrium is an intersection; shifting a curve traces comparative statics through price and quantity.
💡 Each of these diagrams is a coordinate system tailored to a question. Quantum mechanics needs a complex Hilbert space; epidemiology needs coupled ODEs; networks need a distribution on integers. The mathematics follows the representation — not the other way around.
What have we learnt?
Every scientific discipline has quietly negotiated a treaty between its questions and its mathematics. Phase space makes energy and stability visible at a glance. Phylogenetic trees turn molecular divergence into a metric on ancestry. Yield surfaces fold two financial dimensions into one readable relief map. None of these representations arose by accident — each was invented because the field’s characteristic questions were intractable in any other coordinate system. And each, by its shape, constrains what can even be asked: a phase-space diagram has no natural way to represent market sentiment, and a yield surface cannot see mutation rates. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

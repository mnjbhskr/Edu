Superconductivity

When resistance vanishes and quantum mechanics rules the macroscopic world

Chapter 17 · Solid State Physics · Topic 7 · Difficulty 3

In 1911, Heike Kamerlingh Onnes cooled mercury to 4.2 K and watched its electrical resistance plummet to exactly zero. This was not a gradual fade — it was an abrupt phase transition into a new quantum state of matter. Superconductors expel magnetic fields, carry lossless currents, and emerge from a collective dance of electrons called Cooper pairs. The phenomenon connects quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and electromagnetism in one of the most striking demonstrations that macroscopic matter can behave quantum-mechanically.

BCS ground state: all electrons near the Fermi surface pair into bosonic Cooper pairs with binding energy 2Δ, condensing into a single quantum state.
Did you know? In 1986, Bednorz and Müller discovered high-temperature superconductivity in a ceramic material (YBCO) with Tc = 93 K — above the boiling point of liquid nitrogen (77 K). This meant superconductors could be cooled with cheap, abundant liquid nitrogen instead of expensive liquid helium. They won the Nobel Prize in 1987 — the fastest Nobel award in physics history.

Key insight: The drop to zero resistance is not gradual — it is a sharp phase transition. Below Tc, electrons form Cooper pairs that move through the lattice without scattering. The resistance is not merely small; it is exactly zero, as demonstrated by persistent currents that flow for years without measurable decay.

2. The Meissner Effect

Toggle between states to see how the superconductor expels magnetic field lines.
State: Normal · Magnetic field penetrates the material
Meissner effect: Binside = 0   for T < Tc and B < Bc
Surface screening currents generate an opposing field that exactly cancels the applied field inside the bulk.
Key insight: A perfect conductor would merely trap whatever field was present when it became conducting. A superconductor actively expels pre-existing fields — this is the Meissner effect, and it proves superconductivity is a true thermodynamic phase, not just perfect conductivity.

3. Cooper Pairs

Controls

Watch how an electron distorts the lattice, creating an attractive potential for a second electron.
Electron–phonon coupling: lattice distortion creates an effective attraction between electrons
Key insight: Two electrons in free space repel each other via Coulomb interaction. But inside a crystal lattice, the first electron pulls nearby positive ions toward itself, creating a region of excess positive charge. This phonon-mediated attraction can be stronger than the Coulomb repulsion at low temperatures, binding electrons into Cooper pairs with opposite momenta and spins.
The BCS theory predicts a universal relationship: 2Δ(0) ≈ 3.52 kBTc, where Δ(0) is the energy gap at zero temperature and Tc is the critical temperature. This means all conventional superconductors share the same ratio of gap to critical temperature — a remarkable prediction confirmed by experiment.

4. Type I vs Type II Superconductors

Controls

Compare the phase diagrams. Type II superconductors have a mixed (vortex) state between Bc1 and Bc2.
Type I: single critical field Bc · Abrupt transition to normal state
Type I: B > Bc(T) ⇒ normal state
Type II: Bc1 < B < Bc2 ⇒ mixed state (Abrikosov vortex lattice) — magnetic flux penetrates in quantised vortices carrying Φ0 = h/2e
Key insight: Type II superconductors are far more useful technologically because their upper critical field Bc2 can be enormous (tens of teslas). In the mixed state, magnetic flux penetrates in quantised vortices that form a regular triangular lattice — the Abrikosov lattice, predicted in 1957 and directly imaged decades later.
Flux quantisation: Magnetic flux through a superconducting ring is quantised in units of Φ0 = h/2e ≈ 2.07 × 10−15 Wb. The factor of 2e (not e) appears because the charge carriers are Cooper pairs, each carrying charge 2e. This was one of the first direct confirmations that superconductivity involves paired electrons.
Critical surface: Superconductors have three critical parameters forming a ‘critical surface’: critical temperature Tc, critical magnetic field Bc, and critical current density Jc. Exceed any one of these and superconductivity is destroyed. For practical applications, maximising Jc is often the greatest engineering challenge.

The magnetic field decays exponentially inside a superconductor with characteristic length λL.
B(x) = B0 · e−x/λ · λL = 50 nm · B0 = 50 mT
London equation: ∇²B = B / λL²  ⇒  B(x) = B0 · e−x/λL
λL = √(m / μ0 ns e²)   where ns is the superfluid electron density
Key insight: The London penetration depth λL sets the length scale over which screening currents flow at the surface. Larger λL means weaker screening and deeper field penetration. At T = 0, λL depends only on the density of superconducting electrons — as temperature rises toward Tc, ns drops and λL diverges.

The Quantum Macroscopic State
Superconductivity is one of the rare phenomena where quantum mechanics becomes visible at the human scale. A superconducting ring can carry a persistent current indefinitely — not because resistance is very small, but because the current is a property of a macroscopic quantum wavefunction. The magnetic flux through such a ring is quantised in units of Φ0 = h/2e ≈ 2.07 × 10−15 Wb. This quantisation, the Meissner effect, and the energy gap are all consequences of a single idea: below Tc, electrons condense into a coherent quantum ground state described by BCS theory.

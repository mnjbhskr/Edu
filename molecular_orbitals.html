Molecular Orbital Theory

When atoms share, wavefunctions interfere

Chapter 16 · Chemistry · Topic 4 · Difficulty 3

Atoms don’t just share electrons — their wavefunctions interfere. When two atomic orbitals overlap, they combine constructively to form a bonding molecular orbital (lower energy, electrons concentrated between nuclei) and destructively to form an antibonding molecular orbital (higher energy, a node between nuclei). This is the foundation of molecular orbital theory, which explains bond orders, magnetism, and the stability of molecules from H ₂ to benzene.

Bond Order = ½(bonding electrons − antibonding electrons)

Constructive interference adds wavefunction amplitudes between the nuclei, creating a region of high electron density that holds the atoms together. Destructive interference cancels them, producing a node and pushing electron density away from the bonding region.
Think of it like sound waves. Molecular orbitals form just like sound waves combining. When two waves are in phase (peaks align), they add constructively — a bonding orbital with increased electron density between the nuclei. When out of phase (peak meets trough), they cancel destructively — an antibonding orbital with a node between the nuclei.

HOMO & LUMO — the frontier orbitals. The HOMO (Highest Occupied Molecular Orbital) and LUMO (Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbital) are the frontier orbitals that govern chemistry. Chemical reactions occur when electrons move from the HOMO of one molecule to the LUMO of another. The HOMO–LUMO gap determines colour (molecules absorb light matching this energy gap), reactivity (small gap = reactive), and electrical conductivity.

3 · Bond Order & Stability

Bond order predicts molecular stability. Higher bond order means shorter, stronger bonds. He₂ has bond order 0 and doesn’t exist as a stable molecule. O₂ is paramagnetic due to unpaired electrons in π* orbitals. The calculation animates step by step.

O₂ is paramagnetic — it has two unpaired electrons in degenerate π* orbitals. This was one of the great triumphs of MO theory: Lewis structures predict O₂ should be diamagnetic, but experiments show it is attracted to magnets. Only MO theory gets this right.
Did you know? Oxygen (O₂) is paramagnetic — it is attracted to a magnet. This puzzled chemists for decades until molecular orbital theory explained it: O₂ has two unpaired electrons in its π* antibonding orbitals. No other bonding model (Lewis structures, valence bond theory) correctly predicts this. It was MO theory’s greatest early triumph.

5 · Hückel Theory & the Frost Circle

The Frost circle is a beautiful geometric trick: inscribe a regular polygon vertex-down in a circle of radius 2|β|. Each vertex marks an energy level. The 4n+2 rule predicts aromatic stability; 4n electrons lead to anti-aromaticity.

4n+2 = aromatic (extra stable): cyclopropenyl cation (2e), benzene (6e), cyclooctatetraenyl dianion (10e). 4n = anti-aromatic (destabilised): cyclobutadiene (4e), planar cyclooctatetraene (8e). Anti-aromatic systems distort to avoid planarity.
The Woodward–Hoffmann rules predict whether a chemical reaction is ‘allowed’ or ‘forbidden’ based on orbital symmetry. Roald Hoffmann and Robert Burns Woodward showed that conservation of orbital symmetry governs pericyclic reactions — earning Hoffmann the 1981 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.
Why Molecular Orbital Theory Matters
Valence bond theory and Lewis structures can’t explain why O₂ is paramagnetic, why He₂ doesn’t exist, or why benzene is extraordinarily stable. Molecular orbital theory builds orbitals that span entire molecules, correctly predicting magnetic properties, bond strengths, and the special stability of aromatic compounds. It is the bridge between quantum mechanics and chemistry — showing that the mathematics of wave interference governs which molecules can exist and how they behave.

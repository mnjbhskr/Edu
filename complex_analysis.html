Complex Analysis

●●●● Advanced
The beautiful mathematics of complex functions — conformal maps, residues, and analytic continuation
Analysis & Topology · Difficulty 3 (Advanced)

Complex analysis — the study of functions of a complex variable — is often called the most beautiful branch of mathematics. A single condition (differentiability in the complex sense) gives rise to an extraordinary wealth of structure: functions that are differentiable once are differentiable infinitely often, integrals around closed curves vanish (or capture residues), and maps preserve angles locally.

Conformal maps preserve angles between curves. Any analytic function with non-zero derivative is conformal. Möbius transformations (az+b)/(cz+d) map circles and lines to circles and lines — a key tool in complex analysis and physics.

2. Analytic Functions & Cauchy–Riemann

Choose a Function

u(x, y) — real part
v(x, y) — imaginary part
The Cauchy–Riemann equations ∂u/∂x = ∂v/∂y and ∂u/∂y = −∂v/∂x are the necessary and sufficient conditions (given continuity of partials) for a function to be complex-differentiable. When they hold, u and v are harmonic conjugates.

3. Contour Integration

Contour & Function

Watch the integral accumulate as a point traverses the contour. For analytic functions with no singularities inside, the integral is zero.
Cauchy’s Integral Theorem: If f is analytic inside and on a simple closed contour C, then ∮C f(z) dz = 0. When there are poles inside, the integral equals 2πi times the sum of residues — this is the Residue Theorem.

4. Poles & Residues

Domain Colouring with Poles

Drag the poles to move them. The contour integral around them equals 2πi × (sum of enclosed residues).

In domain colouring, the hue encodes the argument (angle) of f(z) and the brightness encodes the modulus. Poles appear as bright spots surrounded by a full colour wheel — every colour appears exactly once around each pole, reflecting the winding of the argument.

Complex analysis has profound connections beyond pure mathematics. In fluid dynamics, analytic functions describe irrotational, incompressible flow. In electrostatics, the real and imaginary parts give the electric potential and stream function. Hadamard called complex analysis “the most harmonious and fertile branch of mathematics.”

Where You'll See This

Complex analysis is “too good to be true” — differentiable complex functions are automatically infinitely differentiable, have convergent power series, and satisfy incredible integral formulae. Aeronautical engineers use conformal mapping to design aerofoil shapes, and the residue theorem evaluates “impossible” real integrals.

Try This

Show that eiπ + 1 = 0 using Euler’s formula eiθ = cos θ + i sin θ. Why is this equation so celebrated?

Set θ = π: eiπ = cos π + i sin π = −1 + 0i = −1. So eiπ + 1 = 0. This single equation links five fundamental constants (e, i, π, 1, 0) and three basic operations (+, ×, exponentiation). It connects exponential functions, trigonometry, and complex numbers in one astonishing identity.

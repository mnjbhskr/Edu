Swap Bootstrapping & Curve Construction

●●●● Advanced

From par swap rates to discount factors — the foundation of every fixed income derivative

1. Why This Is Central

Every fixed income derivative — every interest rate swap, every swaption, every structured note — requires a discount factor curve. Without it, you cannot price a single cash flow. Yet markets do not quote discount factors directly. They quote par swap rates: the fixed rate that makes a newly initiated swap worth zero.

Bootstrapping is the numerical procedure that converts observable par swap rates into a complete discount factor curve. It works sequentially: each new tenor uses the discount factors already solved at shorter maturities, then solves for the one remaining unknown. The result is a set of discount factors consistent with every market quote simultaneously.

From the discount factor curve, every other representation follows: zero rates, forward rates, par rates. The curve is the single object from which all fixed income pricing flows. Understanding how it is built — and what assumptions go into it — is non-negotiable for anyone working in rates.

This is the single most important numerical procedure in fixed income. Every desk builds a curve before pricing anything. If the curve is wrong, every price derived from it is wrong.

2. The Par Swap Rate

Definition

A par swap rate ST is the fixed rate that makes a fixed-for-floating interest rate swap have zero net present value at inception. The fixed leg pays ST on each payment date; the floating leg pays the realised overnight (or term) rate. Setting the two legs equal and solving for ST gives:

ST = (1 − D(T)) / ∑i D(ti) × δi

where D(ti) is the discount factor to payment date ti, δi is the day count fraction for the i-th period, and D(T) is the discount factor to the final maturity. The numerator (1 − D(T)) represents the present value of the floating leg (by the telescoping property of overnight rates). The denominator is the PV of a unit annuity.

▶ Derivation from first principles

The floating leg of a swap can be replicated by borrowing £1 at inception and investing it at the floating rate. At maturity, the borrower returns £1. The net cash flows replicate the floating leg exactly. Therefore:

PV(floating leg) = 1 − D(T)

The fixed leg pays S × δi at each date ti. Its present value is:

PV(fixed leg) = S × ∑i D(ti) × δi

Setting PV(fixed) = PV(floating) and solving for S gives the par swap rate formula above. The key insight is that the floating leg PV depends only on the initial and terminal discount factors — not on any intermediate floating rate fixing.

3. The Bootstrapping Algorithm

Sequential Solving

The algorithm proceeds tenor by tenor, from shortest to longest. At each step, all shorter-dated discount factors are already known. Rearranging the par swap rate formula for the unknown D(Tn):

D(Tn) = (1 − Sn × ∑i=1n−1 D(ti) × δi) / (1 + Sn × δn)

Worked Example — USD Par Swap Rates (Annual Frequency)

Given par rates: 1y = 4.50%, 2y = 4.25%, 3y = 4.10%, 5y = 3.90%, 7y = 3.80%, 10y = 3.75%
Step 1 (1y): D(1) = 1 / (1 + 0.0450) = 0.95694
Step 2 (2y): D(2) = (1 − 0.0425 × 0.95694) / (1 + 0.0425) = 0.91940
Step 3 (3y): D(3) = (1 − 0.0410 × (0.95694 + 0.91940)) / (1 + 0.0410) = 0.88622
Step 4 (5y): Interpolate D(4), then solve D(5) = 0.82485
Step 5 (7y): Interpolate D(6), then solve D(7) = 0.76858
Step 6 (10y): Interpolate D(8), D(9), then solve D(10) = 0.69236
▶ Solving for D(Tn) algebraically

Starting from the par swap rate equation:

Sn = (1 − D(Tn)) / ∑i=1n D(ti) × δi

Multiply both sides by the annuity sum:

Sn × ∑i=1n D(ti) × δi = 1 − D(Tn)

Separate the known terms (i < n) from the unknown D(Tn):

Sn × [∑i=1n−1 D(ti) × δi + D(Tn) × δn] = 1 − D(Tn)

Rearrange to isolate D(Tn):

D(Tn) × (1 + Sn × δn) = 1 − Sn × ∑i=1n−1 D(ti) × δi
D(Tn) = (1 − Sn × ∑i=1n−1 D(ti) × δi) / (1 + Sn × δn)

The crucial point: all D(ti) for i < n are already known from previous bootstrapping steps (or via interpolation for missing tenors). Only D(Tn) is unknown, so we solve a simple linear equation at each step — no root-finding required.

5. Curve Representations

Five Equivalent Ways to Express the Same Curve

All representations contain exactly the same information. Each can be derived from any other. The choice depends on the application: discount factors for pricing, zero rates for comparison, forward rates for expectations.

RepresentationFormulaUsage
Discount Factor D(T) — bootstrapped Pricing: PV = ∑ CFi × D(ti)
Zero Rate (CC) r(T) = −ln(D(T)) / T Model inputs, curve comparison
Zero Rate (Annual) ra(T) = D(T)−1/T − 1 Bond yield comparison
Forward Rate f(T1,T2) = [D(T1)/D(T2) − 1] / (T2−T1) Rate expectations, hedging
Par Swap Rate ST — market observable Trading, quoting, risk
▶ Why forward rates are more volatile than zero rates

A zero rate r(T) is the average of all instantaneous forward rates from 0 to T. Averaging smooths out local variations. The forward rate f(T1,T2), by contrast, captures the marginal rate over a single interval — any local change in the curve appears amplified in forwards.

Mathematically: r(T) = (1/T) ∫0T f(0,s) ds. Differentiating shows that the forward rate is the derivative of the zero-rate × maturity product, making it inherently noisier. This is why forward-rate curves always look more jagged than zero-rate curves.

6. The Multi-Curve Framework

Why One Curve Is No Longer Enough

Pre-2008: A single LIBOR curve served for both discounting and projection. The LIBOR-OIS basis was typically 1–2 basis points — negligible. Banks treated LIBOR as effectively risk-free.

September 2008: Following the Lehman Brothers collapse, the 3-month LIBOR-OIS spread widened to 365 basis points. The assumption that interbank lending was risk-free was shattered overnight. A 3-month deposit at a major bank suddenly carried meaningful credit risk.

Modern framework: Rates desks now maintain separate curves for different purposes. The OIS curve (based on overnight rates like SOFR, SONIA, or ESTR) is used for discounting collateralised derivatives. Separate projection curves are built for each tenor (1M, 3M, 6M) to forecast floating rate fixings.

Curve TypeBased OnPurpose
OIS / Discount SOFR, SONIA, ESTR Discounting collateralised cash flows
3M Projection 3M SOFR futures, 3M IBOR swaps Projecting 3-month floating fixings
6M Projection 6M EURIBOR swaps Projecting 6-month floating fixings
Cross-currency basis XCCY basis swaps FX-adjusted discounting for non-USD collateral
Impact: Using a single curve instead of the multi-curve framework introduces a DV01 error of 1–5 basis points on a typical 10-year swap. On a £500m notional, that is £250k–£1.25m of misvaluation. The error grows with maturity and with the size of the OIS-projection basis.

8. Interactive — DV01 Calculator

Bucketed Interest Rate Risk

DV01 (dollar value of one basis point) measures how much a swap’s value changes when rates move by 1bp. Bucketed DV01 shows which tenor points drive the risk, computed by bumping each par rate individually and repricing.

▶ How bump-and-reprice works

For each tenor bucket i:

1. Build the base curve from unperturbed par rates
2. Compute base swap PV using the fixed rate and base curve
3. Bump par rate i by +1bp (0.01%)
4. Rebuild the curve with the bumped rate
5. Recompute swap PV with the new curve
6. DV01i = |PVbumped − PVbase| × Notional

The sum of all bucket DV01s gives the total DV01. Buckets beyond the swap maturity typically show zero risk (the swap has no sensitivity to rates it never references). The longest bucket within the swap maturity usually carries the largest DV01 because it affects the terminal discount factor most.

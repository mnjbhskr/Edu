Overfitting & Generalisation

The fundamental tension of learning — memorising vs understanding

A model that memorises its training data perfectly is useless — like a student who learns exam answers by heart but cannot solve a new problem. The central challenge of machine learning is generalisation: building models that perform well on data they have never seen. This page lets you watch overfitting happen in real time, see why test error follows a U-shaped curve, and explore the techniques that tame it.

A degree-14 polynomial fits every training point perfectly — and is spectacularly wrong everywhere else. Language models with billions of parameters face exactly this challenge: they have more than enough capacity to memorise their training data, so we need techniques to force them to generalise instead.

More complexity reduces training error but eventually increases test error — the U-shaped curve is the signature of overfitting.

Standard loss: L(θ) = ½n Σ (f(xi) − yi)²
L2 regularised: L(θ) + λ Σ wi²   // penalises large weights
Dropout: randomly zero out each wi with probability p during training
Effect: prevents co-adaptation — forces redundant representations
GPT-4 has an estimated 1.7 trillion parameters but was trained on only ~13 trillion tokens. Without regularisation techniques — dropout, weight decay (L2), and careful architecture design — it would memorise its training data and fail on new prompts. The fact that it generalises at all is one of the deepest puzzles in modern AI.

Regularisation adds a cost for complexity — forcing the model to find simpler, more general solutions.

Why This Matters

Overfitting is not an edge case — it is the default behaviour of any sufficiently powerful model. Every advance in machine learning, from early stopping to dropout to data augmentation, is fundamentally an answer to the same question: how do we build models that learn the pattern, not the noise?

GPT-4 has 1.7 trillion parameters but was trained on only ~13 trillion tokens. Preventing overfitting at this scale requires careful architecture design, dropout, and weight decay. The fact that it generalises — that it can write poetry it has never seen, or solve problems never in its training data — is the central miracle and mystery of deep learning.

Newton’s law → sine wave: F = −kx is a restoring force proportional to displacement. Newton gives mx″ = −kx, i.e. x″ + ω²x = 0. The only functions whose second derivative is proportional to their negative are sine and cosine — so SHM is inevitable.

Phase relationships: Displacement and velocity are 90° out of phase. Velocity is zero at maximum displacement (turning points). Acceleration is always proportional to −x — it points back toward equilibrium.

Doubling the mass does not double the period — it increases it by a factor of √2. The square root is what makes SHM special.

Energy is never lost — it transforms between kinetic and potential twice every cycle. At the extremes, all energy is potential. At the centre, all is kinetic. The total never changes. With damping enabled, watch the total energy drain away — the bars shrink as the oscillation decays.

The Mathematics of Oscillation
Simple harmonic motion is governed by the differential equation x″ + ω²x = 0, whose solutions are sine and cosine. This is why the same mathematics appears in springs, pendulums, electrical circuits, and even the vibrations of molecules. Newton’s second law F = ma, combined with Hooke’s law F = −kx, gives mx″ = −kx — a second-order ODE whose solutions are necessarily sinusoidal.

Trigonometry provides the solution: x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ). Calculus connects position to velocity (dx/dt = −Aω sin(ωt)) and acceleration (d²x/dt² = −ω²x). Energy conservation follows from multiplying the equation of motion by the velocity.

Damping adds a velocity-dependent drag: x″ + 2βx′ + ω²x = 0. This produces exponentially decaying oscillations x(t) = Ae−βtcos(ωdt), where ωd = √(ω² − β²). Energy drains away and the phase-space orbit spirals inward. When β = ω, the system is critically damped — it returns to rest without oscillating.

Driving adds an external periodic force: x″ + 2βx′ + ω²x = (F₀/m)cos(Ωt). When Ω ≈ ω, the system resonates — the amplitude grows dramatically, limited only by damping. This is how bridges vibrate, speakers produce sound, and atoms absorb light at specific frequencies.

The pendulum adds a twist: the true equation is θ″ + (g/L)sin(θ) = 0. For small angles, sin(θ) ≈ θ gives SHM. For large angles, the motion is still periodic but no longer sinusoidal — the period increases and the wave shape distorts. There is no closed-form solution; it requires elliptic integrals or numerical integration.

Together, damping and driving create the complete picture of real-world oscillation — from a child’s swing to the resonant circuits in every radio.

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

The null model of population genetics — when evolution stands still

Chapter 15 · Mathematical Biology · Topic 7 · Difficulty 2

In 1908, G. H. Hardy and Wilhelm Weinberg independently proved a remarkable result: in a large, randomly mating population with no selection, mutation, or migration, allele frequencies never change. If allele A has frequency p and allele a has frequency q = 1 − p, the genotype frequencies settle to p², 2pq, and q² after just one generation of random mating — and stay there forever. This equilibrium is the baseline against which all evolutionary forces are measured.

p² + 2pq + q² = 1
p + q = 1
p = freq(A)  |  q = freq(a)  |  p² = AA  |  2pq = Aa  |  q² = aa

Heterozygotes (Aa) are most frequent when p = q = 0.5, reaching a maximum of 50%. Even a rare recessive allele (small q) produces far more carriers (2pq) than affected homozygotes (q²). For example, if q = 0.01, then q² = 0.0001 (1 in 10,000) but 2pq ≈ 0.02 (1 in 50) are carriers — 200 times more.

In small populations, random sampling causes allele frequencies to wander — a process called genetic drift. Alleles can be lost or fixed purely by chance, regardless of their fitness. Try N = 20 vs N = 10000: small populations show wild fluctuations while large ones stay near equilibrium. This is why endangered species lose genetic diversity.

Directional selection drives one allele to fixation. Heterozygote advantage (overdominance) maintains both alleles — the classic example is sickle cell anaemia, where Aa carriers resist malaria. Underdominance creates an unstable equilibrium: the population tips toward fixing whichever allele is currently more common.

Mutation alone drives allele frequencies toward an equilibrium at p̂ = ν / (μ + ν). Mutation rates are tiny (10−4 to 10−6), so this process is very slow — but relentless. Migration homogenises populations: when two groups exchange migrants at rate m, their allele frequencies converge exponentially toward a shared value.

The χ² test with 1 degree of freedom tells us whether observed genotype frequencies deviate significantly from Hardy-Weinberg expectations. A χ² value above 3.84 (p < 0.05) suggests that some evolutionary force — selection, non-random mating, drift, or population structure — is at work.
Why It Matters

The Hardy-Weinberg principle (1908) is the fundamental null hypothesis of population genetics. It tells us what happens when nothing happens — no selection, no drift, no mutation, no migration, no non-random mating. Any deviation from HW equilibrium is evidence that evolution is occurring.

Medical genetics relies on it daily. Carrier frequencies for genetic diseases are estimated using q² from observed disease prevalence. If cystic fibrosis affects 1 in 2,500 people, then q = 1/50, and the carrier frequency 2pq ≈ 1 in 25 — a calculation used in genetic counselling worldwide.

Forensic DNA profiling uses Hardy-Weinberg to calculate the probability that a DNA match occurred by chance. Population geneticists test for HW equilibrium to detect natural selection, population structure, and inbreeding in natural populations.

Each section above breaks one assumption. Section 2 breaks infinite population size (drift). Section 3 breaks the no-selection assumption. Section 4 breaks no-mutation and no-migration. Together they show how the simple HW model becomes a launchpad for understanding all of evolutionary genetics.

Quadratic roots: Landing time comes from solving h₀ + v₀sin(θ)t − ½gt² = 0. When h₀ = 0, factor out t: t(v₀sinθ − ½gt) = 0, giving t = 0 (launch) and t = 2v₀sinθ/g (landing). With h₀ > 0, you need the full quadratic formula — and the symmetry breaks.

Complementary angles: At h₀ = 0, angles θ and 90°−θ give the same range because sin(2θ) = sin(180°−2θ). Deeper: sin(2θ) = 2sin(θ)cos(θ), so complementary angles swap the sine and cosine — the product stays the same. Try 30° and 60° to see this symmetry. When h₀ > 0, the optimal angle drops below 45°.

vx = v₀ cos(θ)  (constant)
vy = v₀ sin(θ) − gt (decreasing)
|v| = √(vx² + vy²)
The horizontal velocity never changes — there is no horizontal force. Only gravity acts, pulling downward, steadily stealing vertical speed until it reverses direction.

Calculus link: At the apex, vy = 0 — the derivative of height equals zero at the maximum. This is the first-derivative test in action: dy/dt = vy = v₀sin(θ) − gt = 0 at tapex = v₀sin(θ)/g.

3. Angle vs Range

Which angle maximises range?

R(θ) = v₀² sin(2θ) / g (when h₀ = 0)
The sin(2θ) function reaches its maximum at 2θ = 90°, i.e. θ = 45°. This is why 45° maximises range on flat ground. The curve is perfectly symmetric about 45° — every angle has a “twin” that gives the same range. When h₀ > 0, the peak shifts left — you can see the asymmetry emerge.
The Mathematics of Flight
Projectile motion is one of the first places where trigonometry, algebra, and calculus converge on a single physical problem.

Trigonometry decomposes the initial velocity into horizontal and vertical components using sin and cos. Algebra gives us the quadratic y(t) = h₀ + v₀sin(θ)t − ½gt², whose roots are the launch and landing times. Calculus connects position, velocity, and acceleration: velocity is the derivative of position, and acceleration (constant gravity) is the derivative of velocity. At the apex, dy/dt = 0 — the first-derivative test locates the maximum height.

The parabolic trajectory is no coincidence — it is the unique curve produced by constant acceleration in one direction and constant velocity in the perpendicular direction. Galileo was the first to recognise this, publishing his analysis in Discorsi (1638).

With air resistance, the drag force Fd = −kv introduces a velocity-dependent deceleration. The equations no longer have closed-form solutions — we integrate numerically. You can see the result: shorter range, lower apex, and a visibly asymmetric descent (steeper than the ascent). This is why real projectiles never follow perfect parabolas.

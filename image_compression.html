How Images Are Compressed

JPEG, the Discrete Cosine Transform, and the mathematics of visual data — Advanced

Billions of images are shared, streamed, and stored every single day. Yet most of these are JPEG files — typically just a few hundred kilobytes each, despite containing millions of pixels. How is this possible? The answer lies in a brilliant mathematical transformation called the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT), which allows us to represent an image using far fewer numbers while preserving the details that matter most to the human eye. This exploration reveals the hidden mathematics that makes modern image sharing practical.

Why Compression Matters

Imagine a single frame of video at full HD resolution (1920 × 1080 pixels). Each pixel requires 3 bytes to store its red, green, and blue values. That's 1920 × 1080 × 3 = 6,220,800 bytes, roughly 6 MB per frame. At 30 frames per second, a single second of uncompressed video would require 180 MB of storage. A two-hour film would need 1.3 terabytes!

JPEG compression reduces this dramatically, typically achieving 10:1 to 50:1 compression ratios whilst remaining perceptually lossless to the human eye. The key is understanding which information the human visual system is sensitive to, and which can be safely discarded.

The Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT)

The Discrete Cosine Transform is a fundamental technique in signal processing. It decomposes any signal into a sum of cosine functions at different frequencies. Rather than storing pixel values directly, JPEG stores the coefficients of these cosines.

X(k) = Σn=0 to N-1 x(n) cos[π(2n+1)k / 2N]

This formula transforms a sequence of N values x(n) into N frequency coefficients X(k). The low-frequency coefficients (small k) represent the broad patterns in the signal, whilst high-frequency coefficients capture fine details.

Key Insight: The first coefficient (Basis 0) represents the average brightness. Subsequent coefficients add finer and finer oscillations. Most natural images contain very little high-frequency information, so we can discard high-frequency coefficients with minimal visible impact.

The 8×8 Block — Heart of JPEG

JPEG compression operates on 8×8 blocks of pixels. The image is divided into a grid of these blocks, and each block is processed independently. This allows for a good balance between compression and artefacts.

The 2D DCT extends the 1D transform to two dimensions, creating 64 basis patterns. The top-left corner represents low-frequency information (flat regions), whilst the bottom-right contains high-frequency details (edges and texture).

Quantisation — Where Information Is Lost

After the DCT, we have 64 frequency coefficients. The next step is quantisation: dividing each coefficient by a quantisation value and rounding to the nearest integer. This is where JPEG loses information — and where we control the trade-off between file size and visual quality.

High-frequency coefficients are typically divided by larger values, so they often round to zero and are discarded. Low-frequency coefficients are divided by smaller values, preserving them with better precision.

The Complete JPEG Pipeline

JPEG compression involves several stages working together:

  1. Colour space conversion: Convert from RGB to YCbCr (separating brightness from colour)
  2. Chroma subsampling: The human eye is less sensitive to colour details, so we store colour at half resolution
  3. 8×8 blocking: Divide the image into 8×8 blocks
  4. DCT: Transform each block into frequency coefficients
  5. Quantisation: Round coefficients to integers using the quantisation matrix
  6. Zigzag scan: Reorder coefficients from low to high frequency
  7. Run-length & Huffman coding: Compress the coefficients and zeros with entropy coding

Quality Levels in Practice

JPEG files can be saved at different quality levels. Higher quality preserves more high-frequency detail but results in larger files. The sweet spot for web images is typically 75-85%, which appears visually lossless to most viewers whilst achieving significant compression.

Why This Matters

The Discrete Cosine Transform is elegant mathematics in action. By recognising that natural images are sparse in the frequency domain (they contain mostly low frequencies with some mid and high frequencies), JPEG compression achieves extraordinary reduction whilst preserving visual fidelity.

This same principle underlies modern video compression (H.264, H.265), audio compression (AAC, MP3), and even medical imaging. Understanding the DCT reveals not just how JPEG works, but a fundamental principle of signal processing: represent data in the domain where it is most sparse, and discard what matters least.

Deep Insight: JPEG is a lossy format because it discards high-frequency information that the human visual system is insensitive to. This is an example of perceptual coding — exploiting properties of human perception to achieve compression. The mathematics enables the compression, but psychology determines where we can afford information loss.

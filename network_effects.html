Network Effects & Metcalfe’s Law

Why networks become exponentially more valuable as they grow

Chapter 18 · Economics & Game Theory · Topic 3 · Difficulty 2

A telephone is useless if you are the only person who owns one. But connect two people and you have one link; connect five and you have ten; connect a hundred and you have nearly five thousand. This explosive growth in connections is the engine behind network effects — the phenomenon where each new user makes the network more valuable for everyone already on it. From social media platforms to payment systems, network effects create tipping points, winner-take-all dynamics, and power-law distributions that reshape entire industries.

V(n) = n(n − 1) / 2   —   Metcalfe’s Law: network value grows quadratically with users

Key insight: The number of possible connections in a network of n people is n(n−1)/2 — a quadratic function. But the cost of adding users grows only linearly. Beyond the crossover point, every new user adds more value than they cost, creating a powerful incentive for growth.

Higher switching costs amplify lock-in.

Key insight: Two-sided markets face a cold-start problem: buyers will not come without sellers, and sellers will not come without buyers. Successful platforms solve this by subsidising one side (often buyers) to reach critical mass, then monetising the other side. This is why ride-sharing apps offered cheap rides to attract riders before raising prices.

Key insight: Preferential attachment — new connections favouring already-popular nodes — produces a power-law degree distribution P(k) ∼ k−γ. A few hubs have enormous numbers of connections while most nodes have very few. This “scale-free” property appears in the internet, social networks, citation networks, and many biological systems.
Why Network Effects Matter
Network effects are among the most powerful forces in technology and economics. They explain why certain markets tend toward monopoly, why standards become entrenched, and why switching away from a dominant platform is so difficult even when better alternatives exist. Understanding Metcalfe’s Law, tipping points, winner-take-all dynamics, two-sided markets, and power-law distributions gives us a mathematical lens for analysing everything from social media to cryptocurrency adoption to the spread of ideas.

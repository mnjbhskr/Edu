Heron’s Formula & Triangle Area

●●●● Beginner

Find the area of any triangle when you know only its three sides

1. Introduction

The most familiar area formula for a triangle is A = ½ × base × height. But what if you don’t know the height? Suppose you have a triangular plot of land and you can measure all three sides with a tape measure — but there is no easy way to measure the perpendicular height. Is there a formula that uses only the three side lengths?

There is, and it has been known for nearly two thousand years. Heron of Alexandria (c. 10–70 AD), a Greek mathematician and engineer, published it in his work Metrica. Heron was remarkably practical — he also invented the first known steam engine (the aeolipile) and an early vending machine. His area formula reflects that same practical spirit: give me the three sides, and I’ll give you the area.

Heron’s formula is especially useful in surveying, computer graphics, and any situation where you can measure distances but not angles or heights directly.

2. The Formula

Heron’s Formula

Given a triangle with sides a, b, and c, first compute the semi-perimeter:

s = (a + b + c) / 2

Then the area is:

A = √[s × (s − a) × (s − b) × (s − c)]

Worked Example: the 3-4-5 right triangle

Given: a = 3, b = 4, c = 5
Step 1: s = (3 + 4 + 5) / 2 = 6
Step 2: s − a = 6 − 3 = 3
Step 3: s − b = 6 − 4 = 2
Step 4: s − c = 6 − 5 = 1
Step 5: product = 6 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 36
Step 6: A = √36 = 6
Check: for a 3-4-5 right triangle, ½ × 3 × 4 = 6. Heron’s formula agrees perfectly — no height measurement needed.
▶ Derivation from the cosine rule

We start from the standard area formula A = ½ ab sin C and use the cosine rule to eliminate the angle.

Cosine rule: c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C
Rearrange: cos C = (a² + b² − c²) / (2ab)

Since sin² C = 1 − cos² C, and A = ½ ab sin C:

A² = ¼ a²b² sin² C
   = ¼ a²b² (1 − cos² C)
   = ¼ a²b² − ¼ (a² + b² − c²)² / 4

Factoring via difference of squares:

16A² = 4a²b² − (a² + b² − c²)²
      = [2ab + (a² + b² − c²)][2ab − (a² + b² − c²)]
      = [(a + b)² − c²][c² − (a − b)²]
      = (a + b + c)(a + b − c)(c + a − b)(c − a + b)

Substituting s = (a + b + c)/2 and simplifying each factor:

16A² = 2s × 2(s − c) × 2(s − b) × 2(s − a) = 16 s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)
A = √[s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)]

4. Application to Quadrilaterals

Splitting a Quadrilateral into Two Triangles

Any quadrilateral can be divided into two triangles by drawing a diagonal. If you know the four sides and one diagonal, you can apply Heron’s formula twice and sum the results.

Quadrilateral ABCD with diagonal AC = d:
Triangle 1: sides a, b, d
Triangle 2: sides c, e, d
Area = A1 + A2

Worked Example

Quadrilateral ABCD: AB = 5, BC = 6, CD = 7, DA = 8, diagonal AC = 9
Triangle ABC (sides 5, 6, 9):
  s1 = (5 + 6 + 9)/2 = 10
  A1 = √(10 × 5 × 4 × 1) = √200 ≈ 14.14
Triangle ACD (sides 7, 8, 9):
  s2 = (7 + 8 + 9)/2 = 12
  A2 = √(12 × 5 × 4 × 3) = √720 ≈ 26.83
Total area = 14.14 + 26.83 = 40.97

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Scalene Triangle Area

Problem: A triangular field has sides measuring 13 m, 14 m, and 15 m. Find its area.

Step 1: s = (13 + 14 + 15) / 2 = 21
Step 2: s − a = 21 − 13 = 8
Step 3: s − b = 21 − 14 = 7
Step 4: s − c = 21 − 15 = 6
Step 5: product = 21 × 8 × 7 × 6 = 7056
Step 6: A = √7056 = 84 m²

Example 2 — Quadrilateral via Diagonal Splitting

Problem: A quadrilateral park ABCD has sides AB = 20 m, BC = 15 m, CD = 12 m, DA = 17 m, and diagonal AC = 25 m. Find the total area.

Triangle ABC (sides 20, 15, 25):
  s1 = (20 + 15 + 25)/2 = 30
  A1 = √(30 × 10 × 15 × 5) = √22500 = 150 m²
Triangle ACD (sides 12, 17, 25):
  s2 = (12 + 17 + 25)/2 = 27
  A2 = √(27 × 15 × 10 × 2) = √8100 = 90 m²
Total area = 150 + 90 = 240 m²
Notice that triangle ABC with sides 20, 15, 25 is actually a scaled 3-4-5 right triangle (multiply by 5). This is why its area works out to a whole number.

Conservation of Energy

Kinetic, potential, and the flat total — energy never disappears

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it only changes form. A ball rolling down a slope converts gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy; a compressed spring stores elastic potential energy; a hot gas contains internal thermal energy. In every case, the total, carefully counted, remains constant. The work-energy theorem makes this precise: net work done equals the change in kinetic energy.

Why PE = mgh? Lifting an object of mass m through height h against gravity requires work W = ∫mg dh = mgh. The potential energy is the stored work — recoverable when the object falls back down.

Total energy (white line) never changes without friction. At the top of each hill, kinetic energy is low and potential energy is high. At the bottom, all potential energy has converted to kinetic energy and the ball moves fastest.

With friction enabled, mechanical energy drains steadily into heat. The ball slows on each pass and will eventually halt. This is the First Law of Thermodynamics in action: total energy (mechanical + heat) remains constant, but the useful mechanical fraction decreases.
Real-world energy conversions: In a hydroelectric dam, water falls from height (PE) → spins turbines (KE) → generates electricity. In electric vehicles, regenerative braking converts kinetic energy back into electrical energy stored in the battery, recovering up to 70% of the energy that would otherwise be lost as heat in conventional brakes.

The shaded area under the F-x graph is the work done. For a constant force this is simply a rectangle: W = F × x. The work-energy theorem guarantees that this area exactly equals the change in kinetic energy — connecting the geometric (integral) view to velocity.

Energy units: 1 joule = 1 N·m = 1 kg·m²/s². The joule is not arbitrary — it is precisely the work done pushing 1 N through 1 metre.

With damping, the amplitude decays exponentially and total energy bleeds away into air resistance and internal friction. Cross-reference: simple_harmonic_motion.html for the SHM derivation.
The Mathematics of Energy Conservation

Energy is the most unifying concept in physics. Whether you are tracking a falling stone, a vibrating spring, a photon of light, or a chemical bond breaking apart, the same quantity — measured in joules — is conserved. What changes is its form: kinetic, potential, thermal, electromagnetic, nuclear. Conservation of energy says the sum over all forms is constant in a closed system, and it is one of the few principles that holds across every scale from quantum mechanics to cosmology.

The work-energy theorem follows directly from Newton's second law. Starting from F = ma, multiply both sides by displacement dx and integrate: ∫F dx = ∫ma dx = ∫m(dv/dt)v dt = Δ(½mv²). The left side is the definition of work, the right side is the change in kinetic energy. No new physics is required — the theorem is a mathematical consequence of Newton's laws applied along a path.

The deepest explanation for energy conservation is Noether's theorem (1915): every continuous symmetry of the laws of physics corresponds to a conserved quantity. Energy conservation arises from time-translation symmetry — the fact that the laws of physics are the same today as yesterday and tomorrow. If you could design a universe where the laws changed over time, energy would not be conserved. The symmetry is the reason, and the conservation law is the consequence.

When friction is present, mechanical energy is not conserved in isolation — it converts to thermal energy (disordered molecular motion). Including that thermal contribution restores the full conservation law. This is the first law of thermodynamics: ΔU = Q − W. The study of how energy degrades from ordered to disordered forms — entropy — is the subject of thermodynamics.html. The pendulum with damping illustrates the same principle at a macroscopic level: energy is conserved, but it migrates from the swinging bob into invisible molecular jiggling.

Linear Equations in Two Variables

●●●● Beginner

Every point on the line is a solution — and where two lines meet tells a story

1. Introduction

A linear equation in two variables has the general form ax + by + c = 0, where a, b, and c are real numbers with a and b not both zero. Any pair of values (x, y) that makes the equation true is called a solution.

A single linear equation has infinitely many solutions. For instance, 2x + 3y = 12 is satisfied by (0, 4), (6, 0), (3, 2), and countless other pairs. When you plot every solution on the Cartesian plane, they all lie on a straight line — hence the name “linear”.

So why do we study pairs of linear equations? Because a single equation leaves too much freedom. If a problem has two unknowns — say the cost of an apple and the cost of a banana — we need two independent pieces of information (two equations) to pin down both values. Graphically, each equation gives a line, and the intersection point of those two lines is the one pair (x, y) that satisfies both equations simultaneously.

One equation, one line, infinitely many solutions. Two equations, two lines — and typically just one point where they meet. That single point is the answer to both conditions at once.

2. Graphing a Single Equation

From Equation to Line

Given ax + by + c = 0, we can rearrange to find y in terms of x (provided b ≠ 0):

ax + by + c = 0
by = −ax − c
y = (−a/b)x − c/b

This is the slope–intercept form y = mx + q, where the gradient (slope) is m = −a/b and the y-intercept is q = −c/b. To draw the line, we only need two points — substitute any two convenient values of x and compute the corresponding y.

Worked Example

Given: 2x + 3y = 12  ⇒  2x + 3y − 12 = 0
Rearrange: y = (12 − 2x) / 3
When x = 0: y = 12/3 = 4  →  point (0, 4)
When x = 6: y = (12 − 12)/3 = 0  →  point (6, 0)
Gradient: m = −2/3    y-intercept: 4

Plot (0, 4) and (6, 0) on the Cartesian plane, then draw a straight line through them. Every point on that line is a solution to 2x + 3y = 12.

The gradient −2/3 tells us: for every 3 units we move right, the line drops by 2 units. A negative gradient means the line slopes downward from left to right.

4. Pairs of Linear Equations

Three Possible Outcomes

When we have two linear equations in two variables, their graphs are two straight lines. Two lines in a plane can relate to each other in exactly three ways:

CaseGeometric PictureAlgebraic ConditionSolution Type
Intersecting Two lines cross at one point a₁/a₂ ≠ b₁/b₂ Unique solution
Parallel Two lines never meet a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂ ≠ c₁/c₂ No solution
Coincident Lines lie on top of each other a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂ = c₁/c₂ Infinitely many

A pair of equations is called consistent if it has at least one solution (intersecting or coincident lines) and inconsistent if it has no solution (parallel lines).

The ratio test is simple: compare a₁/a₂ with b₁/b₂. If the ratios differ, the lines must intersect. If the ratios are equal, check c₁/c₂ to distinguish parallel from coincident.
▶ Why does the ratio test work?

Two lines have the same gradient when −a₁/b₁ = −a₂/b₂, which simplifies to a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂. Equal gradients mean the lines are parallel (or identical).

If the gradients are equal and the y-intercepts are equal (−c₁/b₁ = −c₂/b₂), then the lines are the same line — i.e. a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂ = c₁/c₂.

If the gradients are equal but the y-intercepts differ, the lines are parallel with no intersection — the system is inconsistent.

6. Algebraic Methods

Substitution Method

Express one variable in terms of the other from one equation, then substitute into the second equation. This reduces the system to a single equation in one variable.

Worked Example

Equations: 2x + 3y = 12  … (i)    x − y = 1  … (ii)
From (ii): x = 1 + y
Substitute into (i): 2(1 + y) + 3y = 12
    2 + 2y + 3y = 12
    5y = 10
    y = 2
Back-substitute: x = 1 + 2 = 3
Solution: (3, 2)

Elimination Method

Multiply the equations by suitable numbers so that one variable has equal (or opposite) coefficients, then add or subtract the equations to eliminate that variable.

Worked Example

Equations: 2x + 3y = 12  … (i)    x − y = 1  … (ii)
Multiply (ii) by 3: 3x − 3y = 3  … (iii)
Add (i) and (iii): (2x + 3y) + (3x − 3y) = 12 + 3
    5x = 15
    x = 3
Substitute into (ii): 3 − y = 1  ⇒  y = 2
Solution: (3, 2)
▶ When to use which method

Substitution is easiest when one of the coefficients is 1 (or −1), because you can isolate that variable without introducing fractions. In the example above, equation (ii) has the coefficient of x equal to 1, making substitution clean.

Elimination is more systematic and scales better. It works well when no coefficient is conveniently 1, and is the foundation of Gaussian elimination used in higher mathematics for larger systems.

Both methods always give the same answer. Choose whichever produces simpler arithmetic for the particular pair of equations you have.

How GPS Finds You

From circles to spheres to relativity — the mathematics that pinpoints your location

Right now, 31 satellites are orbiting Earth at 20,200 kilometres altitude, each carrying an atomic clock accurate to billionths of a second. Your smartphone is receiving signals from several of them, bouncing across the vacuum of space at the speed of light. But here's the stunning part: your phone isn't just listening. It's solving a system of equations in real time, using geometry, algebra, and Einstein's theories of relativity to pinpoint your exact position.

This page reveals the elegant mathematics that powers GPS: how circles become spheres, how an extra satellite solves the "clock problem," and how tiny relativistic effects—just the fact that satellites move fast and sit in weaker gravity—would cause GPS to drift by 11 kilometres every single day if we ignored them.

Section 1: The Basic Idea — Circles of Position

Let's start in two dimensions. Imagine you're in a city and you know you are exactly 5 kilometres from the town hall. That means you're somewhere on a circle of radius 5 km centred on the town hall. You could be north, south, east, west, or anywhere along that circle.

Now suppose you also know you are 8 kilometres from the railway station. That's another circle. The two circles intersect at two points. You must be at one of those two locations.

Finally, if you measure distance to a third landmark and draw a third circle, it will intersect the first two at one unique point. That's your position.

GPS works exactly this way, except the "landmarks" are satellites and the medium is three-dimensional space. Let's visualise this:

The System of Equations
Each satellite-to-receiver measurement gives us one equation. In 2D, with satellites at positions (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂), (x₃, y₃) and your position at (x, y):
(x - x₁)² + (y - y₁)² = r₁²
(x - x₂)² + (y - y₂)² = r₂²
(x - x₃)² + (y - y₃)² = r₃²

These three equations are solved simultaneously to find your (x, y) coordinates. In the real GPS system, the same principle applies but in 3D space.

Section 2: From 2D to 3D — Spheres in Space

The jump to three dimensions is conceptually simple but geometrically rich. A distance from one satellite isn't a circle anymore—it's a sphere. Two spheres intersect in a circle. Three spheres intersect at (generically) two points. That's where the fourth satellite comes in.

One Satellite

Defines a sphere of possible locations.

Two Satellites

Spheres intersect; possible positions form a circle.

Three Satellites

Spheres intersect at two points (above and below Earth).

Four Satellites

Resolves ambiguity and corrects clock bias.

The 3D equations are analogous to the 2D case:

(x - xᵢ)² + (y - yᵢ)² + (z - zᵢ)² = rᵢ²

Here, (xᵢ, yᵢ, zᵢ) is the 3D position of satellite i and rᵢ is the distance from that satellite to the receiver.

Section 3: The Clock Problem — Why You Need Four Satellites

Your smartphone doesn't carry an atomic clock. It has an inexpensive quartz oscillator that's accurate to maybe a millisecond. But GPS works by measuring the time it takes for signals to arrive from satellites. If your clock is even slightly wrong, your calculated position will be dramatically off.

The signal travel time is: time = distance / speed of light. If your internal clock is wrong by just 1 microsecond, you'll be off by:

distance error = 1 μs × 299,792 km/s ≈ 300 metres

That's why the system needs a fourth unknown: the clock bias, often written as Δt. The receiver must solve not just for (x, y, z) but also for this time offset:

rᵢ = c(tᵢ - t₀ + Δt)

Where c is the speed of light, tᵢ is the time the satellite transmitted its signal, and t₀ is the receiver's clock reading. Four equations for four unknowns (x, y, z, Δt).

Why Four Satellites? Three satellites give three equations for three unknowns (x, y, z), but don't account for clock bias. Adding a fourth satellite adds a fourth equation, allowing the receiver to solve for all four parameters simultaneously.

Section 4: Relativistic Corrections — Einstein to the Rescue

Satellites orbit at about 3.9 kilometres per second—fast enough that Einstein's theories of relativity matter. Two relativistic effects compete:

Special Relativity

Effect: Moving clocks run slow. Satellites lose ~7 μs per day.

General Relativity

Effect: Weaker gravity at altitude makes clocks run fast. Gain ~45 μs per day.

The net effect: satellite clocks gain about 38 microseconds per day relative to clocks on Earth's surface. Sounds tiny. But that 38 μs translates to:

distance error = 38 μs/day × 299,792 km/s ≈ 11.4 km/day

Without relativistic corrections, your GPS error would grow by 11 kilometres every single day! The system automatically accounts for this. Satellite clocks are adjusted before launch so that when they're orbiting at full speed in Earth's gravity, they tick at the same rate as clocks on the ground.

The relativistic correction formula (simplified) is:

Δt_rel = (gravitational term) − (velocity term)
≈ (GM/c²r_surface) − (GM/c²r_orbit) − (v²/2c²)

Section 5: Error Sources and Geometric Precision

Even with perfect atomic clocks and relativistic corrections, GPS accuracy depends on where the satellites are relative to you. This is measured by Dilution of Precision, or DOP.

Imagine four satellites clustered overhead in a tight formation. They're all looking at you from nearly the same direction. That geometry is "weak"—small errors in signal timing create large position errors. But if satellites are spread across the sky—north, south, east, west, high and low—errors are diluted; DOP is "good."

GDOP (Geometric)

Combines horizontal and vertical errors; best overall measure.

HDOP (Horizontal)

East-west and north-south position error.

VDOP (Vertical)

Altitude error; typically larger than horizontal.

PDOP (Position)

Horizontal and vertical only (excludes time).

Other Error Sources:

Ionospheric Delay
Charged particles in the ionosphere slow down radio waves. Single-frequency receivers (like older GPS) can be off by metres. Dual-frequency receivers cancel most of this.
Tropospheric Delay
Water vapour and pressure in the lower atmosphere refract signals. Usually adds 2–20 metres of error.
Multipath Error
Signals bounce off buildings, mountains, or water before reaching your antenna. The receiver picks up delayed copies of the signal, confusing the timing measurement.
Ephemeris Error
Small uncertainties in the satellite's actual orbit. Updated regularly but never zero.
Modern Improvements: Differential GPS (DGPS) uses ground reference stations to measure local errors and broadcast corrections. Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) achieves centimetre-level accuracy by using carrier-phase measurements. These techniques layer additional maths on top of the basic trilateration principle.

Putting It All Together

Your smartphone's GPS receiver is solving a marvellous system of equations:

Find (x, y, z, Δt) such that:

rᵢ = c(tᵢ − t₀ + Δt) for each satellite i

where rᵢ = √[(x − xᵢ)² + (y − yᵢ)² + (z − zᵢ)²]
and satellite positions and times are known.

The process:

  1. Receive signals from at least four satellites (including their precise transmission times and positions).
  2. Measure signal arrival times at your receiver.
  3. Compute the signal transit time as (reception time − transmission time).
  4. Solve the system of equations for your position and clock bias.
  5. Apply relativistic corrections (automatically handled by the satellite clocks).
  6. Account for DOP and atmospheric delays to estimate accuracy.

This system was designed in the Cold War (as a military navigational tool) but has become one of the most profound applications of applied mathematics and physics. It merges:

  • Euclidean Geometry (circles, spheres, intersection)
  • Linear Algebra (solving systems of equations)
  • Special Relativity (time dilation due to motion)
  • General Relativity (gravitational time dilation)
  • Signal Processing (filtering noise from atmospheric delays)
  • Optimisation (finding the best-fit solution with measurement uncertainty)

And it works. Billions of people use it every day without thinking about the extraordinary mathematics and physics humming invisibly overhead.

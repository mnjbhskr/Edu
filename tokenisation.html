Tokenisation

How machines read text — from characters to tokens

Beginner

Language models do not read words the way you do. Before any text reaches a neural network, it must be chopped into discrete pieces called tokens. The choice of tokenisation method — character-level, word-level, or subword — profoundly affects what the model can learn, how efficiently it uses its context window, and even which languages it serves well. Here we explore how tokenisation works, step by step.

Language models do not read words — they read tokens. The choice of tokenisation directly affects what the model can learn and how efficiently it uses its context window. Character-level gives the smallest vocabulary but the longest sequences; word-level is compact but cannot handle unseen words; BPE strikes a balance by merging common character pairs into subword units.

2 · Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)

Watch BPE build a vocabulary from scratch — one merge at a time.

BPE Training

5
BPE starts with individual characters and greedily merges the most common adjacent pairs. Common words become single tokens (‘the’, ‘and’), while rare words are split into subword pieces (‘un’ + ‘expect’ + ‘ed’). This balances vocabulary size with coverage — every possible word can be represented, even ones never seen during training.

3 · Tokenisation Quirks

Tokenisation is not as simple as it seems. These examples reveal surprising behaviour.

Surprising Splits

Tokenisation is not neutral. English text typically uses fewer tokens than equivalent Chinese or Arabic text — meaning English speakers get more content per context window. Numbers, whitespace, and code all tokenise in unexpected ways. This is an active area of fairness and efficiency research.

4 · Vocabulary & Embedding Space

Large vocabularies mean fewer tokens per sentence (good for context length) but more parameters in the embedding table (expensive to store and train). GPT-4 uses roughly 100,000 tokens; this is a carefully chosen balance between compression efficiency and model size.

Tokenisation is the first step in any language model pipeline. The way text is split into tokens determines what the model sees, how efficiently it uses its context window, and which languages it serves well. BPE — and its variants like WordPiece and Unigram — has become the industry standard because it balances vocabulary size, sequence length, and coverage of rare words.

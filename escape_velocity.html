Escape Velocity ★★ Intermediate

The speed needed to break free from gravity

1. What Is Escape Velocity?

Launch Simulation

Launch a projectile from the surface of a planet. If its kinetic energy equals or exceeds the magnitude of gravitational potential energy, it escapes to infinity. Below that threshold, gravity wins and the projectile falls back.

At exactly vesc: total energy = KE + PE = 0  (barely escapes with zero speed at infinity)
Red trails (v < vesc): the projectile rises, decelerates, and falls back in a parabolic arc. Its total energy is negative — it is gravitationally bound.

Amber trails (v ≈ vesc): the projectile barely escapes, reaching infinity with nearly zero residual speed. Total energy is approximately zero.

Teal trails (v > vesc): the projectile escapes with energy to spare. It reaches infinity still moving — this excess is called hyperbolic excess velocity, and it determines how fast interplanetary probes travel between planets.

2. The Derivation

Energy Conservation

The derivation is beautifully simple. At the surface, set total energy to zero (the minimum for escape). At infinity, both kinetic and potential energy vanish. Energy conservation gives us the answer immediately.

At escape:   ½mv² − GMm/r = 0
The mass m cancels:   ½v² = GM/r
Therefore:   vesc = √(2GM/r)

vesc = 11.2 km/s (Earth)
Notice that the projectile’s mass m cancels out completely. A feather and a battleship have the same escape velocity. This is because both kinetic energy (½mv²) and gravitational potential energy (GMm/r) are proportional to m. The escape velocity depends only on the mass M and radius r of the body you are escaping from.

Jupiter’s enormous escape velocity (59.5 km/s) is why it has retained a thick hydrogen-helium atmosphere for billions of years — even the lightest, fastest-moving hydrogen molecules at Jovian temperatures cannot reach this speed. The Moon, with only 2.4 km/s, lost any atmosphere it might once have had; even water vapour molecules at moderate temperatures exceed this speed, so they gradually leak away into space. This process is called atmospheric escape or Jeans escape.

4. What Have We Learnt?

Key Equations
Escape velocity: vesc = √(2GM/r)
Circular orbital velocity: vorb = √(GM/r)
Relationship: vesc = √2 × vorb ≈ 1.414 × vorb
Energy at escape: ½mv² − GMm/r = 0  ⇒  total energy = 0
Schwarzschild radius: rs = 2GM/c²  (where vesc = c)
Connections
Escape velocity connects to the deepest ideas in physics. The relationship vesc = √2 × vorb means that an object in a circular orbit needs only a 41% speed boost to escape entirely — this is the basis of transfer orbits used by every interplanetary mission.

At the extreme, when escape velocity equals the speed of light, we arrive at the concept of a black hole. Setting vesc = c in our equation and solving for r gives the Schwarzschild radius rs = 2GM/c² — the event horizon, the boundary from which nothing can escape. (This Newtonian derivation gives the correct answer, though a rigorous treatment requires general relativity.)

Rockets do not actually need to reach escape velocity instantaneously. Sustained thrust can achieve escape at any speed, but the total energy expenditure always comes back to this fundamental equation. The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation determines how much fuel is needed to supply that energy.
Where You’ll See This
  • Rocket launches — mission design is built around the energy budget to reach escape velocity; the Saturn V had to achieve 11.2 km/s to send Apollo to the Moon
  • Why the Moon has no atmosphere — gas molecules at even modest temperatures exceed the Moon’s 2.4 km/s escape velocity
  • Black holes — when vesc ≥ c, not even light escapes; the event horizon is defined by vesc = c
  • Planetary formation — determines which gases a protoplanet retains during accretion from the solar nebula
  • Interstellar travel — the Voyager probes achieved solar escape velocity (>16.7 km/s) and are now leaving the Solar System

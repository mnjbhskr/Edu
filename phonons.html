Phonons & Lattice Vibrations

Quantised waves in the crystal lattice

Chapter 17 · Solid State Physics · Topic 5 · Difficulty 3

Atoms in a crystal are not static — they vibrate about their equilibrium positions, connected to neighbours by interatomic forces that act like tiny springs. These collective vibrations, when quantised, are called phonons. They carry heat, scatter electrons, and determine whether a material is a good conductor or a good insulator. The simplest model begins with a one-dimensional chain of atoms.

ω(k) = 2√(K/M) |sin(ka/2)|
A crystal lattice behaves like a huge network of tiny masses connected by springs. Just as a guitar string vibrates at specific frequencies, a crystal vibrates at quantised frequencies. Each quantum of vibration is called a phonon — the ‘sound particle’ of a crystal.

At the zone boundary k = π/a, neighbouring atoms oscillate exactly out of phase — the shortest wavelength the lattice can support. Wavelengths shorter than 2a are aliased back into the first Brillouin zone.

In the acoustic mode, both atom types move in the same direction (like sound waves). In the optical mode, they oscillate against each other — this can couple to light in ionic crystals, hence the name.

The band gap arises because the optical mode frequency at k=0 is ω = √(2K/M₂) while the acoustic mode at the zone edge is ω = √(2K/M₁) when M₁ < M₂. No phonon states exist in this gap.

4. Density of States

The density of states g(ω) counts how many modes exist at each frequency. Near the band edges, where the dispersion flattens, the density of states diverges — these are Van Hove singularities. Compare with the Debye and Einstein approximations.

Controls

The Debye model assumes a linear dispersion (g(ω) ∝ ω² in 3D, ∝ constant in 1D) up to a cutoff ωD. The Einstein model places all modes at a single frequency. Reality lies between the two, with Van Hove singularities that neither model captures.

5. Specific Heat

The heat capacity Cv reveals how phonons store thermal energy. At high temperatures, classical equipartition gives Cv = 3NkB (Dulong–Petit). At low temperatures, quantum freezing reduces Cv following T³ (Debye) or an exponential (Einstein).

Diamond has an extremely high Debye temperature (θD ≈ 2230 K) due to its stiff, light lattice. At room temperature its Cv is far below the classical limit, a fact that puzzled 19th-century physicists until Einstein introduced quantised vibrations in 1907.
Why Phonons Matter

Phonons are not just theoretical constructs — they are quasiparticles that carry measurable momentum and energy. They govern thermal conductivity, mediate superconductivity (through electron–phonon coupling in BCS theory), determine the temperature dependence of electrical resistance, and set limits on the performance of semiconductor devices. Understanding phonons bridges quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics, and materials science.

Thermal conductivity. Phonons carry heat through crystals. Materials with stiff bonds and light atoms (like diamond) have phonons that travel fast and far — giving diamond the highest thermal conductivity of any natural material, five times better than copper. This is why diamond feels cold to the touch despite being an electrical insulator.
Electron–phonon scattering. Phonons scatter electrons, creating electrical resistance. As temperature rises, more phonons are excited, scattering electrons more frequently — this is why metals become more resistive when heated. At very low temperatures, phonons freeze out, and resistance drops dramatically (or vanishes entirely in superconductors).
Did you know? Phonons are literally sound waves, but at frequencies far too high to hear. The highest phonon frequencies in diamond reach ~40 THz — about a billion times higher than the highest note on a piano. Yet they obey the same wave physics, just at atomic scales.

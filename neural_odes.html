Neural ODEs

When neural networks meet differential equations

Advanced

A residual network adds a small change at each layer: ht+1 = ht + f(ht). Make the layers infinitesimally thin and you get a differential equation: dh/dt = f(h, t). That single shift — from discrete layers to continuous depth — is the idea behind Neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018). It unifies deep learning with the mathematics of dynamical systems, letting a network learn to flow through state space rather than hop through it.

Key insight: By the uniqueness theorem for ODEs, trajectories in the same state space can never cross. This is both a strength (invertibility is guaranteed) and a limitation (some transformations require augmenting the state with extra dimensions).

5 · Advantages and Trade-offs

Neural ODEs offer unique benefits but come with real costs. Understanding the trade-offs is essential for choosing the right architecture.

O(1) Memory

The adjoint method computes gradients by solving a second ODE backwards in time, avoiding the need to store intermediate activations. Memory stays constant regardless of depth.

Advantage

Continuous Depth

Instead of choosing a fixed number of layers, the network learns how long to integrate. Depth becomes a continuous, learnable quantity — the solver decides how many steps are needed.

Advantage

Irregular Time Series

Standard RNNs need evenly spaced observations. Neural ODEs handle irregular timestamps naturally — just integrate to whatever time you need the prediction at.

Advantage

Training Cost

Each forward pass requires solving an ODE, and each backward pass requires solving the adjoint ODE. Adaptive solvers may take many function evaluations, making training slower than a standard ResNet.

Trade-off

No Trajectory Crossing

ODE trajectories cannot cross in state space (uniqueness theorem). This limits the transformations a neural ODE can represent without augmenting the state dimension.

Trade-off

Exact Log-Likelihoods

Continuous normalising flows give exact log-likelihoods via the instantaneous change of variables formula, unlike GANs (no likelihood) or VAEs (approximate bound).

Summary

Neural ODEs reveal a deep connection between residual networks and differential equations. By replacing discrete layers with a continuous ODE, we gain memory efficiency, adaptive computation, and a principled framework for modelling dynamics and generative flows. The trade-off is computational cost and a topological constraint on trajectory crossing — but the elegance and flexibility of the continuous-depth perspective has opened entirely new directions in deep learning.

The foundational paper by Chen et al. (2018), “Neural Ordinary Differential Equations,” won the Best Paper award at NeurIPS and launched a wave of research into continuous-depth models, including augmented neural ODEs, stochastic differential equation networks, and flow matching.

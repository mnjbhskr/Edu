What have we learnt?

Parametric and polar representations free us from the constraint y = f(x). A single-valued function can only pass through each x-value once, but parametric curves can loop, cross themselves, and form shapes impossible in Cartesian coordinates. By letting both x and y depend on a third variable t, we gain the full expressive power of curves in the plane.

Lissajous figures appear on oscilloscopes when two perpendicular signals of different frequencies are combined. The ratio of frequencies determines the shape: rational ratios produce closed curves, while irrational ratios cause the curve to eventually fill a region. Jules Antoine Lissajous studied them in the 1850s using vibrating tuning forks and mirrors.

Spirals in nature — the logarithmic spiral (r = ekθ) appears in nautilus shells, hurricane clouds, spiral galaxies, and the arrangement of seeds in sunflowers. Its defining property is self-similarity: every portion, when magnified, looks identical to the whole. It connects to the golden ratio when k = ln(φ)/(π/2).

The cycloid is the curve traced by a point on a rolling wheel. It is the brachistochrone — the curve of fastest descent under gravity — and also the tautochrone: a ball released from any point on an inverted cycloid reaches the bottom in the same time. Johann Bernoulli posed the brachistochrone problem in 1696; its solution launched the calculus of variations.

Rose curves reveal a surprising pattern: r = cos(nθ) has n petals when n is odd, but 2n petals when n is even. When n is irrational, the curve never closes and gradually fills the entire unit disk — a beautiful connection between number theory and geometry.

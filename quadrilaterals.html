Quadrilaterals & Their Properties

●●●● Beginner

Exploring the family of four-sided figures — from parallelograms to kites

1. Introduction — What Is a Quadrilateral?

A quadrilateral is any polygon with exactly four sides and four vertices. The word comes from the Latin quadri- (four) and latus (side). Every quadrilateral, no matter how irregular, shares one fundamental property:

Sum of interior angles = 360°

This follows directly from the fact that any quadrilateral can be split into two triangles by drawing a diagonal. Since each triangle has an angle sum of 180°, the quadrilateral’s total is 2 × 180° = 360°.

Classification Tree

The hierarchy of quadrilaterals — every square is a rectangle, every rectangle is a parallelogram

The classification forms a hierarchy: a square is a special rectangle, which is a special parallelogram, which is a special quadrilateral. Moving down the tree adds constraints; moving up relaxes them.

2. Types of Quadrilaterals

Properties at a Glance

Shape Sides Angles Diagonals
Parallelogram Opposite sides equal & parallel Opposite angles equal; consecutive supplementary Bisect each other
Rectangle Opposite sides equal & parallel All angles 90° Bisect each other; equal in length
Rhombus All four sides equal; opposite parallel Opposite angles equal Bisect each other at right angles
Square All four sides equal; opposite parallel All angles 90° Bisect at right angles; equal in length
Trapezium Exactly one pair of parallel sides Co-interior angles between parallels are supplementary No general bisection property
Kite Two pairs of consecutive sides equal One pair of opposite angles equal One bisects the other at right angles
Notice how each special quadrilateral inherits properties from its parent in the hierarchy. A square possesses every property of a rhombus and every property of a rectangle.

3. Parallelogram Properties

Four Key Theorems

A parallelogram is defined as a quadrilateral with both pairs of opposite sides parallel. From this single condition, four important properties follow:

(a) A diagonal of a parallelogram divides it into two congruent triangles.

(b) Opposite sides of a parallelogram are equal.

(c) Opposite angles of a parallelogram are equal.

(d) The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other.

▶ Proof of (a): Diagonal divides into two congruent triangles

Consider parallelogram ABCD with diagonal AC.

In ▵ABC and ▵CDA:

1. ∠BAC = ∠DCA   (alternate interior angles, AB ∥ CD)
2. AC = CA   (common side)
3. ∠BCA = ∠DAC   (alternate interior angles, BC ∥ AD)

∴ ▵ABC ≅ ▵CDA   (ASA congruence)

Since the triangles are congruent, corresponding sides are equal: AB = CD and BC = DA — this proves property (b). Corresponding angles give ∠ABC = ∠CDA — proving property (c).

For property (d), consider diagonals AC and BD meeting at O. Using the congruence of ▵AOB and ▵COD (by ASA), we get AO = CO and BO = DO — hence the diagonals bisect each other.

5. Midpoint Theorem

Connecting Midpoints

The midpoint theorem states that the line segment joining the midpoints of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third side and equal to half its length.

If D is the midpoint of AB and E is the midpoint of AC, then:
DE ∥ BC  and  DE = ½ BC

This theorem connects triangle geometry to parallelogram theory: extending DE to a point F such that EF = DE creates parallelogram DBCF, from which the result follows.

Drag triangle vertices A, B, C. The midpoint segment DE is shown in red. Observe DE ∥ BC and DE = ½ BC.

This theorem has a beautiful consequence for quadrilaterals: the midpoints of any quadrilateral always form a parallelogram (Varignon’s theorem). Try it on the Shape Explorer above!

6. Worked Examples

CBSE-Style Problems

Example 1: In a parallelogram ABCD, ∠A = 65°. Find all the other angles.

Given: ABCD is a parallelogram, ∠A = 65°
Step 1: Opposite angles are equal ⇒ ∠C = ∠A = 65°
Step 2: Consecutive angles are supplementary ⇒ ∠B = 180° − 65° = 115°
Step 3: ∠D = ∠B = 115° (opposite angles equal)
Check: 65° + 115° + 65° + 115° = 360° ✓

Example 2: PQRS is a rhombus. The diagonals PR and QS intersect at O. If PR = 16 cm and QS = 12 cm, find the side length of the rhombus.

Given: PR = 16 cm, QS = 12 cm
Step 1: Diagonals of a rhombus bisect each other at right angles
Step 2: PO = PR / 2 = 16 / 2 = 8 cm
Step 3: QO = QS / 2 = 12 / 2 = 6 cm
Step 4: In right-angled ▵POQ:
    PQ² = PO² + QO² = 64 + 36 = 100
    PQ = √100 = 10 cm
Answer: Each side of the rhombus is 10 cm.

Markov Chains & Random Walks

The mathematics of forgetting the past — where you are is all that matters

CS, AI & Cryptography · Difficulty 2

In 1906, the Russian mathematician Andrey Markov introduced a startling idea: a process whose future depends only on its present state, never on its history. In a Markov process, the future depends only on the present state, not on the past. This “memoryless” property — known as the Markov property — seems limiting, yet Markov chains model an astonishing range of phenomena — from weather patterns to stock prices, from DNA sequences to the very algorithm that made Google a $400 billion company. The chain’s power lies in a beautiful convergence: no matter where you start, given enough time, you always end up in the same long-run distribution. The past is forgotten. Only the present matters.

A simple weather model (2 states). Imagine weather that is either Sunny or Rainy. If today is Sunny, there is a 70% chance tomorrow is Sunny and a 30% chance it turns Rainy. If today is Rainy, there is a 40% chance tomorrow is Sunny and a 60% chance it stays Rainy. This is a complete Markov chain — to predict tomorrow, you only need to know today. The transition matrix is:

          Sunny  Rainy
  Sunny  [ 0.70   0.30 ]
  Rainy  [ 0.40   0.60 ]

Over time, this chain settles to a steady state where roughly 57% of days are Sunny and 43% are Rainy — regardless of whether you started on a sunny or rainy day. Try the “Weather” preset in Section 1 to see this in action.

“The Markov property: the future depends only on the present, not the past.”
The Markov property says that given the current state, the future is independent of the past. This is not an approximation — many real systems genuinely have this property. A chess position, for instance, contains everything you need to play optimally; the sequence of moves that led there is irrelevant.

Convergence to equilibrium is the deepest fact about ergodic Markov chains. No matter where the particle starts, after enough steps, the probability of being in each state settles to a fixed distribution π. This is the ergodic theorem in action: time averages equal ensemble averages. The past is truly forgotten.
Absorbing states. Not every Markov chain reaches a balanced equilibrium. An absorbing state is one that, once entered, can never be left — the probability of staying is 1. The classic example is the Gambler’s Ruin: a gambler repeatedly bets $1 on a fair coin flip. The states “$0” (bankruptcy) and “$N” (reaching the target) are absorbing — the game ends when either is reached. Other examples include extinction in population models and checkmate in chess. In an absorbing chain, the key question shifts from “what is the steady state?” to “what is the probability of being absorbed into each absorbing state, and how long does it take?” Try the “Gambler’s Ruin” preset in Section 1 to explore this.

3. PageRank: The Google Algorithm

A random surfer on the web

Node sizes reflect PageRank. Edges show hyperlinks. The random surfer follows links with probability d, jumps to a random page with probability (1−d).

0.85
PR(i) = (1 − d) / N + d · Σj→i PR(j) / L(j)
where d = damping factor, N = number of pages, L(j) = outgoing links from page j
PageRank IS a Markov chain. Larry Page and Sergey Brin modelled the web as a directed graph and asked: if a “random surfer” follows links randomly (with occasional random jumps), which pages would they visit most often? The answer — the steady-state distribution of this Markov chain — became the core of the algorithm that built a $400 billion company. The damping factor d (typically 0.85) represents the probability of following a link rather than jumping randomly.

In the limit: modern language models (Ch 13)
From Markov to GPT. A word-level Markov chain is the simplest possible language model. It predicts the next word using only the previous k words. Increasing k makes text more coherent but requires exponentially more data. Modern transformer-based language models (Chapter 13) generalise this idea enormously — they look at all previous tokens, weighted by attention — but the fundamental principle is the same: predict the next token given context.
Did you know? Speech recognition historically used Hidden Markov Models (HMMs), where the underlying states are not directly observable — only their outputs are. Imagine someone in another room speaking: the hidden states are the words they intend to say, and the observations are the sound waves you hear. An HMM uses probabilities to infer the most likely hidden sequence from the observations. This idea powered virtually all commercial speech-to-text systems from the 1980s until the 2010s, and bridges directly to modern AI — today’s deep learning models solve the same problem but learn richer representations.
Why Markov Chains Matter
  • Google PageRank — the algorithm that made the modern web possible, ranking billions of pages using the steady-state of a random surfer’s Markov chain.
  • Weather forecasting — tomorrow’s weather depends primarily on today’s, making Markov models natural for short-term prediction.
  • Speech recognition — Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) powered speech-to-text systems for decades before deep learning.
  • Financial modelling — credit ratings, stock regimes, and interest rate models often use Markov chains.
  • MCMC in Bayesian statistics — Markov Chain Monte Carlo is the workhorse of modern Bayesian inference, sampling from complex posterior distributions.
  • DNA sequence analysis — gene-finding algorithms use HMMs to identify coding regions in genomes.

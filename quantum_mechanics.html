Quantum Mechanics

The rules governing atomic and subatomic behaviour

In quantum mechanics, particles are described not by definite positions and velocities but by wavefunctions — complex-valued amplitudes whose squared magnitude gives the probability of finding the particle at a given location. This leads to phenomena with no classical counterpart: superposition, interference, measurement collapse, and a fundamental limit on how precisely position and momentum can be known simultaneously.

The wavefunction ψ(x) is complex-valued. Its real and imaginary parts oscillate, but the physically measurable quantity is |ψ|² — the probability density. Notice how the probability envelope can remain smooth even when Re(ψ) and Im(ψ) oscillate rapidly.

Each measurement yields a single definite position, drawn randomly from |ψ|². After measurement, the wavefunction collapses to a narrow peak at the measured location. Over many measurements, the histogram converges to the original |ψ|² distribution — quantum mechanics predicts statistics, not individual outcomes.

4. The Uncertainty Principle

0.30

A narrow position-space Gaussian (ψ(x) tightly peaked) has a broad Fourier transform (φ(p) spread out), and vice versa. The product Δx · Δp is always at least ℏ/2. This is not a limitation of our instruments — it is a fundamental property of waves.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Summary
  • The wavefunction — ψ(x) is a complex amplitude. The physically observable probability density is |ψ(x)|². Different waveforms (Gaussian, plane wave, eigenstates) encode different physical situations.
  • Superposition — Quantum states can be added: ψ = c&sub1;ψ&sub1; + c&sub2;ψ&sub2;. The resulting probability |ψ|² contains cross terms that produce interference — this is fundamentally different from classical probability.
  • Measurement — Observing a quantum system forces it into a definite state. The probability of each outcome is given by |ψ|², and after measurement the wavefunction collapses. Repeated measurements build up the predicted distribution.
  • The uncertainty principle — Position and momentum cannot both be precisely defined: Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2. This is a mathematical consequence of the wave nature of matter, visible as the reciprocal widths of Fourier-transform pairs.
  • Connections — These ideas underpin all of quantum physics: atomic energy levels, chemical bonding, semiconductors, quantum computing, and particle physics. The Schrödinger equation governs how ψ evolves in time.

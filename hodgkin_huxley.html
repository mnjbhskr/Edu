The Hodgkin-Huxley Neuron

How ions, channels, and mathematics produce the spark of thought

Chapter 15 · Mathematical Biology · Topic 8 · Difficulty 3

In 1952, Alan Hodgkin and Andrew Huxley described the electrical activity of the squid giant axon with a set of differential equations that remain the gold standard of computational neuroscience. Their model explains how voltage-gated sodium and potassium channels orchestrate the action potential — the fundamental unit of neural communication. Explore the mathematics below.

A neuron is a biological wire. Dendrites receive signals from other neurons, the cell body integrates them, and if the signal is strong enough, the axon fires an electrical pulse — the action potential — that travels to the next neuron across a synapse. The Hodgkin-Huxley model captures the mathematics of this firing with stunning precision.

1 · The Action Potential

The Hodgkin-Huxley equation couples the membrane capacitance to three ionic currents. Inject enough current past the threshold and the membrane fires an all-or-nothing spike.

Below threshold (~6 μA/cm²), the membrane responds passively. Above threshold, a rapid depolarisation fires an action potential: Na² channels open explosively, then inactivate while K² channels repolarise the membrane.

2 · Gating Variables

Three gating variables control the ion channels: m (Na² activation, fast), h (Na² inactivation, slow), and n (K² activation, intermediate). Their interplay creates the spike.

Controls

10.0 μA/cm²
During a spike: m rises first (Na² activation), causing depolarisation. Then h drops (Na² inactivation) and n rises (K² activation), repolarising the membrane. The staggered timing is the heart of the action potential.

3 · Phase Portrait

The V–n phase plane reveals the system's dynamical structure. At low current, a stable fixed point attracts all trajectories. As current increases past a critical value, a Hopf bifurcation births a stable limit cycle — the neuron oscillates.

The V-nullcline (dV/dt = 0) and n-nullcline (dn/dt = 0) intersect at equilibria. When the fixed point loses stability, a limit cycle emerges — this is the Hopf bifurcation that underlies rhythmic neural firing.

4 · Frequency-Current Relationship

The HH model shows a sharp onset of firing at a threshold current (Type II excitability) with a minimum firing frequency. The leaky integrate-and-fire model, by contrast, produces a smoother, monotonically increasing f-I curve starting from zero frequency.
The full Hodgkin-Huxley model has four differential equations and dozens of parameters. Simpler models capture the essential dynamics: the FitzHugh-Nagumo model reduces it to just two equations (fast voltage + slow recovery), while the Izhikevich model can reproduce 20 different firing patterns with only two equations and four parameters. These simplified models are used in large-scale brain simulations.

Did you know? Hodgkin and Huxley chose the giant axon of the Atlantic squid (up to 1mm diameter — 100 times larger than human neurons) because it was big enough to insert electrodes into. Their 1952 paper required weeks of hand calculation on a mechanical calculator. They won the Nobel Prize in 1963 — and their equations remain the foundation of computational neuroscience today.
Connections: The oscillatory dynamics here share deep mathematical structure with chemical oscillations (both are limit-cycle oscillators described by similar equations). The gating equations are differential equations of the same kind you meet throughout this course, and the phase portrait above uses the same phase-plane techniques applied to predator-prey dynamics.
The Hodgkin-Huxley Legacy

Published in 1952, the Hodgkin-Huxley model earned its creators the 1963 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. It was the first biophysical model to quantitatively predict the shape, speed, and threshold of action potentials from first principles. The model introduced the concept of voltage-dependent gating variables, which has since been extended to describe hundreds of ion channel types across all excitable cells. From cardiac pacemakers to epileptic seizures, the mathematical framework Hodgkin and Huxley pioneered remains indispensable in modern neuroscience and biomedical engineering.

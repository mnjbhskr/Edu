Stochastic Calculus

Calculus for random processes — Itô’s lemma and SDEs

Ordinary calculus works beautifully for smooth functions. But financial prices, particle positions, and many natural phenomena follow random paths that are continuous yet nowhere differentiable. Stochastic calculus — built on Itô’s revolutionary insight — extends the tools of calculus to these jagged, unpredictable trajectories. The key surprise: a new term appears in the chain rule, and that small correction underpins all of modern mathematical finance.

Brownian motion has a strange property: its quadratic variation is not zero (as it would be for any smooth function). It equals t — this simple fact is why ordinary calculus breaks down and Itô calculus is needed.

2. Why Ordinary Calculus Fails

f(W) = W² — naive chain rule vs Itô correction

True W(t)²
Naive ∫2W dW
Itô ∫2W dW + t
Ordinary: d(W²) = 2W dW
Itô:      d(W²) = 2W dW + dt
When you differentiate f(W(t)) = W², ordinary calculus gives df = 2W dW. But that is wrong by exactly dt. The extra term comes from the non-zero quadratic variation of Brownian motion. This correction — Itô’s insight — is the foundation of mathematical finance.

(dW)² = dt is the key rule. All terms with dt² or dt·dW vanish.

df = f′(X)μ dt + ½f″(X)σ² dt + f′(X)σ dW
Itô’s lemma is the chain rule for stochastic calculus. The extra ½f″(X)σ² dt term — which has no analogue in ordinary calculus — comes from the non-vanishing quadratic variation. It is this term that transforms geometric Brownian motion into the Black–Scholes equation.

Derivation via Itô’s lemma:

1. The SDE: dS/S = μ dt + σ dW
2. Apply Itô with f = ln: d(ln S) = (μ − ½σ²) dt + σ dW
3. Integrate: ln S(t) − ln S(0) = (μ − ½σ²)t + σW(t)
4. Exponentiate: S(t) = S₀ exp((μ − ½σ²)t + σW(t))
E[S(t)] = S₀ eμt   |   Median = S₀ e(μ − ½σ²)t

The mean exceeds the median because log-normal distributions are right-skewed. The −σ²/2 term is pure Itô.

The −σ²/2 term in the exponent is pure Itô. Without it, you would predict that the average stock price grows faster than it actually does. This seemingly small correction is the difference between getting Black–Scholes right and getting it wrong.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Summary

We followed the chain: Brownian motion (continuous but nowhere differentiable) → quadratic variation (equals t, not zero) → Itô’s lemma (the corrected chain rule) → geometric Brownian motion (the standard model for stock prices) → Black–Scholes (a direct consequence).

The crucial insight is that (dW)² = dt, which forces a correction term ½f″σ² dt into every application of the chain rule. This term has no analogue in deterministic calculus.

Itô’s Lemma: df = f′(X) dX + ½ f″(X) (dX)²
GBM Solution: S(t) = S₀ exp((μ − ½σ²)t + σW(t))

The Itô–Stratonovich distinction: Itô integrals evaluate the integrand at the left endpoint of each interval; Stratonovich integrals use the midpoint. In finance, the Itô convention is standard because it preserves the martingale property (no peeking into the future). In physics, Stratonovich is sometimes preferred because it obeys the ordinary chain rule.

As fluid enters a narrower section, it must speed up (continuity). By Bernoulli's equation, faster flow means lower pressure — this is the principle behind aeroplane wings, carburettors, and Venturi meters.

2. Laminar vs Turbulent Flow

The Reynolds number determines whether flow is smooth (laminar) or chaotic (turbulent). At intermediate values, beautiful von Kármán vortex streets form — alternating vortices shed from opposite sides of the obstacle.

An aerofoil generates lift by deflecting air downward. As angle of attack increases, lift grows — until the flow separates from the upper surface and the wing stalls, causing a sudden loss of lift and sharp increase in drag.

Point vortices are idealised rotational flows. The velocity falls off as 1/r from the centre. Counter-rotating vortex pairs translate together — this is the basis of wingtip vortices and smoke rings.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Key Equations
Bernoulli: P + ½ρv² = constant along a streamline
Continuity: A₁v₁ = A₂v₂
Reynolds number: Re = ρvL / μ
Lift: L = ½ρv²S CL
Drag: D = ½ρv²S CD
Vortex velocity: v = Γ / (2πr)
Connections
Fluid dynamics connects deeply to many branches of physics and mathematics. Bernoulli's principle is an expression of conservation of energy applied to flowing fluids. The Reynolds number links viscous forces to inertial forces, determining whether the elegant order of laminar flow gives way to the chaos of turbulence — one of the great unsolved problems in classical physics. Lift and drag on aerofoils are central to aerospace engineering, while vortex dynamics underpins weather systems, ocean currents, and even the formation of galaxies. The Navier–Stokes equations that govern all fluid motion remain one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems — proving their smooth solutions exist (or do not) would be worth one million dollars.

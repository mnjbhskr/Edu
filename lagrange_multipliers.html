Lagrange Multipliers

●●●● Advanced

Optimising functions subject to constraints — where gradients align, optima live

1. Constrained Optimisation

Suppose you want to maximise (or minimise) a function f(x, y), but you are not free to choose any point in the plane. Instead, your inputs must satisfy a constraint g(x, y) = 0. This is constrained optimisation — one of the most important problems in applied mathematics, economics, and engineering.

The standard unconstrained methods — setting ∇f = 0 — will not work here. The unconstrained maximum of f may lie far from the constraint curve, and the constrained optimum is almost never a critical point of f on its own.

The key geometric insight is beautiful in its simplicity: at a constrained optimum, the contour lines of f are tangent to the constraint curve. If they were not tangent, you could slide along the constraint and increase f further. Tangency means the gradients ∇f and ∇g point in the same (or opposite) direction — they are parallel. This is exactly the condition that Lagrange multipliers capture.

Think of walking along a hillside contour while constrained to a path. You reach the highest point on the path precisely when the path becomes tangent to a contour line — moving further along the path would take you downhill.

2. The Method

The Lagrange Condition

At a constrained optimum, the gradient of f must be a scalar multiple of the gradient of g:

∇f = λ ∇g

This single vector equation, together with the constraint, yields a system of equations. For functions of two variables:

(1)  ∂f/∂x = λ · ∂g/∂x
(2)  ∂f/∂y = λ · ∂g/∂y
(3)  g(x, y) = 0

Three equations in three unknowns (x, y, λ). Solve them simultaneously to find the constrained optima.

What is λ?

The multiplier λ is not just an algebraic convenience. It measures the sensitivity of the optimal value to changes in the constraint. If the constraint is g(x, y) = c instead of g(x, y) = 0, then λ = df*/dc — the rate at which the optimum changes as the constraint is relaxed. In economics, this is called the shadow price.

▶ Why ∇f and ∇g must be parallel

The gradient ∇f is perpendicular to the contour lines of f, and ∇g is perpendicular to the constraint curve g = 0. At the optimum, moving along the constraint must not change f (to first order) — otherwise you could increase f by moving in that direction. This means the constraint curve is tangent to a contour of f, so their normals (∇f and ∇g) are parallel.

Formally, parametrise the constraint curve as r(t). Then g(r(t)) = 0, so ∇g · r′ = 0. At an optimum, d/dt f(r(t)) = ∇f · r′ = 0. Both ∇f and ∇g are orthogonal to the tangent r′, so in two dimensions they must be parallel: ∇f = λ ∇g.

3. Worked Example

Maximise f(x,y) = xy subject to x² + y² = 1

We wish to find the largest value of the product xy for points on the unit circle. Write the constraint as g(x, y) = x² + y² − 1 = 0.

Step 1: Compute gradients

∇f = (y, x)     ∇g = (2x, 2y)

Step 2: Set up ∇f = λ∇g

(1)  y = 2λx
(2)  x = 2λy
(3)  x² + y² = 1

Step 3: Solve

From (1): y = 2λx  ⇒  substitute into (2): x = 2λ(2λx) = 4λ²x
If x ≠ 0: 4λ² = 1  ⇒  λ = ±1/2
λ = +1/2: y = x  ⇒  2x² = 1  ⇒  x = y = ±1/√2
λ = −1/2: y = −x  ⇒  2x² = 1  ⇒  (x, y) = (±1/√2, ∓1/√2)
Constrained Maximum
f(1/√2, 1/√2) = 1/2
The four candidate points are (±1/√2, ±1/√2). The maximum of f = xy is 1/2 at (1/√2, 1/√2) and (−1/√2, −1/√2). The minimum is −1/2 at the other two points.

5. Multiple Constraints

Extending to Several Constraints

When optimising f(x, y, z) subject to two constraints g1(x, y, z) = 0 and g2(x, y, z) = 0, the feasible set is the intersection of two surfaces — typically a curve in three-dimensional space.

At the optimum, ∇f must lie in the plane spanned by ∇g1 and ∇g2:

∇f = λ1 ∇g1 + λ2 ∇g2

This gives (for three variables) five equations in five unknowns: three from the vector equation ∇f = λ1∇g1 + λ2∇g2, plus the two constraint equations g1 = 0 and g2 = 0.

The logic generalises naturally: with k constraints, the gradient of the objective must be a linear combination of the k constraint gradients, introducing one multiplier per constraint. The constraint qualification condition requires that the constraint gradients be linearly independent at the optimum.

Each additional constraint reduces the dimension of the feasible set by one and introduces one new multiplier. With n variables and k constraints, you solve n + k equations in n + k unknowns.

6. Interpretation of λ

The Shadow Price

The multiplier λ has a precise economic and mathematical meaning. If we write the constraint as g(x, y) = c (instead of g(x, y) = 0), and let f* denote the optimal value of f as a function of c, then:

λ = df*/dc

The multiplier tells you how much the optimum would change if the constraint were relaxed by one unit. In economics, this is the shadow price — the value of having one more unit of the constrained resource.

Worked Example: Sensitivity

Problem: maximise f = xy subject to x² + y² = c
Solution: at the maximum, x = y = √(c/2), so f* = c/2
Therefore: df*/dc = 1/2
Check: we found λ = 1/2 in the worked example — it matches exactly.

If the radius of the circle increases from 1 to 1.01 (so c goes from 1 to 1.0201), the maximum of xy increases by approximately λ × Δc = 0.5 × 0.0201 ≈ 0.01. The actual new maximum is (1.01)²/2 = 0.51005 — an increase of 0.01005, confirming the approximation.

In a manufacturing problem, if g represents a budget constraint and f represents profit, then λ tells you exactly how much additional profit you would gain per additional pound of budget — the marginal value of money.

Supernova Blast Waves

From stellar death to self-similar shockwaves

 University level
A massive star dies in seconds, releasing roughly 1044 joules of kinetic energy into the surrounding interstellar medium. The resulting blast wave expands at thousands of kilometres per second, sweeping up ambient gas into a thin, dense shell. Remarkably, the evolution of this shell follows a simple power law — a result that can be derived from dimensional analysis alone.

1 — The Explosion: 1D Blast Wave Profiles

We model the explosion as a spherically symmetric problem. The gas obeys the compressible Euler equations — conservation of mass, momentum, and energy — with an ideal-gas equation of state (adiabatic index γ = 5/3).

The numerical method is Lax–Friedrichs, a first-order finite-difference scheme that is simple, robust, and naturally diffusive enough to capture the shock without oscillations. The grid uses 400 radial cells with a CFL number of 0.4 for stability. Press Play to watch the blast wave emerge and propagate.

The Numerical Scheme
The conservative variables are U = (ρ, ρv, E) representing mass density, momentum density, and total energy density. In spherical symmetry the Euler equations become:

∂U/∂t + ∂F(U)/∂r = S(U, r)

where the fluxes are F = (ρv, ρv² + P, (E+P)v) and the geometric source S = −(2/r)(ρv, ρv², (E+P)v) accounts for the divergence of spherical shells. Pressure closes the system via the ideal-gas law: P = (γ−1)(E − ½ρv²).

2 — Sedov–Taylor Self-Similar Solution

One of the most elegant results in fluid dynamics emerges when we ask: given an explosion of energy E in a uniform medium of density ρ0, how does the shock radius R grow with time t? The answer requires only dimensional analysis.

The log–log plot below tracks the simulated shock position alongside the analytical Sedov–Taylor prediction. As the simulation runs, the data points should fall on a straight line with slope 2/5 — confirming the power law.

Step 1 — Identify the governing parameters:
  Energy E  [M L² T²],   Density ρ₀  [M L³],   Time t  [T]

Step 2 — Form a dimensionless combination:
  R = C · (E / ρ₀)α · tβ
  [L] = [M L² T² · L³ / M]α · [T]β
  [L] = [L5 T−2]α · [T]β

Step 3 — Match exponents:
  L: 1 = 5α  →  α = 1/5
  T: 0 = −2α + β  →  β = 2/5

Result:
  R(t) = C · (E / ρ₀)1/5 · t2/5
  where C ≈ 1.15 for γ = 5/3
This 2/5 scaling was derived independently by Leonid Sedov and Geoffrey Taylor in the 1940s. Taylor famously estimated the yield of the first nuclear test (Trinity, 1945) from a sequence of declassified photographs, using nothing more than this dimensional argument and a ruler. His estimate of approximately 22 kilotonnes was remarkably close to the classified value. The result demonstrates the extraordinary power of dimensional analysis — no differential equations were solved, yet the answer captures the essential physics.
Why self-similar? The blast wave has no characteristic length scale of its own — the explosion is a point source and the ambient medium is uniform. Therefore the solution at any time must be a rescaled version of the solution at any other time. All profiles collapse onto a single universal curve when plotted against the similarity variable ξ = r/R(t).

4 — What Have We Learnt?

Key Equations
Euler equations (spherically symmetric, conservative form):
∂U/∂t + ∂F/∂r = S
where U = (ρ, ρv, E), fluxes F = (ρv, ρv²+P, (E+P)v), and the geometric source S = −(2/r)(ρv, ρv², (E+P)v) accounts for spherical divergence.

Equation of state:
P = (γ − 1)(E − ½ρv²),   γ = 5/3

Sedov–Taylor scaling:
R(t) = C (E/ρ₀)1/5 t2/5
A pure consequence of dimensional analysis — no detailed solution required.

Rankine–Hugoniot jump conditions at the shock front:
ρ2/ρ1 = (γ+1)/(γ−1) = 4   (for γ = 5/3)
The strong-shock compression ratio sets the peak density at the shock front to exactly four times the ambient density, regardless of the explosion energy.
Phases of Blast Wave Evolution
A supernova remnant passes through several distinct phases as it evolves:

1. Free expansion (first few hundred years): the ejecta expand freely, having not yet swept up a significant mass of ambient gas. The shock radius grows linearly with time: R ∝ t.

2. Sedov–Taylor (102–104 years): the swept-up mass dominates the ejecta mass, and the solution becomes self-similar with R ∝ t2/5. This is the regime our simulation captures.

3. Snowplough (104–105 years): radiative cooling becomes important; the hot interior loses pressure and the shell momentum is approximately conserved, giving R ∝ t1/4.

4. Fade-out: the remnant decelerates to the sound speed of the ambient medium and merges into the general turbulence of the interstellar gas.

Where You'll See This

  • Type Ia and Type II supernovae shaping the interstellar medium, sweeping up gas and dust into expanding shells that eventually fragment and trigger new star formation
  • Supernova remnants such as the Crab Nebula (SN 1054) and Cassiopeia A, whose X-ray and radio morphology encodes the blast wave history
  • Nuclear weapons testing — Taylor's classified derivation of the Trinity yield from blast photographs, later declassified in 1950
  • Volcanic eruptions and industrial explosions, where the same Sedov–Taylor scaling governs the expanding blast front at atmospheric scales
  • Laser-driven inertial confinement fusion, where tiny capsules implode and create converging blast waves at millimetre scales

📉Econometrics

The science of measuring economic relationships

Econometrics is the application of statistical methods to economic data, with the central goal of measuring causal relationships and testing economic theories. It sits at the intersection of economics, statistics, and mathematics — drawing on probability theory for its foundations, linear algebra for its mechanics, and economic theory for its identifying assumptions.

This chapter covers the full arc from introductory regression analysis through the modern causal inference revolution, including the time series methods essential for macroeconomics and finance. The curriculum draws on the canonical textbook sequence: Stock & Watson and Gujarati for foundations, Greene and Wooldridge for core methods, Enders and Hamilton for time series, and Angrist & Pischke for causal inference.

Every page features interactive Monte Carlo simulations — the single most powerful pedagogical tool in econometrics. Watching a thousand estimates build a sampling distribution teaches what ‘unbiased’ and ‘efficient’ mean in a way that no amount of algebra can match.

You Should Know First:
Probability and statistics (hypothesis testing, distributions), calculus (derivatives, integration), and linear algebra (matrices, vectors) form the essential prerequisites. Familiarity with basic economics (supply and demand, market equilibrium) provides helpful context.

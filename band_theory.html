Band Theory of Solids

Why some materials conduct, some insulate, and some do both

Chapter 17 · Solid State Physics · Topic 4 · Difficulty 3

A single atom has sharp, discrete energy levels. But bring many atoms together into a crystal lattice and something remarkable happens: each level splits into a dense cluster of states — an energy band. The gaps between bands, and whether electrons fill them completely, determine everything: whether a material is a metal, a semiconductor, or an insulator. Band theory is the quantum-mechanical foundation of all modern electronics.

For N atoms, each discrete level splits into N closely-spaced states forming a band.
Band gap Eg: Metal ≈ 0 eV   Semiconductor ≈ 1 eV   Insulator ≈ 5+ eV

1. From Atoms to Bands

Controls

1
Increase N to see discrete energy levels split and merge into continuous bands.
N = 1 atom · 2 discrete energy levels
Key insight: When atoms are isolated, each has identical sharp energy levels. As they come together in a crystal, the Pauli exclusion principle forces each level to split into N distinct states. For macroscopic N (~1023), these states are so closely packed that they form a continuous band. The gaps between bands — where no electron states exist — are the band gaps that determine a material's electrical properties.
Conceptual bridge: In a crystal, atoms repeat in a regular pattern. Because the potential energy also repeats, electrons behave like waves moving through a periodic landscape. The mathematics of waves in periodic structures — first solved by Kronig and Penney — reveals why some energies are allowed and others forbidden.

Key insight: The Kronig–Penney model is a simplified 1D crystal: electrons move through a periodic array of rectangular potential barriers. Solving the Schrödinger equation yields allowed energy bands separated by forbidden gaps. The stronger the barriers (higher V0, wider b), the wider the gaps — exactly what determines whether a material conducts or insulates.
ψk(x) = uk(x) · eikx
Bloch’s theorem states that electrons in a periodic potential behave as modulated plane waves. The function uk(x) has the same periodicity as the lattice. This is the central theorem of band theory.
Effective mass: Near a band edge, an electron responds to forces as if it had a different mass — the effective mass m* = ℏ²/(d²E/dk²). In regions where the band curves sharply, m* is small and electrons accelerate easily. Near flat band edges, m* is large. In semiconductors, the effective mass determines carrier mobility and device speed.

3. Metals, Semiconductors, Insulators

1.1

Classification: Semiconductor · Eg = 1.1 eV · Fermi level shown as dashed line
Key insight: The distinction between metals, semiconductors, and insulators comes down to the band gap — the energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band. Metals have no gap (or overlapping bands). Semiconductors have a small gap that thermal energy can bridge. Insulators have such a wide gap that virtually no electrons can jump across at room temperature.
Did you know?
Silicon’s band gap of 1.1 eV is a Goldilocks value — small enough that thermal energy and light can excite carriers, large enough to avoid thermal runaway at operating temperatures. This accident of nature is why silicon dominates the $500 billion semiconductor industry.

4. Direct vs Indirect Band Gap

Controls

In a direct gap, electron transitions are vertical (conserve momentum). Indirect gaps require a phonon to supply the momentum difference.
GaAs: direct gap · Efficient light emission → used in LEDs and laser diodes
Key insight: In a direct band gap (GaAs), the conduction band minimum and valence band maximum occur at the same crystal momentum k. Electrons can transition by absorbing or emitting a photon alone — making these materials ideal for LEDs and lasers. In an indirect gap (Si), the extrema are at different k values, so transitions also need a phonon (lattice vibration) to conserve momentum. This makes Si poor for light emission but excellent for solar cells, where absorbed light creates long-lived carriers.

Type: n-type · Fermi level shifted toward conduction band
Key insight: Doping is the controlled introduction of impurity atoms. Donors (e.g. phosphorus in silicon) have an extra electron that sits in a state just below the conduction band — thermal energy easily promotes it, creating n-type conductivity. Acceptors (e.g. boron) create empty states just above the valence band, producing p-type conductivity via holes. Doping shifts the Fermi level and is the foundation of all semiconductor devices: diodes, transistors, and solar cells.
Why band theory matters
Band theory unifies our understanding of electrical, optical, and thermal properties of solids under a single quantum-mechanical framework. It explains why copper conducts but diamond does not, why silicon can be made to conduct in precisely controlled ways through doping, and why GaAs emits light while silicon does not. Every transistor in every computer, every LED, every solar cell — all are engineered applications of band theory. The difference between a metal, a semiconductor, and an insulator is not a difference in kind, but a difference in the width of a forbidden energy gap.

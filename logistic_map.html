The Logistic Map & Chaos

How the simplest equation creates infinite complexity

Chapter 14 · Dynamical Systems & Chaos · Topic 1 · Difficulty 2

Take a number between 0 and 1. Multiply it by r, then by one minus itself. Repeat. This absurdly simple rule — the logistic map — generates fixed points, periodic cycles, and full-blown chaos depending on a single parameter. Discovered as a model for population growth, it became the gateway through which physicists first glimpsed universal laws hidden inside nonlinear systems. The equation is:

xn+1 = r · xn · (1 − xn)
Historical note: Robert May's landmark 1976 paper 'Simple mathematical models with very complicated dynamics' brought the logistic map to the attention of the scientific world, showing that even the simplest nonlinear equations can produce chaos. What began as a humble model of population growth became one of the most important objects in all of mathematics.

Key insight: The cobweb diagram reveals how iteration works geometrically. The parabola y = rx(1−x) maps each x to its successor, while the line y = x reflects the output back as the next input. Where the web spirals inward, you have stability. Where it spirals outward or bounces chaotically, you see the onset of disorder.

Key insight: The ratio of successive bifurcation intervals converges to the Feigenbaum constant δ ≈ 4.6692. This number is universal — it appears in every one-dimensional map with a quadratic maximum, not just the logistic map. Zoom into the chaotic region and you will find miniature copies of the entire diagram: chaos contains windows of order at every scale.
Universality: The same constant δ ≈ 4.669 appears in many completely different chaotic systems — from fluid dynamics to laser physics. This universality is one of the deepest results in chaos theory. Discovered by Mitchell Feigenbaum in 1978, it means that the route to chaos follows the same quantitative law regardless of the underlying physics.
Connection to the Mandelbrot set: The bifurcation diagram of the logistic map is intimately connected to the Mandelbrot set. Each point on the real axis of the Mandelbrot set corresponds to a value of r in the logistic map. The two objects are related by a simple change of variables: the logistic map x → rx(1−x) is conjugate to the quadratic map z → z² + c that generates the Mandelbrot set.
Did you know? The logistic map contains an infinite number of miniature copies of itself. Zoom anywhere in the bifurcation diagram and you will see the same period-doubling cascade repeating — self-similarity at every scale. This fractal structure means that no matter how deeply you zoom, you will never run out of new detail to discover.

Lyapunov exponent: λ = limn→∞ (1/n) Σ ln|f′(xi)|   where f′(x) = r(1 − 2x)
λ < 0 ⇒ stable    λ = 0 ⇒ edge of chaos    λ > 0 ⇒ chaotic
Key insight: In the chaotic regime, two orbits that start 0.0001 apart diverge exponentially. The Lyapunov exponent λ quantifies this rate: when λ > 0, nearby trajectories separate by a factor of eλ per iteration, making long-term prediction impossible even though the system is entirely deterministic.
Why It Matters
The logistic map is where modern chaos theory begins. From a single quadratic equation emerges the full spectrum of dynamical behaviour: fixed points, periodic orbits, period-doubling cascades, and deterministic chaos.

Connection to the Mandelbrot Set (Ch 10): The logistic map is conjugate to the quadratic map z → z² + c. Each horizontal slice through the Mandelbrot set along the real axis corresponds to a parameter value of the logistic map. The bifurcation diagram you explored above is literally a cross-section of the Mandelbrot set.

Population Biology: This same equation was introduced by Robert May in 1976 to model population growth with limited resources. When a species reproduces too rapidly for its environment (high r), the population does not simply crash — it oscillates, doubles its period, and eventually becomes chaotic.

Financial Markets: Chaotic dynamics appear in economic models where small feedback loops amplify into large, unpredictable swings — deterministic systems that look random.

The Feigenbaum Constant: Perhaps most remarkable of all: the constant δ ≈ 4.6692... is universal. It appears in every one-dimensional map that has a quadratic maximum — not just the logistic map, but the sine map, the Gaussian map, and infinitely many others. This universality, discovered by Mitchell Feigenbaum in 1978, was the first hint that chaos obeys deep mathematical laws.

Fermi-Dirac Statistics

How quantum mechanics fills energy levels in solids

Chapter 17 · Solid State Physics · Topic 3 · Difficulty 3

Electrons are fermions — no two can occupy the same quantum state. This single fact, captured by the Fermi-Dirac distribution, governs the electronic properties of every metal, semiconductor, and insulator. At absolute zero the distribution is a sharp step: every state below the Fermi energy is filled, every state above is empty. As temperature rises, thermal excitations smear the boundary, and the consequences — from electrical conductivity to the colours of stars — follow from the mathematics of:

f(E) = 1 / ( e(E − μ) / kBT + 1 )
Pauli exclusion principle: Each quantum state can hold at most 2 electrons (one spin-up ↑, one spin-down ↓). This is the Pauli exclusion principle — the reason electrons stack up to high energies even at absolute zero, rather than all falling to the ground state. Without it, every electron in a metal would collapse into the lowest energy level and the Fermi-Dirac distribution would be unnecessary.

Key insight — temperature smearing: At T = 0 the distribution is a perfect step function — all states below EF filled, all above empty. As temperature rises, the step “smears” over a width of ~4kBT. At room temperature this is only ~0.1 eV compared to EF ≈ 7 eV in copper — the electron gas is barely disturbed. The classical Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution has no such exclusion: it allows unlimited occupation at low energies and diverges from reality for degenerate quantum systems.
The Fermi sphere: In momentum space, all occupied states at T = 0 fill a sphere — the Fermi sphere. Its radius is the Fermi wave vector kF. Only electrons near the surface of this sphere (within ~kBT of the Fermi energy) can participate in conduction and absorb heat. This explains why metals conduct electricity so well and why the electronic specific heat is so much smaller than classical theory predicts.

Key insight: The density of states g(E) ∝ √E grows with energy for a free electron gas in 3D. The occupied density n(E) = g(E)·f(E) combines the increasing availability of states with the decreasing probability of occupation, producing a peak below the Fermi energy. At T = 0 the occupied region has a sharp cutoff at μ; at finite T it develops a soft tail above μ.
Did you know? The Fermi energy of electrons in copper is about 7 eV, equivalent to a temperature of ~80,000 K. Room temperature (300 K) is less than 0.4% of this. The electron gas in a metal is effectively “frozen” — only the thinnest shell near the Fermi surface participates in thermal physics. This is why metals do not glow white-hot from their electrons alone.

Key insight: Classical physics predicts every electron contributes (3/2)kB to specific heat, giving a huge constant value. In reality, only electrons within ~kBT of the Fermi surface can be thermally excited (the Pauli exclusion principle blocks the rest). This yields Ce = γT, linear in temperature — a triumph of quantum statistics that resolved a long-standing puzzle in the theory of metals.

Key insight: The band gap determines everything. Metals have overlapping bands (no gap): electrons flow freely. Semiconductors have a small gap (~1 eV): thermal excitation promotes some electrons to the conduction band, and conductivity increases with temperature. Insulators have a large gap (>3 eV): virtually no carriers at room temperature. The Fermi-Dirac tail penetrating into the conduction band is the mechanism.

Key insight: In an intrinsic (undoped) semiconductor, the chemical potential sits near the middle of the band gap and shifts only slightly with temperature. Adding donor impurities (n-type) pushes μ up toward the conduction band; adding acceptors (p-type) pushes it down toward the valence band. At very high temperatures, intrinsic carriers dominate and μ returns toward mid-gap regardless of doping.
Why Fermi-Dirac statistics matter
Before quantum mechanics, the classical Drude model predicted that electrons in a metal should contribute far more to specific heat than observed — the so-called specific heat anomaly. Fermi-Dirac statistics resolved this by showing that the Pauli exclusion principle freezes out most electrons from thermal excitation. Only the thin shell near the Fermi surface participates. This insight underlies all of modern solid-state physics: from understanding why some materials conduct and others insulate, to designing the transistors and diodes that power our civilisation.

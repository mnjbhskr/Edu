Circular Motion

●●●● Advanced

When speed is constant but velocity is not — the geometry of motion in circles

1. Introduction — Uniform Circular Motion

An object moving in a circle at constant speed is undergoing uniform circular motion. The speed is constant, but the velocity is not — because the direction of motion is continually changing. A changing velocity means there must be an acceleration, and that acceleration must be caused by a net force.

We describe the rotation using angular velocity ω, measured in radians per second. If the object completes one full revolution (2π radians) in time T (the period), then:

ω = 2π / T = 2πf    where f = 1 / T is the frequency (Hz)

The linear (tangential) speed relates to the angular velocity by v = ωr, where r is the radius of the circle. A larger radius at the same angular velocity means a greater linear speed.

Every point on a spinning disc shares the same angular velocity ω, but points further from the centre have a greater linear speed. This is why the outer edge of a vinyl record moves faster than the groove near the label.

2. Centripetal Acceleration & Force

Acceleration Directed Towards the Centre

For an object moving in a circle of radius r at speed v, the acceleration is directed radially inward — towards the centre of the circle. This is the centripetal acceleration:

a = v² / r = ω²r    (directed towards the centre)

By Newton’s second law, this acceleration requires a net inward force — the centripetal force:

F = ma = m v² / r = m ω²r
Centripetal force is not a separate force. It is the resultant of real forces acting on the object — tension in a string, gravitational pull, the normal reaction of a surface, friction, or some combination of these. Never draw “centripetal force” as an additional arrow on a free-body diagram.
▶ Derivation of a = v²/r

Consider an object moving from point A to point B on a circle in a small time Δt. The velocity vectors at A and B have the same magnitude v but different directions.

The change in velocity Δv points towards the centre. If the angle subtended is Δθ, then |Δv| = v Δθ (for small angles).

a = |Δv| / Δt = v Δθ / Δt = vω = v² / r

Since Δv always points towards the centre, the acceleration is centripetal (centre-seeking).

Worked Example

Given: mass m = 0.50 kg, radius r = 1.2 m, speed v = 3.0 m/s
Step 1: a = v² / r = 9.0 / 1.2 = 7.5 m/s²
Step 2: F = ma = 0.50 × 7.5 = 3.75 N
Step 3: ω = v / r = 3.0 / 1.2 = 2.5 rad/s
Step 4: T = 2π / ω = 6.283 / 2.5 = 2.51 s

4. The Conical Pendulum

A Mass Tracing a Horizontal Circle

A conical pendulum consists of a mass on the end of a string, swinging in a horizontal circle so that the string traces out a cone. Resolving the tension T in the string into horizontal and vertical components:

Vertical equilibrium:   T cos θ = mg
Horizontal (centripetal):   T sin θ = mv² / r

Dividing the second equation by the first gives tan θ = v² / (rg). Since r = L sin θ (where L is the string length), we can find the period:

T = 2π √(L cos θ / g)

5. Vertical Circles

Motion in a Vertical Loop

When an object moves in a vertical circle, gravity assists the motion on the way down and opposes it on the way up. At an angle θ measured from the top of the circle, the tension in the string (or normal reaction) is:

T = mv² / r − mg cos θ    (θ from top; cos θ = +1 at top, −1 at bottom)

At the top of the loop (θ = 0), the critical condition for maintaining contact is T ≥ 0. Setting T = 0 gives the minimum speed at the top:

vmin² = gr   ⇒   vmin = √(gr)

At the bottom (θ = π), the tension is at its maximum: T = mv²/r + mg.

6. Banked Curves

Turning Without Friction

A banked road or track is tilted so that the horizontal component of the normal reaction provides the centripetal force. For a curve of radius r at the ideal speed (no friction needed):

Vertical:   N cos θ = mg
Horizontal:   N sin θ = mv² / r

Dividing:   tan θ = v² / (rg)  ⇒  videal = √(rg tan θ)

At the ideal speed, the car needs no friction at all to negotiate the curve. If the car travels faster, friction acts down the slope; if slower, friction acts up the slope.

▶ Safe speed range with friction (coefficient μ)

With friction coefficient μ, the maximum and minimum safe speeds are:

vmax = √[rg(tan θ + μ) / (1 − μ tan θ)]
vmin = √[rg(tan θ − μ) / (1 + μ tan θ)]   (if tan θ > μ, otherwise vmin = 0)

These come from resolving forces parallel and perpendicular to the banked surface, with friction acting in the appropriate direction.

Worked Example

Given: bank angle θ = 20°, radius r = 200 m, μ = 0.30
Step 1: videal = √(200 × 9.81 × tan 20°) = √(714.1) = 26.7 m/s (96.2 km/h)
Step 2: vmax = √[200 × 9.81 × (0.364 + 0.30) / (1 − 0.30 × 0.364)]
          = √[200 × 9.81 × 0.664 / 0.891] = √(1461) = 38.2 m/s (137.6 km/h)
Step 3: vmin = √[200 × 9.81 × (0.364 − 0.30) / (1 + 0.30 × 0.364)]
          = √[200 × 9.81 × 0.0636 / 1.109] = √(112.5) = 10.6 m/s (38.2 km/h)

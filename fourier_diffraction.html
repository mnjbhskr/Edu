Fourier Analysis in Diffraction

How crystals decompose X-rays into reciprocal-space harmonics

Chapter 17 · Solid State Physics · Topic 2 · Difficulty 3

When an X-ray beam strikes a crystal, each atom scatters a tiny fraction of the wave. The scattered wavelets interfere — constructively at special angles, destructively everywhere else — producing a diffraction pattern that is, mathematically, the Fourier transform of the crystal’s electron density. Understanding this connection lets us work backwards: from the pattern of bright spots, we reconstruct the atomic arrangement inside the crystal.

F(G) = ∑j fj · eiG·rj     (Structure Factor)
The conceptual chain: Atoms → electron density field ρ(r) → Fourier transform → diffraction spots. The diffraction pattern is literally the Fourier transform of the electron density. Each bright spot’s position encodes a spatial frequency present in the crystal, and its intensity encodes how strongly that frequency contributes to the structure. For a deeper exploration of how the Fourier transform itself works, see Fourier Transform.

Key insight: The diffraction pattern is the Fourier transform of the lattice. Real space and reciprocal space are inversely related: large lattice spacing produces tightly spaced diffraction spots, and vice versa. This reciprocal relationship is the foundation of all crystallographic analysis.
The inverse problem: Crystallography is the inverse problem: from the Fourier transform (the diffraction pattern) we must infer the original structure. This is why the “phase problem” — the loss of phase information in measured intensities — is one of the central challenges of crystallography. Detectors record only |F(G)|², discarding the complex phase angles needed to reconstruct ρ(r) by inverse Fourier transform.

Bragg’s Law:   nλ = 2d sin(θ)    where n = 1, 2, 3, …
Did you know? In 1912, Max von Laue discovered X-ray diffraction, confirming that crystals are periodic structures and that X-rays are electromagnetic waves. He won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1914 — just two years later. William and Lawrence Bragg (father and son) then developed Bragg’s Law, winning their own Nobel Prize in 1915. Lawrence Bragg remains the youngest-ever Nobel laureate in Physics — he was just 25 years old.

Toggle between structures to see how the basis atoms cause certain reflections to vanish. The structure factor F(hkl) determines peak intensity: when F = 0, that reflection is systematically absent.
Structure: SC · All (hkl) reflections allowed
Key insight: BCC crystals forbid reflections where h+k+l is odd, because waves from the body-centre atom cancel those from the corners. FCC crystals allow only reflections where h, k, l are all odd or all even. These “selection rules” let crystallographers identify the lattice type from the diffraction pattern alone.

Reading a Powder Pattern
In a powder sample, millions of tiny crystallites are oriented randomly. This means every possible Bragg angle is sampled simultaneously, producing a set of concentric cones (Debye–Scherrer rings). When projected onto a detector, we record intensity as a function of 2θ. The positions of the peaks encode the lattice parameters; their relative intensities carry information about the atomic arrangement within the unit cell.

Centres of Mass

●●●● Advanced

Finding the balance point of composite bodies and exploring when objects topple or slide

1. Introduction

What Is the Centre of Mass?

The centre of mass is the balance point of a body — the single point at which the entire mass can be considered to act. If you could support an object at its centre of mass, it would balance perfectly in any orientation.

For uniform bodies (where mass is evenly distributed), the centre of mass coincides with the geometric centre. A uniform rod balances at its midpoint; a uniform square lamina balances at the intersection of its diagonals.

For composite bodies — objects made from several simpler shapes joined together — the centre of mass is found by taking a weighted average of the individual centres, where each is weighted by its mass.

The centre of mass is fundamental to mechanics. It determines whether a body topples or remains stable, how it responds to forces, and the trajectory it follows when thrown. A high jumper arches their body so that their centre of mass passes under the bar even as their body goes over it.

2. Formulae

Discrete and Continuous Bodies

For a system of n particles with masses m1, m2, …, mn at positions (x1, y1), (x2, y2), …, (xn, yn):

x̄ = Σ mixi / Σ mi      ȳ = Σ miyi / Σ mi

For a continuous body with variable density ρ(x, y):

x̄ = ∫ x · dm / ∫ dm      ȳ = ∫ y · dm / ∫ dm

▶ Derivation: semicircle centre of mass

Consider a uniform semicircular lamina of radius r with the diameter along the x-axis. By symmetry, x̄ = 0. For ȳ, we integrate over horizontal strips.

Strip at height y: width = 2√(r² − y²), thickness = dy
Area of strip: dA = 2√(r² − y²) dy
Total area: A = πr²/2

ȳ = (1/A) ∫0r y · 2√(r² − y²) dy
   = (2/πr²) · [−⅔(r² − y²)3/2]0r
   = (2/πr²) · (2r³/3) = 4r / (3π)

3. Composite Bodies

The Add / Subtract Approach

Many real shapes can be decomposed into simpler components whose centres of mass are known. The strategy is straightforward: treat each component as a particle located at its own centre of mass, with mass proportional to its area (for uniform laminae).

For shapes with holes, use subtraction: treat the removed region as having negative mass and apply the same weighted-average formula.

Worked Example: L-Shaped Lamina

A uniform L-shaped lamina is formed from a 6 × 4 rectangle with a 3 × 2 rectangle removed from the top-right corner. Find the centre of mass.

Step 1: Full rectangle — area A1 = 6 × 4 = 24, centre at (3, 2)
Step 2: Removed rectangle — area A2 = 3 × 2 = 6, centre at (4.5, 3)
Step 3: Composite area = 24 − 6 = 18
Step 4: x̄ = (24×3 − 6×4.5) / 18 = (72 − 27) / 18 = 2.50
Step 5: ȳ = (24×2 − 6×3) / 18 = (48 − 18) / 18 = 1.67
Centre of mass: (2.50, 1.67)
Notice the centre of mass shifts away from the removed corner. The more material you remove, the further the balance point moves towards the remaining material. This principle is used in engineering to design stable structures.

5. Toppling & Sliding

When Does a Body Topple or Slide?

Place a block on an inclined plane and gradually increase the angle. Two things can happen:

Toppling occurs when the vertical line through the centre of mass falls beyond the base edge in contact with the plane. At this point the weight creates a net turning moment about the lower edge, and the body rotates.

Toppling condition: tan(θ) ≥ base width / (2 × height)   ⇒   θtopple = arctan(b / (2h))

Sliding occurs when the component of weight along the slope exceeds the maximum friction force. For a block on a rough plane with coefficient of friction μ:

Sliding condition: mg sin(θ) ≥ μ mg cos(θ)   ⇒   θslide = arctan(μ)
Whichever critical angle is smaller determines what happens first. Tall, narrow objects tend to topple; short, wide objects on smooth surfaces tend to slide. The interplay depends on the block’s aspect ratio and the friction coefficient.

6. Worked Examples

Problem 1: T-Shaped Lamina

A uniform T-shaped lamina is formed from two rectangles: a horizontal top piece measuring 8 cm × 2 cm, and a vertical stem measuring 2 cm × 6 cm. The stem is centred below the top piece. Find the distance of the centre of mass from the base.

▶ Show solution

Take the origin at the bottom-centre of the stem.

Top piece: area = 8 × 2 = 16 cm²
  Centre at y = 6 + 1 = 7 cm from base

Stem: area = 2 × 6 = 12 cm²
  Centre at y = 3 cm from base

ȳ = (16 × 7 + 12 × 3) / (16 + 12)
  = (112 + 36) / 28
  = 148 / 28 = 5.29 cm from the base

By symmetry, x̄ = 0 (centre of the stem). The centre of mass is 5.29 cm above the base, along the axis of symmetry.

Problem 2: Disc with Circular Hole

A uniform circular disc of radius 4 cm has a circular hole of radius 1 cm cut from it. The centre of the hole is 2 cm from the centre of the disc. Find the distance of the centre of mass from the centre of the original disc.

▶ Show solution

Take the origin at the centre of the full disc. The hole centre is at x = 2 cm along the x-axis.

Full disc: area A1 = π(4)² = 16π cm², centre at x = 0
Hole: area A2 = π(1)² = π cm², centre at x = 2

Using subtraction:
x̄ = (16π × 0 − π × 2) / (16π − π)
  = −2π / 15π
  = −2/15 cm ≈ −0.133 cm

The centre of mass is 2/15 cm ≈ 0.133 cm from the disc centre, on the opposite side from the hole. This makes physical sense: removing material shifts the balance point away from the hole.

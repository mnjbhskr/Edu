Entropy and Information Theory

Disorder, uncertainty, and why they are the same mathematical idea
Chapter 12 · Physics · Topic 11 · Difficulty: Intermediate

Entropy appears in two quite different fields: thermodynamics and information theory. Boltzmann wrote S = kB ln Ω in 1877, where Ω is the number of microscopic configurations consistent with what we observe. Shannon wrote H = −Σ pi log2 pi in 1948 to measure the uncertainty in a message. The formulas are identical in structure — and this is not a coincidence.

Insight: The Second Law is probability, not an absolute law. With N = 64 particles, the chance of all being on one side is (½)64 ≈ 5 × 10−20. There is nothing preventing it — it is simply so vastly improbable that you would never observe it. Entropy increases because there are overwhelmingly more high-entropy configurations than low-entropy ones.

Entropy is missing information — it measures what we do not know about the microscopic state of a system. A low-entropy state (all particles on one side) is one we can almost fully describe. A high-entropy state (particles spread everywhere) is one where our macroscopic description leaves almost everything unspecified.

A fair coin has H = 1 bit — meaning exactly one yes/no question is needed to determine the outcome. Entropy measures how many binary questions you need, on average, to learn the result.

Insight: Uniform distribution gives maximum uncertainty (H = Hmax). A peaked distribution yields low H, meaning you already know most of the outcome. Data compression exploits this: frequent symbols get short codes (Huffman coding), rare symbols get long ones.

AI connection: Modern language models minimise cross-entropy loss: L = −Σ p(x) log q(x), which is directly Shannon entropy applied to predicted vs actual word distributions — where p(x) is the true distribution and q(x) is the model’s predicted distribution. Insight: The same mathematical structure was discovered 71 years apart. When Shannon showed his formula to von Neumann, von Neumann said “call it entropy — nobody knows what entropy really is, so in a debate you will always have the advantage.” Concretely: 1 bit of information corresponds to kB ln 2 ≈ 9.57 × 10−24 J/K of thermodynamic entropy. The Second Law restated in information terms: microscopic information about particle positions is spontaneously and irreversibly lost.
The Mathematics of Disorder and Knowledge

Boltzmann’s insight (1877). How can macroscopic irreversibility — hot objects cool, gases expand, eggs break — arise from microscopic laws of motion that are perfectly time-reversible? Boltzmann’s answer was statistical. He defined entropy as S = kB ln Ω, where Ω counts the number of microscopic arrangements (microstates) compatible with the observed macroscopic state. The Second Law is not a statement about individual particles; it is a statement about combinatorics. There are simply so many more high-entropy configurations than low-entropy ones that any random evolution of the system overwhelmingly tends toward them.

Shannon’s entropy (1948). Claude Shannon, working on the mathematics of communication at Bell Labs, asked: how much information is in a message? If an event has probability p, learning that it occurred conveys −log2(p) bits of information — rare events are more surprising. The average surprise over a distribution is H = −Σ pi log2 pi, the Shannon entropy. It is simultaneously a measure of uncertainty before the message and information content after. Shannon entropy underlies data compression (Huffman codes, gzip), channel capacity, cryptographic strength, and the cross-entropy loss function used to train language models.

The Gibbs form as the unifying bridge. Josiah Willard Gibbs had already generalised Boltzmann’s formula to systems with non-uniform state probabilities: SGibbs = −kB Σ pi ln pi. This is structurally identical to Shannon’s H, with the change of base from log2 to ln introducing a factor of ln 2, giving S = kB ln(2) × H. The two quantities measure the same abstract object — missing information — in different units. A nat (natural unit of information, base e) is to a bit (base 2) as kB is to kB ln 2.

Landauer’s principle and the physics of computation. If entropy is information, then erasing information has a physical cost. Rolf Landauer proved in 1961 that erasing one bit of information dissipates at least kB T ln 2 of energy as heat. At room temperature (T ≈ 293 K) this is about 2.8 × 10−21 J — tiny by human standards, but a fundamental thermodynamic limit. It explains why Maxwell’s Demon (a hypothetical creature that sorts particles to decrease entropy) cannot violate the Second Law: the demon must store and eventually erase its observations, paying the entropy cost. Modern processors are still many orders of magnitude above this limit, but as transistors shrink, Landauer’s bound becomes an engineering constraint.

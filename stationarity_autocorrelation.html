Stationarity & Autocorrelation

●●●● Intermediate

Understanding the memory of time series — from white noise to random walks

1. Three Time Series Compared

White noise has no memory. Stationary AR(1) has decaying memory. A random walk has infinite memory. Their ACF and PACF patterns are diagnostic.

Pure AR(p): PACF cuts off after lag p, ACF decays. Pure MA(q): ACF cuts off after lag q, PACF decays. ARMA: both decay. This is the Box-Jenkins identification strategy.
Formal definitions of stationarity
Strict stationarity: The joint distribution of (yt, yt+1, ..., yt+k) does not depend on t for any k.
Weak (covariance) stationarity: (1) E[yt] = μ is constant; (2) Var(yt) = σ² is constant; (3) Cov(yt, yt+h) = γ(h) depends only on the lag h, not on t.
White noise: yt is weakly stationary with γ(h) = 0 for all h ≠ 0. It is the building block of all ARMA processes.
Random walk: yt = yt−1 + εt. Var(yt) = tσ² grows without bound — not stationary.

Monte Carlo: ACF under different AR coefficients

For each φ from 0.1 to 0.95, generate 500 AR(1) series and compute the average sample ACF at lag 1. Compare with the theoretical value γ(1) = φ.

3. Spurious Regression

Two completely independent random walks regressed on each other often produce high R² and significant t-statistics. This is the Granger-Newbold problem — the regression is meaningless.

Credit Derivatives: Spread to Upfront

●●●● Advanced

From running spreads to standardised coupons — how the CDS market changed after 2009

1. Historical Background

Before 2009, credit default swaps were quoted as running par spreads. A CDS contract was struck at a spread that made its present value exactly zero at inception — the protection buyer paid a periodic premium, and no money changed hands up front. If a name traded at 250 basis points, that meant a quarterly payment of 62.5bp per year of notional for the life of the contract.

The 2008 financial crisis exposed the fragility of this system. With thousands of bespoke contracts outstanding, netting was nearly impossible, and counterparty exposure was opaque. In April 2009, ISDA’s “Big Bang” protocol standardised North American CDS to fixed coupons of 100bp or 500bp with an upfront payment to compensate the difference. Three months later, the “Small Bang” extended the same convention to European contracts.

The market question shifted: from “what spread makes this zero-cost?” to “what upfront payment, given this standard coupon, makes this zero-cost?”

Why standardisation happened. When every CDS has a bespoke coupon, netting bilateral exposures is a nightmare — two contracts on the same reference entity with different spreads cannot simply offset. Standardised coupons enabled multilateral netting, central clearing through CCPs, and dramatically improved transparency. The upfront payment is the price of that simplicity.

2. Survival Probability Framework

From Hazard Rate to Survival

The hazard rate h(t) is the instantaneous conditional probability of default at time t, given survival to t. The survival probability Q(t) — the probability that the reference entity has not defaulted by time t — is derived from the hazard rate:

Q(t) = exp(−∫0t h(s) ds)

In practice, we model the hazard rate as piecewise constant between CDS tenors. If h(s) = hi for ti ≤ s < ti+1, then:

Q(ti+1) = Q(ti) × exp(−hi × (ti+1 − ti))
▶ Full derivation of survival probability from hazard rate

Define the default time τ as a non-negative random variable. The survival function is Q(t) = P(τ > t). The hazard rate is defined as:

h(t) = limΔt→0 P(τ ≤ t + Δt | τ > t) / Δt = −Q′(t) / Q(t)

This is a first-order ODE. Separating variables:

dQ / Q = −h(t) dt
ln Q(t) − ln Q(0) = −∫0t h(s) ds
Q(t) = exp(−∫0t h(s) ds)

since Q(0) = 1 (certainty of survival at time zero). When h is piecewise constant at level hi on [ti, ti+1), the integral becomes a sum of rectangles, giving the product formula above.

3. Par Spread Formula

Equating the Two Legs

A CDS has two legs. The protection leg pays (1 − R) at default, where R is the recovery rate. The premium leg pays a periodic spread s. The par spread is the value of s that equates the two legs at inception:

spar = Protection Leg PV / RPV01

where:

Protection Leg PV = (1 − R) × Σ D(ti) × [Q(ti−1) − Q(ti)]
RPV01 (Risky Annuity) = Σ D(ti) × Q(ti) × Δti

D(t) = exp(−r × t) is the risk-free discount factor. R is the recovery rate, standardly assumed to be 40% for senior unsecured debt. The sums run over quarterly payment dates (market convention: Δt = 0.25).

▶ Derivation of both legs

Protection leg. The protection buyer receives (1 − R) × Notional if default occurs. Discretising time into small intervals [ti−1, ti], the probability of default in that interval is Q(ti−1) − Q(ti). Discounting back:

PVprot = (1 − R) × Σi D(ti) × [Q(ti−1) − Q(ti)]

Premium leg. The protection buyer pays spread s at each period end, conditional on survival. The expected discounted premium per unit spread is the risky annuity (RPV01):

PVprem = s × Σi D(ti) × Q(ti) × Δti = s × RPV01

Setting PVprot = PVprem and solving for s gives spar = PVprot / RPV01.

4. Spread-to-Upfront Conversion

The Key Formula

Under the standardised convention, the contract carries a fixed coupon Cstd (100bp or 500bp). The upfront payment U compensates for the difference between the par spread and the standard coupon:

U = (spar − Cstd) × RPV01

Positive U: the protection buyer pays upfront (par spread exceeds the standard coupon — the buyer is getting “too low” a running premium relative to fair value).
Negative U: the protection seller pays upfront (par spread is below the standard coupon — the buyer is overpaying in running premium).

In practice, an accrued interest adjustment is also applied: the buyer pays the accrued premium from the previous coupon date to the settlement date. This ensures the first coupon payment covers only the period from settlement to the next coupon date.

Worked Example

Given: 5y CDS, spar = 250 bp, Cstd = 100 bp, RPV01 ≈ 4.25
Step 1: Spread excess = 250 − 100 = 150 bp = 0.0150
Step 2: U = 0.0150 × 4.25 = 0.06375 = 6.375% of notional
On £10m: £637,500 upfront (buyer pays)

8. Where It Connects

Building Blocks & Extensions

Bootstrapping. The hazard rate bootstrap in Section 6 is the credit-curve analogue of the discount factor bootstrap covered in MC-7. Both use sequential root-finding to extract implied term structures from market quotes.

Day counts. CDS premiums accrue on an Act/360 basis — the same convention covered in MC-1. The quarterly payment schedule uses Modified Following business day conventions.

Bond pricing. Credit spreads are closely related to bond credit spreads. The Z-spread of a bond and the CDS spread on the same issuer are linked through the bond pricing framework.

Yield curves. The risk-free discount factors used throughout CDS pricing are derived from the yield curve.

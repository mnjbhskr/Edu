Reinforcement Learning

Learning through trial, error, and reward

Intermediate

How does an agent learn to navigate a world it knows nothing about? Not by being told the answer, but by trying things, receiving rewards or penalties, and gradually building a map of what works. Reinforcement learning is the mathematics of learning from consequences — the same principle behind how children learn to walk, how AlphaGo mastered Go, and how robots learn to grasp objects.

Summary

Reinforcement learning is how agents learn from consequences rather than from labelled examples. The grid world gives us a concrete playground: the agent bumps into walls, falls into traps, and eventually finds the goal. Q-learning provides the mechanism — updating value estimates from experience until the optimal path emerges.

The exploration-exploitation dilemma is not unique to RL — it appears whenever you must choose between gathering information and acting on what you already know. From clinical trials to restaurant choices to AlphaGo’s game-changing moves, the same tension applies. Understanding it here gives you a lens for decision-making far beyond grid worlds.

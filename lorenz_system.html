Edward Lorenz discovered this system in 1963 whilst modelling atmospheric convection. With the classic parameters σ=10, ρ=28, β=8/3, the trajectory never settles — it traces a butterfly-shaped attractor forever.

5 — What Have We Learnt?

Deterministic Chaos
The Lorenz system is deterministic — given exact initial conditions, the future is completely determined. Yet in practice, it behaves unpredictably because infinitesimal differences in starting conditions grow exponentially. This is the essence of deterministic chaos.
Strange Attractors
The trajectory never repeats, yet it remains confined to a bounded region of state space — the strange attractor. It has a fractal structure with dimension ≈2.06, meaning it is geometrically more complex than any surface but does not fill a volume.
The Butterfly Effect
Lorenz’s 1963 paper, “Deterministic Nonperiodic Flow”, revealed that even a simple three-equation model of convection could exhibit sensitive dependence on initial conditions. His later talk title — “Does the flap of a butterfly’s wings in Brazil set off a tornado in Texas?” — gave chaos theory its most famous metaphor.
Connections
The Lorenz system appears throughout science: weather and climate modelling (its original purpose), fluid turbulence, laser dynamics, chemical reactions, and even financial markets where similar nonlinear feedback mechanisms create unpredictable dynamics. The mathematics of chaos — Lyapunov exponents, fractal dimensions, bifurcation theory — provides the tools to characterise such systems even when long-term prediction is impossible.

Information & Entropy

Why a coin flip carries more information than a certain outcome — and what this has to do with your data

Core idea Shannon entropy measures how much uncertainty a distribution contains — and therefore how much information a representation must carry to be faithful.

1. Information as Surprise — The Arithmetic of Uncertainty

Information in outcome x:   I(x) = −log₂ p(x)   [bits]

"Certain outcome" (p=1):    I = −log₂(1) = 0 bits   ← no surprise
"Fair coin" (p=0.5):        I = −log₂(0.5) = 1 bit
"Rare event" (p=0.01):      I = −log₂(0.01) ≈ 6.64 bits

Shannon Entropy:   H(X) = −Σ p(x) log₂ p(x)   [average information]

H is maximised when all outcomes are equally likely:
    H_max = log₂ n  for n equally likely outcomes

H = 0 when one outcome is certain.
💡 Entropy is the average amount of surprise in a distribution. A weather forecast that says "100% chance of rain" carries zero bits of information — you already know exactly what will happen. A forecast that says "50% chance" carries 1 bit. Shannon showed that entropy is the fundamental limit on how much a distribution can be compressed.
Precision note — entropy vs. useful information
High entropy does not necessarily mean useful information. A sequence of random coin flips has maximum entropy — but it tells you nothing about the world. What matters for representation is structured information: uncertainty that is correlated with something you care about. This is captured by mutual information I(X;Y) — how much knowing X reduces uncertainty about Y. When choosing which dimensions of your data to represent, the question is not 'which dimensions have highest entropy?' but 'which dimensions have highest mutual information with the question I am trying to answer?' Entropy sets the upper bound on compressibility; mutual information determines usefulness.

2. Mutual Information — What One Variable Says About Another

MUTUAL INFORMATION EXPLORER

+0.60
Pearson r = +0.60    I(X;Y) = 0.32 bits
Mutual Information:
    I(X;Y) = H(X) + H(Y) − H(X,Y)
           = H(X) − H(X|Y)

For a bivariate Gaussian with correlation ρ:
    I(X;Y) = −½ log₂(1 − ρ²)

Properties:
    I(X;Y) ≥ 0       always non-negative
    I(X;Y) = 0         iff X and Y are independent
    I(X;Y) = H(X)      iff Y completely determines X

Unlike Pearson correlation, I(X;Y) detects ANY statistical dependence.
💡 Pearson correlation only detects linear relationships. Mutual information detects any statistical dependence — linear, quadratic, periodic, or otherwise. Switch to the quadratic or sinusoidal mode: Pearson r collapses towards zero, yet I(X;Y) remains clearly positive. This is why I(X;Y) is used in feature selection for machine learning.

3. Huffman Coding — Entropy as Compression Limit

HUFFMAN CODING VISUALISER

String: AABABCABD    9 chars × 3 bits = 27 bits (fixed)
Shannon's Source Coding Theorem:
    Average code length L ≥ H(X)

Huffman coding is optimal among prefix codes — it achieves the
lower bound to within one bit:
    H(X) ≤ L_Huffman < H(X) + 1

For "AABABCABD" (A:4, B:3, C:1, D:1):
    p = (4/9, 3/9, 1/9, 1/9)
    H = ≈ 1.75 bits/symbol

Fixed-length encoding: 2 bits/symbol (for 4 symbols) or 3 bits as shown.
Huffman encoding approaches H — you cannot do better.
💡 Huffman coding assigns shorter codes to more frequent symbols, achieving near-optimal compression. Shannon proved that the average code length can never be shorter than the entropy — it is a fundamental lower bound, not a practical limitation. When you choose a representation for data, you are implicitly making a compression decision: what to preserve (high-entropy dimensions) and what to discard.
What have we learnt?
Information theory gives us a mathematical framework for reasoning about the value of data. Shannon entropy measures how much uncertainty a distribution contains — and therefore how much information a faithful representation must carry. Mutual information tells us how much two variables share, independently of whether their relationship is linear. Huffman coding shows that entropy is not an abstract quantity: it is the precise lower bound on how compactly a source can be encoded. When we choose a representation — whether to include a variable, which dimensions to project onto, which features to keep — we are making information-theoretic decisions. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

Conservation of Momentum

Collisions, rockets, and the law that no force can break

Momentum — the product of mass and velocity — is the quantity that force changes over time. Newton’s Second Law says dp/dt = F, which means that when two objects interact with no external force, their total momentum cannot change. This law, encoded in F12 = −F21, is why rockets work in the vacuum of space.

Key insight: When m₁ = m₂ the velocities exchange exactly — the teal ball stops and the amber ball takes over. When m₁ ≫ m₂ the heavy ball barely slows, while the light ball rebounds at nearly twice the heavy ball’s speed. KE is conserved in elastic collisions: no energy is lost to heat or deformation.

In the centre-of-mass frame, elastic collisions simply reverse the velocities of both particles. This is why equal-mass collisions exchange velocities — the centre-of-mass frame reduces every elastic collision to a simple reflection.

This is the physics behind billiards: when the cue ball hits a stationary ball of equal mass head-on, the cue ball stops dead and the target ball moves off at the cue ball’s original speed. Newton’s cradle demonstrates the same principle with a chain of equal-mass spheres.

Key insight: The coefficient of restitution e smoothly interpolates between perfectly inelastic (e = 0, balls stick together, maximum KE loss) and perfectly elastic (e = 1, no KE loss). Momentum is always conserved; kinetic energy is not. The lost KE goes into heat, sound, and permanent deformation.

Think of e as a bounciness dial: e = 1 means perfectly bouncy (superball), e = 0 means perfectly sticky (putty). Most real collisions fall somewhere in between — a tennis ball on hard court has e ≈ 0.75, a bean bag has e ≈ 0.05.

The Mathematics of Collisions

Momentum conservation is a direct consequence of Newton’s Third Law. When ball 1 pushes on ball 2, ball 2 pushes back with an equal and opposite force: F12 = −F21. Integrating over the collision time gives impulse, and since the impulses are equal and opposite, the total change in momentum is exactly zero: Δptotal = 0. No external force — no change in total momentum.

For a two-body elastic collision we have two conservation laws — momentum and kinetic energy — giving two equations for two unknowns (the final velocities). The solution is unique and produces the familiar velocity-exchange formulas shown in Section 1. When the collision is inelastic, energy conservation no longer constrains the outcome; the coefficient of restitution e encodes empirically how much relative velocity is preserved. Setting e = 1 recovers the elastic result; e = 0 gives the perfectly inelastic case where the balls stick together.

The reduced mass μ = m₁m₂/(m₁+m₂) simplifies collision analysis: the relative motion behaves exactly like a single particle of mass μ hitting a fixed wall. The kinetic energy lost in an inelastic collision is ΔKE = ½μvrel²(1−e²), where vrel = v₁−v₂ is the relative approach speed. When e = 0 this gives the maximum possible energy loss consistent with momentum conservation.

The rocket equation is the continuous-mass limit of collision physics. At each instant the rocket ejects a small mass dm of exhaust at speed vₑ relative to itself. By momentum conservation: m·dv = −vₑ·dm. Integrating from initial mass m₀ to final mass mₑ yields Δv = vₑ ln(m₀/mₑ) — the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation (1903). The logarithm is inescapable and is why reaching orbit is fundamentally a propellant storage problem.

Magnetic Fields

●●●● Intermediate

Circular field lines, the Lorentz force, and the solenoid — magnetism visualised

Magnetic fields are invisible but ubiquitous — wrapping around every current-carrying wire in concentric circles, curving the paths of cosmic ray particles through the atmosphere, and making MRI scanners possible. The key equations are simple: B = μ₀I/(2πr) for a long wire, and F = qv × B for the force on a moving charge. The cross product in the force law is why charged particles orbit in circles.

B falls as 1/r (not 1/r²) because the wire extends infinitely in one dimension — the 3-D geometry reduces by one power of r. The field lines are closed loops (∇·B = 0), never starting or ending on a source.

Magnetic force does no work: The magnetic force is always perpendicular to velocity: F · v = 0. This means the magnetic field changes direction but never speed — it does no work on the particle. The kinetic energy remains constant throughout the orbit.
The cyclotron period T = 2πm/(qB) is independent of speed — faster particles travel larger circles in exactly the same time. This is the principle behind cyclotron accelerators and MRI scanners.
Real-world applications: Cyclotrons use circular orbits to accelerate particles to near light speed for cancer therapy and nuclear research. The aurora borealis occurs when solar wind particles spiral along Earth’s magnetic field lines into the atmosphere, exciting air molecules to glow.

The solenoid produces the most uniform accessible magnetic field. Doubling the winding density n = N/L doubles B instantaneously. Ampère’s law shows that the return paths outside cancel, driving B → 0 outside an ideal (infinite) solenoid.
The Mathematics of Magnetism

Magnetism is not a separate phenomenon from electricity — it is electricity viewed from a moving frame of reference. A wire carrying current creates a magnetic field purely because of the relativistic contraction of charge densities between frames. Einstein’s special relativity shows that what one observer calls a pure electric force, another moving observer necessarily experiences as a magnetic force. The two fields are facets of a single electromagnetic tensor.

The Biot–Savart law, dB = (μ₀/4π) I dl × r̂/r², is the magnetic analogue of Coulomb’s law: it gives the infinitesimal B contribution from each current element. For an infinite straight wire the integral collapses to B = μ₀I/(2πr), falling as 1/r rather than 1/r² because the source is one-dimensional. Ampère’s law, ∮ B · dl = μ₀Ienc, gives the same result far more efficiently for symmetric geometries.

The Lorentz force F = q(v × B) is a cross product, so F is always perpendicular to v. Perpendicular force means no component along the velocity — the magnetic force does zero work. The particle’s speed stays constant; only its direction changes. The resulting circular motion has radius r = mv/(qB) and period T = 2πm/(qB). The period’s independence from speed is the insight that makes particle accelerators and MRI scanners practical.

The solenoid, analysed via Ampère’s law, is the bridge from magnetostatics to Maxwell’s full equations. Maxwell added the displacement current term μ₀ε₀ ∂E/∂t to Ampère’s law, making it consistent with charge conservation and predicting that oscillating E and B fields would propagate at 1/√(μ₀ε₀) = c. Maxwell recognised that value as the speed of light, unifying optics and electromagnetism. See also: Electric Field Lines, Physics of Light, and Maxwell’s Equations.

Try This

A wire carries 10A of current. At what distance does the magnetic field equal Earth’s field (~50 μT)?

B = μ₀I/(2πr). So r = μ₀I/(2πB) = (4π×10⁻&sup7; × 10)/(2π × 50×10⁻&sup6;) = 0.04m = 4cm. At just 4cm from a household wire, its magnetic field matches Earth’s! This is why transformer coils and motors work — concentrated current creates substantial fields.

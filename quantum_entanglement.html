Quantum Entanglement

Bell’s theorem, non-locality, and quantum correlations

When two particles are entangled, measuring one instantly constrains what you’ll find when you measure the other — no matter the distance between them. Einstein called it “spooky action at a distance.” Bell’s theorem proved that no local hidden variable theory can reproduce the correlations predicted by quantum mechanics. Here you can run Bell test experiments yourself and see why.

The CHSH inequality states |S| ≤ 2 for any local hidden variable theory. Quantum mechanics reaches a maximum of |S| = 2√2 ≈ 2.828 when the four measurement angles are evenly spaced by 45°. This violation has been confirmed experimentally, ruling out local realism.

4. What Entanglement Is NOT

✗ “Entanglement sends information faster than light”

Measurement outcomes are random — you cannot encode a message in randomness. Alice’s result looks completely random to her; only when she compares with Bob do correlations appear. No usable signal travels faster than light.

✗ “Measuring one particle instantly changes the other”

Entanglement does not transmit a physical force or disturbance. Bob’s particle has no detectable change after Alice measures. The correlation is revealed only upon comparing results — which requires classical communication.

✗ “It’s just like matching socks in separate suitcases”

If it were pre-determined (like socks), correlations would obey Bell’s inequality. They don’t. The violation proves that outcomes are not pre-set — they are genuinely created at measurement, yet remain correlated. No classical analogy captures this.

✓ What entanglement actually is

Two particles share a joint quantum state that cannot be factored into independent states for each particle. Measurements on the pair exhibit correlations stronger than any local hidden variable model allows, but these correlations cannot be used to communicate. The universe is non-local in its correlations, yet respects the no-signalling principle.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Summary

  • Entangled pairs — Two particles in a joint quantum state show anti-correlated measurements. The probability of same outcomes is cos²(θ/2) for spin-½ singlets.
  • Bell’s theorem — No local hidden variable theory can reproduce all quantum mechanical predictions. The proof relies on an inequality that quantum correlations violate.
  • CHSH inequality — For any local realistic theory, the CHSH parameter |S| ≤ 2. Quantum mechanics achieves S = 2√2, the Tsirelson bound.
  • Non-locality without signalling — Quantum correlations are non-local but cannot transmit information, preserving special relativity.
  • Quantum computing — Entanglement is a key resource for quantum algorithms, enabling exponential speedups in certain problems and powering quantum error correction.
  • Quantum cryptography — The E91 protocol uses Bell test violations to guarantee secure key distribution — any eavesdropper necessarily disturbs the correlations.
Entanglement is not a loophole or a curiosity — it is a fundamental feature of nature. Bell’s theorem, confirmed by decades of experiments culminating in the 2022 Nobel Prize, tells us that the universe does not operate by local hidden variables. Quantum mechanics is non-local in a very precise, experimentally verified sense.

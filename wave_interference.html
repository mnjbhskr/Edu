When two waves of equal amplitude are exactly 180° out of phase, they cancel completely — destructive interference. This is how noise-cancelling headphones work: they generate an anti-phase copy of the incoming sound.

A standing wave is two travelling waves moving in opposite directions. The points where they always cancel (nodes) never move. Musical instruments produce standing waves to create specific pitches — the fundamental and its harmonics.
The Mathematics of Waves
Wave interference rests on the principle of superposition: when two waves occupy the same space, the total displacement is simply the sum of the individual displacements. This seemingly simple rule produces extraordinary complexity.

Trigonometric identities explain why: adding two sine waves of the same frequency but different phase gives sin(A) + sin(B) = 2 cos((A−B)/2) sin((A+B)/2). The cos factor determines the amplitude modulation — the interference pattern.

The Fourier transform generalises this: any wave, no matter how complex, can be decomposed into a sum of pure sinusoidal components. Interference is Fourier analysis made visible.

All of this follows from the wave equation: ∂²y/∂t² = v² ∂²y/∂x², a linear partial differential equation. Linearity is what makes superposition work.

Assumptions: linear medium (no nonlinear effects), no attenuation, no dispersion (wave speed independent of frequency).

Try This

Two speakers emit the same frequency. At a point equidistant from both, do you hear louder or quieter sound? What if one speaker is moved half a wavelength further away?

Equidistant: waves arrive in phase → constructive interference → louder (double amplitude, quadruple intensity). Half wavelength path difference: waves arrive exactly out of phase → destructive interference → silence! This is exactly how noise-cancelling headphones work — they produce an “anti-wave” that cancels the noise.

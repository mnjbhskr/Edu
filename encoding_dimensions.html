Encoding Extra Dimensions

Position is the most powerful encoding — and everything else is a compromise

Core idea Human perception has mathematical limits — and the choice of visual encoding channel determines how accurately those limits allow information to be decoded.

1. The Encoding Hierarchy — Six Channels, One Dataset

The Same Four Values, Rendered Six Ways

Answer each "Which is largest?" dropdown. Empirical perception studies place position at ~95–99% accuracy; angle, area, and 3D fall to 50–70%.
Weber's Law:    ΔI / I = k   (the just-noticeable difference is proportional to stimulus intensity)

Stevens' Power Law:   S = k · I^n
    n > 1:  area, volume       — people underestimate differences
    n ≈ 1:  length, position   — near-linear perception
    n < 1:  brightness, loudness — people overestimate small differences

Empirical exponents for visualisation:
    Area encoding:    perceived ∝ actual^0.87
    Volume encoding:  perceived ∝ actual^0.67

Cleveland & McGill hierarchy (most → least accurate):
    position > length > angle > area > volume > colour intensity
💡 A 3D bar chart is mathematically worse than a 2D bar chart because it adds a visual dimension — depth — that carries no additional information but introduces systematic perceptual bias. Every extra visual dimension that does not encode data is interference.

2. Colour as a Mathematical Channel

Sequential, Diverging, Categorical — and the Mismatches

💡 A sequential scale implies ordering; a diverging scale implies a meaningful midpoint; a categorical scale implies no order. Applying the wrong type is not just aesthetically weak — it imposes a false mathematical structure on the data.

3. When Is 3D Justified?

Three Cases — Good, Bad, Alternative

Case A · Justified
A loss surface f(x,y) = x² + 0.3xy + y². The 3D wireframe is the insight — the minimum and the curvature of the bowl only make sense in three axes.
Case B · Unjustified
A 1D data series rendered as 3D bars. Depth encodes nothing; rear bars occlude front bars; perspective shrinks distant values. Two wasted pixels for every meaningful one.
Case C · Alternative
Small multiples: the same data faceted as four side-by-side 2D plots. Position does the encoding; comparison is exact; no occlusion. Often mathematically superior to any 3D option.
💡 3D is justified when the third axis carries independent information that the reader must decode — a loss surface, a phase diagram, a molecular structure. It is never justified as decoration for 1D or 2D data.
What have we learnt?
Visual encoding is constrained by human perception, and human perception is a mathematical subject. Weber's law tells us that perceptual thresholds scale logarithmically. Stevens' power law tells us that area and volume are systematically underestimated. The visual channel hierarchy — position, then length, then angle, then area, then colour — is not a design preference but an empirical measurement of decoding accuracy. Colour scales impose mathematical structure: sequential implies order, diverging implies a midpoint, categorical implies no order. A 3D chart adds a dimension only when the data genuinely needs one; otherwise it is interference dressed as insight. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

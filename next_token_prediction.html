Next-Token Prediction

Everything an LLM does reduces to one question: what comes next?

A large language model does not think, plan, or reason in the way humans do. At every step it performs a single mathematical operation: given all the tokens so far, compute a probability distribution over every possible next token, then sample from that distribution. The entire miracle of fluent text generation — from poetry to code to medical diagnosis — emerges from this one act repeated thousands of times.

temperature: P(wi) = exp(logiti / τ) / Σ exp(logitj / τ)
An LLM doesn’t choose the right answer — it samples from a probability distribution. At temperature 1.0, GPT-4 is literally rolling a very sophisticated weighted die.
Temperature extremes — the entropy connection:
At temperature τ → 0:
Entropy → 0 bits
Perplexity → 1
Behaviour: always picks the single most likely token (greedy decoding)
At temperature τ → ∞:
Entropy → log2(V) bits  (V = vocabulary size)
Perplexity → V
Behaviour: uniform random selection from entire vocabulary

Language generation is repeated sampling from a conditional probability distribution — nothing more, nothing less.

H = −Σ p(xi) × log2 p(xi)
perplexity = 2H
Cross-entropy loss = −log2 P(true token)
If the model assigns P = 0.9 to the correct next token:  loss = 0.15 bits
If the model assigns P = 0.1 to the correct next token:  loss = 3.32 bits

This is exactly the training objective. Every step of gradient descent reduces this number across billions of examples.

Perplexity is how we measure language model quality. A perplexity of 8 means the model is, on average, as confused as if it were choosing between 8 equally likely next tokens.

Entropy measures surprise. Low entropy = confident prediction. High entropy = the model is guessing.

3. Hallucination — The Mathematical View

Error point

Watch how one wrong token shifts all subsequent probabilities, producing fluent but incorrect text.

correct: “Paris is the capital of France” ← P ≈ 0.92
hallucination: “Paris is the capital of Germany” ← P ≈ 0.03
Once “Germany” is sampled, the model conditions on it — and continues fluently but wrongly.
Hallucination = sampling from a distribution whose high-probability region does not encode factual truth. The model maximises P(grammatically coherent sequence), not P(factually correct sequence). These objectives diverge when the training data contains contradictions or the model generalises beyond its evidence.

Every token is a fork in the road. One wrong turn, and the model confidently walks the wrong path.

Why This Matters

Next-token prediction is the atomic operation of modern AI. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — they all reduce to this single mathematical act performed trillions of times during training. The chain rule of probability, applied at scale with neural networks, produces systems that can write code, translate languages, and explain quantum physics. Understanding this mechanism is the key to understanding both the power and the limitations of large language models.

Shannon showed in 1948 that entropy sets a fundamental limit on compression and prediction. Bengio et al. (2003) demonstrated that neural networks could learn distributed word representations. Together, these ideas underpin every language model in use today.

References: Shannon, C. E. (1948). A Mathematical Theory of Communication. Bell System Technical Journal. · Bengio, Y. et al. (2003). A Neural Probabilistic Language Model. JMLR.

When Should You Stop Looking?

The same number that governs compound interest and the spiral of a nautilus tells you exactly when to stop looking — and the answer might surprise you.

At some point, most of us face a version of the same impossible problem. You're looking for something — a partner, a flat, a job. Each option arrives one at a time. If you pass, it's gone. If you settle too early, you'll always wonder. Too late, and the best ones have moved on.

You're going on 10 dates, in random order. After each one you must decide: is this the one? You can never go back. What's your strategy?

6. Where Does 37% Come From?

Track A — Intuitive
Track B — Mathematical

For a large pool of n candidates, if we observe the first k and then pick the next candidate who is the best so far, the probability of landing the overall best is approximately:

P(k) ≈ (k/n) × ln(n/k)

This has a clean shape — it starts at 0 (observe nobody), rises to a peak, then falls back. The peak is where the strategy works best. Click through the argument:

Step 1: Why (k/n)?
If the best candidate overall is at position j (somewhere after position k), they will be chosen only if no one between position k+1 and j−1 was already the best-so-far. The factor k/n captures the probability that the observation window is large enough to set a meaningful benchmark.
Step 2: Why ln(n/k)?
We sum over all possible positions where the best candidate could appear: positions k+1 through n. The sum of 1/(j−1) over those positions approximates the natural logarithm ln(n/k) for large n. This is the harmonic series connection.
Step 3: Finding the peak
To maximise (k/n)×ln(n/k), we take the derivative with respect to k and set it to zero. Let x = k/n. Then P = x×ln(1/x) = −x×ln(x). Differentiating: dP/dx = −ln(x) − 1 = 0, so ln(x) = −1, giving x = e−1 = 1/e ≈ 0.3679. The natural logarithm — and therefore e — emerges inevitably.

The exact probability for n candidates with cutoff k is:

P(k, n) = (k/n) × Σj=k+1..n 1/(j−1)

This is a partial harmonic sum. As n → ∞ with k = ⌊n/e⌋:

(k/n) × Σj=k+1..n 1/(j−1) → (1/e) × ln(e) = 1/e ≈ 0.3679
Convergence from above: For any finite n, the optimal probability is always slightly higher than 1/e. The limiting value 1/e ≈ 36.79% is approached from above as n grows. This is visible in the pattern table and the convergence plot.

Key facts:

Optimal cutoff: k* = ⌊n/e⌋
Success probability → 1/e ≈ 36.79% as n → ∞
e = limn→∞(1 + 1/n)n ≈ 2.71828...

⚠ Common Misconceptions

Misconception: The 37% rule guarantees you find the best
Reality:

It maximises your probability of finding the best, but that probability is still only about 37%. In roughly 2 out of 3 cases, you will not select the best candidate — the strategy just means no other strategy does better.

Misconception: You should apply this to real life literally
Reality:

The Secretary Problem assumes you cannot recall previous candidates, you can perfectly rank each new arrival against all past ones, and you know n in advance. Real decisions rarely satisfy all these conditions. The rule offers insight, not a formula.

Misconception: Observing 37% means rejecting 37% of your life
Reality:

You apply the rule to a defined search window. The mathematics says nothing about how long that window should be — only how to behave within it.

The exact fraction to observe — always about 37% — is the same whether you're choosing from 10 options or 10,000. The same number appears in your savings account, in the spiral of a shell, and in the mathematics of love.

Why This Matters
The Secretary Problem is one of the most elegant results in probability theory. It shows that a simple, principled strategy — observe then commit — beats every other approach, and that the mathematics of optimal decisions connects to the same fundamental constant e that appears across all of mathematics. The problem teaches us that gathering information has value, that there is a cost to both impatience and indecision, and that mathematics can guide us even when outcomes are uncertain.

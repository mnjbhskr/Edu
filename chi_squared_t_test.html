Chi-Squared Tests & t-Distribution

●●●● Advanced

Two workhorses of statistical inference — testing categorical counts and making inferences about means

1. Introduction

Statistics offers a rich toolkit for testing hypotheses, but two tests stand above the rest in frequency of use: the χ² (chi-squared) test and the t-test. They answer fundamentally different questions:

TestData TypeQuestion
χ² test Categorical (counts) Do observed frequencies match expected frequencies?
t-test Continuous (means) Is the sample mean consistent with a hypothesised population mean?

The chi-squared test works with counts — how many items fell into each category. It makes no assumptions about the underlying distribution of the raw data. The t-test works with measurements — it assumes approximately normal data (or large samples) and is designed for situations where the population standard deviation σ is unknown and must be estimated from the sample.

Rule of thumb: if your data are category labels you can count, reach for χ². If your data are numbers you can average, reach for a t-test.

2. Chi-Squared Goodness of Fit

The Test Statistic

The goodness-of-fit test asks: do the observed frequencies differ significantly from what we would expect under a given hypothesis? The test statistic measures the total squared discrepancy, normalised by the expected count in each category:

X² = Σ (Oi − Ei)² / Ei

where Oi is the observed count and Ei is the expected count for category i. The degrees of freedom are df = k − 1, where k is the number of categories.

If X² exceeds the critical value from the χ² distribution at the chosen significance level α, we reject H0.

Worked Example: Is a Die Fair?

A die is rolled 60 times. Under H0 (fair die), each face has E = 60/6 = 10.

Observed: 8, 12, 7, 11, 14, 8
Expected: 10, 10, 10, 10, 10, 10
X² = (8−10)²/10 + (12−10)²/10 + (7−10)²/10 + (11−10)²/10 + (14−10)²/10 + (8−10)²/10
     = 0.4 + 0.4 + 0.9 + 0.1 + 1.6 + 0.4 = 3.8
df = 6 − 1 = 5
Critical value (α = 0.05, df = 5): 11.07
Since 3.8 < 11.07, fail to reject H0 — insufficient evidence the die is unfair.
▶ Why divide by Ei?

Dividing by Ei normalises the squared difference. A deviation of 5 from an expected count of 10 is much more significant than a deviation of 5 from an expected count of 1000. Without this normalisation, categories with large expected counts would dominate the test statistic regardless of relative discrepancy.

Under H0, as sample size grows, the distribution of X² approaches the χ² distribution with k − 1 degrees of freedom. This approximation works well when all Ei ≥ 5.

4. Contingency Tables — Test of Independence

Are Two Categorical Variables Independent?

A contingency table cross-classifies observations by two categorical variables. The χ² test of independence asks whether the row and column variables are associated.

Expected count:   Eij = (row totali × col totalj) / grand total
Degrees of freedom:   df = (r − 1)(c − 1)

Worked Example: Preference by Gender

A survey asks 200 people whether they prefer tea, coffee, or juice, recorded by gender. H0: preference is independent of gender.

TeaCoffeeJuiceRow Total
Male304525100
Female403525100
Col Total708050200
E(Male, Tea) = 100 × 70 / 200 = 35
E(Male, Coffee) = 100 × 80 / 200 = 40
E(Male, Juice) = 100 × 50 / 200 = 25
X² = (30−35)²/35 + (45−40)²/40 + (25−25)²/25 + (40−35)²/35 + (35−40)²/40 + (25−25)²/25
     = 0.714 + 0.625 + 0 + 0.714 + 0.625 + 0 = 2.679
df = (2−1)(3−1) = 2
Critical value (α = 0.05, df = 2): 5.991
Since 2.679 < 5.991, fail to reject H0 — no significant association between gender and drink preference.
▶ Why (r − 1)(c − 1) degrees of freedom?

Once the row and column totals are fixed, only (r − 1)(c − 1) cells are free to vary — the remaining cells are determined by the marginal constraints. For a 2 × 3 table, knowing just 2 cells (one in each column of the first row, for example) determines all 6 cells via the marginals.

5. The t-Distribution

When Normal Isn’t Quite Enough

When sampling from a normal population with known σ, the standardised sample mean follows a standard normal distribution. But when σ is unknown and estimated by the sample standard deviation s, the extra uncertainty fattens the tails — the result is the t-distribution.

t = (x̄ − μ0) / (s / √n)      df = n − 1

The t-distribution has heavier tails than the standard normal, meaning extreme values are more probable. As the degrees of freedom increase, the t-distribution converges to the normal — by df ≈ 30, the difference is negligible.

7. Confidence Intervals

Estimating the True Mean

A confidence interval provides a range of plausible values for the unknown population mean μ. When σ is unknown:

CI = x̄ ± t* × s / √n      where t* is the critical value for the chosen confidence level

The margin of error depends on three quantities: the confidence level (higher → wider), the sample standard deviation (more variability → wider), and the sample size (larger → narrower). This is the fundamental trade-off of statistical estimation.

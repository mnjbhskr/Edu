1. Comparing LPM, Probit & Logit

The LPM (dashed grey) predicts probabilities below 0 and above 1. Probit (blue) and logit (green) use CDF transformations to keep predictions bounded in [0, 1]. In practice, marginal effects at the mean are very similar.

Why not just use the LPM?
The LPM has three problems: (1) predicted probabilities can lie outside [0, 1]; (2) the error term is necessarily heteroskedastic (variance depends on x); and (3) the linear functional form is usually misspecified. Despite this, the LPM remains popular in applied work because its coefficients are directly interpretable as marginal effects, and with robust standard errors, inference is often reliable. Probit and logit avoid problems (1) and (3), but their coefficients require transformation to obtain marginal effects.

The Navier–Stokes Equations ★★★★ Advanced

The equations that govern all fluid motion

1. The Equations

Incompressible Navier–Stokes

Every river current, ocean wave, weather system, and blood flow in your veins is governed by these two equations. Written down by Claude-Louis Navier in 1822 and George Gabriel Stokes in 1845, they express Newton’s second law for a continuous fluid — conservation of momentum coupled with conservation of mass.

∂u/∂t  +  (u·∇)u  =  −∇p/ρ  +  ν∇²u
∇·u = 0

Each term has a precise physical meaning:

∂u/∂t Local acceleration — how the velocity at a fixed point changes in time. Imagine standing at a particular spot in the river and measuring how the current changes.
(u·∇)u Advection — the fluid carries its own momentum downstream. This nonlinear term is the source of turbulence and the reason the equations are so difficult to solve.
−∇p/ρ Pressure gradient — fluid accelerates from high pressure to low pressure. This is what pushes air from high-pressure weather systems towards low-pressure ones.
ν∇²u Viscous diffusion — friction between adjacent fluid layers smooths out velocity differences. Honey has high ν; air has low ν.
∇·u = 0 Incompressibility — fluid density stays constant. Whatever flows into a region must flow out — the fluid can neither pile up nor develop voids.
These equations are deceptively simple to write down, yet no one has proven whether smooth solutions always exist in three dimensions. They are one of the seven Clay Mathematics Institute Millennium Prize Problems — a proof (or counterexample) is worth $1,000,000.
Historical note: Claude-Louis Navier first derived the equations in 1822 using a molecular stress argument, though his reasoning contained errors. George Gabriel Stokes rederived them rigorously in 1845 using continuum mechanics. Osborne Reynolds (1883) then showed experimentally that the ratio of inertial to viscous forces — now called the Reynolds number — determines whether flow is laminar or turbulent.

Low viscosity (ν ≈ 0.0001): vortices persist for hundreds of time steps and develop intricate filamentary structure as the advection term stretches and folds the vorticity field. This is the regime relevant to atmospheric and oceanic flows.

High viscosity (ν ≈ 0.01): diffusion rapidly smooths everything into a uniform field. This is the regime of very viscous flows like honey or lava.

The fundamental tension in fluid mechanics is precisely this: the nonlinear advection term creates ever-finer scales, while viscosity destroys them. The Reynolds number Re ∼ 1/ν measures their balance.

The $1,000,000 Question
In the year 2000, the Clay Mathematics Institute listed seven Millennium Prize Problems, each carrying a million-dollar reward. One of them asks: given smooth initial conditions, do the 3D incompressible Navier–Stokes equations always produce smooth solutions for all future time?

The key concern is whether vorticity can concentrate so intensely that it becomes infinite in finite time — a “blow-up” or singularity. Such an event would mean the equations break down.

In our 2D simulation, this cannot happen. Olga Ladyzhenskaya proved 2D regularity in 1959 — the enstrophy inequality dΕ/dt ≤ 0 guarantees decay. But in three dimensions, there is an additional vortex stretching term that can amplify vorticity, and no one has been able to rule out blow-up.

Watch the enstrophy curve (teal): it always decays monotonically in 2D. In a hypothetical 3D blow-up, it would surge to infinity in finite time.
The seven Millennium Prize Problems (Clay Mathematics Institute, 2000):
1. Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture   2. Hodge conjecture   3. Navier–Stokes existence and smoothness   4. P vs NP   5. Poincaré conjecture (solved, 2003)   6. Riemann hypothesis   7. Yang–Mills existence and mass gap

4. What Have We Learnt?

Key Equations
Navier–Stokes (momentum): ∂u/∂t + (u·∇)u = −∇p/ρ + ν∇²u
Incompressibility (mass): ∇·u = 0
Vorticity form (2D): ∂ω/∂t + u·∇ω = ν∇²ω
Vorticity form (3D): ∂ω/∂t + u·∇ω = (ω·∇)u + ν∇²ω
Enstrophy: Ε = ½ ∫ |ω|² dV
Stream function: ∇²ψ = −ω,   u = ∂ψ/∂y,   v = −∂ψ/∂x
Reynolds number: Re = UL/ν
3D vortex stretching: (ω·∇)u  (absent in 2D — the key difficulty for regularity)
Connections
The Navier–Stokes equations sit at the heart of an enormous web of science and engineering. In the limit of zero viscosity (ν → 0), they reduce to the Euler equations of inviscid flow. Adding temperature and buoyancy gives the Boussinesq equations for convection. Coupling with Maxwell’s equations yields magnetohydrodynamics. The 3D regularity question is not merely academic: a blow-up would mean that real fluids develop infinite velocities, which would signal a breakdown of the continuum model itself — perhaps pointing to deeper physics at the molecular scale.
Where You’ll See This
  • Weather forecasting — atmospheric circulation is governed by Navier–Stokes with rotation and stratification
  • Aircraft design — computational fluid dynamics (CFD) solves Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes over wing geometries
  • Blood flow — haemodynamics models use these equations to study flow in arteries, aneurysms, and heart valves
  • Ocean currents — global ocean models solve variants of these equations at every time step
  • Climate models — every general circulation model (GCM) is built on coupled fluid dynamics
  • Astrophysics — star formation, accretion discs, and supernova explosions all require fluid dynamics

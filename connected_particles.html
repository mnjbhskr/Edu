Connected Particles & Equilibrium

●●●● Intermediate

Forces in connected systems — pulleys, inclined planes, and the conditions for equilibrium

1. Introduction

Many problems in mechanics involve two or more particles connected by a light, inextensible string. The string may pass over a smooth pulley, link objects on different inclined planes, or connect a particle on a table to one hanging over an edge.

The key principle is that a light, inextensible string has two important properties: it has negligible mass (so the tension is the same throughout), and it cannot stretch (so connected particles share the same magnitude of acceleration).

To solve these problems we apply Newton’s second law (F = ma) separately to each particle, drawing a free-body diagram for each one. The tension T appears in both equations — pulling one particle up and the other along (or down). By solving the simultaneous equations we find both the acceleration and the tension.

The approach is always the same: isolate each particle, draw all forces, write F = ma for each, then solve simultaneously. Mastering this technique unlocks a huge range of mechanics problems.

2. Pulleys — Atwood’s Machine

Two Masses Over a Frictionless Pulley

The simplest connected-particle system is Atwood’s machine: two masses m₁ and m₂ joined by a light string over a smooth pulley. If m₁ > m₂, then m₁ descends and m₂ ascends with the same acceleration a.

Setting up the equations

Apply F = ma to each particle separately, taking the direction of motion as positive:

For m₁ (descending):   m₁g − T = m₁a
For m₂ (ascending):   T − m₂g = m₂a

Adding the two equations eliminates T:

m₁g − m₂g = (m₁ + m₂)a

a = (m₁ − m₂)g / (m₁ + m₂)

Substituting back to find the tension:

T = 2m₁m₂g / (m₁ + m₂)

Worked Example

Given: m₁ = 5 kg, m₂ = 3 kg, g = 9.8 m/s²
Step 1: a = (5 − 3) × 9.8 / (5 + 3)
Step 2: a = 19.6 / 8 = 2.45 m/s²
Step 3: T = 2 × 5 × 3 × 9.8 / (5 + 3)
Step 4: T = 294 / 8 = 36.75 N
▶ Why is T the same throughout the string?

If the string has mass ms and the system accelerates at a, Newton’s second law applied to the string gives T₁ − T₂ = msa. When the string is light (ms ≈ 0), the difference T₁ − T₂ ≈ 0, so the tension is uniform. Similarly, the pulley being smooth means no friction acts to change the tension as the string passes over it.

Notice that the tension is always between m₁g and m₂g. If the masses are equal, a = 0 and T = mg — the system is in equilibrium. If m₂ = 0, we recover free fall: a = g and T = 0.

4. Inclined Planes

Resolving Forces on a Slope

When a particle of mass m sits on a plane inclined at angle θ to the horizontal, we resolve its weight into two components:

Parallel to slope (down):   mg sin θ
Perpendicular to slope (into surface):   mg cos θ

The normal reaction R balances the perpendicular component: R = mg cos θ. Friction F acts parallel to the slope, opposing motion, and satisfies F ≤ μR where μ is the coefficient of friction.

On the point of sliding down:   mg sin θ = μ mg cos θ
⇒  tan θ = μ

5. Connected Particles on Inclines

Particle on a Table Connected to a Hanging Mass

A common problem: mass m₁ sits on a rough horizontal table, connected by a light string over a smooth pulley at the edge to mass m₂ hanging freely. If the system moves, m₂ descends and m₁ slides across the table.

Setting up the equations

For m₁ (on table, friction μ):   T − μm₁g = m₁a
For m₂ (hanging):   m₂g − T = m₂a

Adding to eliminate T:

a = (m₂ − μm₁)g / (m₁ + m₂)

T = m₁m₂(1 + μ)g / (m₁ + m₂)

Worked Example

Given: m₁ = 4 kg (on table), m₂ = 3 kg (hanging), μ = 0.25, g = 9.8 m/s²
Step 1: Friction = μm₁g = 0.25 × 4 × 9.8 = 9.8 N
Step 2: a = (3 − 0.25 × 4) × 9.8 / (4 + 3)
Step 3: a = 2 × 9.8 / 7 = 2.80 m/s²
Step 4: T = m₂(g − a) = 3 × (9.8 − 2.80) = 21.0 N
Check: T − μm₁g = 21.0 − 9.8 = 11.2 = m₁a = 4 × 2.80 ✓

▶ What if the system doesn’t move?

The system moves only if the hanging weight exceeds the maximum friction force: m₂g > μm₁g, i.e. m₂ > μm₁. If m₂ ≤ μm₁, the system remains in equilibrium and the tension equals m₂g (the friction force adjusts to balance).

6. Equilibrium

Conditions for Equilibrium

A particle is in equilibrium when the resultant force acting on it is zero. For a rigid body we also require the resultant moment about any point to be zero.

Translational equilibrium:   ΣF = 0  (resolve in two perpendicular directions)
Rotational equilibrium:   ΣM = 0  (moments about any point)

In practice, for a particle acted on by coplanar forces, we resolve horizontally and vertically:

Horizontal:   ΣFx = 0
Vertical:   ΣFy = 0

Triangle of Forces

If exactly three forces act on a particle in equilibrium, they can be represented as the sides of a closed triangle drawn head-to-tail. This is because F₁ + F₂ + F₃ = 0 means that placing the vectors end-to-end returns to the starting point.

Lami’s theorem provides an elegant shortcut for three-force equilibrium: each force is proportional to the sine of the angle between the other two. Formally, F₁/sin α₁ = F₂/sin α₂ = F₃/sin α₃, where αi is the angle opposite Fi in the triangle of forces.

Visualising Pi

Three ways to discover pi through interactive experiment

1. Monte Carlo Estimation

Imagine a square dartboard with a circle perfectly inscribed inside it. The ratio of the circle's area to the square's area is pi/4. If we throw darts randomly, the fraction that land inside the circle should approach pi/4 — so we can estimate pi as 4 × (darts inside) / (total darts). The more darts, the closer we get.

2. Buffon's Needle

In 1777, Comte de Buffon posed a remarkable question: if you drop a needle of length L onto a floor with parallel lines spaced L apart, what is the probability that the needle crosses a line? The answer turns out to be 2/pi. This means we can estimate pi as 2 × (total needles) / (needles crossing a line). Probability and geometry are deeply intertwined.

3. Archimedes' Method — Pi from Polygons

Around 250 BC, Archimedes trapped pi between two bounds. A regular polygon inscribed inside a circle has a perimeter slightly less than the circumference; a circumscribed polygon has a perimeter slightly more. As the number of sides grows, both perimeters squeeze toward the true circumference, and dividing by the diameter gives increasingly tight bounds on pi. With just a 96-gon, Archimedes proved that 3.1408 < pi < 3.1429.

What connects these three methods?
Each method discovers the same constant from a completely different direction. Monte Carlo uses raw probability — the ratio of areas reveals pi through random sampling. Buffon's Needle connects probability with geometry — the chance of a needle crossing a line is governed by pi. Archimedes' polygons use pure geometry — squeezing a circle between straight-sided shapes.

The fact that all three converge on 3.14159... is not a coincidence. Pi is woven into the fabric of mathematics: it appears wherever circles, rotations, periodicity, or probability meet geometry. These visualisations let you watch that convergence unfold in real time.

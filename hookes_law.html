Hooke’s Law & Elastic Strings

●●●● Intermediate

How springs store energy, obey linear laws — and what happens when they don’t

1. Introduction

Robert Hooke published his discovery in 1678 as the Latin anagram ceiiinosssttuv, which unscrambles to ut tensio, sic vis — “as the extension, so the force.” This elegant principle underpins everything from bridge engineering to molecular bonding.

F = kx   where k = spring constant (N/m), x = extension beyond natural length (m)

The law holds only within the elastic limit — the maximum extension from which the material can return to its original length. Beyond this point, permanent (plastic) deformation occurs and the linear relationship breaks down.

Every spring has a natural length l0. The extension x is the difference between the current length and l0. The spring constant k measures stiffness: a large k means the spring resists deformation strongly.

For elastic strings (which can stretch but not compress), the tension is expressed using the modulus of elasticity λ:

T = λx / l0   where λ = modulus of elasticity (N), x = extension (m), l0 = natural length (m)
The modulus of elasticity λ is a property of the string material and cross-section, whereas k = λ/l0 depends on the string’s natural length as well. Doubling the natural length of a string halves its effective spring constant.

3. Elastic Potential Energy

Energy Stored in a Spring

The elastic potential energy (EPE) stored in a spring equals the area under the force–extension graph. For a linear spring obeying Hooke’s law:

EPE = ½kx² = ½Fx
▶ Derivation: area under the F–x graph

The work done stretching a spring from 0 to x is:

W = ∫0x F dx′ = ∫0x kx′ dx′ = ½kx²

Geometrically, F = kx is a straight line through the origin. The area under it from 0 to x is a triangle with base x and height kx, giving area = ½ × x × kx = ½kx².

The energy scales with x² — doubling the extension quadruples the stored energy. This quadratic relationship is why catapults and bows can store enormous energy with relatively modest displacements.

4. Springs in Series & Parallel

Combining Springs

When springs are combined, the effective spring constant depends on the configuration:

Series:   1/ktotal = 1/k1 + 1/k2   ⇒   ktotal = k1k2 / (k1 + k2)
Parallel:   ktotal = k1 + k2

Springs in series are always softer than the weakest individual spring. Springs in parallel are always stiffer than the stiffest individual spring. This mirrors the behaviour of resistors in electrical circuits — but inverted: resistors in series add, while springs in series combine reciprocally.

5. Elastic Collisions with Springs

Mass–Spring Collision Simulator

A mass slides frictionlessly towards a spring fixed to a wall. On impact, kinetic energy converts to elastic potential energy. At maximum compression, all kinetic energy is stored in the spring:

½mv² = ½kxmax²   ⇒   xmax = v√(m/k)

6. Worked Examples

Problem 1: Extension of an Elastic String

An elastic string has natural length l0 = 2 m and modulus of elasticity λ = 40 N. A load of 10 N is applied. Find the extension and the stretched length.

▶ Show solution
Using: T = λx / l0
Rearrange: x = T × l0 / λ
Substitute: x = 10 × 2 / 40 = 0.50 m
Stretched length: l = l0 + x = 2 + 0.50 = 2.50 m
Effective k: k = λ / l0 = 40 / 2 = 20 N/m

Check: F = kx = 20 × 0.50 = 10 N ✓

Problem 2: Mass Dropped onto a Spring

A mass of 0.5 kg is dropped from rest at a height of 1 m onto a vertical spring with k = 200 N/m. Find the maximum compression of the spring. (Take g = 9.8 m/s².)

▶ Show solution

At maximum compression d, the mass has fallen a total distance (h + d). All gravitational PE converts to elastic PE (kinetic energy is zero at maximum compression):

mg(h + d) = ½kd²
Substitute: 0.5 × 9.8 × (1 + d) = ½ × 200 × d²
Expand: 4.9 + 4.9d = 100d²
Rearrange: 100d² − 4.9d − 4.9 = 0
Quadratic formula: d = [4.9 ± √(4.9² + 4 × 100 × 4.9)] / (2 × 100)
Discriminant: √(24.01 + 1960) = √1984.01 ≈ 44.54
Positive root: d = (4.9 + 44.54) / 200 = 0.247 m

Check: EPE = ½ × 200 × 0.247² ≈ 6.10 J. GPE lost = 0.5 × 9.8 × (1 + 0.247) ≈ 6.11 J ✓

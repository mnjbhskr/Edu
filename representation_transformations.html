Same Data, Different Space

The Fourier transform does not change your signal — it changes how you see it.

Core idea A transformation changes coordinate system without changing the underlying reality — and the right coordinate system makes invisible structure visible.

Fourier Transform:
    F(ω) = ∫ f(t) e^(−iωt) dt

Inverse:
    f(t) = (1/2π) ∫ F(ω) e^(iωt) dω

Discrete form (N samples):
    F[k] = Σₐ₀ f[n] ⋅ e^(−2πi⋅kn/N)

Same signal, two representations:
    f(t)  ↔  F(ω)
   time domain     frequency domain

What is preserved: all information (the transform is invertible)
What becomes visible: frequency components, periodicity, harmonics
💡 When you listen to music through an equaliser, you are looking at a real-time Fourier transform of the audio signal. The bass, midrange and treble are the low, middle and high frequency components of the same waveform. No information is added or removed — only the representation changes. The same signal looks completely different in the frequency domain, and completely different structure becomes visible.

2. Graph Embeddings — From Connections to Coordinates

GRAPH → EMBEDDING

💡 Word2Vec transforms a graph of word co-occurrences into a vector space where semantic similarity becomes geometric proximity. PageRank transforms a graph into a probability distribution. Every graph algorithm is, at its core, a transformation that maps discrete relational structure into something computationally tractable — a matrix, a vector, a probability.

log(xy) = log(x) + log(y). A log transform turns multiplicative structure into additive structure: exponential growth becomes a straight line. The transform is invertible (via exp), but the visual sense of "equal spacing" is destroyed — a step from 1 to 10 looks the same as 10 to 100. 1 10 100 1000 → 0 1 2 3 linear log₁₀

Dividing each row by its total (or subtracting a mean and dividing by SD) preserves shape but loses scale. Two countries with populations of 1,000 and 100,000,000 look identical after per-capita normalisation — useful when you want to compare structure, misleading if scale itself is the story. 100 200 300 → 0.17 0.33 0.50 1 2 3 → 0.17 0.33 0.50 raw counts identical proportions
💡 An invertible transformation changes representation without losing information — like translating between English and French. A lossy transformation discards information in exchange for something useful — like summarising a book. PCA with all components is invertible. PCA with fewer components is a deliberate, mathematically-principled act of forgetting.
What have we learnt?
Every representation is a coordinate system. A transformation changes the coordinate system — it does not change the underlying reality. The Fourier transform makes periodic structure visible. PCA makes variance structure visible. Graph embeddings make relational similarity visible. Understanding which transformation to apply requires understanding what structure you are trying to see. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

Moments & Turning Forces

●●●● Intermediate

Understanding turning effects, lever arms, and the balance of rigid bodies

1. What Is a Moment?

Turning Effect of a Force

A moment (or torque) measures the turning effect of a force about a pivot point. It depends on two things: the size of the force and how far that force acts from the pivot.

Moment = Force × perpendicular distance from pivot
M = F × d

The SI unit of moment is the newton metre (N m). A 10 N force acting 0.5 m from a pivot produces a moment of 5 N m.

Moments are classified by their direction of rotation:

↻ Clockwise
Turning in the same direction as clock hands. Conventionally treated as positive in many textbooks.
↺ Anticlockwise
Turning opposite to clock hands. Conventionally treated as negative (or vice versa, depending on convention).
The “perpendicular distance” is crucial. If a force acts at an angle to the lever arm, only the component perpendicular to the arm contributes to the moment: M = F × d × sin θ.

2. Principle of Moments

Equilibrium Condition

For a body in rotational equilibrium (not rotating), the principle of moments states:

Sum of clockwise moments = Sum of anticlockwise moments

This is equivalent to saying the net moment about any point is zero. It is one of the most fundamental tools in mechanics and engineering.

Worked Example — Seesaw Balancing

A child of weight 400 N sits 2.0 m from the pivot of a seesaw. Where must a child of weight 500 N sit to balance the seesaw?

For equilibrium: clockwise moments = anticlockwise moments
Taking moments about the pivot:
  400 × 2.0 = 500 × d
  800 = 500d
  d = 800 / 500 = 1.6 m
The 500 N child must sit 1.6 m from the pivot on the opposite side.
The principle of moments holds about any point, not just the pivot. Choosing the pivot as the point of calculation eliminates the unknown reaction force there — a powerful problem-solving technique.

4. Couples

Two Equal and Opposite Parallel Forces

A couple consists of two forces that are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act along parallel lines. A couple produces pure rotation with no translational motion.

Moment of a couple = F × d
where d is the perpendicular distance between the two force lines of action

A remarkable property of couples: the moment is independent of the choice of pivot. No matter where you calculate the moment, the result is the same.

▶ Proof that the moment of a couple is independent of the pivot

Consider two forces +F and −F separated by distance d. Take moments about an arbitrary point P at distance x from the first force.

Moment about P:
M = F × x − F × (x − d)
M = Fx − Fx + Fd
M = F × d

The distance x cancels completely. The moment depends only on F and d, confirming it is independent of the pivot position.

Everyday examples of couples

Steering wheel
Two hands push in opposite directions to turn
Tap / spanner
Fingers apply equal and opposite forces to turn a tap
Compass needle
Earth’s magnetic field exerts a couple on the magnetised needle

5. Equilibrium of Rigid Bodies

Three Conditions for Complete Equilibrium

For a rigid body to be in complete static equilibrium, three conditions must be satisfied simultaneously:

ΣFx = 0
Net horizontal force is zero
ΣFy = 0
Net vertical force is zero
ΣM = 0
Net moment about any point is zero

Worked Example — Beam on Two Supports

A uniform beam of weight 200 N and length 4.0 m rests on two supports at A (left end) and B (right end). A point load of 300 N acts 1.0 m from A. Find the reaction forces RA and RB.

Taking moments about A:
  RB × 4.0 = 300 × 1.0 + 200 × 2.0
  RB × 4.0 = 300 + 400 = 700
  RB = 175 N
Resolving vertically (ΣFy = 0):
  RA + RB = 300 + 200
  RA = 500 − 175 = 325 N
Check: 325 × 0 + 175 × 4 = 700;   300 × 1 + 200 × 2 = 700   ✓
When solving beam problems, always take moments about a point where an unknown force acts. This eliminates that unknown from the equation, making the algebra simpler.

6. Worked Examples

A-Level Problem 1 — Non-Uniform Beam

A non-uniform plank AB of length 5.0 m and weight 80 N is supported horizontally at A and at a point C, where AC = 3.5 m. The centre of mass of the plank is 2.0 m from A. A child of weight 250 N stands at B. The plank is on the point of tilting about C. Find the reaction at A.

When the plank is about to tilt about C, RA = 0
Taking moments about C:
  Clockwise moments:
    Child at B: 250 × (5.0 − 3.5) = 250 × 1.5 = 375 N m
  Anticlockwise moments:
    Weight at centre of mass: 80 × (3.5 − 2.0) = 80 × 1.5 = 120 N m
Net clockwise moment about C:
  375 − 120 = 255 N m
Since the plank is on the point of tilting about C, RA = 0.
The reaction at C supports the entire system.
Resolving vertically: RC = 250 + 80 = 330 N

A-Level Problem 2 — Hinged Rod with Wire

A uniform rod AB of length 3.0 m and weight 120 N is hinged at A to a vertical wall. The rod is held horizontal by a wire attached from B to a point on the wall directly above A, making an angle of 30° with the rod. A load of 200 N hangs from B. Find the tension in the wire.

Taking moments about A (eliminates hinge reaction):
  Clockwise moments:
    Weight of rod: 120 × 1.5 = 180 N m
    Load at B: 200 × 3.0 = 600 N m
    Total clockwise = 180 + 600 = 780 N m
  Anticlockwise moment from wire tension T:
    Perpendicular component: T × sin 30° × 3.0
    = T × 0.5 × 3.0 = 1.5T
For equilibrium:
  1.5T = 780
  T = 520 N
In both problems the key technique is the same: take moments about a strategically chosen point to eliminate unknowns. For hinged beams, always take moments about the hinge; for beams on the point of tilting, recognise that the reaction at the lifting point is zero.

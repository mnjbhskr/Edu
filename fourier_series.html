Fourier Series

●●●● Intermediate

Decomposing periodic functions into sines and cosines — Fourier’s revolutionary insight

1. Introduction

In 1807, Joseph Fourier made a claim so bold that leading mathematicians — including Lagrange and Laplace — initially rejected it: any periodic function can be written as a sum of sines and cosines. Not just smooth, well-behaved functions, but jagged square waves, sharp-cornered triangles, and everything in between.

Fourier’s motivation was the heat equation. He needed to express arbitrary initial temperature distributions as sums of simple sinusoidal solutions. The technique he developed turned out to be far more general than heat conduction — it became one of the most powerful tools in all of mathematics and engineering.

The core idea is strikingly simple: sine and cosine waves of different frequencies form a kind of “basis” for periodic functions. Just as any colour can be decomposed into red, green, and blue components, any periodic signal can be decomposed into its harmonic frequencies. This page lets you build that decomposition interactively, term by term, and watch the approximation converge.

Fourier analysis underpins almost every area of modern science and technology: signal processing, image compression (JPEG), audio synthesis, quantum mechanics, and the solution of partial differential equations. Understanding it opens doors everywhere.

2. The Formulae

Fourier Series Representation

For a periodic function f(x) with period 2L, the Fourier series representation is:

f(x) = a₀/2 + Σn=1∞ [an cos(nπx/L) + bn sin(nπx/L)]

The Fourier coefficients are computed by integration over one full period:

a₀ = (1/L) ∫−LL f(x) dx
an = (1/L) ∫−LL f(x) cos(nπx/L) dx
bn = (1/L) ∫−LL f(x) sin(nπx/L) dx
▶ Why these formulae work: orthogonality of sines and cosines

The key is orthogonality. Over the interval [−L, L], sine and cosine functions satisfy:

∫−LL cos(mπx/L) cos(nπx/L) dx = L · δmn  (Kronecker delta)
∫−LL sin(mπx/L) sin(nπx/L) dx = L · δmn
∫−LL cos(mπx/L) sin(nπx/L) dx = 0  (always)

To find an, multiply both sides of the Fourier series by cos(nπx/L) and integrate. Every term vanishes except the one matching n, thanks to orthogonality. The same trick with sin(nπx/L) extracts bn. This is why the (1/L) normalisation factor appears — it cancels the L from the orthogonality integral.

The orthogonality of sines and cosines is analogous to the orthogonality of unit vectors in Euclidean space. Just as you can find the x-component of a vector by dotting with &hat;x, you find the n-th Fourier coefficient by “dotting” (integrating) f(x) with the corresponding sine or cosine.

4. Gibbs Phenomenon

The Stubborn Overshoot

At points where f(x) has a jump discontinuity, the Fourier partial sum always overshoots (and undershoots) by approximately 9% of the jump — no matter how many terms you include. As N → ∞, the overshoot does not vanish; it merely becomes narrower. This is the Gibbs phenomenon, first observed by Henry Wilbraham (1848) and later rediscovered by J. Willard Gibbs (1899).

More precisely, the overshoot converges to (1/π) ∫0π (sin t / t) dt − 1/2 ≈ 0.0895, or about 8.95% of the jump magnitude. The partial sums converge pointwise everywhere except at the discontinuity itself, but they do not converge uniformly near it.

The Gibbs phenomenon explains why digital audio systems use windowing functions (Hanning, Hamming, Blackman) to taper signals before computing their Fourier transforms — sharp truncation introduces ringing artefacts analogous to the Gibbs overshoot.

5. Even & Odd Functions

Symmetry Simplifies Everything

The symmetry of f(x) determines which coefficients survive. If the function has a definite parity, half the work disappears.

SymmetryConditionNon-zero CoefficientsSeries Contains
Even function f(−x) = f(x) a₀, an only Cosines only (cosine series)
Odd function f(−x) = −f(x) bn only Sines only (sine series)
Neither No symmetry a₀, an, bn Both sines and cosines

Half-range expansions. If f(x) is defined only on [0, L], you can extend it to [−L, L] in two ways:

Even extension: reflect f(x) symmetrically → cosine series
Odd extension: reflect f(x) antisymmetrically → sine series
▶ Why does symmetry eliminate terms?

Consider an even function f(x). The product f(x) · sin(nπx/L) is odd (even × odd = odd), and the integral of an odd function over a symmetric interval [−L, L] is always zero. Therefore all bn = 0. Similarly, for an odd f(x), the product f(x) · cos(nπx/L) is odd, so all an = 0.

The square wave is odd ⇒ only sine terms. The triangle wave is even ⇒ only cosine terms. Recognising symmetry before computing saves substantial effort.

6. Parseval’s Theorem

Energy Conservation in Frequency Space

Parseval’s theorem states that the total “energy” (mean square value) of a function equals the sum of the squared magnitudes of its Fourier coefficients:

(1/L) ∫−LL |f(x)|² dx = a₀²/2 + Σn=1∞ (an² + bn²)

This is the frequency-domain analogue of the Pythagorean theorem: energy is preserved when you change from the time domain to the frequency domain. The bar chart below shows the magnitude of each coefficient alongside the function.

For the square wave, the coefficients decay as 1/n, so their squares decay as 1/n². This slow decay reflects the sharp discontinuities. The triangle wave’s coefficients decay as 1/n² (squares as 1/n⁴) — much faster, because the function is continuous.

7. Convergence

When Does the Series Actually Converge?

Fourier’s claim was audacious, and the question of exactly when a Fourier series converges occupied mathematicians for over a century. There are three distinct notions of convergence:

TypeDefinitionRequirement
Pointwise SN(x) → f(x) for each x individually Dirichlet conditions (see below)
Uniform sup|SN(x) − f(x)| → 0 f must be continuous (no jumps)
L² (mean-square) ∫|SN − f|² dx → 0 f must be square-integrable (weakest condition)

Dirichlet conditions (sufficient for pointwise convergence):

1. f(x) is periodic with period 2L
2. f(x) has at most finitely many discontinuities in each period
3. f(x) has at most finitely many extrema in each period
4. ∫|f(x)| dx converges (f is absolutely integrable)

At points of continuity, the Fourier series converges to f(x). At jump discontinuities, it converges to the average of the left and right limits: [f(x−) + f(x+)] / 2.

L² convergence is the most permissive — it allows functions with infinitely many discontinuities, provided the total “energy” is finite. This is the convergence relevant in quantum mechanics, where wave functions live in L² spaces.

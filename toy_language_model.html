Train Your Own Language Model

●●●● Intermediate

A vocabulary of 12 words, trained in your browser — the full pipeline in miniature

Every large language model — from GPT-4 to Claude — does exactly three things: reads text, counts patterns, and predicts the next word. The mathematics is probability. Here you will build that entire pipeline from scratch: tokenise a micro-corpus, estimate bigram probabilities by maximum likelihood, and generate new text by sampling. The model is tiny. The principle is identical to what runs at trillion-parameter scale.

Vocabulary V = {sun, rain, cloud, warm, cold, ...}
Tokenisation: "the sun is warm" → [the, sun, is, warm]
P(warm | sun) = count(sun warm) / count(sun)
Tokenisation is the first step of every language model. GPT-4 uses byte-pair encoding to split text into ~100,000 sub-word tokens. Here we use the simplest possible scheme: split on spaces and punctuation, lowercase everything. The vocabulary that emerges from a tiny weather corpus is the foundation of the entire model.

A language model begins with text. Tokenise it, count the words, and you have a vocabulary.

Log-probabilities are how neural networks actually compute — cross-entropy loss is the negative log-probability of the correct token.

Hover over a cell to inspect bigram probabilities.
P(wnext | wcurrent) = count(wcurrent, wnext) / Σ count(wcurrent, *)
This is maximum likelihood estimation — the simplest way to fit a probability distribution to data.
This 12×12 table is a language model. GPT-4 has a vocabulary of 100,000 tokens and works in 12,288 dimensions — but the core idea is identical: estimate P(next token | context). The only difference is scale and the depth of context the model can consider.
Most word pairs never appear together. This sparsity is a fundamental property of language — it means the model only needs to learn a small fraction of all possible transitions.

A bigram table is a complete language model — just the smallest possible one.

GPT-4 uses the same sampling mechanism across 100 trillion parameters with a context window of 128,000 tokens. Scale creates the illusion of understanding.

Generation is sampling — roll the probabilistic dice one word at a time.

Why This Matters

You just built the complete pipeline that powers every language model: tokenise text, estimate conditional probabilities, and generate by sampling. The bigram model here uses a 12×12 table. GPT-4 uses the same mathematics across 1.8 trillion parameters and 12,288-dimensional embeddings. The difference is not the idea — it is the scale at which the idea is applied, and the depth of context the model can attend to.

Modern LLMs replace bigram counts with neural networks that can condition on thousands of preceding tokens. But at inference time, the final step is always the same: compute a probability distribution over the vocabulary and sample the next token. What you see here is that final step, laid bare.

Try This

Build a simple bigram model: given the text “the cat sat on the mat”, what is P(cat|the)? P(mat|the)? P(sat|cat)?

“the” appears 2 times, followed by “cat” once and “mat” once. So P(cat|the) = 1/2, P(mat|the) = 1/2. “cat” appears once, followed by “sat”. P(sat|cat) = 1/1 = 1.0. This bigram model generates text by sampling one word at a time — it has no understanding, just statistics. Real LLMs are the same idea but with much larger context windows and billions of parameters.

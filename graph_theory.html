Graph Theory & Networks

The mathematics of connections — from Euler’s bridges to social networks

CS, AI & Cryptography · Difficulty 2

In 1736, the citizens of Königsberg wondered whether they could walk through their city crossing each of its seven bridges exactly once. Leonhard Euler proved it impossible — and in doing so, invented an entirely new branch of mathematics. Graph theory studies objects (vertices) and the connections between them (edges). It is the mathematics of relationship itself: friendships, hyperlinks, chemical bonds, neural pathways. Every network you have ever used is, at heart, a graph.

2. Euler Paths & Circuits

Trace each edge exactly once

Click a starting vertex, then click adjacent vertices to build a path. Each edge can only be traversed once.

Click a vertex to begin tracing a path.
Euler path exists ⇔ at most 2 vertices have odd degree
Euler circuit exists ⇔ every vertex has even degree
Euler proved that the Königsberg bridge walk is impossible because all four land masses have odd degree. You need at most two odd-degree vertices for an Euler path — and they must be your start and end.

χ(G) = chromatic number = minimum colours needed
Four Colour Theorem: every planar graph has χ(G) ≤ 4
The Four Colour Theorem (1976) was the first major theorem proved with computer assistance. It states that any map — no matter how complex — can be coloured with at most four colours so that no two adjacent regions share a colour.

Degree distribution:

Clustering coefficient C(v) = (edges among neighbours) / (possible edges among neighbours)
Average path length L = mean shortest path between all pairs
Small-world networks (like social networks) combine high clustering (your friends know each other) with short average paths (six degrees of separation). This is what makes them so efficient at spreading information — and disease.
Centrality measures — not all nodes in a network are equally important. Graph theory gives us several ways to quantify influence:
• Degree centrality — the simplest: how many connections does a node have?
• Betweenness centrality — how often does a node sit on the shortest path between other pairs? High betweenness = a critical bridge or broker.
• PageRank — a node is important if it is linked to by other important nodes (recursive definition solved via eigenvectors).
These measures are fundamental in social network analysis, identifying influential spreaders in epidemics, and ranking search results.

5. Shortest Paths — Dijkstra’s Algorithm

Click a start node, then run Dijkstra’s algorithm

Click a node to select it as the source. Press “Run Dijkstra” to animate the algorithm. Visited nodes turn green; the shortest-path tree is highlighted.

Click a node to choose the source vertex.
Dijkstra’s algorithm: greedily visit the unvisited node with smallest tentative distance.
Time complexity: O((V + E) log V) with a priority queue.
Minimum spanning trees — a spanning tree connects every vertex with the fewest possible edges and no cycles. A minimum spanning tree (MST) additionally minimises total edge weight. Prim’s and Kruskal’s algorithms find MSTs efficiently.

Real-world applications: designing power grids with minimum cable length, laying fibre-optic internet networks, circuit board wiring, and approximating solutions to the Travelling Salesman Problem.
Did you know? — Google’s original algorithm, PageRank, treats the entire web as a giant graph and finds the most “central” pages — a direct application of graph theory. Larry Page and Sergey Brin modelled each hyperlink as a directed edge and computed a random-walk stationary distribution to rank billions of pages. The maths behind the world’s most-used search engine is, at heart, a graph problem.
Why It Matters
  • Computer science foundation — algorithms for searching, sorting, and optimising all operate on graphs (trees, DAGs, flow networks).
  • Social networks — Facebook, Twitter, and LinkedIn are massive graphs. Understanding their structure reveals how information (and misinformation) spreads.
  • Internet routing — packets find their way across the globe using shortest-path algorithms on the network graph.
  • Google’s PageRank — treats the web as a directed graph and ranks pages by a random-walk process (a Markov chain on the link graph).
  • DNA sequencing — modern genome assemblers use Euler paths through de Bruijn graphs to reconstruct DNA from short fragments.
  • Neural networks — deep learning models are themselves computational graphs, with data flowing along weighted edges between nodes.

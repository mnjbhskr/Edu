Crystal Lattices & Unit Cells

The repeating geometry of solid matter

Chapter 17 · Solid State Physics · Topic 1 · Difficulty 2

Crystalline solids are built from atoms arranged in perfectly repeating patterns that extend in all directions. The smallest repeating unit — the unit cell — tiles space like an infinite wallpaper to create the full crystal. Understanding these lattice geometries reveals why metals are strong, why diamonds sparkle, and why salt forms cubes. The key idea is:

Crystal = Lattice + Basis  —  the lattice defines the repeating points, the basis defines what sits at each point

2 · 3D Unit Cells

The three cubic unit cells: Simple Cubic (SC), Body-Centred Cubic (BCC), and Face-Centred Cubic (FCC). Atoms sit at corners, body centre, and face centres respectively. The cells auto-rotate; drag to reorient.

Structure

Coordination numbers: SC → CN = 6 (each atom touches 6 neighbours), BCC → CN = 8, FCC → CN = 12. Don’t confuse packing efficiency with coordination number — BCC packs less efficiently than FCC but has a different coordination geometry. Coordination number tells you how many nearest neighbours surround each atom; packing fraction tells you how much volume they fill.

Did you know? FCC and HCP (hexagonal close-packed) both achieve 74% packing efficiency — the densest possible packing of identical spheres. Kepler conjectured this in 1611, but it wasn’t proven until Thomas Hales’s computer-assisted proof in 1998 — nearly 400 years later.

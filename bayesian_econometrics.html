1. Prior to Posterior

Set a normal prior for the mean μ. Data arrives and the posterior updates via Bayes' rule. As sample size grows, the posterior concentrates around the true value and the prior becomes irrelevant.

2. Prior Sensitivity

Three different priors (sceptical, moderate, enthusiastic) applied to the same data. With little data the posteriors diverge; with lots of data they converge.

Bernstein-von Mises theorem: Under regularity conditions, the posterior converges to a normal centred at the MLE as sample size grows. Priors become asymptotically irrelevant — but in finite samples they matter enormously.

3. Bayesian vs Frequentist

Side-by-side comparison of a 95% confidence interval and a 95% credible interval for the mean. They look similar but have fundamentally different interpretations.

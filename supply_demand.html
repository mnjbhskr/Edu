Supply & Demand

The invisible hand that sets every price

Chapter 18 · Economics & Game Theory · Topic 1 · Difficulty 1

Why does a cup of coffee cost what it does? The answer lies in the intersection of two curves. Demand slopes downward — consumers buy more when the price falls. Supply slopes upward — producers offer more when the price rises. Where these curves meet, a market finds its equilibrium: the price at which every unit produced finds a willing buyer. This elegant mechanism underlies every market transaction on earth.

Qd = a − b·P   (demand)      Qs = c + d·P   (supply)      P* = (a − c) / (b + d)

A surplus means suppliers cannot sell all they produce — they lower prices. A shortage means buyers cannot find enough — they bid prices up. Both forces push the market toward equilibrium, Adam Smith's invisible hand at work.
Consumer surplus is the triangle above the price line and below the demand curve — it represents the extra value buyers receive beyond what they pay. Producer surplus is the triangle below the price line and above the supply curve — the extra revenue sellers receive beyond their costs. Together, they measure the total welfare a market creates.
Equilibrium occurs where marginal cost equals marginal benefit. The demand curve represents marginal benefit (what the next buyer is willing to pay), and the supply curve represents marginal cost (what it costs to produce the next unit). The market naturally finds this balance — Adam Smith’s ‘invisible hand’ in mathematical form.

ε = (ΔQ / Q) / (ΔP / P) = (dQ/dP) · (P / Q)
When |ε| > 1, demand is elastic: a price increase reduces revenue. When |ε| < 1, demand is inelastic: a price increase raises revenue. At |ε| = 1, revenue is maximised. Necessities tend to be inelastic; luxuries tend to be elastic.
Different markets have very different elasticities: Housing (inelastic supply — can’t build houses overnight), Coffee (moderate elasticity — farmers can switch crops over a few years), Oil (inelastic in the short run — drilling takes time, but elastic long-term as alternatives emerge), Uber rides (highly elastic — surge pricing adjusts supply in minutes).
Did you know?
When OPEC cut oil production in 1973, prices quadrupled overnight. Because both supply and demand for oil are inelastic in the short run, even a small supply reduction causes a huge price spike. The same mathematics explains why concert ticket prices and housing rents can swing so dramatically.

Price controls create winners and losers. A ceiling below P* helps buyers who can purchase at the lower price, but creates a shortage — some buyers are shut out. A floor above P* helps sellers who can sell, but creates a surplus. Both generate a dead-weight loss: transactions that would benefit both buyer and seller never occur.

The more inelastic side of the market bears a greater share of the tax. If demand is perfectly inelastic, consumers pay the entire tax. If supply is perfectly inelastic, producers absorb it all. The dead-weight loss triangle grows with the square of the tax rate — doubling the tax quadruples the loss.
Why supply and demand matters
Supply and demand is the foundational model of economics. It explains why prices exist, how markets allocate scarce resources, and what happens when governments intervene. Every policy debate about housing, healthcare, labour, or taxation rests on these curves. The model is deliberately simple — linear curves, perfect competition, full information — yet its predictions are remarkably robust. Understanding this framework is the first step toward thinking like an economist.

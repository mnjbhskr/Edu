Risk Measures

Value at Risk, Expected Shortfall, and coherent risk

Every portfolio carries risk — the question is how to measure it. A single number that captures “how much could we lose?” is essential for capital allocation, regulatory compliance, and sound decision-making. But not all risk measures are created equal. We begin with the loss distribution, move to Value at Risk (VaR) and its superior successor Expected Shortfall (ES), and finish with the mathematical axioms that distinguish good risk measures from flawed ones.

VaR answers: “What is the worst loss I can expect X% of the time?” But it says nothing about how bad things get in the remaining tail. A portfolio with VaR = £1M could lose £2M or £200M in the worst cases.

Expected Shortfall is the average loss in the worst cases — it captures tail risk that VaR ignores. Under Basel III (FRTB), banks must now use ES rather than VaR for market risk capital requirements.

4. Coherent Risk Measures

The four axioms

1. Monotonicity
If X ≤ Y everywhere, then ρ(X) ≥ ρ(Y)
If portfolio X always loses more than Y, then X is riskier.
2. Translation Invariance
ρ(X + c) = ρ(X) − c
Adding cash c reduces risk by exactly c. Risk is denominated in the same units as the portfolio.
3. Positive Homogeneity
ρ(λX) = λρ(X)   for λ > 0
Doubling the position doubles the risk. No hidden non-linearities.
4. Subadditivity
ρ(X + Y) ≤ ρ(X) + ρ(Y)
Diversification never increases risk. The whole is no worse than the sum of parts.
Key Result
VaR is not coherent — it fails subadditivity.
Expected Shortfall is coherent.

Subadditivity Failure: The Two-Bond Example

Two bonds, each defaulting with 1% probability independently. Loss on default = £100. We use VaR at the 99% confidence level.

Bond A alone
VaR99 = £0
99% of the time: no default
Bond B alone
VaR99 = £0
99% of the time: no default
Portfolio A + B
VaR99 = £100
P(at least one default) = 1.99%

ρ(A+B) = £100 > £0 + £0 = ρ(A) + ρ(B) — diversification increased measured risk under VaR!

Subadditivity is the mathematical expression of diversification. A risk measure that violates it can punish you for diversifying — which is economically absurd. This is why regulators moved from VaR to ES.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Summary
We built risk measurement from the ground up: starting with the loss distribution generated by Monte Carlo simulation, we defined Value at Risk as a percentile of that distribution — a single threshold answering “how much could I lose?” at a given confidence. We then saw that VaR ignores everything beyond its threshold, which led us to Expected Shortfall — the average of the tail losses. Finally, we examined the four axioms of coherent risk measures and proved that VaR fails subadditivity while ES does not.
VaRα(L) = inf { x : P(L > x) ≤ 1 − α }
ESα(L) = E[ L | L > VaRα ]

Regulatory context: The Basel III Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB) mandates Expected Shortfall at the 97.5% level for market risk capital. In the UK, PRA Supervisory Statement SS1/23 sets out the implementation requirements.

From a histogram of simulated losses to the axioms of coherent risk, the thread is clear: a good risk measure must respect diversification. VaR does not; Expected Shortfall does. That mathematical distinction now underpins global banking regulation.

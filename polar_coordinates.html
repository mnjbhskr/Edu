7 · What Have We Learnt?

Key Ideas

Polar coordinates (r, θ) locate a point by distance and angle from the origin.
Conversion: x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ, r = √(x² + y²), θ = atan2(y, x).
Classic curves — circles, cardioids, limaçons, roses, spirals, and lemniscates — each have elegant polar equations.
Rose curves r = a cos(nθ) have n petals (odd n) or 2n petals (even n).
Negative r reflects a point through the origin: (−r, θ) = (r, θ + π).
Area in polar form: A = ½ ∫ r² dθ, visualised as wedge-shaped sectors.

Key Formulae

r = √(x² + y²)
θ = atan2(y, x)
x = r cos θ
y = r sin θ
A = ½ ∫ r² dθ
r = a(1 + cos θ)  [cardioid]
Connections: Polar coordinates connect naturally to complex numbers (r eiθ), parametric curves (x(θ), y(θ)), and Fourier analysis. They are essential in physics for radial symmetry problems.

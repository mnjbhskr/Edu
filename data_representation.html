The Geometry of Information

Every chart is a mathematical act — and every mathematical act has consequences

Core idea Every representation preserves some mathematical structure and destroys another — and that choice has consequences.

1. The Central Question — What Harry Beck Threw Away

In 1931, a draughtsman named Harry Beck made a radical decision. He was tasked with redesigning the London Underground map, and the existing version — geographically accurate — was a mess of overlapping lines and cramped central stations. Beck’s insight was simple and profound: travellers don’t care where stations are. They care which stations connect.

So he threw geography away. He kept topology — the pattern of connections — and discarded metric distance entirely. The result was the most copied transit map in the world. And it was, in a precise mathematical sense, a lie.

This chapter is about that choice. Every time data is represented — as a chart, a map, a network, a table — something is preserved and something is destroyed. The choice of representation is not aesthetic. It is mathematical.

2. The Four Questions — What This Chapter Teaches

Every act of representation demands four questions in sequence. Click a card to expand.

Question 1
Structure — what is the mathematical shape of your data?
+

Before any chart is drawn, data has a mathematical structure. Identifying it is the first act of honest representation.

  • Relations between objects → graph
  • Distances between objects → metric space
  • Vectors describing objects → vector space
  • Uncertainty about objects → probability distribution
  • Causal links between objects → directed acyclic graph
Question 2
Transformation — how do we move between representations?
+

A transformation changes coordinates without changing reality. The right one makes hidden structure visible.

  • High-dimensional → 2D: projection, PCA, t-SNE
  • Time → frequency: Fourier transform
  • Graph → coordinates: embedding
  • Continuous → discrete: sampling, binning
Question 3
Encoding — how do we communicate a representation visually?
+

Human perception is a mathematical channel with measurable noise. The encoding hierarchy, from most to least accurately decoded:

  • Position — the most precise
  • Length, then angle
  • Area, colour intensity
  • Shape, size, motion — least precise
Question 4
Pitfalls — what does every representation hide?
+

Every representation makes an invariant visible and destroys another. Naming the trade-off is a critical skill.

  • Topology hides distance (the Tube map)
  • Mercator hides area (world maps)
  • Bar charts hide distribution (vs violin or box plots)
  • Correlation hides causation (scatter plots)
💡 The four questions form the pedagogical arc of the entire chapter: Structure → Transformation → Encoding → Pitfalls. Every module that follows focuses on one or two of these stages.

What have we learnt?
Data is not truth. It is a representation — and the choice of representation changes what you see. Harry Beck’s Tube map is the archetypal example: by deciding that connection mattered and distance did not, he encoded a specific mathematical commitment. Every chart, map, network diagram, and scatter plot you will ever see is doing the same thing. It has invariants it preserves and distortions it introduces. This chapter teaches you to read those choices — to ask what mathematical structure the data actually has, what transformation has been applied to it, how that transformation has been encoded into pixels, and what the encoding hides. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

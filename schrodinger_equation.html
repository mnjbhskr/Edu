The Schrödinger Equation

Wavefunctions, energy quantisation, and the quantum world

The Schrödinger equation is quantum mechanics in a single formula: iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ. For time-independent systems, it reduces to Ĥψ = Eψ — an eigenvalue problem. The solutions ψn are stationary states with definite energy En. Three canonical systems — the particle in a box, quantum tunnelling, and the harmonic oscillator — reveal the universal features of quantum mechanics: discrete energy levels, zero-point energy, and the ability to penetrate classically forbidden regions.

The quantum mental model: Quantum mechanics replaces the classical trajectory of a particle with a wavefunction ψ(x,t). The wavefunction is a probability amplitude — not directly observable. What we measure is |ψ(x)|², the probability density: the chance of finding the particle near position x. The three systems below show how boundary conditions and potentials shape ψ and determine which energies are allowed.

Key insight: Quantisation arises from boundary conditions ψ(0) = ψ(L) = 0. Energy scales as E ∝ n². Zero-point energy E1 > 0 from the uncertainty principle: confining a particle to length L forces Δp ≥ ℏ/L, giving minimum kinetic energy. The particle can never be at rest inside the box.

Real-world quantum wells: electrons confined in thin semiconductor layers (GaAs/AlGaAs, ~5–20 nm wide) behave almost exactly like particles in a box. This physics powers quantum dot displays, LED colour tuning, and semiconductor lasers — the box width controls the energy gap and thus the colour of emitted light.

Not metaphorical: Quantum tunnelling powers the Sun (nuclear fusion), enables alpha decay, is exploited in tunnel diodes, and is the operating principle of the scanning tunnelling microscope (STM). Exponential sensitivity: doubling the barrier width drops the transmission by a factor of ~1010.

Barrier parameters shown in natural units. The barrier region is shaded grey. Dashed teal line = particle energy E; dashed amber line = barrier height V0.

Classical vs quantum: A classical ball rolling toward a hill taller than its kinetic energy simply bounces back — 100% reflection, 0% transmission. A quantum particle facing the same barrier has a non-zero probability of appearing on the other side. The wavefunction does not stop at the barrier edge; it decays exponentially through it. If the barrier is thin enough, a transmitted wave emerges. This has no classical analogue.

Equally spaced levels mean each photon or phonon carries exactly ℏω — the foundation of quantum field theory, where each EM field mode is a QHO. Zero-point energy E0 = ½ℏω is real and measurable: the Casimir effect (force between uncharged plates in vacuum) and zero-point motion of atoms persist even at absolute zero.

Rendered in natural units (ℏ = m = 1). Turning points xtp = ±√(2n+1) x0 where classically E = V(x). The wavefunction extends beyond the turning points — tunnelling into the classically forbidden region.

Correspondence principle: At high quantum numbers (n ≫ 10), the probability density |ψn|² approaches the classical distribution P(x) ∝ 1/√(xtp² − x²), which peaks at the turning points where the classical oscillator spends the most time. Quantum mechanics smoothly recovers classical behaviour — the “wiggles” average out as n → ∞.

Algebraic elegance: The harmonic oscillator can also be solved without differential equations using ladder operators: the creation operator a† raises the energy by one level, while the annihilation operator a lowers it. The condition a|0⟩ = 0 defines the ground state, and En = (n + ½)ℏω follows from pure algebra. This operator approach is the foundation of quantum field theory.

The Mathematics of Quantum Mechanics

The Schrödinger equation is the quantum analogue of Newton’s second law. Where F = ma governs the trajectory of a classical particle, iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ governs the time evolution of the quantum state ψ. The Hamiltonian operator Ĥ = −(ℏ²/2m)∇² + V encodes kinetic plus potential energy, acting on ψ as a differential operator rather than multiplying a number.

The three systems on this page are the canonical archetypes of quantum mechanics. The infinite square well illustrates hard quantisation from boundary conditions. Tunnelling reveals that quantum particles can exist where classical energy is negative. The harmonic oscillator, with its ladder of equally spaced levels, is the exact model used in quantum field theory to describe photons, phonons, and all bosonic particles.

Time-independent Schrödinger Ĥψ = Eψ is a matrix eigenvalue problem — the same mathematics as eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The allowed energies En are eigenvalues; the wavefunctions ψn are eigenvectors. Diagonalising the Hamiltonian matrix gives the complete energy spectrum, a technique used in quantum chemistry and condensed matter physics.

The measurement postulate is the strangest part: |ψ(x)|² is the probability density of finding the particle at position x. The wavefunction encodes all possible outcomes simultaneously; measurement collapses it to one. Before measurement, the particle genuinely does not have a definite position — this is not ignorance but the fundamental structure of quantum reality (confirmed by Bell inequality experiments).

The Schrödinger equation underpins virtually all of modern technology and science. Lasers exploit stimulated emission between quantised atomic levels (see atomic energy levels). MRI scanners exploit nuclear spin quantisation. Transistors and tunnel diodes rely on quantum tunnelling. All of chemistry follows from the electronic structure of atoms, which is nothing but the Schrödinger equation applied to Coulomb potentials.

# Literature Review: Best Practices in Mathematics Education

*Compiled March 2026 for mathsedu.org*

---

## 1. Key Researchers and Their Findings

### Jo Boaler (Stanford University)
**Core work:** *Mathematical Mindsets* (2015); youcubed.org; "Fluency Without Fear" (2015)

- **Visual mathematics and brain science:** Neuroimaging shows mathematical thinking is grounded in visual processing even for purely numerical tasks. When students engage visual brain pathways alongside numerical ones, brain communication is enhanced.
- **Number sense over memorisation:** High-achieving students use number sense (flexible strategies), not rote recall. Low achievers are often on the wrong path because they were taught to memorise rather than understand (Gray & Tall, 1994).
- **Timed tests cause maths anxiety:** ~1/3 of students develop anxiety from timed testing. MRI studies show stress blocks working memory, preventing access to facts students actually know (Beilock, 2011; Ramirez et al, 2013).
- **Two brain pathways:** The most successful maths students use *both* numerical/symbolic AND intuitive/spatial reasoning pathways (Park & Brannon, 2013).
- **Strategy beats drill:** Brain research shows learning through strategies (not memorisation) leads to "superior performance" and better transfer to new problems (Delazer et al, 2005).
- **PISA data:** From 13 million students worldwide, the lowest achievers are those who focus on memorisation. Highest achievers focus on big ideas and connections between concepts (Boaler & Zoido).
- **Growth mindset:** An 18-lesson teaching intervention raised students' test scores by an average of 50%.

**Important caveat:** Boaler's work has faced academic scrutiny. A formal complaint documented 52 instances of alleged misstatement of cited studies. Developmental psychologist Daniel Ansari noted newer, larger studies indicate growth mindset effects are "at best, modest — and possibly, nonexistent." These controversies are important context.

**Key citations:**
- Boaler, J. (2015). *Mathematical Mindsets*. Jossey-Bass.
- Boaler, J. (2015). "Fluency Without Fear." youcubed, Stanford University.
- Boaler, J. (2021). "The Transformative Impact of a Mathematical Mindset Experience Taught at Scale." *Frontiers in Education*, 6, 784393.

### Richard Skemp (1919–1995)
**Core work:** "Relational Understanding and Instrumental Understanding" (1976)

- **Instrumental understanding:** Knowing rules and procedures without knowing why ("rules without reasons"). Produces quick results, is easy to test, but is brittle and non-transferable.
- **Relational understanding:** Knowing both what to do and why. More adaptable to new tasks because students understand the reasoning behind methods.
- Skemp argued that instrumental understanding dominates because it produces quick rewards and easy-to-mark tests, but relational understanding is far more beneficial for long-term learning and transfer.

**Key citation:** Skemp, R.R. (1976). "Relational Understanding and Instrumental Understanding." *Mathematics Teaching*, 77, 20–26.

### Jerome Bruner (1915–2016)
**Core work:** *Toward a Theory of Instruction* (1966); CPA framework

Three modes of representation through which learning occurs:

1. **Enactive (Concrete):** Learning through physical action — manipulating objects such as base-ten blocks, counters, multi-link cubes.
2. **Iconic (Pictorial):** Learning through visual representations — sketches, bar models, part-whole diagrams.
3. **Symbolic (Abstract):** Learning through notation — numbers, letters, operation signs.

The CPA (Concrete–Pictorial–Abstract) approach derives directly from Bruner's sequence and has become the backbone of Singapore Mathematics.

**Key citation:** Bruner, J.S. (1966). *Toward a Theory of Instruction*. Harvard University Press.

### Lev Vygotsky (1896–1934)
**Core work:** *Mind in Society* (1978); Zone of Proximal Development

- **Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD):** The gap between what a learner can do independently and what they can achieve with guidance. Learning occurs most effectively within this zone.
- **Scaffolding** (term coined by Wood, Bruner & Ross, 1976): Temporary support that helps learners progress through their ZPD, gradually withdrawn as competence grows.
- In maths, the ZPD is operationalised through worked examples, teacher modelling before independent practice, and peer explanation during group work.

**Key citations:**
- Vygotsky, L.S. (1978). *Mind in Society*. Harvard University Press.
- Wood, D., Bruner, J.S., & Ross, G. (1976). "The Role of Tutoring in Problem Solving." *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry*, 17(2), 89–100.

### George Polya (1887–1985)
**Core work:** *How to Solve It* (1945)

Four-step problem-solving framework:
1. **Understand the problem** — restate in your own words, clarify unknowns and givens
2. **Devise a plan** — select a strategy connecting the given data to the desired conclusion
3. **Carry out the plan** — execute the strategy, pivoting if needed
4. **Look back** — verify results, interpret the solution, reflect on the process

This framework develops metacognition — the ability to think about one's own thinking. Continuously in print since 1945.

**Key citation:** Polya, G. (1945). *How to Solve It*. Princeton University Press.

### John Hattie
**Core work:** *Visible Learning* (2008); *Visible Learning for Mathematics* (2017)

Mega-meta-analysis synthesising 2,100+ meta-analyses covering 300 million+ students:

- **Benchmark:** Effect size of 0.40 represents one year's typical growth.
- **Highest-impact strategies:** Collective teacher efficacy (1.57), self-assessment (1.33), formative evaluation (0.90), classroom discussion (0.82), scaffolding (0.82), deliberate practice (0.79), teacher clarity (0.75), feedback (0.70–0.73).
- **Mathematics-specific:** Problem-solving teaching outperforms problem-based learning. Worked examples reduce cognitive load. The right strategy depends on the learning phase: surface (direct instruction, worked examples), deep (rich tasks, discussion), transfer (real-world application).

**Caveat:** Schulmeister and Loviscach (2014) identified "considerable issues" in study selection and statistical methods. Effect sizes can be misleading when aggregated across diverse interventions.

**Key citations:**
- Hattie, J. (2008). *Visible Learning*. Routledge.
- Hattie, J., Fisher, D., & Frey, N. (2017). *Visible Learning for Mathematics*. Corwin.

### Seymour Papert (1928–2016)
**Core work:** *Mindstorms: Children, Computers, and Powerful Ideas* (1980)

- **Constructionism:** People build knowledge most effectively when actively engaged in constructing things in the world.
- **Logo and Turtle Graphics:** Children learn geometry and mathematics by writing code, developing computational thinking alongside mathematical thinking.
- **Key insight:** Computer programming and debugging give children a way to think about their own thinking (metacognition).
- **Legacy:** Directly influenced Scratch, LEGO Mindstorms, and the maker movement.

**Key citation:** Papert, S. (1980). *Mindstorms: Children, Computers, and Powerful Ideas*. Basic Books.

### Zoltan Dienes (1916–2014)
**Core work:** Six Stages of Learning Mathematics; Dienes blocks; Variability Principles

- **Multiple embodiments:** Mathematical structures should be taught through manipulatives, games, stories, and dance. By experiencing the same abstract structure through multiple representations, learners discover the underlying concept.
- **Dienes blocks:** Invented for teaching place value.
- **Key claim:** Using manipulative materials, games and stories, children can learn more complex maths at a younger age than previously thought.

**Key citation:** Dienes, Z.P. (1960). *Building Up Mathematics*. Hutchinson.

## 2. Major Frameworks and Approaches

### Singapore Mathematics
- Built on Bruner's CPA approach with bar modelling as a central problem-solving tool.
- Teaches fewer topics in greater depth ("mastery" approach).
- Singapore consistently tops TIMSS and PISA international benchmarks.
- Now used in 40+ countries including UK and US.

**Key citation:** Kho, T.H., Yeo, S.M., & Lim, J. (2009). *The Singapore Model Method for Learning Mathematics*. EPB Pan Pacific.

### Realistic Mathematics Education (RME)
- Developed at the Freudenthal Institute, Netherlands, from the 1970s.
- Core principle: Mathematics is a human activity, not a pre-formed body of knowledge to be transmitted.
- Three principles: guided reinvention, didactic phenomenology, emergent models.
- Students start from contextual problems, progress through horizontal mathematisation (informal strategies) to vertical mathematisation (formal mathematics).
- Research shows large effect sizes: d = 1.19 for achievement, d = 2.61 for attitudes.

**Key citation:** Freudenthal, H. (1991). *Revisiting Mathematics Education*. Kluwer.

### Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL)
- Strong empirical evidence that IBL enhances conceptual understanding, problem-solving, and motivation.
- Most effective when structured with appropriate scaffolding rather than pure discovery.

### Mastery-Based Learning (NCETM)
- NCETM awarded £40M to spread "Teaching for Mastery" via 40 Maths Hubs across England.
- Based on East Asian approaches: whole-class teaching, depth before breadth, CPA progression.

### Productive Struggle and Desirable Difficulties
- **Productive struggle:** Students benefit from engaging with problems without obvious solutions but within reach.
- **Spaced practice:** Increases long-term retention significantly vs massed practice.
- **Interleaving:** In one study, interleavers scored 63% vs 20% for blockers on delayed tests.
- **Desirable difficulties** (Robert Bjork): Spacing, interleaving, and retrieval practice improve retention by up to 80% vs cramming.

**Key citations:**
- Bjork, R.A. (1994). "Memory and metamemory considerations in the training of human beings." In *Metacognition: Knowing about Knowing*.
- Rohrer, D., Dedrick, R.F., & Stershic, S. (2015). "Interleaved practice improves mathematics learning." *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 107(3), 900–908.

## 3. Key Research Findings

### Visualisation in Mathematical Understanding
- Brain imaging confirms mathematical thinking recruits visual-spatial processing areas (bilateral parietal lobes) even for purely numerical tasks.
- Professional mathematicians show nearly double the activity in the inferotemporal region when presented with numbers/equations.
- **Implication:** Curricula that encourage visual thinking align with how the brain naturally processes mathematics.

**Key citation:** Dehaene, S., et al. (1999). "Sources of mathematical thinking: Behavioral and brain-imaging evidence." *Science*, 284(5416), 970–974.

### Interactive vs Passive Learning
- Meta-analysis of 225 STEM studies: active learning led to increased exam performance; students in traditional lectures were **1.5 times more likely to fail**.
- Interactive simulations (PhET, GeoGebra) consistently outperform static content.

**Key citation:** Freeman, S., et al. (2014). "Active learning increases student performance in science, engineering, and mathematics." *PNAS*, 111(23), 8410–8415.

### Growth Mindset — Current Evidence
- **Nature study (2019):** National US experiment found growth mindset interventions improved grades for lower-achieving students, particularly in schools with supportive norms.
- **Current consensus:** Effects are real but modest, highly context-dependent, and most effective when paired with changes in teaching practice.

**Key citation:** Yeager, D.S., et al. (2019). "A national experiment reveals where a growth mindset improves achievement." *Nature*, 573, 364–369.

### Timed Tests and Maths Anxiety
- Timed testing is associated with early onset of maths anxiety in ~1/3 of students.
- Anxiety is not correlated with ability — anxious students may perform well on untimed assessments.
- NCTM position: timed tests do not assess fluency and can negatively affect students.

### Conceptual Understanding vs Procedural Fluency
- **Current consensus:** Not either/or but both/and. The relationship is bidirectional and iterative.
- Conceptual understanding should precede or accompany procedural instruction, with explicit connections.

### Multiple Representations
- Using algebraic, graphical, numerical, and verbal representations together deepens understanding.
- Technology enabling simultaneous access to multiple representations moves students from procedural to conceptual thinking.
- Lesh's translation model: real-world situations, manipulatives, pictures/diagrams, verbal symbols, written symbols.

### Technology in Mathematics Education
- Meta-analysis of 92 studies: digital tool use has a positive overall effect on student learning.
- Intelligent tutoring systems and dynamic tools (GeoGebra, Desmos) are significantly more beneficial than drill-and-practice.
- Most effective when used **in addition to** other instruction, not as a substitute.
- PISA 2022: students spending up to 1 hour/day on digital devices for learning scored 14 points higher.

## 4. Leading Organisations and Their Recommendations

### NCTM — *Principles to Actions* (2014)
Eight effective mathematics teaching practices:
1. Establish mathematics goals to focus learning
2. Implement tasks that promote reasoning and problem-solving
3. Use and connect mathematical representations
4. Facilitate meaningful mathematical discourse
5. Pose purposeful questions
6. Build procedural fluency from conceptual understanding
7. Support productive struggle in learning mathematics
8. Elicit and use evidence of student thinking

**Key citation:** NCTM (2014). *Principles to Actions: Ensuring Mathematical Success for All*.

### National Academies — *Adding It Up* (2001)
Five intertwined strands of mathematical proficiency:
1. **Conceptual understanding** — comprehension of concepts, operations, relations
2. **Procedural fluency** — carrying out procedures flexibly, accurately, efficiently
3. **Strategic competence** — formulating, representing, solving problems
4. **Adaptive reasoning** — logical thought, reflection, justification
5. **Productive disposition** — seeing maths as sensible, useful, worthwhile

**Key citation:** National Research Council (2001). *Adding It Up: Helping Children Learn Mathematics*.

### OECD PISA 2022
- Mean maths performance across OECD fell by a record 15 points between 2018–2022.
- Singapore led global rankings (575 points vs OECD average 472).
- Teacher availability to help had the strongest relationship to performance.
- Moderate digital device use (up to 1 hr/day) associated with 14-point advantage.

### Royal Society / ACME (UK) — Mathematical Futures (2024)
- Calls for radical reform: fusing mathematics, statistics, and data science.
- Recommends wider classroom use of calculators, spreadsheets, apps, and AI tools.
- Proposes a 10–15 year implementation timeline.

### UNESCO
- Globally, only 44% of primary students achieve minimum maths proficiency.
- Recommends greater focus on statistics, data analysis, and computational thinking.
- Focus on STEM education equity, especially for girls.

## 5. Benchmark: Exemplary Digital Mathematics Resources

### 3Blue1Brown (Grant Sanderson)
- 7.85M+ YouTube subscribers, 224 videos on higher mathematics from a visual perspective.
- Custom Python animation library (Manim) for visualising abstract concepts.
- Philosophy: emphasises emotion, wonder, and imagination over utility.
- Won Joint Policy Board for Mathematics Communications Award (2023).

### Desmos
- Interactive graphing calculator and classroom activity platform.
- Research shows statistically significant improvement in function understanding.
- Dynamic, real-time feedback on input-output relationships.

### GeoGebra
- Dynamic geometry, algebra, calculus, and statistics combined.
- Positive effect on achievement vs traditional methods.
- Free and open-source.

### Khan Academy
- Free, structured learning paths with mastery-based progression.
- Multiple research studies show positive learning outcomes.
- Procedural practice emphasis with conceptual explanation via video.

### Brilliant.org
- Interactive, problem-solving-first approach.
- Emphasises learning through doing rather than watching.

### NRICH (Cambridge University)
- Problem-solving resources for ages 3–19.
- "Low-threshold/high-ceiling" philosophy.
- Free resources for teachers and parents.

### PhET Simulations (University of Colorado Boulder)
- Founded by Nobel Laureate Carl Wieman (2002).
- Free interactive simulations based on extensive education research.
- Game-like environment promoting exploration and discovery.
- Research shows significantly higher achievement vs traditional lecture.

### Mathigon
- Interactive textbook format with built-in interactivity.

### Cut the Knot (Alexander Bogomolny, 1946–2018)
- Pioneer of mathematical education on the internet (founded 1996).
- Focused on popular exposition, heuristics, and mathematical puzzles.

### Better Explained (Kalid Azad)
- Philosophy: build deep, natural intuition rather than rote memorisation.
- Emphasis on clarity, analogy, and conceptual understanding.

## 6. Convergent Principles

The research literature converges on ten principles:

1. **Visual-first design is neurologically sound.** Brain imaging confirms mathematical thinking is grounded in visual-spatial processing.
2. **Active exploration beats passive consumption.** Active learning reduces failure rates by 1.5x.
3. **Conceptual understanding must come first, or at least simultaneously.** Understanding *why* should precede or accompany learning *how*.
4. **Multiple representations deepen understanding.** Connecting algebraic, graphical, numerical, and verbal forms builds robust knowledge.
5. **Low floor, high ceiling tasks engage all learners.** Accessible entry points with room for extension.
6. **Productive struggle is valuable, but scaffolding is essential.** Carefully calibrated challenge with appropriate support.
7. **Spaced practice and interleaving dramatically improve retention.** Among the most robust findings in cognitive science.
8. **Avoid timed pressure; cultivate mathematical identity.** Timed tests cause anxiety without assessing fluency.
9. **Digital tools work best as supplements with guidance.** Technology alone is insufficient; it needs pedagogical intent.
10. **Context and real-world connections motivate.** Mathematics as human activity, not abstract symbol manipulation.

Ad Auctions and Mechanism Design

The mathematics that powers billions of daily auctions — and why truth-telling matters

Every second, you search Google. Every second, an auction happens. Not an auction you see — an invisible auction where advertisers bid for that precious real estate beside your search results. Billions of times per day, without fanfare, the mathematics of mechanism design decides which ad you see and how much the advertiser pays. The stakes are vast: Google generated over £69 billion in advertising revenue in 2023. The mathematics underlying these auctions is elegant, counterintuitive, and worth understanding.

1. The Problem with First-Price Auctions

Imagine a simple auction: the highest bidder wins and pays their own bid. This is called a first-price auction. It seems fair — you bid, you win, you pay what you said. But there's a hidden problem.

If you truly value something at £100, should you bid £100? No. If you bid £100 and win, you pay £100. But you might have won for less. Everyone knows this, so everyone shades their bids — bidding less than their true value. The result is a delicate dance of guess-the-guess-the-bid.

The Nash Equilibrium Problem

In a first-price auction, there is a unique Nash equilibrium where every bidder shades their bid by exactly the same amount. But this equilibrium depends on the number of bidders and their value distributions — information no one fully knows. The result: bidders must guess, and the auction is inefficient.

Key insight: In a first-price auction, truthful bidding is never optimal. You always want to shade your bid below your true value. This creates uncertainty and inefficiency.

2. Vickrey's Breakthrough: Second-Price Auctions

In 1961, economist William Vickrey proposed a stunning idea: what if the winner pays the second-highest bid instead of their own? This simple change transforms incentives completely.

Vickrey's insight: In a second-price auction, bidding your true value is a dominant strategy — it's your best move regardless of what others bid.

Here's why: Suppose your true value is £100. If you bid £100, you win if anyone bids less than £100, and you pay their second-highest bid. If you bid £120 instead, you still pay the same second price if you win (because it depends only on others' bids, not yours). But now you win some auctions where the second price is between £100 and £120 — auctions you should never win because they make you lose money. Conversely, if you bid £80, you lose auctions where the second price is between £80 and £100, but those auctions would have made you money.

The Dominant Strategy Proof

Let v be your true value, b be your bid, and p be the second-highest bid.

Case 1: You bid b > p (you win) Payoff = v - p Case 2: You bid b < p (you lose) Payoff = 0 Case 3: Truthful bidding (b = v) If v > p, you win and earn v - p If v < p, you lose and earn 0 This is optimal because: - If you bid above v, you sometimes win when p > v (negative payoff) - If you bid below v, you sometimes lose when p < v (missed positive payoff)

Vickrey's mechanism elegantly aligns individual incentives with truthfulness. No matter what you think others will bid, telling the truth is always best.

Why did Vickrey win the Nobel Prize? Vickrey's 1961 paper wasn't recognised immediately. But decades later, mechanism designers realised his insight was foundational. He showed that you could design rules that make honest behaviour optimal. This unlocked an entire field. He won the Nobel Prize in 1996 at age 82, just three days before his death.

3. Google's Ad Auction: Generalised Second-Price

Google doesn't run a simple second-price auction. There are multiple ad slots (top position, second position, third position), and each slot has a different click-through rate (CTR). An ad in the top position gets more clicks than one in position three.

Google uses Generalised Second-Price (GSP) auction. The key insight: rank advertisers by bid × quality score, and charge each the minimum bid they'd need to keep their position.

How GSP Works

Ranking Formula Position is determined by: Bid × Quality Score. Higher scores get higher positions (more clicks). But you pay only enough to beat the advertiser below you.

There are three ad slots with click-through rates (CTR) of 5%, 2%, and 0.5%. This means an ad in the top slot has a 5% chance of being clicked per search, compared to 0.5% in position three.

4. Mechanism Design: The Revelation Principle

Mechanism design is sometimes called "reverse game theory". Instead of analysing a given game and predicting behaviour, mechanism designers start with desired outcomes and design rules to achieve them.

The foundational result is the Revelation Principle (discovered independently by Gibbard, Satterthwaite, and others): any outcome achievable by any mechanism can be achieved by a mechanism where participants have incentive to tell the truth about their preferences.

Key Desiderata of an Auction Mechanism

Incentive Compatibility (IC) Truth-telling is optimal for every participant, regardless of what others do. Vickrey auctions satisfy this; first-price auctions do not.
Individual Rationality (IR) Participating is better than not participating. No one should be forced to lose money by taking part.
Efficiency The item goes to the person who values it most (in a single-item auction). Resources are allocated to those who will create the most value.
Budget Balance (for auctioneer) The auctioneer should not lose money. Revenue should cover costs (or better).

Myerson's Optimal Auction

Roger Myerson (2007 Nobel Prize) proved a deeper result: there is an optimal auction that maximises the auctioneer's expected revenue whilst maintaining incentive compatibility and individual rationality.

The result is surprising: the optimal auction sometimes does not allocate the item to the highest bidder. Instead, it raises the "reserve price" faced by bidders to extract more surplus from the market.

Myerson's Optimal Auction The auctioneer should set a reserve price r such that: r = arg max E[Revenue] Even if the highest bidder exceeds r, other bids must also exceed r to be considered. This balances two goals: - Extracting surplus (high reserve price) - Ensuring enough competition (low reserve price)

5. The Scale of It: Why the Mathematics Matters

Google processes over 8.5 billion searches per day. For each search, an ad auction runs in milliseconds. The stakes are enormous.

Numbers That Illustrate the Impact

Metric Value Impact
Daily Google searches ~8.5 billion Each one might trigger an ad auction
Annual Google advertising revenue (2023) £69+ billion Largest portion of Google's £279 billion total revenue
Auctions per second ~98,000 Millisecond-precision optimisation needed
Estimated efficiency gain from IC mechanisms 5–10% 5–10% of £69 billion is £3.5–6.9 billion per year
Why incentive compatibility matters: If switching from a non-IC to an IC mechanism increases efficiency by even 1%, that's hundreds of millions of pounds per year. Getting the mathematics right has enormous economic consequences. This is why top companies invest in auction theory research.

Beyond Search Ads

The same principles apply to display advertising (banner ads across the web), video ads (YouTube), and programmatic advertising (real-time bidding for ad placements). The VCG mechanism is used in many of these systems.

Even non-auction contexts use mechanism design: spectrum auctions (where governments sell radio frequencies), market design (how Airbnb or Uber set commission structures), and voting systems all rely on similar principles.

The story of auctions is the story of incentives. When you design a system — whether an auction, a market, or a tax — you're designing incentives. Small changes in rules create large changes in behaviour. William Vickrey showed that one simple change (pay the second price instead of your own) transforms dishonesty into honesty. That insight, worth a Nobel Prize, has shaped how billions of pounds flow through the global ad market.

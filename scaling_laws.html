Scaling Laws

Why bigger is reliably better — the power laws that predict intelligence

The most remarkable discovery in modern AI is that performance follows simple power laws. Double the parameters, and loss drops by a predictable amount. Double the data, and loss drops again. These scaling laws — straight lines on log-log paper — have guided every major training decision from GPT-2 to GPT-4. They turn the art of AI into something closer to engineering.

Loss here refers to cross-entropy loss on next-token prediction — the standard training objective for all large language models.

L(N) ≈ (N0 / N)αN   where αN ≈ 0.076
L(D) ≈ (D0 / D)αD   where αD ≈ 0.095
L(C) ≈ (C0 / C)αC   where αC ≈ 0.050
If you plot loss vs parameters on log-log axes, you get a straight line. This means AI capabilities follow a power law — predictable, systematic, and (so far) unbroken. Each 10× increase in parameters reduces loss by a fixed percentage. This is why labs spend billions on scale: the returns are reliable.
Diminishing absolute returns — same 10× increase, shrinking gains:
10M → 100M params:   loss drops from ~3.65 to ~3.07   ΔL ≈ 0.58
10B → 100B params:   loss drops from ~2.17 to ~1.82   ΔL ≈ 0.35
Same 10× increase in parameters. Same relative improvement (~19%). But dramatically smaller absolute gain. This is the nature of power laws — each order of magnitude buys less.

On log-log axes, bigger is better — and the relationship is a straight line.

Adjust compute budget to see optimal allocation.
C ≈ 6 × N × D   (compute ≈ 6 × params × tokens)
Chinchilla rule: D ≈ 20 × N
So: Nopt ≈ (C / 120)0.5   and   Dopt ≈ (C × 20 / 6)0.5
The Chinchilla paper (Hoffmann et al., 2022) showed that for optimal performance, you should train on approximately 20 tokens per parameter. GPT-3 used only 1.7 tokens per parameter — it was starved of data. Chinchilla, with 70B parameters trained on 1.4T tokens, outperformed the 175B-parameter GPT-3 whilst using the same compute budget.
Loss never reaches zero. There is an irreducible entropy floor — Lirr ≈ 1.69 nats — determined by the inherent unpredictability of natural language. Even a perfect model cannot predict every next token.

Optimal scaling means balancing model size and data — not just making the model bigger.

Why This Matters

Scaling laws are the closest thing AI has to a law of nature. They tell you, before spending a single GPU-hour, roughly how well a model of a given size will perform. This transforms AI development from expensive trial-and-error into principled engineering. Every major lab — OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic — uses scaling laws to decide how to allocate billions of dollars of compute.

The Chinchilla insight reshaped the industry: LLaMA, Mistral, and subsequent models all adopted data-rich training regimes. The era of "just make it bigger" gave way to "make it bigger and feed it proportionally more data."

References: Kaplan et al., "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" (2020) · Hoffmann et al., "Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models" (Chinchilla, 2022)

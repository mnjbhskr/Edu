Chemical Kinetics

How fast reactions happen — and why

Chapter 16 · Chemistry · Topic 2 · Difficulty 2

Chemical kinetics is the study of reaction rates — how quickly reactants are consumed and products are formed. While thermodynamics tells us whether a reaction can occur, kinetics tells us how fast it will proceed. The rate depends on concentration, temperature, and the presence of catalysts, all governed by elegant mathematical laws. From enzyme activity in your cells to the rusting of iron, kinetics shapes the pace of the chemical world.

Rate = k[A]n    (rate law)
k = A·e−Ea/RT    (Arrhenius equation)
k = rate constant  |  n = reaction order  |  Ea = activation energy

The trick to finding reaction order: plot [A] vs t, ln[A] vs t, and 1/[A] vs t. Whichever gives a straight line reveals the order (0, 1, or 2 respectively). The slope of that line gives ±k. Adjust k to match the data points.

The Arrhenius plot of ln(k) vs 1/T gives a straight line with slope −Ea/R. A steeper slope means the reaction is more sensitive to temperature. Doubling Ea can change the rate by orders of magnitude. This is why refrigerating food slows spoilage — the chemistry of decay has a significant Ea.
For a reaction to occur, molecules must collide with (1) sufficient energy to overcome the activation barrier, and (2) the correct orientation. Only a tiny fraction of collisions satisfy both conditions — this is why reactions have activation energies.
The Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution tells us what fraction of molecules have enough energy to react. At temperature T, the fraction with energy above Ea is approximately e−Ea/RT. Raising the temperature shifts the distribution, dramatically increasing this fraction — a 10°C rise typically doubles the reaction rate.

The intermediate B rises then falls — it is produced by the first step and consumed by the second. When k₂ >> k₁, B barely accumulates: this is the steady-state approximation, where d[B]/dt ≈ 0 and [B] ≈ k₁[A]/k₂. The rate-determining step is the slowest one.

A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. It is not consumed in the reaction — it lowers the barrier for both forward and reverse reactions equally. Even a modest reduction in Ea can increase the rate by factors of thousands, because the rate depends exponentially on Ea.
Enzymes are biological catalysts that can accelerate reactions by factors of 106 to 1012 — far more effective than any industrial catalyst. They work by binding the substrate in a precise orientation (the ‘lock and key’ model), lowering both the energy barrier and the orientation requirement simultaneously.
Did you know?  The Haber process (N2 + 3H2 → 2NH3) uses an iron catalyst at 450°C and 200 atm. Without the catalyst, the activation energy is so high that the reaction essentially never occurs at any temperature. This single reaction produces 150 million tonnes of ammonia per year — feeding roughly half the world’s population through fertiliser.
Why It Matters

Chemical kinetics underpins all of chemistry and biochemistry. Every industrial process, from petroleum refining to pharmaceutical manufacturing, is optimised by understanding how reaction rates depend on concentration, temperature, and catalysts.

The Arrhenius equation (1889) revealed the profound connection between temperature and reaction rate. Its exponential dependence on activation energy explains why small temperature changes can have dramatic effects — a 10°C rise often doubles or triples a reaction rate.

Catalysis is transformative. Enzymes — biological catalysts — accelerate reactions by factors of 106 to 1017, making life possible. Industrial catalysts like those in the Haber process (ammonia synthesis) feed billions of people. Catalytic converters clean exhaust gases in every modern car.

Connections: Rate equations are ordinary differential equations (Chapter 6). The exponential decay of first-order kinetics connects to radioactive decay (Chapter 12 — Nuclear Physics). The Boltzmann distribution underlying the Arrhenius equation links to statistical mechanics and thermodynamics.

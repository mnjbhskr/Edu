Quantum Tunnelling

Particles penetrating barriers classically forbidden

Physics · Difficulty 3 (Advanced)

In classical mechanics a ball rolling towards a hill with insufficient energy simply bounces back. Quantum mechanics tells a different story. A particle described by a wavefunction has a non-zero probability of appearing on the far side of a barrier it classically cannot surmount. This phenomenon — quantum tunnelling — underpins radioactive decay, scanning tunnelling microscopes, and the nuclear fusion that powers the stars.

Classical vs quantum: A classical particle with E < V₀ is reflected with 100% certainty. Quantum mechanically, the wavefunction decays exponentially inside the barrier but does not vanish — there is always a transmitted component on the other side.

Exponential decay inside the barrier: When E < V₀, the wavefunction inside the barrier has the form ψ ∼ e−κx where κ = √(2m(V₀ − E))/ℏ. The thicker or taller the barrier, the more the wavefunction is suppressed — but it never reaches exactly zero.

T = 1 / ( 1 + V₀² sin²(k′a) / (4E(E − V₀)) )    for E > V₀
Ramsauer–Townsend resonances: When E > V₀, the sin² term in the denominator can vanish whenever k′a = nπ. At these resonance energies the barrier becomes perfectly transparent (T = 1) — a purely wave-mechanical phenomenon with no classical analogue.

4. Applications of Tunnelling

α Decay

An alpha particle trapped inside a nucleus by the strong force tunnels through the Coulomb barrier. The decay rate depends exponentially on the barrier width — explaining the Geiger–Nuttall law relating half-life to alpha energy.

Scanning Tunnelling Microscope

A sharp tip is brought within ∼1 nm of a surface. Electrons tunnel across the vacuum gap, producing a current exponentially sensitive to distance. By scanning the tip, atomic-resolution images of surfaces are obtained.

Tunnel Diode

In a heavily doped p–n junction, electrons tunnel through the depletion region. This produces a region of negative differential resistance — as voltage increases, current initially decreases — enabling high-speed oscillators.

Stellar Fusion

At the core of the Sun, protons lack the energy to overcome their mutual Coulomb repulsion classically. Quantum tunnelling allows them to fuse, powering the pp chain that converts hydrogen to helium and sustains the star.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Summary

  • A quantum particle encountering a potential barrier V₀ > E has a non-zero probability of appearing on the far side — quantum tunnelling.
  • Inside the barrier the wavefunction decays exponentially: ψ ∼ e−κx where κ = √(2m(V₀ − E))/ℏ.
  • The transmission coefficient is dominated by the exponential factor:
T ≈ e−2κa
  • Tunnelling probability is exquisitely sensitive to barrier width (a) and height (V₀ − E).
  • For E > V₀, wave-mechanical resonances (Ramsauer–Townsend) produce perfect transmission at specific energies.
  • Tunnelling is not a curiosity — it drives nuclear decay, powers stars, and enables atomic-resolution microscopy.
Connections: Quantum tunnelling connects to the wave–particle duality explored in our wave mechanics pages, to nuclear physics via alpha decay, and to the Schrödinger equation that governs all non-relativistic quantum behaviour.

Random Walks & Diffusion

From pollen grains to bacteria — how randomness creates order

Chapter 15 — Biology & Life Sciences · Topic 6 · Difficulty 2
Conceptual arc: This page connects random microscopic steps to macroscopic diffusion laws in four stages.  1. 2D Random Walk — watch particles wander and discover the √t law.  2. The Diffusion Equation — from particles to smooth concentration profiles.  3. Chemotaxis — how bacteria bias their random walks to find food.  4. Diffusion in Biology — why cells are small and why we need blood.
A simple question: If a drunk person walks randomly left or right, how far do they end up after 100 steps? Answer: distance ≈ √n ≈ 10 steps — one of the most surprising results in mathematics. Not 100, not 50, but just 10. Random steps cancel each other out, so you drift far more slowly than you walk.

Key insight: Each walker goes in a random direction every step, yet the average distance from origin grows as √t, not t. After 100 steps you are only ~10 step-lengths away. This is why diffusion is slow over large distances.
From random walks to the diffusion equation:
Random walk  →  ∂P/∂t = D∇²P
When you zoom out from millions of random-walking particles, their collective behaviour obeys the diffusion equation exactly. The random motion of individual particles produces smooth, predictable diffusion at large scales — disorder at the micro level creates order at the macro level.

4. Diffusion in Biology — Why Cells Are Small

Diffusion Time: t ≈ x² / 2D

The scaling problem: Diffusion time scales as x². Oxygen crosses a 10 μm cell in ~0.25 ms — plenty fast. But a 1 cm tissue? That takes ~14 hours. A 1 m organism? ~160 years. This is precisely why multicellular life invented circulatory systems — convection beats diffusion at every scale above a few hundred microns.
Applications of random walks: The mathematics on this page appears across science and beyond.
• Brownian motion — pollen grains jittering in water, first observed by Robert Brown in 1827.
• Stock prices — financial models treat price changes as a random walk (the basis of the Black–Scholes equation).
• Animal foraging — organisms from albatrosses to bacteria use random-walk strategies (Lévy flights) to search for patchy food.
• Molecular diffusion — oxygen, nutrients, and signalling molecules all reach their targets by diffusion through cells and tissues.
Did you know? Einstein’s 1905 paper on Brownian motion explained the random jittering of pollen grains in water using the mathematics of random walks. By predicting the exact relationship between particle displacement and time, he provided the first definitive evidence that atoms exist — a fact still debated by some scientists at the time.
Why It Matters
Einstein’s 1905 paper on Brownian motion proved atoms exist by showing that random molecular collisions explain the jittery movement of pollen grains in water. The same diffusion equation ∂C/∂t = D∇²C governs heat flow in physics, drug delivery in pharmacology, cellular signalling in biology, trait frequency evolution in population genetics, and even option pricing (Black–Scholes) in finance.

1. Exponential vs Logistic Growth

Key insight: Exponential growth (dN/dt = rN) assumes unlimited resources. The logistic model (dN/dt = rN(1 − N/K)) adds a braking term that slows growth as the population approaches the carrying capacity K — the maximum population the environment can sustain.
Real-world examples: Logistic growth appears everywhere in nature. Bacteria in a flask multiply exponentially for hours, then plateau as nutrients run out — classic lab demonstrations use E. coli doubling every 20 minutes until the culture saturates. White-tailed deer introduced to the Kaibab Plateau in the 1900s exploded from 4,000 to nearly 100,000 after predator removal, then crashed below carrying capacity due to overgrazing. World human population grew nearly exponentially from 1 billion (1800) to 8 billion (2022), but growth rates have been declining since the 1960s — suggesting a logistic-like transition with an estimated carrying capacity debated between 9 and 11 billion.

Equilibria: The logistic growth rate dN/dt = rN(1 − N/K) forms a downward parabola peaking at N = K/2. Equilibria exist at N = 0 (unstable — any perturbation causes growth) and N = K (stable — the population returns if displaced). Maximum growth rate occurs at half the carrying capacity.
Inflection point: The logistic S-curve changes from concave-up to concave-down at exactly N = K/2
At this point: d²N/dt² = 0   and   dN/dt = rK/4  (maximum growth rate)
Biological meaning: Populations grow fastest at half their carrying capacity. Below K/2,
acceleration is positive (growth speeds up). Above K/2, it is negative (growth slows down).
Practical rule: Wildlife managers aiming for maximum sustainable yield harvest
populations down to N = K/2, where regrowth is fastest.

Conservation biology: The harvesting model dN/dt = rN(1 − N/K) − hN introduces a removal rate h. When h < r(1 − N/K), the population can sustain harvesting. But when h exceeds the critical threshold, the population collapses to extinction — a mathematical warning that governs fisheries management, conservation policy, and sustainable resource use.
Why It Matters
Thomas Malthus (1798) warned that human populations grow exponentially while resources grow linearly — predicting inevitable famine. Pierre-François Verhulst (1838) refined this with the logistic equation, introducing the carrying capacity K as nature's brake.

These models are foundational across biology: from bacterial growth in a petri dish to wildlife management and predicting the spread of invasive species. The discrete logistic map unexpectedly bridges ecology and chaos theory, showing that even simple population models can produce infinitely complex behaviour.

Connections: The logistic equation reappears in the SIR Epidemic Model (Chapter 15, Topic 1), the Logistic Map in dynamical systems, and the Differential Equations chapter (Chapter 6).

Did you know? — The Rule of 70
If a population grows at 2% per year, it doubles every 35 years. At 7% growth, doubling takes just 10 years. This is the Rule of 70: doubling time ≈ 70 / growth rate (%). It comes from solving N₀ ert = 2N₀, giving t = ln(2)/r ≈ 0.693/r. Converting r to a percentage and multiplying: 0.693 × 100 ≈ 70. Bankers use this rule for compound interest, epidemiologists for disease spread, and ecologists for population forecasting — a single formula connecting finance, biology, and public health.

Newton's Laws of Motion

●●●● Intermediate

Force, mass, and acceleration — the equations that built the industrial revolution

Every rocket launch, every braking car, every spinning gyroscope obeys three laws written down in 1687. Newton's first law defines inertia; the second quantifies the relationship F = ma; the third guarantees that forces always come in equal and opposite pairs. Together, they describe all of classical mechanics.

Newton's Second Law is a differential equation: F = m d²x/dt². For constant force it integrates to give uniform acceleration: v = at and x = ½at². The velocity–time graph is a straight line whose slope equals the acceleration — a direct geometric display of F/m. Adding friction introduces a constant opposing force, reducing the net force and therefore the slope.

The Third Law forces act on different objects — they do not cancel. The impulse transferred to each is exactly equal and opposite, which is why momentum is conserved. Notice that when m₁ ≠ m₂, the balls reach different speeds after collision, yet the impulse J = Δp is identical in magnitude for both.

The Mathematics of Force and Motion

Newton's three laws form a complete axiomatic system for classical mechanics. The first law (inertia) establishes the reference frame: an object at rest or moving at constant velocity has zero net force. The second law, F = ma, is more precisely written as F = dp/dt, a first-order ODE in momentum. For constant mass this reduces to the familiar scalar form a = F/m, directly relating cause (force) to effect (acceleration). The third law closes the system by insisting that forces are relational: every interaction generates a pair of forces equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, ensuring that the total momentum of an isolated system is conserved.

The inclined plane is a masterclass in coordinate geometry. Gravity acts vertically downward with magnitude mg, but the constraint of the surface means we must resolve this vector into two components: one perpendicular to the slope (balanced by the normal force N) and one parallel (which drives or resists motion). This decomposition is a rotation of axes by angle θ, producing W‖ = mg sinθ and W⊥ = mg cosθ. The normal force N equals W⊥ because the block does not accelerate through the surface. Friction then acts as f = μN = μmg cosθ, opposing motion. The ratio of driving to restraining force gives the condition for sliding: tanθ > μ.

These laws underpin all of classical mechanics. Projectile motion is simply Newton's second law applied independently in two directions with gravity as the only vertical force. Orbital mechanics adds a radially directed gravitational force and shows that the same F = ma gives elliptical orbits (Kepler's laws follow as a consequence). Simple harmonic motion arises when the restoring force is proportional to displacement — F = −kx — leading to the ODE m&ddot;x; = −kx, whose solution is sinusoidal oscillation at frequency ω = √(k/m).

Try This

A 1000 kg car accelerates from 0 to 100 km/h (27.8 m/s) in 8 seconds. What net force is required? How does this compare to the car’s weight?

F = ma = 1000 × (27.8/8) = 3,475 N. The car’s weight is mg = 9,800 N. So the acceleration force is about 35% of the car’s weight. In Formula 1, cars can decelerate at 5g — the braking force is 5 times the car’s weight!

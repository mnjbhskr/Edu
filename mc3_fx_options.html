FX Options: Delta Conventions & Vol Pillars

●●●● Advanced

How three market quotes define an entire volatility surface

1. Why FX Is Its Own World

In equity and rates markets, a volatility surface is quoted as a grid of strikes and maturities. Each cell holds an implied volatility. The FX market does something radically different: it compresses an entire smile into just three numbers per maturity — the ATM straddle, the 25-delta risk reversal, and the 25-delta butterfly.

This compression is elegant but treacherous. The same three numbers can produce different vol smiles depending on which delta convention and which ATM definition you adopt. A 25-delta call in EURUSD can map to different strikes depending on whether you use premium-included or premium-excluded delta — and the difference can be several delta points for long-dated options.

This module unpacks every layer of that compression. We start with the three market pillars, then address the ATM and premium convention ambiguities, and finally build the tools to recover a full vol smile from market quotes.

What appears to be a single volatility is actually a compressed description of an entire vol surface. Understanding that compression — and the conventions that govern it — is the difference between a model that prices correctly and one that misprice by tens of basis points.

2. The Three Pillars

Market Quotes

Every FX vol maturity is described by three standardised quotes. From these three numbers, you can recover the implied volatility at any delta point on the smile.

PillarNotationMeaning
ATM Straddle σATM Vol at the ATM strike — the centre of the smile
Risk Reversal 25Δ RR Skew: 25Δ call vol minus 25Δ put vol
Butterfly 25Δ BF Curvature: average wing vol minus ATM vol

Recovery Formulae

Given the three pillars, the wing volatilities are:

σcall(25Δ) = σATM + BF25 + ½ × RR25
σput(25Δ)  = σATM + BF25 − ½ × RR25

Worked Example: EURUSD 1Y

Suppose the market quotes: σATM = 8.5%, RR25 = −1.2%, BF25 = 0.3%

25Δ call vol: 8.5 + 0.3 + ½(−1.2) = 8.5 + 0.3 − 0.6 = 8.2%
25Δ put vol: 8.5 + 0.3 − ½(−1.2) = 8.5 + 0.3 + 0.6 = 9.4%
The negative risk reversal tells us put vol exceeds call vol — the market prices downside protection on EURUSD more dearly than upside. The positive butterfly confirms convexity: both wings trade above ATM.

3. The ATM Convention Problem

Three Definitions of “At-The-Money”

In equity options, “ATM” is unambiguous: strike equals spot. In FX, there are three competing definitions, and the choice affects every downstream calculation.

ConventionStrike DefinitionUsage
DNS (Delta-Neutral Straddle) K where Δcall + Δput = 0 Most common in FX interbank market
ATMF (At-The-Money Forward) K = F Equity-style, used in some crosses
ATMS (At-The-Money Spot) K = S Rarely used for vanilla FX options

DNS Strike Formula

Under Black–Scholes with zero rates for simplicity, the DNS strike satisfies:

KDNS = F × exp(½ σ² T)

The DNS strike is always above the forward. For a 1-year option with σ = 8.5%, the shift is exp(0.5 × 0.085² × 1) ≈ 1.0036, or about 36 basis points above F.

▶ Why DNS differs from the forward

A straddle is the sum of a call and a put at the same strike. The straddle delta is Δcall + Δput. Under Black–Scholes:

Δcall = N(d1),   Δput = N(d1) − 1

Setting the total delta to zero: N(d1) + N(d1) − 1 = 0, so N(d1) = 0.5, which means d1 = 0.

d1 = [ln(F/K) + ½σ²T] / (σ√T) = 0

Solving: ln(F/K) = −½σ²T, hence K = F × exp(½σ²T). Because σ²T > 0, KDNS > F always. The shift grows with volatility and maturity — it is negligible for short-dated, low-vol options but material for long-dated EM crosses.

4. The Premium Convention Problem

Premium-Excluded vs Premium-Included Delta

In most markets, delta is the standard Black–Scholes sensitivity: how much the option price changes per unit move in the underlying. But when the option premium is paid in the foreign currency (as is common in FX), the premium itself has delta exposure that must be netted out.

ConventionFormulaUsage
Premium-Excluded Δ = N(d1) Standard Black–Scholes delta
Premium-Included Δprem = N(d1) − C / (S · erfT) EM pairs (e.g. USDBRL, USDTRY)

The difference between the two conventions is typically 2–4 delta points for 1-year maturities. For a 25-delta call, this means the strike mapped by premium-included delta is further out-of-the-money, producing a wider smile when plotted against strike.

▶ Derivation of premium-included delta adjustment

When the premium is paid in foreign currency, the hedge must account for the premium’s own FX exposure. For a call option with value C (in domestic per unit foreign), the total foreign-currency cost of the hedge is:

Total hedge = Δ × notional − (C / S) × notional

The “effective” or premium-included delta is therefore:

Δprem = Δ − C / S = N(d1) − C / S

More precisely, when foreign discount factors are included:

Δprem = e−rfT × N(d1) − C / S

Because C/S is always positive for a call, premium-included delta is always less than premium-excluded delta. To achieve the same delta value (e.g. 0.25), the premium-included strike must be closer to ATM, producing a materially different smile shape.

8. Full Surface Recovery

Moving from three quotes to a continuous vol surface requires several careful steps. The procedure below is the standard approach used on most FX options desks.

▶ Four-step surface recovery procedure

Step 1: Recover wing vols from pillars

Apply the recovery formulae to obtain σ(25Δ call), σ(25Δ put), and optionally σ(10Δ call), σ(10Δ put) from the market quotes.

σcall(25Δ) = σATM + BF25 + ½ RR25
σput(25Δ) = σATM + BF25 − ½ RR25

Step 2: Convert delta to strikes

For each delta point, invert the Black–Scholes delta formula to find the corresponding strike. For a call with delta Δ:

K = F × exp(−N−1(Δ) × σ√T + ½σ²T)

If using premium-included delta, this step requires an iterative solver (Newton’s method) because the strike appears implicitly in both the delta and the premium term.

Step 3: Fit an interpolant

With 3 or 5 (strike, vol) pairs, fit a smooth interpolant — typically a cubic spline or polynomial in delta space. The SABR model or Vanna–Volga method are popular choices for extrapolation beyond the quoted range.

Step 4: Output σ(K)

The fitted curve gives σ(K) for any strike, completing the smile for that maturity. Repeating across all quoted maturities and interpolating in the time dimension yields the full surface σ(K, T).

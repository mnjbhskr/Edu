Maxwell's Equations

The four equations that unified electricity, magnetism, and light

In 1865 James Clerk Maxwell wrote down four equations that unified all of electricity and magnetism into a single mathematical framework. Then he noticed that the equations predicted waves — and calculated their speed as 3 × 10&sup8; m/s. Recognising this as the speed of light, Maxwell deduced that light itself is an electromagnetic wave. It is one of the greatest predictions in the history of science.

1 — The Four Equations

The total electric flux through any closed surface equals the enclosed charge divided by ε0. Integrating gives Coulomb's law: E = Q/(4πε0r²).
Field behaviours — two operators
∇·  (divergence) — measures sources and sinks: field lines radiate outward from positive charges or inward toward negative charges. A non-zero divergence means there is a source at that point.
∇×  (curl) — measures rotation: field lines circulate in loops around a point. A non-zero curl means the field is swirling, driven by a changing flux or a current.
Equations I & II use divergence (sources). Equations III & IV use curl (circulation).
ALL FOUR EQUATIONS — integral and differential form
I.   ∮E·dA = Q/ε0
     div E = ρ/ε0
II.  ∮B·dA = 0
     div B = 0
III. EMF = −dΦB/dt    (Faraday)
     curl E = −∂B/∂t
IV.  ∮B·dl = μ0(I + ε0 dΦE/dt)    (Ampere-Maxwell)
     curl B = μ0J + μ0ε0 ∂E/∂t
Maxwell's critical addition was the displacement current term ε0 dΦE/dt in equation IV. Without it, the equations were inconsistent for time-varying fields. With it, they predict self-sustaining electromagnetic waves propagating at c = 1/√(μ0ε0).

Maxwell took the curl of Faraday's law and substituted Ampere-Maxwell: the result is a wave equation with speed c = 1/√(μ0ε0). He knew μ0 and ε0 from electrostatics experiments — and the number matched the measured speed of light exactly. This led directly to Einstein's Special Relativity.

The elegance: two constants, one universe. Maxwell's equations contain only two fundamental constants: μ0 (the magnetic permeability of free space) and ε0 (the electric permittivity of free space). From these two constants alone, the speed of light emerges: c = 299,792,458 m/s. The speed of light is not an independent fact about the universe — it is determined entirely by the electrical and magnetic properties of empty space.
The Mathematics of Unification

Michael Faraday spent decades in the laboratory discovering the phenomena: electric fields, magnetic fields, induction, the connection between electricity and magnetism. He thought in terms of field lines and physical pictures. Maxwell's genius was to translate Faraday's physical intuition into precise mathematical language — four partial differential equations that captured everything Faraday had observed and more.

In differential form, two of the equations involve divergence (∇·), which measures how much a field radiates outward from a point (its “source strength”), and two involve curl (∇×), which measures how much a field circulates around a point. Gauss's laws are divergence equations — electric fields diverge from charges; magnetic fields never diverge (no monopoles). Faraday's and Ampere-Maxwell's are curl equations — changing magnetic fields curl electric fields into loops, and currents (and changing electric fields) curl magnetic fields into loops.

The displacement current term ε0 ∂E/∂t was Maxwell's own addition to Ampère's original law. Without it, taking the divergence of Ampère's law gives a contradiction: ∇·J = 0, which violates charge conservation in time-varying situations (e.g., between capacitor plates where no real current flows but the electric field changes). Adding ε0 ∂E/∂t restores consistency and closes the system.

The wave equation emerges by taking the curl of Faraday's law: ∇×(∇×E) = −∂/∂t(∇×B). Using the vector identity ∇×(∇×E) = ∇(∇·E) − ∇²E and substituting Ampere-Maxwell gives ∇²E = μ0ε0 ∂²E/∂t² — a wave equation with speed c = 1/√(μ0ε0) = 2.998 × 10&sup8; m/s. Maxwell computed this from constants measured in bench experiments and recognised the speed of light.

Charges moving through these fields experience the Lorentz force F = qE + qv × B: the electric part acts along the field, the magnetic part acts perpendicular to both the velocity and the field (see Magnetic Fields). Together, Maxwell's equations and the Lorentz force constitute the complete classical theory of electromagnetism.

Maxwell's equations underpin the entire electromagnetic world: radio, television, WiFi, GPS (which requires both Special and General Relativity, both of which trace to Maxwell), MRI scanners, fibre optic communications, microwave ovens, and every antenna ever built. They also contain Special Relativity implicitly: the wave speed c is fixed regardless of the observer's frame, which Einstein took as a postulate.

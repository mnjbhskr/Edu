Day Count & Compounding Conventions

●●●● Intermediate

How markets measure time and grow money — and why the conventions matter

1. The Problem

Two bonds both quote a yield of “5%”. One uses Actual/365 with annual compounding; the other uses Actual/360 with semi-annual compounding. Are they offering the same return?

They are not. The difference can be significant. Consider a 5.00% rate quoted on an Act/360 basis for a 90-day period. Converting to Act/365 gives 5.069% — a 6.9 basis-point difference. On a £1 billion position, that represents approximately £172,000.

Markets evolved their conventions under pressures that have nothing to do with mathematical elegance: speed (a trader quoting “vol” saves time across thousands of transactions), legacy (the 360-day year exists because Roman bankers divided months into 30 days for mental arithmetic), and intuition (basis points convey magnitude more immediately than percentage of par).

Quants need representations that are internally consistent, model-ready, and mathematically continuous. This module establishes the machinery to convert between any pair of conventions — so both worlds can communicate precisely.

Every derivatives desk has a version of this conversation on day one. The new quant sees a screen full of rates and asks: which convention? This module answers that question systematically.

2. Day Count Conventions

How Markets Measure Time

A day count fraction (DCF) converts a calendar period into a decimal fraction of a year. Different markets use different conventions, so the same calendar period produces different fractions — and therefore different accrued amounts.

ConventionFormulaMarket Usage
Actual/360 DCF = (d2 − d1) / 360 Money markets: T-bills, repos, LIBOR/SOFR
Actual/365 Fixed DCF = (d2 − d1) / 365 Sterling money markets, Asian markets
30/360 (Bond Basis) DCF = [360(Y2−Y1) + 30(M2−M1) + (D2−D1)] / 360 US corporate bonds, Eurobonds
30E/360 (Eurobond) As 30/360, European EOM rules European fixed income, covered bonds
Actual/Actual ISDA Leap days / 366 + non-leap / 365 Government bonds (Treasuries, Gilts)
Actual/Actual ICMA days / (freq × coupon period) Bond coupon accrual calculations
▶ Full derivation: 30/360 end-of-month adjustment rules

The 30/360 convention treats every month as having exactly 30 days. End-of-month adjustments are needed when actual dates fall on the 31st (or 28th/29th of February):

30/360 (Bond Basis / US):
• If D1 = 31, set D1 = 30
• If D2 = 31 and D1 ≥ 30, set D2 = 30
• DCF = [360(Y2−Y1) + 30(M2−M1) + (D2−D1)] / 360
30E/360 (Eurobond Basis):
• If D1 > 30, set D1 = 30
• If D2 > 30, set D2 = 30
• Same formula as above

The European convention is simpler: both dates are capped at 30 unconditionally. The US convention only adjusts D2 when D1 has already been adjusted — a subtlety that matters at month boundaries.

4. Converting Between Day Count Conventions

The Core Identity

A rate r1 quoted under day count convention DCF1 can be converted to an equivalent rate r2 under DCF2 by requiring that the accrued amount be equal:

1 + r1 × DCF1 = 1 + r2 × DCF2   ⇒   r2 = r1 × (DCF1 / DCF2)

Worked Example

Given: r1 = 5.00% on Act/360 basis, period = 90 days
Step 1: DCF1 (Act/360) = 90 / 360 = 0.250000
Step 2: DCF2 (Act/365) = 90 / 365 = 0.246575
Step 3: r2 = 5.00% × (0.250000 / 0.246575) = 5.069%
Difference: +6.9 basis points
On £1bn: ≈ £172,000
▶ Why this identity works

The day count convention is simply a labelling choice — it determines how we express the time fraction T, but it does not change the actual cash flow. If £1 accrues to £(1 + r1 × T1) under one convention, it must accrue to the same amount under any other. Setting the two expressions equal and solving for r2 gives the ratio formula above.

This applies to simple (money market) rates only. For compounded rates, a different transformation is needed — see Section 5.

5. Compounding Conventions

Four Ways to Grow Money

The compounding convention determines how interest is reinvested. The same nominal rate produces very different terminal values depending on the compounding frequency.

CompoundingGrowth of £1Typical Usage
Simple 1 + r × T Money market instruments (< 1 year)
Annual (1 + r)T Bond yields, annual swap rates
Semi-annual (1 + r/2)2T US Treasuries, US fixed income
Continuous erT All derivatives models, Black-Scholes

Equivalence Derivations

All conventions must produce the same terminal value for equivalent rates. Setting growth factors equal and solving for the continuous rate rc:

Semi-annual → continuous:   rc = 2 × ln(1 + rsa/2)
Annual → continuous:   rc = ln(1 + ra)
Simple → continuous:   rc = ln(1 + rs × T) / T

Worked Example

Given: semi-annual rate rsa = 5.00%
Step 1: rc = 2 × ln(1 + 0.025)
Step 2: rc = 2 × 0.024693 = 4.938%
Meaning: a 5% semi-annual bond yield and a 4.938% continuous rate produce the same terminal value.
▶ Why derivatives use continuous compounding

1. Addition replaces multiplication. If two periods have continuous rates r1 and r2, the combined growth factor is er1T1 × er2T2 = er1T1 + r2T2. Exponents add — this simplifies every multi-period calculation.

2. Natural limit. Continuous compounding is the mathematical limit of compounding at ever-higher frequencies: limn→∞ (1 + r/n)nT = erT.

3. Stochastic calculus alignment. Itô’s lemma and the Black-Scholes derivation operate on continuous processes. Using continuous compounding means model inputs and outputs are in the same convention — no conversion required.

Where It Matters

Bond relative value. Comparing yields across markets requires convention alignment. A 5% GBP gilt yield (Act/Act ICMA, semi-annual) is not the same as a 5% EUR government bond yield (Act/Act ISDA, annual) without conversion.

Derivatives pricing. Black-Scholes assumes continuous compounding. Feeding a semi-annual rate into the formula without conversion introduces a pricing error that grows with maturity.

Risk management. VaR and DV01 calculations must use consistent conventions across a portfolio. Mixing conventions silently corrupts risk aggregation.

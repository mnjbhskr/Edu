Interest Rates

Term structure and the mathematics of borrowing costs

Interest rates are the price of money over time. The term structure — how rates vary with maturity — encodes the market’s collective view on growth, inflation, and risk. From the yield curve that traders watch obsessively to the discount factors that underpin every bond price, the mathematics of interest rates is the foundation of fixed-income finance.

The yield curve reflects expectations, risk premia, and liquidity effects — not just forecasts of future interest rates. An inverted yield curve — where short rates exceed long rates — has preceded every US recession since 1955.

Forward rates are not predictions of future rates — they are the rates implied by the current term structure such that no arbitrage exists. If you could lock in different rates, you would have a money machine.

A bond’s price is simply the sum of its discounted cash flows. When market yields rise, bond prices fall — and vice versa. This inverse relationship is the most important concept in fixed income.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Key Equations
Discount factor:   D(t) = exp( −∫0t r(u) du )
Forward rate:     f(t&sub1;,t&sub2;) = [ r(t&sub2;)·t&sub2; − r(t&sub1;)·t&sub1; ] / (t&sub2; − t&sub1;)
Bond price:      P = ∑ c·D(ti) + F·D(T)
The yield curve is a snapshot of the market’s pricing of time. From it we derive discount factors — the exchange rate between future and present money — and forward rates — the rates implied by no-arbitrage. Together these are sufficient to price any fixed-income instrument.
Connections: Compound interest (the simplest term structure), yield curve construction, and bond pricing are explored in more detail in their own pages on this site.
From a single yield curve, the entire fixed-income universe unfolds: discount factors price cash flows, forward rates eliminate arbitrage, and bond prices follow as inevitable consequences. The mathematics is elegant; the market implications are profound.

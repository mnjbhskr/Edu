Generalised Method of Moments

●●●● Expert

One framework to rule them all — OLS, IV, and more as special cases of moment conditions

1. Nesting: OLS, IV & GMM

GMM unifies estimators through moment conditions E[g(data, θ)] = 0. Toggle between modes to see the moment conditions that define each estimator.

2. GMM Objective Function Surface

With more moment conditions than parameters, the GMM objective Q(θ) = g(θ)' W g(θ) forms a surface. The weighting matrix W determines the shape. Optimal W uses the inverse of the variance of moment conditions.

Under identity weighting, the objective surface may be elongated. Optimal weighting reshapes it to be more symmetric, yielding the efficient GMM estimator with minimum asymptotic variance.
Two-step GMM and continuously-updated GMM
Two-step GMM: (1) Estimate with W = I to get a preliminary θ̂. (2) Use θ̂ to estimate the optimal weighting matrix Ŝ−1. (3) Re-estimate with the optimal W. This is efficient but can have finite-sample bias.
Iterated GMM: Repeat steps 2-3 until convergence. Typically converges in 3-5 iterations.
Continuously-updated GMM (CUE): Jointly minimise Q(θ) = g(θ)' S(θ)−1 g(θ) where the weighting matrix itself depends on θ. More robust but harder to optimise (non-convex).
Hansen-Sargan J-test: n × Q(θ̂) ~ χ²(m − k) under correct specification. Rejection implies at least one moment condition is violated.
Where you will see this: GMM is the estimation workhorse in empirical finance and macroeconomics. Hansen (1982) won the Nobel Prize for this framework. Asset pricing models (Euler equations), macro models with rational expectations, and dynamic panel data (Arellano-Bond) all use GMM. The nesting property means OLS, IV, and 2SLS are all special cases.

3. J-Test for Over-Identification

Under correct specification, the J-statistic = n × Q(θ̂) follows a χ² distribution with (moments − parameters) degrees of freedom. Monte Carlo builds this distribution; misspecification pushes it rightward into rejection.

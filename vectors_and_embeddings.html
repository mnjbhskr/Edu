Vectors & Embeddings

How a word becomes a point in space — and why meaning is geometry

A neural network cannot read text. It sees only lists of numbers — vectors. The breakthrough idea behind modern AI is that if you train a network on enough language, words with similar meanings end up at nearby positions in a high-dimensional space. “King” and “queen” are neighbours. “Happy” and “sad” point in opposite directions. Meaning is geometry.

A neural network never “reads” words — it works with vectors of numbers. Meaning is encoded as direction and distance in space. Words that appear in similar contexts (e.g. “cat” and “dog”) are pushed to nearby positions during training.

Words are points. Similarity is distance. Categories are clusters.

king − man + woman ≈ queen
The “royalty” direction = king − man. Adding it to woman yields queen.
In 2013, Tomas Mikolov and colleagues at Google published Word2Vec and showed that word vectors capture semantic relationships as linear directions. The “gender direction” (king − man) is approximately the same as (queen − woman). This was the first major evidence that meaning has geometry.

Analogies are vector arithmetic — subtract one concept, add another, and land on the answer.

Embedding dimension of real models:
Word2Vec (2013): 300 dimensions
BERT (2018): 768 dimensions
GPT-3 (2020): 12,288 dimensions
GPT-4 (2023): 12,288 dimensions (estimated)
Humans think in 3 dimensions. Transformers think in 12,288. In high dimensions, almost all pairs of random vectors are nearly orthogonal — which means a model can store an enormous number of independent directions, each encoding a different concept. This is why large language models can “know” so many things simultaneously.

More dimensions = more directions = more concepts a model can represent.

Why This Matters

GPT-class models represent every word as a vector of thousands of numbers (GPT-3 uses 12,288; GPT-4’s exact architecture is not public). The entire English language is a geometry problem. When the model processes your sentence, it transforms these vectors through dozens of layers, each one rotating, stretching, and combining them until the final vector points toward the right next word.

The idea that meaning can be captured as direction in space is perhaps the single most important insight in modern AI. It is why machines can now translate languages, answer questions, and write code — they learned the geometry of meaning from billions of sentences.

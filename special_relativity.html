Where space and time become one

2 — Time Dilation: Light Clocks

A light clock ticks once every time a photon bounces between two mirrors. At rest, the photon bounces straight up and down. In a moving clock, it traces a longer diagonal path — so the moving clock ticks slower. This is not an illusion: time genuinely passes more slowly for the moving observer.

γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) = 1.155
Stationary ticks: 0  |  Moving ticks: 0

3 — Length Contraction

An object moving at relativistic speed is measured shorter in its direction of motion by an observer at rest. The proper length L₀ contracts to L = L₀/γ. At 87% of the speed of light, objects are half their rest length.

L = L₀ / γ  →  L = 1.000 L₀    γ = 1.000

4 — The Twin Paradox

One twin stays on Earth while the other travels to a distant star and back at relativistic speed. When the traveller returns, they are younger. The asymmetry comes from the traveller's acceleration at the turnaround — only the traveller changes reference frames.

The Mathematics of Spacetime

Special relativity rests on two postulates: the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames, and the speed of light c is constant for all observers. From these follow the Lorentz transformations:

t' = γ(t − vx/c²)    x' = γ(x − vt)

where the Lorentz factor is:

γ = 1 / √(1 − v²/c²)

The spacetime interval is invariant under Lorentz transformations:

s² = (ct)² − x² = (ct')² − x'²

Connections to Mathematics

Minkowski spacetime uses hyperbolic geometry — the Lorentz boost is a hyperbolic rotation with rapidity φ = atanh(v/c). The invariant interval defines a pseudo-Riemannian metric with signature (−,+,+,+). The γ factor arises naturally from the Pythagorean theorem applied to the light clock's diagonal path. Matrix form of the boost:

[ct'] [ γ −γβ] [ct]

[ x'] = [−γβ γ ] [ x]

At everyday speeds, γ ≈ 1 + v²/(2c²) — special relativity reduces to Newtonian mechanics.

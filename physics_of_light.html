Physics of Light

Refraction, dispersion, and interference — how light reveals its wave nature

Light bends because its speed changes in matter. Light splits into colours because that speed depends on wavelength. Light interferes because it behaves as a wave. Light propagates because electric and magnetic fields sustain each other.

Stays constant: wavelength λ
d sin θ = mλ   (bright fringes, m = 0, ±1, ±2, …)
Δy = λL/d   (fringe spacing)
I(θ) ∝ cos²(πd sin θ / λ)   (double-slit intensity)
Two tiny openings proved light is a wave. The bright bands appear where crests from both slits arrive in step — constructive interference. Watch the phase arrows: when they point the same way, the waves add; when they oppose, they cancel.

Non-obvious: Larger slit separation → fringes closer together. This follows directly from Δy = λL/d — d is in the denominator.

Maxwell’s equations predict that oscillating electric and magnetic fields sustain each other and travel at exactly the speed of light. Light IS an electromagnetic wave. Notice E⊥B⊥k — the fields oscillate perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel.

Move the wavelength slider and watch: shorter λ → faster oscillation. The animation speed is exaggerated to make the frequency difference visible.

Visible light is just a sliver of the electromagnetic spectrum — radio waves, microwaves, X-rays and gamma rays all obey the same physics, just at different wavelengths.

The Nature of Light
This page tells a single story in four acts. Snell’s law shows that light bends because its speed changes at a boundary — the wavefront tilts as one side slows before the other. Dispersion deepens this: the refractive index depends on wavelength, so each colour bends by a different amount, and a prism fans white light into a rainbow. Young’s double-slit experiment provides the decisive evidence that light is a wave — two openings produce an interference pattern that no particle theory can explain. Finally, Maxwell’s electromagnetic theory reveals what light actually is: self-sustaining oscillations of electric and magnetic fields travelling at 3 × 10⁸ m/s.

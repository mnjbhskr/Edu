All Maps Lie

Gauss proved in 1827 that no flat map of a curved surface can be faithful — and this shapes everything from navigation to data visualisation

Core idea Gauss proved that no flat map of a curved surface can be faithful — and this theorem applies to every 2D projection of high-dimensional data.

1. The Sphere Cannot Be Flattened — Gauss's Remarkable Theorem

Curvature Explorer

40°
0% 
Gaussian curvature:   K = κ₁ × κ₂

Sphere of radius R:   K = 1 / R²  > 0   (positive curvature)
Flat plane:           K = 0           (zero curvature)
Saddle surface:       K < 0           (negative curvature)

Theorema Egregium (Gauss, 1827):
    K is preserved under any isometry (distance-preserving map).

Corollary:
    No isometry exists from sphere to plane.
    Every flat map introduces distortion.
    This is not a failure of technology — it is a mathematical theorem.
💡 Gauss called his theorem "remarkable" (egregium) because it showed that curvature is intrinsic — a property of the surface itself, detectable by creatures living on the surface without reference to anything outside it. A flat map cannot be faithful to a sphere for the same reason a cone cannot equal a cube: they are fundamentally different mathematical objects.

2. What Each Projection Preserves — Tissot's Indicatrix

Projection Comparison

Mercator (conformal):    x = λ,   y = ln( tan(π/4 + φ/2) )
Mollweide (equal-area):  x = (2√2/π) λ cos(θ),  y = √2 sin(θ)
                        where 2θ + sin(2θ) = π sin(φ)
Equidistant:            x = λ cos(φ₀),  y = φ
Gnomonic (from pole):   x = cot(φ) sin(λ),  y = −cot(φ) cos(λ)
Winkel Tripel:          average of equidistant and Aitoff

Tissot's indicatrix: project an infinitesimal circle of radius dφ = 0.05 rad
centred at each lat/long. Its image on the plane is an ellipse.
Axis lengths are h (along meridian) and k (along parallel).
    • Conformal ⇒ h = k (circles preserved, area varies).
    • Equal-area ⇒ h · k = 1 (area preserved, shape varies).
💡 On the Mercator projection, Greenland appears larger than Africa. In reality, Africa is fourteen times larger. Mercator was designed in 1569 for nautical navigation — it preserves angles, making it perfect for plotting compass bearings. It was never designed to convey area. That it became the default world map is a historical accident with real political consequences: it systematically exaggerates the size of northern (wealthier) nations.

3. Projection in Data Visualisation — The Same Mathematics

The Data Visualisation Parallel

Every scatter plot of high-dimensional data is a projection — exactly as a map projection is a transformation from sphere to plane. The choice of method is a choice about which structural property to preserve, and which to sacrifice.

Cartographic Projection Data Visualisation Analogy (loosely) What each prioritises
Mercator Linear projection (standard scatter) Local angles / directions
Equal-area (Mollweide) PCA Variance — proportional representation of spread
Conformal (shape-preserving) t-SNE / UMAP Local neighbourhood structure
Equidistant MDS (Multi-Dimensional Scaling) Pairwise distances
These analogies are deliberately approximate — they are intended to build intuition by connecting a familiar domain (cartography) to a less familiar one (high-dimensional data projection), not to assert formal mathematical equivalence. The underlying mathematics differs; the representational trade-offs are genuinely parallel.
💡 Each dimensionality-reduction algorithm is a "projection" with its own Tissot-like distortion pattern. A t-SNE plot preserves which points are neighbours but destroys information about how far apart distant clusters are. A PCA plot preserves global variance but squashes local structure. No algorithm escapes Gauss's corollary: something must be sacrificed.
What have we learnt?
Gauss proved in 1827 that any flat map of a sphere must lie. This is not a practical limitation to be overcome with better technology — it is a mathematical theorem, as certain as Pythagoras's. Every map projection makes an explicit choice: what to preserve (area, shape, distance, direction) and what to sacrifice. The same is true of every data visualisation. When you project high-dimensional data onto a 2D scatter plot, you are making a map — and your map is lying in a precise, mathematically-characterised way. Understanding projections means understanding what your visualisation cannot show, no matter how well it is designed. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

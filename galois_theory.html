The roots of a polynomial with rational coefficients come in conjugate pairs: if a + bi is a root, so is a − bi. The symmetries between these roots are the starting point of Galois theory.

2. Permutations of Roots

Some permutations of roots preserve algebraic relations between them. These special permutations form the Galois group.

Not every permutation of roots is a field automorphism. The Galois group consists only of those permutations that respect all polynomial relations with rational coefficients. This is what makes it a powerful invariant.

3. The Galois Group

The Galois group captures the full symmetry structure of a polynomial’s roots. Its Cayley table reveals the group operation.

Group Operations

The Galois group information will appear here.
A group is solvable if it can be broken down into a chain of subgroups where each quotient is abelian (commutative). The fundamental theorem of Galois theory says: a polynomial is solvable by radicals if and only if its Galois group is solvable.

4. The Tower of Field Extensions

Solving a polynomial means extending the rationals by adjoining roots. Each extension has a degree — the dimension of the new field over the old.

Choose an Extension

Choose an extension to see the tower.
Adjoining a root means adding it to our number system. Starting from the rationals Q, each step up the tower corresponds to a subgroup of the Galois group. This correspondence — the Galois correspondence — is one of the most beautiful results in all of mathematics.

5. Why the Quintic Has No Formula

The quadratic formula, cubic formula, and quartic formula all exist. But Abel and Galois proved no such formula exists for degree five and above.

Évariste Galois developed these ideas at the age of 20, the night before a duel in which he was killed. His hastily written letter, addressed to his friend Auguste Chevalier, contained the seeds of modern group theory and changed mathematics forever.

Timeline

1799 Ruffini publishes an incomplete proof that the general quintic cannot be solved by radicals.
1824 Abel gives the first rigorous proof of the impossibility of the quintic formula (the Abel–Ruffini theorem).
1832 Galois writes his final letter, establishing the theory of groups and the criterion for solvability by radicals. He dies the next day, aged 20.
1846 Liouville publishes Galois’s manuscripts, bringing his ideas to a wider audience.
1870 Jordan publishes Traité des substitutions, formalising Galois theory in the modern group-theoretic language.
Galois theory is not just about “why there’s no quintic formula”. It reveals a deep duality between field extensions and group theory that pervades modern mathematics — from number theory and algebraic geometry to cryptography and physics.

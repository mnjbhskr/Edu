The inverse-square law appears everywhere in physics: gravity, electric fields, light intensity. It is a direct consequence of living in three-dimensional space, where the surface area of a sphere grows as r².
The Mathematics of Fields
An electric field assigns a vector to every point in space — the direction and magnitude of the force that a unit positive charge would experience. This is a vector field, one of the central objects of multivariable calculus.

The field is the negative gradient of the potential: E = −∇V. Equipotential surfaces (contours of constant V) are always perpendicular to field lines. Where the potential changes rapidly, the field is strong.

Gauss’s law says the total flux of E through any closed surface equals the enclosed charge divided by ε₀. This connects the surface integral of a field to its sources — a deep result that generalises to the divergence theorem of vector calculus.

Assumptions: electrostatics only (charges are stationary), point charges (no finite size), vacuum (no dielectric materials).

Try This

Two equal positive charges are placed 1m apart. Where is the electric field zero? Where is it strongest?

The field is zero exactly at the midpoint (0.5m from each charge) — the two fields cancel by symmetry. The field is strongest very close to either charge (it follows an inverse-square law). Between the charges, field lines curve away from the midpoint — positive charges repel, so the lines never connect between two like charges.

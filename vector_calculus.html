Divergence measures how much a field spreads out (positive, green circles) or converges (negative, pink circles) at each point. A source field has positive divergence everywhere; a sink has negative divergence.

4. Curl — Local Rotation

Choose a field

12

curl F = ∂Q/∂x − ∂P/∂y = −2.00

Curl measures the local rotation of a field. Imagine placing tiny paddle wheels in the flow: where the curl is positive they spin anticlockwise (orange); where negative, clockwise (blue). The shear field (y, x) has zero curl — no net rotation anywhere.

Green’s theorem says the circulation of a field around a closed curve equals the total curl inside the region. The boundary knows what happens in the interior — a profound link between local and global.

6. The Big Picture Ladder

1D

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

∫ab f′(x) dx = f(b) − f(a)

The total change across an interval equals the sum of all the tiny changes inside it.

2D

Green’s Theorem

∮C F · dr = ∫∫D (∂Q/∂x − ∂P/∂y) dA

Circulation around a boundary equals total curl inside the region.

3D

Stokes’ Theorem

∮C F · dr = ∫∫S (∇ × F) · dS

Circulation around a boundary curve equals the flux of curl through any surface it bounds.

3D

Divergence Theorem

&oiint;S F · dS = ∫∫∫V (∇ · F) dV

Total outward flux through a closed surface equals the total divergence inside the volume.

All four theorems say the same thing at different dimensions: what happens on the boundary tells you what happens inside. This is the unifying idea of vector calculus — and it extends further into differential geometry and physics.

7. What Have We Learnt?

  • Vector fields assign a vector to every point — they model wind, gravity, electric forces, and fluid flow.
  • Line integrals measure the total work done by a field along a path, accumulating the dot product F · dr.
  • Divergence captures sources (positive) and sinks (negative) — how much the field spreads out or converges at each point.
  • Curl captures local rotation — which way a tiny paddle wheel would spin in the flow.
  • Green’s theorem connects circulation around a boundary to the total curl inside, uniting local and global.
  • The generalisation ladder shows that the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Green’s, Stokes’, and the Divergence Theorem are all the same idea in different dimensions.
Connections: Vector calculus is the language of Maxwell’s equations in electromagnetism, the Navier–Stokes equations in fluid dynamics, and the curvature tensors of differential geometry. Every force field, every flowing fluid, every curved space speaks this language.

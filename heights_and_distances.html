Heights & Distances

●●●● Intermediate

Trigonometry in action — measuring the world with angles

1. Introduction

How tall is that building? How wide is that river? How high is the mountain peak? These are questions that have been answered for thousands of years using nothing more than trigonometric ratios, a measured distance, and a carefully observed angle.

The essential idea is simple: if you can measure one side of a right triangle and one acute angle, trigonometry gives you every other measurement. A surveyor standing 50 metres from a building who measures a 60° angle of elevation can calculate the building’s height without climbing it.

Two key angles drive every problem in this topic:

  • Angle of elevation — the angle between the horizontal and your line of sight when looking up at an object
  • Angle of depression — the angle between the horizontal and your line of sight when looking down at an object
Ancient civilisations used these principles to measure the heights of pyramids, the widths of rivers, and the distances to stars. The mathematics has not changed — only the precision of our instruments.

2. Key Concepts

Angles of Elevation and Depression

Angle of elevation: When you look upward from the horizontal, the angle your line of sight makes with the horizontal is the angle of elevation. If you tilt your gaze 45° upward, the angle of elevation is 45°.

Angle of depression: When you look downward from the horizontal, the angle your line of sight makes with the horizontal is the angle of depression. A lighthouse keeper looking down at a ship sees it at an angle of depression.

Key identity: The angle of depression from the top of a structure equals the angle of elevation from the bottom (alternate interior angles with a horizontal transversal).

All CBSE problems use only three standard angles: 30°, 45°, and 60°. Memorise these values:

Angle θsin θcos θtan θ
30° ½ √3 / 2 1 / √3
45° 1 / √2 1 / √2 1
60° √3 / 2 ½ √3
In a right triangle: tan θ = opposite / adjacent. For height problems, the “opposite” is typically the height and the “adjacent” is the horizontal distance. This single ratio solves most problems.

5. Two-Triangle Problems

Finding the Width of a River

CBSE limits height-and-distance problems to at most two right triangles. A classic example: finding the width of a river by taking two angle-of-elevation measurements from opposite banks to the top of a tower.

Worked Example

A tower stands on the bank of a river. From the opposite bank, the angle of elevation to the top of the tower is 60°. Moving 40 m further away from the river, the angle of elevation becomes 30°. Find the width of the river and the height of the tower.

Let: h = height of tower, d = width of river
From near bank: tan(60°) = h / d  ⇒  √3 = h / d  ⇒  h = d√3  … (i)
From far point: tan(30°) = h / (d + 40)  ⇒  1/√3 = h / (d + 40)  ⇒  h = (d + 40) / √3  … (ii)
Equate (i) and (ii):
  d√3 = (d + 40) / √3
  3d = d + 40
  2d = 40  ⇒  d = 20 m
Height: h = 20√3 ≈ 34.64 m
▶ Why two triangles are enough

Two angle measurements from known positions give two equations in two unknowns (height and distance). The system is always solvable when the angles differ. CBSE restricts problems to at most two right triangles to keep the algebra manageable — but the technique extends to surveying problems with many observation points.

The key technique: form two equations using tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent, then eliminate the unknown height to find the distance (or vice versa). Both equations share the same height variable — that is what makes the system solvable.

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Shadow Problem

A vertical pole casts a shadow 12√3 m long when the angle of elevation of the sun is 30°. Find the height of the pole.

Given: shadow = 12√3 m, angle = 30°
Formula: tan(30°) = h / shadow
Step 1: 1/√3 = h / 12√3
Step 2: h = 12√3 / √3 = 12 m

Example 2 — Lighthouse

From the top of a 75 m lighthouse, the angle of depression of a ship is 45°. How far is the ship from the base of the lighthouse?

Given: h = 75 m, angle of depression = 45°
Key: angle of depression = angle of elevation (alternate interior angles)
Formula: tan(45°) = h / d
Step 1: 1 = 75 / d
Step 2: d = 75 m

Example 3 — Two-Point Problem

The angles of elevation of the top of a tower from two points on the ground at distances a and b from the base (in the same line) are 60° and 30° respectively. Show that the height of the tower is √(ab).

From distance a: tan(60°) = h / a  ⇒  √3 = h / a  ⇒  h = a√3  … (i)
From distance b: tan(30°) = h / b  ⇒  1/√3 = h / b  ⇒  h = b / √3  … (ii)
Multiply (i) × (ii):
  h² = a√3 × b / √3 = ab
  h = √(ab)   ■
This elegant result — the height is the geometric mean of the two distances — only holds when the angles are complementary (30° and 60°). It is a favourite CBSE “show that” question.

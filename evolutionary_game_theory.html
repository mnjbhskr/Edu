Evolutionary Game Theory

How nature plays games — the mathematics of strategy, survival, and cooperation

In 1973, John Maynard Smith and George Price asked a deceptively simple question: if hawks always fight and doves always share, what mix of strategies will evolution produce? Their answer — the Evolutionarily Stable Strategy — united biology and mathematics, explaining everything from animal fighting conventions to antibiotic resistance. The same equations that govern survival in nature also describe economic competition and the emergence of social norms.

Key insight: When fighting is costly (V < C), evolution produces a stable mix of hawks and doves. The proportion of hawks equals exactly V/C — the ratio of resource value to fighting cost. When V ≥ C, fighting always pays and hawks dominate.

2. Replicator Dynamics

The replicator equation describes how strategy frequencies change: strategies that perform above average grow; those below average shrink.

dxi/dt = xi(fi − f̄)

where xi is the frequency of strategy i, fi is its fitness, and f̄ is average population fitness

3. Evolutionarily Stable Strategies

An ESS is a strategy that, once adopted by a population, cannot be invaded by any rare mutant. It is a refinement of Nash equilibrium.

ESS Condition

Strategy I is ESS if for all J ≠ I:
E(I, I) > E(J, I)
or [E(I, I) = E(J, I) and E(I, J) > E(J, J)]

Key insight: A Nash equilibrium says no player wants to deviate. An ESS goes further — it says no mutant can invade. The ESS is Nash equilibrium with evolutionary stability: it must resist not just single deviations but also drift by small groups of mutants.
Fitness landscapes: Imagine a landscape where each point is a strategy and the height is its fitness (payoff). An Evolutionarily Stable Strategy sits at a peak — any mutant strategy that deviates rolls downhill and is eliminated by selection. Multiple peaks mean multiple possible ESSs, and which one a population reaches depends on its history.

Key insight: In a well-mixed population, defection is the only ESS in a Prisoner's Dilemma. But when players are arranged on a grid and interact only with neighbours, cooperators can survive by forming clusters that protect their interior members. This explains why cooperation persists in nature — from bacterial biofilms to social insects.
Evolutionary game theory in nature: (1) Side-blotched lizards use three mating strategies that cycle like rock-paper-scissors. (2) Bacteria produce costly toxins to kill competitors, a biological prisoner's dilemma. (3) Antibiotic resistance spreads because resistant bacteria ‘defect’ against the cooperative population. (4) Virus evolution balances transmission against virulence — too deadly and the host dies before spreading the infection.
Connection to economics: The same mathematics governs biological evolution and market competition. Firms choosing pricing strategies face a prisoner's dilemma (price wars). Companies entering markets play a hawk-dove game (aggressive vs cautious entry). Evolutionary game theory was born in biology but has been re-imported into economics, closing a remarkable intellectual circle.
Did you know? John Maynard Smith borrowed the concept of strategy from economics and applied it to animal behaviour in 1973, creating evolutionary game theory. The irony: economists had been struggling with the question of how players find Nash equilibria. Evolution provided the answer — natural selection IS the algorithm that finds equilibria, no rational calculation required.
Why It Matters

Maynard Smith and Price's 1973 paper founded evolutionary game theory, showing that the logic of strategic interaction applies not just to rational agents but to all of evolving life. The mathematics of ESS explains why animals follow fighting conventions instead of always fighting to the death, why signalling evolves, and why cooperation persists in a world of selfish genes.

The same equations govern antibiotic resistance (bacteria playing evolutionary games against drugs), immune system dynamics (host-pathogen co-evolution), and the emergence of social norms in human societies. Evolutionary game theory is the common language connecting biology, economics, and the mathematics of strategic interaction.

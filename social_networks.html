The Mathematics of Social Networks

From six degrees of separation to TikTok's feedback loops — how graph theory shapes your online world

You have probably spent thousands of hours inside a social network. You know what it feels like. What you may not know is that the network has a shape — and that mathematicians have been studying that shape, and what it does to the people inside it, since long before Myspace existed.

Think of five people you know well. Now think of five people they know, who you don't. How many people do you think you could reach in six steps — through chains of mutual acquaintances?

Most people guess a few thousand. The actual answer, for a typical person in a connected society, is: almost everyone on Earth.

Era 1 — The First Networks (1985–2002)

Graph Theory: Nodes, Edges, Degree

Before social networking existed as a product, sociologists were already building mathematical models of human connection. Stanley Milgram's 1967 "small-world experiment" — the origin of "six degrees of separation" — found that the average chain length between two random Americans was approximately 5.5. In 1998, Duncan Watts and Steven Strogatz gave this a mathematical foundation.

A social network is a graph G = (V, E) where V is a set of people (nodes) and E is a set of connections (edges). The key measurements — degree, diameter, average path length, clustering coefficient — turn out to determine everything about how information moves through a network.

What just happened mathematically?

You explored three fundamental graph properties: path length (how many steps between any two nodes), clustering (do your friends know each other?), and degree distribution (how many connections does each person have?). Real social networks are "small-world" — they have both short paths and high clustering, something random graphs cannot achieve. And their degree distributions follow a power law: most people have few connections, but a handful of hubs have enormous numbers.

Intuitive track

Imagine everyone stands in a ring and connects only to their nearest neighbours. Information travels slowly around the ring — you might need hundreds of steps. Now randomly rewire just 1% of those connections to reach someone far away. Suddenly, paths collapse. A few long-range shortcuts transform a slow lattice into a fast small-world network — without losing the local clustering that makes communities possible.

Mathematical track
Graph G = (V, E) Degree of node v: deg(v) = |{u : (u,v) in E}| Clustering coefficient: C(v) = 2T(v) / (deg(v)(deg(v)-1)) where T(v) = triangles through v Average path length: L = (2/n(n-1)) * Sum d(u,v) Small-world property: L ~ O(log N) (short paths) C >> C_random (high clustering)

For the Curious: Building a Small World (Watts-Strogatz)

Watts and Strogatz (1998) showed how to build a small-world graph. Start with a regular ring lattice where everyone connects to their k nearest neighbours. High clustering, long paths. Now randomly rewire a fraction p of edges. For remarkably small p (say 0.01), path lengths drop dramatically while clustering barely changes. The transition is a mathematical phase transition — the same phenomenon that governs magnetisation and percolation.

For the Curious: Why Hubs Exist (Power Law)

Random graphs have Poisson degree distributions — bell-shaped around the average. Real social networks follow a power law: P(k) ~ k^(-gamma) for gamma typically 2–3. The heavy tail means extreme values (hubs with millions of followers) appear far more often than a bell curve would predict. Power laws arise through preferential attachment — new nodes connect preferentially to already well-connected nodes. "The rich get richer." Barabasi and Albert formalised this in 1999.

Era 2 — Myspace and the Friendship Paradox (2003–2008)

The Friendship Paradox

Myspace launched in August 2003 and reached 100 million users by 2006. It was the first platform where ordinary people built public social profiles and accumulated friends at scale. The observable phenomenon: everyone seemed to have fewer friends than their friends did. This feeling was not imaginary — it is a mathematical theorem.

The Friendship Paradox (Scott Feld, 1991): on average, your friends have more friends than you do. When you pick a random person, you are equally likely to pick anyone. But when you pick a random friend of a random person, you are more likely to pick someone highly connected — because popular people appear on many friend lists.

What just happened mathematically?

You discovered that in almost every graph, most nodes have fewer connections than the average of their neighbours' connections. This is because sampling via edges biases toward high-degree nodes. Formally: E[degree of friend] = E[degree squared] / E[degree], which by Jensen's inequality is always at least E[degree]. The paradox holds whenever degree variance is positive — which is always, unless everyone has exactly the same number of friends.

Go deeper
Intuitive track

Think of it this way: the popular kids appear on everyone's friend list. So when you look at your friends and count their friends, the popular ones drag up the average. It is a sampling bias — you are more likely to encounter popular people when you sample through connections rather than at random. The same mechanism explains why your co-authors have more publications, why your gym friends seem fitter, and why social media makes everyone feel inadequate.

Mathematical track
Friendship Paradox (Feld, 1991): Sample random edge (u,v). The expected degree of v: E[deg(v) | sampled via edge] = E[deg^2] / E[deg] By Jensen's inequality (since Var(deg) > 0): E[deg^2] / E[deg] >= E[deg] Equality only when Var(deg) = 0 (regular graph). Generalised: any attribute positively correlated with degree is subject to the same paradox.

Era 3 — Facebook and the News Feed (2006–2015)

Weighted Graphs and Edge Ranking

Facebook launched News Feed in September 2006. Before this, a connection was a connection — all edges equal. News Feed introduced weighted connections: the algorithm decided how much each relationship "mattered" based on interactions, messages, mutual friends. The social graph transformed from an unweighted graph to a weighted directed graph.

The original EdgeRank formula (circa 2007) was Facebook's first explicit mathematical model:

EdgeRank(e) = Sum( u_e * w_e * d_e ) u_e = affinity score (how close are you?) w_e = edge weight (comment > like > view) d_e = time decay factor: e^(-lambda*t)

What just happened mathematically?

You turned an unweighted graph into a weighted one, assigning importance to each edge. The News Feed is a ranking function over weighted edges — a linear combination of affinity, interaction type, and time decay. Small changes in weights produce dramatically different feeds. This is not an opinion — it is a mathematical consequence of how linear combinations work when the input variables are correlated.

Go deeper
Intuitive track

Imagine you have a dial for "how much I care about recency" and another for "how much I care about closeness". At one extreme, your feed is purely chronological — whatever was posted most recently appears first. At the other extreme, only your closest friends appear, regardless of when they posted. Every News Feed algorithm is a point in this space of dials. The platform chooses that point, not you.

Mathematical track
Weighted directed graph: G = (V, E, w) w: E -> R+ (edge weights) Adjacency matrix A is no longer symmetric: A_ij != A_ji (A follows B != B follows A) Time decay: d(t) = e^(-lambda*t) Same mathematics as radioactive decay EdgeRank = linear combination of features: score = alpha*interaction + beta*relationship + gamma*decay Ranking = argsort(scores, descending)

For the Curious: Metcalfe's Law

Robert Metcalfe proposed in 1980 that the value of a network is proportional to the square of its number of users: V ~ n squared. The reasoning: n users can form n(n-1)/2 ~ n squared / 2 unique connections. This explains winner-take-all dynamics — a network with twice as many users is roughly four times as valuable. More nuanced models suggest V ~ n*log(n) may be more accurate, but the superlinear scaling is real, and it is the mathematical reason social networks tend toward monopoly.

Era 4 — Twitter and the Mathematics of Going Viral (2006–2014)

Branching Processes and R0

Twitter launched in 2006. The retweet mechanic created a mathematically tractable model of information contagion. Each user who receives a tweet decides independently whether to retweet it. If on average each carrier passes it to more than one new person, the spread is exponential. If fewer than one, it dies out. The parameter that determines the outcome is R0 — the basic reproductive number, borrowed from epidemiology.

What just happened mathematically?

You simulated an SIR process on a network — the same mathematics used to model disease outbreaks. The key parameter R0 = beta/gamma determines whether content goes viral (R0 > 1) or dies out (R0 < 1). You also saw that network structure matters: immunising high-degree hubs is disproportionately effective because they sit on many shortest paths. This is the mathematical foundation of targeted vaccination strategies.

Go deeper
Intuitive track

Virality is a phase transition, not a spectrum. Below the critical threshold (R0 = 1), every outbreak fizzles. Above it, there is a positive probability of explosive spread. This is not gradual — it is a sharp boundary, like water freezing at exactly 0 degrees C. The structure of the network shifts this boundary: in a hub-and-spoke network, removing the hubs is like raising the freezing point — it makes virality harder to achieve.

Mathematical track
Branching process: E[Z_n] = mu^n * Z_0 mu < 1: dies out (subcritical) mu = 1: critical mu > 1: exponential growth (supercritical) SIR model on a network: dS/dt = -beta*S*I/N dI/dt = beta*S*I/N - gamma*I dR/dt = gamma*I R_0 = beta / gamma Network vaccination: removing fraction f of highest-degree nodes reduces effective R_0 by more than removing fraction f at random.

Era 5 — Instagram, Reddit, and the Echo Chamber (2010–2018)

Community Detection and Modularity

As platforms matured, users self-organised into communities — interest groups, political tribes, subcultures. Reddit's subreddits made this explicit. Researchers began asking: can we mathematically detect these communities? And when detected, are they healthy (diverse bridges between groups) or unhealthy (echo chambers with no inter-group connections)?

"An echo chamber is not a metaphor. It is a precise mathematical condition: a subgraph with high modularity and low betweenness on its boundary. It can be measured, compared, and — in principle — designed against."

What just happened mathematically?

You explored modularity Q — a measure comparing the actual number of within-community edges to the expected number in a random graph. You also saw betweenness centrality: bridge nodes that sit on many shortest paths between communities. Removing bridges increases modularity (communities become more isolated) and can make cross-community communication impossible. This is the mathematics of echo chambers and filter bubbles.

Go deeper
Intuitive track

Imagine three islands connected by a few bridges. People on each island talk mostly to each other. The bridges are the only way ideas travel between islands. Now imagine someone removes those bridges. Each island becomes an echo chamber — hearing only its own ideas, reinforcing its own beliefs. A filter bubble is exactly this: the algorithm removes the bridges from the graph you experience, even if those bridges still exist in the underlying network.

Mathematical track
Modularity (Newman & Girvan, 2004): Q = (1/2m) * Sum_ij [ A_ij - k_i*k_j/(2m) ] * delta(c_i, c_j) Q near 0: no community structure Q near 1: strong community structure Betweenness centrality: C_B(v) = Sum_{s!=v!=t} sigma(s,t|v) / sigma(s,t) High C_B = bridge between communities Low C_B + high internal degree = echo chamber hub

Era 6 — TikTok and the Algorithmic Graph (2016–present)

Reinforcement Learning and Feedback Loops

TikTok's For You Page represented a qualitative shift. Previous platforms served your social graph: content from people you chose to follow. TikTok served a latent interest graph it inferred about you — a graph you never saw and never chose. The algorithm observes your behaviour, updates its model, serves new content, observes again. The social network became a feedback loop.

What just happened mathematically?

You simulated a coupled dynamical system: an algorithm that optimises engagement, applied to a user whose preferences shift based on what they see. With engagement-only optimisation, preferences collapse to 1–2 categories — a preference drift spiral. Adding a diversity term to the objective function maintains a richer distribution. The mathematics of objective function design has measurable consequences on the dynamical system it governs.

Go deeper
Intuitive track

Imagine you slightly prefer comedy over news. The algorithm shows you more comedy. You engage more with comedy (because you see more of it), so the algorithm thinks you prefer comedy even more. It shows you even more comedy. After a few cycles, your feed is entirely comedy — not because you chose it, but because the feedback loop amplified a small initial preference into a dominant one. This is preference drift, and it is a mathematical property of any system where output feeds back into input.

Mathematical track
Recommendation as MDP: State s_t: user preference vector in R^n Action a_t: content shown Reward r_t: engagement signal Transition: s_{t+1} = f(s_t, a_t, noise) Optimisation objective: max_pi E[ Sum_t gamma^t * r(s_t, a_t) ] Feedback loop: content_t ~ pi(.|s_t) If exposure increases appetite for similar content, the system enters a preference drift spiral — an attractor state far from initial preferences. No term for user wellbeing, accuracy, or diversity unless explicitly added to the objective.

Era 7 — Where Is This Heading? (Open Questions)

Mathematical Frontiers

This final section is different from the previous six. These are not settled mathematics — they are open problems and emerging ideas, explicitly speculative.

Frontier 1 — Protocol Graphs and Decentralisation

Platforms like Mastodon, Bluesky, and Nostr are built on open protocols rather than centralised servers. The social graph is distributed across many independent servers that federate with each other.

The mathematical question: What are the equilibrium properties of a federated graph? If individual servers can choose which other servers to federate with (or block), what graph structures emerge? Can a decentralised network achieve the small-world property without centralised amplification?

This is an active research area involving game theory, distributed systems, and graph theory. There is no settled answer.

Frontier 2 — Synthetic Social Graphs

AI agents are beginning to participate in social networks — generating content, responding to posts, forming apparent communities. If a significant fraction of nodes are not human, the mathematical properties of the graph may change fundamentally.

The mathematical question: What happens to the small-world property, the Friendship Paradox, virality dynamics, and community structure when a fraction alpha of nodes are synthetic agents with designed behaviour? This is largely an open problem.

Frontier 3 — Mathematics of Healthy Networks

Given that we can mathematically define echo chambers, preference drift, and virality thresholds, can we define conditions for a "healthy" information ecosystem?

The mathematical question: What graph topology, edge-weighting scheme, and recommendation objective function would jointly satisfy: short paths (information spreads), high clustering (communities form), low modularity (echo chambers don't form), subcritical virality (misinformation doesn't explode), and stable preferences (no feedback drift)?

These may be contradictory conditions. The tension between them is a real mathematical problem, not just a policy question.

The Shape of the Network Shapes the People in It

Six mathematical ideas, each demanded by the social behaviour of its era:

Graph theory — explains why misinformation spreads faster than corrections (short paths to hubs)

The Friendship Paradox — explains why your friends seem more popular than you (sampling bias via edges)

Weighted graphs — explains why your feed feels curated (because it is — by a ranking function you never chose)

Branching processes — explains why some content reaches billions and most dies immediately (phase transition at R0 = 1)

Modularity — explains why political opinions cluster (high within-group density, low between-group bridges)

Feedback loops — explains why recommendation algorithms can change what you believe (coupled dynamical system with no diversity constraint)

Six decades of graph theory, a century of probability, fifty years of dynamical systems theory — all of it turns out to be relevant to something three billion people interact with every day. The mathematics existed before the platforms. The platforms just made the mathematics urgent.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: Social networks are just the online version of real social life

Reality: Real social networks have specific mathematical properties (small-world, power-law degree distribution, high clustering) that online platforms both reflect and distort. The platform's design choices change the mathematical structure — and therefore the social dynamics within it.

Misconception: Going viral is unpredictable

Reality: Virality is a phase transition. Below R0 = 1, content almost certainly dies out. Above R0 = 1, it has a positive probability of exponential spread. The threshold is mathematically precise, even if the outcome of any individual post is uncertain.

Misconception: Filter bubbles are caused by platforms showing you what you like

Reality: More precisely, a filter bubble is a graph-theoretic phenomenon: the effective graph a user experiences has low betweenness centrality on its boundary — inter-community edges are absent or suppressed. Showing you what you like is the mechanism; the echo chamber is the mathematical consequence.

Misconception: TikTok's algorithm "knows" what you want

Reality: It knows what maximises engagement in the short term. These are not the same thing. The mathematics optimises an objective function. Whether that objective function aligns with user wellbeing is a separate question.

Recommendation Engines

Collaborative filtering, matrix factorisation, and the mathematics of choice — An exploration of how systems predict what you want before you do

Imagine you're on Netflix, and in your feed appears the exact film you were thinking about watching. You log into Spotify and discover a song from a band you love, but never heard of. Amazon suggests a book that feels personally curated. How do these systems know?

Behind the scenes, invisible algorithms are at work, solving a mathematical puzzle: given incomplete information about what millions of users like, predict what a specific user will enjoy. This is the heart of recommendation engines—and the mathematics is beautiful.

1. The Recommendation Problem

At its core, recommendation is about filling in missing data. Imagine a giant matrix where rows are users and columns are items (films, songs, products). Each cell contains a rating—how much a user liked an item. But most cells are empty: users have only rated a tiny fraction of all items.

The Challenge: Given the known ratings, predict the unknown ones. Which films will this user rate highly? Which songs should this user hear?

Why This Matters: The Netflix Prize (2006–2009) offered $1 million to teams that could improve their recommendation accuracy by 10%. This wasn't vanity—a 1% improvement in prediction means more users stay happy, engage more, watch more content.

Why Most Entries Are Missing
Netflix has ~10,000 films and 300 million users. A completely filled matrix would require 3 trillion ratings. In reality, the average user has rated fewer than 50 films. The matrix is 99.999% empty. Yet recommendation engines must work with this sparsity.

2. Collaborative Filtering: "People Like You Also Liked..."

The oldest and most intuitive approach: if two users have similar tastes, they'll probably like similar things in the future. This is collaborative filtering—leveraging the "wisdom of crowds."

Cosine Similarity: similarity(u, v) = (u · v) / (||u|| × ||v||) This measures the angle between two rating vectors. A value of 1 means identical taste; 0 means unrelated.

How it works:

  1. Compute how similar you are to every other user (based on ratings you both gave).
  2. Find your nearest neighbours—users most similar to you.
  3. Look at what they rated highly that you haven't seen.
  4. Recommend those items to you.

Cosine Similarity Intuition
Imagine each user as a point in a high-dimensional space (one dimension per film). Users who gave similar ratings point in the same direction. The cosine of the angle between them tells us how aligned their tastes are. The angle approaches 0° (and cosine → 1) when they agree; 90° (cosine → 0) when unrelated.
Limitation of Collaborative Filtering: What happens for a new user with no rating history? Or a new film that nobody has rated? Collaborative filtering struggles with the "cold-start problem." Matrix factorisation offers a solution.

3. Matrix Factorisation & Singular Value Decomposition

Rather than comparing users directly, modern recommendation engines learn latent factors—hidden features that explain ratings. These might correspond to genre, mood, or director style (though we never name them explicitly).

The Matrix Factorisation Approximation: R ≈ U · Σ · V^T Where: R = the ratings matrix (users × items) U = user factors (users × k latent dimensions) Σ = singular values (strength of each factor) V^T = item factors (k latent dimensions × items) The rank k controls how many latent factors we use.

Why this works: Instead of storing millions of ratings, we store two smaller matrices: one that describes each user as a combination of k latent factors, and one that describes each item the same way. The predicted rating for user u and item i is simply the dot product of their factor vectors.

The Elbow Curve
As we increase k (use more latent factors), reconstruction error drops sharply at first, then more slowly. The "elbow"—where the curve flattens—often indicates the optimal trade-off between accuracy and overfitting. Too few factors and we miss important patterns; too many and we fit noise.
Why SVD is Powerful: Singular Value Decomposition is the mathematical foundation for Netflix's algorithms (along with gradient descent optimisation). It works even with incomplete data (alternating least squares), scales to millions of users and items, and naturally handles the cold-start problem via factor embeddings.
Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): RMSE = √( Σ(predicted rating - actual rating)² / n ) Lower RMSE means more accurate predictions.

4. The Netflix Prize Challenge

In 2006, Netflix released a dataset of 100 million anonymous ratings from 480,000 users on 18,000 films. They challenged the world: improve our recommendation accuracy by 10% and win $1 million.

The Target: Netflix's baseline RMSE was 0.9525. Teams needed to reach 0.8563—a 10% improvement in RMSE. This sounds small, but across millions of predictions, it's significant.

The Winner: In September 2009, the team BellKor's Pragmatic Chaos (an ensemble combining SVD with other techniques) crossed the finish line with RMSE 0.8567—just barely beating the deadline. The competition drove innovation in recommendation systems globally.

Ensemble Methods
The winning solution didn't rely on a single algorithm. Instead, it combined dozens of different models (SVD, Restricted Boltzmann Machines, nearest-neighbour methods, etc.) and averaged their predictions. This ensembling reduced overfitting and pushed closer to the optimal 10% improvement.

5. Content-Based vs. Collaborative Filtering & Modern Hybrid Systems

Collaborative Filtering

  • Uses only user-item interaction history
  • Finds similar users or items
  • Scales well with data
  • Suffers from cold-start problem

Content-Based Filtering

  • Uses item metadata (genre, director, keywords)
  • Recommends similar items
  • Works for new items
  • Limited by description quality

The Cold-Start Problem: A new user has no history. A new item has no ratings. How do you recommend?

  • For new users: Use demographics, ask for initial preferences, or show popular items.
  • For new items: Use content-based features (metadata) until enough ratings accumulate.
  • For both: Hybrid systems combine collaborative + content-based approaches.
Modern Systems Are Hybrid: Netflix, Spotify, Amazon, YouTube, and TikTok all use combinations of collaborative filtering, content metadata, user context (time, device, location), and deep learning. The goal is to balance personalisation with exploration—recommending items users will love while occasionally surprising them with discoveries.
Hybrid Score (Example): final_score = 0.6 × collab_score + 0.3 × content_score + 0.1 × popularity Weights are tuned via A/B testing and offline evaluation.

Key Insights from the Netflix Prize and Beyond:

  • Data sparsity is the fundamental challenge. Most user-item pairs are unobserved.
  • Dimensionality reduction works. Learning low-rank latent factors captures patterns well.
  • Ensembles beat single models. Combining diverse algorithms reduces error.
  • Context matters. Time, location, device, and explicit feedback all improve accuracy.
  • Serendipity matters too. Pure accuracy isn't everything—users value novelty and diversity.

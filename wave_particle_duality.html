Wave-Particle Duality

●●●● Intermediate

De Broglie’s wavelength, the double-slit, and Heisenberg’s uncertainty — quantum mechanics made visual

A beam of electrons fired through two slits produces an interference pattern — just like light. A single electron goes through both slits simultaneously. Yet when detected, it lands at a single point. This paradox is wave-particle duality: the electron has a wavelength λ = h/(mv), but it is also a particle. Quantum mechanics reconciles these by assigning a wavefunction — a probability amplitude — to every particle.

Electron microscopes exploit the short de Broglie wavelength of electrons (keV energies → λ < 0.1 nm) to image individual atoms — far beyond the optical diffraction limit of ~500 nm. For a baseball (145 g at 40 m/s), λ ≈ 1.1 × 10⁻³⁴ m, twenty orders of magnitude below any measurable scale. Wave behaviour crosses into the classical regime when λ ≪ object size.

Modern experiments have demonstrated wave interference with increasingly large molecules: C60 buckyballs (1999), molecules of over 2,000 atoms (2019), and even biological porphyrin molecules. The frontier keeps pushing upward — the question “how big can a quantum object be?” remains open.

Each particle is fired individually — yet after thousands of detections, the interference pattern emerges. The particle “interferes with itself”: its wavefunction passes through both slits and interferes. Placing a detector at one slit to ask “which slit?” collapses the wavefunction and destroys the pattern entirely. The act of measurement changes the outcome.

Historical milestone: In 1961, Claus Jönsson performed the double-slit experiment with electrons, confirming particle-by-particle interference. In 1974, Merli, Missiroli and Pozzi filmed individual electrons building the pattern one dot at a time — widely cited as the “most beautiful experiment in physics” (Physics World, 2002).

Classical expectation: If particles were tiny bullets, firing them through two slits would produce two bright bands directly behind each slit — no interference. The fact that an interference pattern appears, even when particles are fired one at a time, proves that something wave-like passes through both slits simultaneously. That “something” is the probability amplitude ψ.

This is not about clumsy measurement disturbing the particle. It is a fundamental property of waves: any wavepacket localised to width Δx must contain a spread of frequencies Δk ≥ 1/(2Δx). This is Fourier’s theorem. Consequences pervade technology: laser linewidth, MRI pulse bandwidth, electron energy spread in microscopes, and the natural linewidth of atomic transitions all obey the same relationship.
The Mathematics of Quantum Reality

The wavefunction ψ(x,t) is not a physical wave shaking in space — it is a probability amplitude. The probability of finding a particle at position x is |ψ(x,t)|². Born’s rule converts the complex-valued wavefunction into the real-valued probabilities we observe. Schrödinger’s equation governs how ψ evolves deterministically between measurements; measurement itself causes “collapse” to a definite outcome, a process whose interpretation remains debated a century later.

The double-slit experiment is the prototype of every quantum measurement paradox. Before detection the particle exists in a superposition of “went through left slit” and “went through right slit”. The two amplitudes interfere, giving I(y) ∝ cos²(πdy/λL). The moment a detector identifies which slit was used, the superposition is broken: the two paths become distinguishable, interference vanishes, and the pattern collapses to two bright bands. Information about the path destroys the interference — not because the detector nudges the particle, but because quantum coherence requires indistinguishable paths.

Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle is Fourier’s theorem in disguise. A pulse of sound localised to one millisecond must contain frequencies spanning at least 1 kHz; a particle localised to one nanometre must contain momenta spanning ℏ/(2 × 1 nm) ≈ 5 × 10⁻²⁵ kg·m/s. The Gaussian wavepacket is special: it saturates the inequality ΔxΔp = ℏ/2, the minimum physically possible product. Every other shape gives a larger product — coherent states of the quantum harmonic oscillator are Gaussian, which is why they are “most classical.”

At human scales, de Broglie wavelengths are ≈10⁻³⁰ m, undetectable against any object. The quantum–classical crossover occurs roughly when λ ∼ object size. Electrons in atoms (λ ~ 0.1 nm) are manifestly quantum. Buckyballs (C₆₀, λ ~ 0.003 nm) show measurable interference. The hierarchy is continuous: quantum mechanics does not switch off, it simply becomes irrelevant at scales far above λ. Try This

An electron has mass 9.11×10⁻³¹ kg and moves at 10&sup6; m/s. What is its de Broglie wavelength? Compare this to the size of an atom (~10⁻¹⁰ m).

λ = h/(mv) = 6.626×10⁻³&sup4;/(9.11×10⁻³¹ × 10&sup6;) = 7.27×10⁻¹⁰ m ≈ 0.73 nm. This is comparable to atomic spacings — which is why electron diffraction works and why electron microscopes can “see” atoms. For a tennis ball at 50 m/s, λ ≈ 10⁻³&sup4; m — utterly undetectable. Quantum effects only matter at tiny scales.

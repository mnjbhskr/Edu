Chemical Thermodynamics

The energy book-keeping that decides every chemical reaction

Chapter 16 · Chemistry · Topic 5 · Difficulty 2

Whether a reaction proceeds, which direction it favours, and how far it goes — all of these questions are answered by thermodynamics. At its heart lies the Gibbs free energy, a single quantity that weighs the competing drives of enthalpy (the desire to minimise energy) and entropy (the desire to maximise disorder). Together they produce:

ΔG = ΔH − T·ΔS

1 · Gibbs Free Energy vs Temperature

Adjust ΔH and ΔS to see how the sign of ΔG changes with temperature. The crossover where ΔG = 0 marks the temperature at which the reaction switches spontaneity.

Four regimes: When ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0, the reaction is spontaneous at all temperatures. When ΔH > 0 and ΔS < 0, it is never spontaneous. The remaining two combinations have a crossover temperature T = ΔH / ΔS.
Enthalpy vs entropy — the two competing drives: Enthalpy (ΔH) measures heat flow: ice melting absorbs heat (ΔH > 0, endothermic), combustion releases heat (ΔH < 0, exothermic). Entropy (ΔS) measures disorder: ice melting increases disorder (ΔS > 0), gas compressing decreases disorder (ΔS < 0). Gibbs free energy ΔG = ΔH − TΔS balances these competing drives.

Van ’t Hoff plot: Plotting ln(K) against 1/T gives a straight line with slope −ΔH°/R and intercept ΔS°/R. Endothermic reactions (positive ΔH°) have K increasing with temperature; exothermic reactions show the opposite.
Chemical potential: μ = ∂G / ∂n — the thermodynamic “pressure” driving reactions. Molecules flow from high μ to low μ, just as water flows downhill. At equilibrium, the chemical potential is equal in all phases and for all species — this is the deepest statement of chemical equilibrium.

Forward vs reverse barrier: The forward activation energy is ΔG‡. The reverse activation energy is ΔG‡ − ΔG. An exothermic reaction (ΔG < 0) always has a larger reverse barrier.

4 · Le Chatelier’s Principle

Start at equilibrium (A ⇔ B). Perturb the system by adding A, removing B, or changing temperature, then watch Q vs K and the concentrations shift to restore equilibrium.

Perturbations

The principle: When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts in the direction that partially counteracts the disturbance. If Q < K the reaction proceeds forward; if Q > K it proceeds in reverse.
Phase diagrams: A phase diagram maps which state of matter (solid, liquid, gas) is stable at each temperature and pressure. The boundaries are where ΔG = 0 between phases. The triple point (where all three phases coexist) and the critical point (where liquid and gas become indistinguishable) are two of the most remarkable features of matter.

Did you know? Diamonds are thermodynamically unstable at room temperature — graphite has lower Gibbs free energy. Diamonds should spontaneously convert to graphite! But the activation energy barrier is so enormous that the process takes billions of years. Thermodynamics says what can happen; kinetics says how fast it happens.
Why Thermodynamics Matters

Chemical thermodynamics tells us whether a reaction can occur and how far it proceeds — questions that kinetics alone cannot answer. The interplay of enthalpy and entropy governs everything from the rusting of iron to the folding of proteins, from the combustion of fuels to the chemistry of life itself. Understanding these principles reveals why some processes are irreversible, why equilibrium exists, and why the second law of thermodynamics shapes the arrow of time.

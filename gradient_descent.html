Gradient Descent

Learning is a ball rolling downhill in a billion-dimensional space

Every neural network learns the same way: start with random guesses, measure how wrong they are, then adjust every parameter slightly in the direction that reduces the error. Repeat billions of times. The mathematics is a single equation — the update rule — applied at a scale that turns algebra into intelligence.

Learning is optimisation — find the valley by following the slope downhill.

η too small: takes thousands of steps to converge
η just right: converges in ~100 steps ← Goldilocks zone
η too large: loss oscillates and explodes
💡 A learning rate that is too large causes the ball to overshoot the valley and bounce back and forth. Real LLM training uses learning rate schedules — large at first, then reduced.

Too slow wastes compute. Too fast diverges. The learning rate is the single most important choice in training.

Full gradient: use ALL training examples → expensive but exact
Mini-batch SGD: use B examples → noisy but fast, generalises better
Single sample: B=1 → cheapest, noisiest
In practice: B = 256 to 4096 for large language models
💡 The noise in SGD is a feature, not a bug. It helps models escape sharp local minima and find flatter minima that generalise better. Sharp minima tend to overfit; flat minima are robust to small perturbations in the weights. This is partly why LLMs trained on mini-batches generalise so well.

A little randomness in the descent helps find better solutions — noise is the path to generalisation.

Why This Matters

Every weight in GPT-4 was set by gradient descent. Nobody programmed what it knows — the model discovered it by descending a loss landscape of unimaginable dimension. The mathematics here — a single update rule applied trillions of times — is the engine of the AI revolution.

Training GPT-3 consumed approximately 3.14×10²³ floating-point operations. Every one of those operations was a step of gradient descent.

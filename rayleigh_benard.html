Rayleigh–Bénard Convection

When heating from below defeats gravity — the birth of convection cells

1 · Thermal Stratification

Imagine a thin layer of fluid confined between two horizontal plates. The bottom plate is heated to a temperature Thot, while the top plate is held at a cooler temperature Tcold. Gravity pulls the denser, cooler fluid downward. When the temperature difference ΔT is small, heat travels upward purely by conduction — the molecules pass energy to their neighbours without any bulk fluid motion. This quiet state is called thermal stratification.

Ra = g · α · ΔT · L³ / (ν · κ)

The Rayleigh number Ra is the dimensionless ratio that governs Rayleigh–Bénard convection. Each symbol carries physical meaning:

  • g — gravitational acceleration (m/s²)
  • α — thermal expansion coefficient (1/K), how much the fluid expands per degree
  • ΔT — temperature difference between hot and cold plates (K)
  • L — depth of the fluid layer (m)
  • ν — kinematic viscosity (m²/s), the fluid’s resistance to shearing
  • κ — thermal diffusivity (m²/s), how quickly heat spreads by conduction
Ra = 854 — Stable (Ra < Rac ≈ 1708)
The critical Rayleigh number Rac ≈ 1708 marks the threshold for convection onset in a layer with rigid, no-slip boundaries (first derived by Lord Rayleigh in 1916). Below this value, conduction is the sole heat-transport mechanism. Above it, buoyancy overcomes viscous damping and thermal diffusion, and the fluid spontaneously organises into rotating convection cells.

2 · Onset of Convection

When Ra exceeds Rac, the stratified state becomes unstable. Tiny perturbations — inevitable in any real fluid — grow into organised convection cells. Hot fluid rises from the bottom, cools at the top plate, and sinks back down, forming a continuous circulation.

This simulation solves the two-dimensional Boussinesq equations using the vorticity–streamfunction formulation on a 100×50 grid. The Prandtl number Pr = ν/κ compares momentum diffusivity to thermal diffusivity. For air, Pr ≈ 0.7; for water, Pr ≈ 7. At low Pr the thermal field is smoother than the velocity field; at high Pr the opposite holds. The Nusselt number Nu measures the ratio of total heat flux (convection + conduction) to the purely conductive flux. Nu = 1 means conduction alone; higher values indicate vigorous convective transport.

3 · Convection Cell Patterns

Tracer particles carried by the flow reveal the large-scale structure of convection cells. Each particle is advected by the local velocity field and leaves a short trail behind it, making the rolling motion visible.

The number of cells that form depends strongly on the aspect ratio Γ = width/height of the domain. A wider container allows more counter-rotating rolls to fit side by side. Adjust the aspect ratio below and watch the pattern reorganise.

The number of convection cells depends on the aspect ratio. In a container twice as wide as it is tall (Γ = 2), you typically see two counter-rotating rolls. Widen it to Γ = 4 and additional pairs emerge. This is because the preferred wavelength of the instability is close to 2L (twice the layer depth), so the system tries to tile the domain with rolls of roughly square cross-section.

4 · What Have We Learnt?

Key Equations

Rayleigh number: Ra = gαΔTL³ / (νκ)
Prandtl number: Pr = ν / κ
Nusselt number: Nu = total heat flux / conductive heat flux
Critical threshold: Rac ≈ 1708 (rigid boundaries)
Boussinesq approximation: density variations appear only in the buoyancy term

The Rayleigh number quantifies the competition between buoyancy (which drives motion) and dissipation (viscosity + thermal diffusion, which resist it). The Nusselt number tells us how much more efficiently convection transports heat compared to pure conduction. Under the Boussinesq approximation, the fluid density is treated as constant everywhere except in the gravitational body-force term, where a linear dependence on temperature is retained. This simplification is valid when density variations are small (αΔT « 1) yet large enough to drive buoyancy.

Beyond Rac, steady two-dimensional rolls give way to increasingly complex dynamics as Ra is raised further. Time-dependent oscillations appear, then three- dimensional structures, and ultimately turbulent convection at very high Ra. The relationship between Nu and Ra in the turbulent regime — often written Nu ∼ Raβ with β near 1/3 — remains one of the most actively studied problems in fluid dynamics.

Where You’ll See This

  • Earth’s mantle — slow convection currents in the mantle (Ra ~ 107) drive plate tectonics over millions of years
  • Weather — atmospheric convection cells (Hadley, Ferrel, Polar) redistribute heat from the equator to the poles and shape global wind patterns
  • Ocean circulation — thermohaline circulation is driven by density differences arising from both temperature and salinity gradients
  • Cooking — convection currents in a pot of boiling water carry heat from the base upward, mixing the fluid and cooking food evenly
  • Stars — convective zones in stellar interiors transport energy outward where radiative transfer alone is insufficient; in our Sun, the outer 30% by radius is convective

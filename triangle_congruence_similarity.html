Triangle Congruence & Similarity

●●●● Intermediate

When triangles are equal, when they are proportional — and how to prove it

1. Congruence vs Similarity

Two triangles are congruent (written ≅) when they have exactly the same shape and size. Every side of one equals the corresponding side of the other, and every angle matches. Think of it as photocopying a triangle at 100 % — the copy is indistinguishable from the original.

Two triangles are similar (written ∼) when they have the same shape but not necessarily the same size. Corresponding angles are equal, and corresponding sides are in proportion. Think of enlarging a photograph — the image keeps its shape, but everything scales up or down by the same factor.

Key distinction: Congruent triangles are always similar (with scale factor 1), but similar triangles are not necessarily congruent. Congruence is the special case of similarity where the ratio of corresponding sides equals exactly 1.

2. Congruence Criteria

A triangle has six measurements — three sides and three angles. Remarkably, you do not need all six to determine a unique triangle. The following minimum sets are sufficient:

Criterion What Must Match Statement
SAS Two sides + included angle If two sides and the included angle of one triangle are equal to two sides and the included angle of another, the triangles are congruent.
ASA Two angles + included side If two angles and the included side of one triangle are equal to two angles and the included side of another, the triangles are congruent.
SSS All three sides If three sides of one triangle are equal to three sides of another, the triangles are congruent.
RHS Hypotenuse + one side (right triangles) If the hypotenuse and one side of a right triangle are equal to the hypotenuse and one side of another right triangle, they are congruent.
Why do these criteria work? (Degrees of freedom)

A triangle in a plane has three degrees of freedom — once you fix three independent measurements, the triangle is uniquely determined (up to reflection).

SAS: Two side lengths fix the positions of two vertices relative to a third. The included angle then locks the third vertex — there is only one place it can go.

ASA: A side and two angles determine the directions of the other two sides. Since these directions are fixed, they intersect at a unique point, determining the triangle.

SSS: Three side lengths define three circles. The intersection of the circles around one side gives exactly one valid third vertex (above or below the base).

RHS: In a right triangle, Pythagoras’ theorem gives the third side from the hypotenuse and one other side. This reduces to SSS.

Caution: “SSA” (two sides and a non-included angle) is not a valid congruence criterion — it can produce two different triangles (the “ambiguous case”).

4. Properties of Isosceles Triangles

An isosceles triangle has (at least) two equal sides. The angles opposite those equal sides are themselves equal — and conversely, if two angles of a triangle are equal, the sides opposite them are equal.

  • The angles opposite the equal sides are called base angles.
  • The bisector of the vertical angle (the angle between the equal sides) is also the perpendicular bisector of the base.
  • This bisector is simultaneously the median and the altitude from the apex.

5. Similarity Criteria

Similar triangles have the same shape but may differ in size. The ratio of any pair of corresponding sides is constant — this constant is the scale factor (or ratio of similitude).

Criterion What Must Match Statement
AA Two angles equal If two angles of one triangle are equal to two angles of another, the triangles are similar. (The third angle is automatically equal since angles sum to 180°.)
SAS Similarity One angle equal + adjacent sides in proportion If one angle of a triangle equals one angle of another, and the sides enclosing those angles are in proportion, the triangles are similar.
SSS Similarity All three sides in proportion If the three sides of one triangle are proportional to the three sides of another, the triangles are similar.
Key insight: In similar triangles, corresponding sides are proportional and corresponding angles are equal. If ▵ABC ∼ ▵DEF with scale factor k, then AB/DE = BC/EF = CA/FD = k, and ∠A = ∠D, ∠B = ∠E, ∠C = ∠F.

6. Basic Proportionality Theorem (Thales)

Theorem: If a line is drawn parallel to one side of a triangle, it divides the other two sides in the same ratio.

In ▵ABC, if line DE is drawn parallel to BC (with D on AB and E on AC), then AD/DB = AE/EC.

Proof of the Basic Proportionality Theorem

Given: In ▵ABC, line DE ∥ BC, with D on AB and E on AC.

To prove: AD/DB = AE/EC.

Construction: Draw perpendiculars DN ⊥ AE and EM ⊥ AD. Join BE and CD.

Proof:

Area(▵ADE) = ½ × AD × EM  … (i)

Area(▵BDE) = ½ × DB × EM  … (ii)

From (i) and (ii): Area(▵ADE) / Area(▵BDE) = AD / DB  … (iii)

Similarly, Area(▵ADE) = ½ × AE × DN and Area(▵CDE) = ½ × EC × DN.

So Area(▵ADE) / Area(▵CDE) = AE / EC  … (iv)

Now, ▵BDE and ▵CDE share the same base DE and lie between the same parallels DE and BC. Therefore Area(▵BDE) = Area(▵CDE)  … (v)

From (iii), (iv), and (v): AD / DB = AE / EC.   ■

Converse (also useful): If a line divides two sides of a triangle in the same ratio, then that line is parallel to the third side.

7. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Congruence

In triangles ABC and DEF, AB = DE, BC = EF, and ∠B = ∠E. Are the triangles congruent? Which criterion applies?

Solution

We are given:

Side: AB = DE   Side: BC = EF   Angle: ∠B = ∠E

The angle ∠B is the included angle between sides AB and BC. Similarly, ∠E is the included angle between sides DE and EF.

By the SAS (Side-Angle-Side) congruence criterion:

▵ABC ≅ ▵DEF

Therefore the triangles are congruent by SAS.

Example 2 — Similarity and Scale Factor

Two triangles have sides 3, 4, 5 and 6, 8, 10 respectively. Show they are similar, find the scale factor, and verify the angles are equal.

Solution

Check proportionality of corresponding sides:

Ratio 1: 6/3 = 2.000
Ratio 2: 8/4 = 2.000
Ratio 3: 10/5 = 2.000

All three ratios are equal, so by the SSS Similarity criterion the triangles are similar. The scale factor is k = 2.

Both triangles are right-angled (since 3² + 4² = 5² and 6² + 8² = 10²). The angles in the first triangle are:

∠A = arctan(3/4) ≈ 36.87°
∠B = arctan(4/3) ≈ 53.13°
∠C = 90.00°

The second triangle has exactly the same angles, confirming similarity. The triangles are not congruent because the scale factor is 2 (not 1).

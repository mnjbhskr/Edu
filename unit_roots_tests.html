Unit Roots & Stationarity Tests

●●●● Advanced

Testing whether shocks are transient or permanent — the foundation of time series econometrics

1. Augmented Dickey-Fuller Test Simulator

Generate a near-unit-root or true unit-root process. The ADF test statistic follows a non-standard distribution under H₀: unit root. Compare with Dickey-Fuller critical values.

1.000
The ADF critical values are more negative than normal critical values: −2.86 (5%) vs −1.645. This is because under H₀, the t-statistic has a Dickey-Fuller distribution, not a standard normal.
Why is the Dickey-Fuller distribution non-standard?
Under the null of a unit root, the regressor yt−1 is itself a random walk. Standard asymptotic theory requires regressors to be “well-behaved” (e.g., stationary or deterministic trends). When the regressor is I(1), the OLS estimator converges at rate T (not √T), and the t-statistic converges to a functional of Brownian motion rather than a standard normal. This is why Dickey and Fuller (1979) had to tabulate new critical values via simulation. The distribution is left-skewed and has heavier left tails than the normal.

Size Distortion: ADF vs Normal Critical Values

What happens if you mistakenly use normal critical values (−1.645) instead of DF critical values (−2.86)? The over-rejection rate under the null is dramatic.

2. Power Analysis

As ρ approaches 1, the ADF test loses power rapidly. Even at ρ = 0.95 with T = 100, rejection rates can be disappointingly low.

3. Trend vs Difference Stationarity

A trend-stationary process (y = a + bt + ε) and a random walk with drift can look nearly identical. But they need completely different treatment: detrending vs differencing.

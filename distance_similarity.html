Distance & Similarity

Similarity is distance — and distance depends entirely on how you measure

Core idea Similarity is distance, and your choice of distance metric is a mathematical assumption about the structure of your data.

💡 The Manhattan metric is called “taxi-cab” distance because in a grid city you can only travel along streets — you cannot cut diagonally. Your choice of metric encodes an assumption about how space is structured. In image processing, pixel differences use L1 because it is less sensitive to outliers; in NLP, cosine similarity is used because the direction of a word vector matters, not its magnitude. 
Euclidean (L2):    d(x,y) = √(Σ(xᵢ − yᵢ)²)     ← straight-line distance
Manhattan (L1):    d(x,y) = Σ|xᵢ − yᵢ|           ← taxi-cab distance
Chebyshev (L∞):    d(x,y) = max|xᵢ − yᵢ|         ← chessboard distance
Cosine:            d(x,y) = 1 − (x·y)/(‖x‖‖y‖)   ← angle, not magnitude
Minkowski (Lp):    d(x,y) = (Σ|xᵢ − yᵢ|ᵖ)^(1/p)  ← generalises all above

💡 In word embedding models, “king” and “queen” are close in cosine similarity (similar direction) but may differ greatly in Euclidean distance if one word appears more frequently in training data (larger magnitude). Cosine similarity is the right metric when magnitude is noise and direction is signal — which is why every modern language model uses it. 
cosine_similarity(u, v) = (u · v) / (‖u‖ × ‖v‖)

                   = cos(θ)   where θ is the angle between the vectors

Range: −1 (opposite)   0 (orthogonal)   +1 (identical direction)

Key property: invariant to scaling
    cosine_similarity(2u, v) = cosine_similarity(u, v)

💡 K-means finds clusters by minimising total within-cluster L2 distance. But if you changed the metric to L1, you would get different clusters — not wrong, just different. Every clustering algorithm embeds an implicit assumption about what “close” means. The choice of metric is a modelling decision, not a technical one.
What have we learnt?
The notion of “similarity” is not self-evident — it is a mathematical choice. A metric space is a set together with a distance function satisfying four axioms, and different distance functions produce fundamentally different geometries. The Euclidean metric treats all directions equally. The cosine metric cares only about angle, ignoring magnitude. The Manhattan metric follows grid paths. Modern machine learning is, in large part, the art of choosing or learning the right metric for the task: when a recommendation system says two films are “similar”, it is making a statement about distance in a high-dimensional metric space. The choice of representation is not neutral — it is a mathematical act with consequences.

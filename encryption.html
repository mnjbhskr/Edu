The Mathematics of Encryption

How secrets travel safely across public networks — a visual exploration of RSA, Diffie-Hellman, and elliptic curves

Every time you visit a secure website, send an encrypted message, or perform a digital transaction, you rely on mathematical algorithms that would have seemed like magic to cryptographers of just fifty years ago. The challenge is deceptively simple: Alice wants to send a secret message to Bob across a public channel where anyone can listen. How can they ensure that even if an eavesdropper intercepts every transmission, the message remains incomprehensible? The answer lies in the deep mathematics of number theory and elliptic curves—asymmetric cryptography, where the algorithm to encrypt is public, but computing the decryption key remains computationally infeasible. In this exploration, you'll discover how multiplying large primes is easy, yet factoring their product is devastatingly hard; how two parties can agree on a secret without ever directly sharing it; and how points on abstract algebraic curves provide the modern foundation for digital security.

1. The Key Exchange Problem

Before modern public-key cryptography existed, encrypting a message required that both Alice and Bob already shared a secret key. But how do you establish that shared secret when your only communication channel is public? This is the fundamental paradox that the Diffie-Hellman key exchange elegantly solves.

Imagine Alice and Bob are in a crowded marketplace where anyone can hear their conversation. They want to agree on a colour (their secret), but they're constrained: they can only discuss operations involving mixing colours, and once mixed, colours cannot be unmixed. Diffie-Hellman works this way: a shared mixing function (modular exponentiation) allows them to combine their private choices with public information to arrive at the same secret—whilst an eavesdropper, despite hearing everything, cannot compute what that secret is.

Interactive Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange

How it works: Choose a prime p and generator g. Alice picks secret a, Bob picks secret b. They compute and exchange public values A = ga mod p and B = gb mod p. Both then compute the shared secret: K = Ba mod p = Ab mod p.

Why is this secure? An eavesdropper knows p, g, A, and B, but computing a from A = ga mod p requires solving the discrete logarithm problem—which is computationally infeasible for large primes. This is the mathematical bedrock of the algorithm's security.

2. RSA Encryption: The Magic of Prime Factorisation

RSA, named after Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman, is perhaps the most famous public-key cryptosystem. It exploits a profound asymmetry in computational complexity: multiplying two large primes is trivial, but factoring their product back into those primes is extraordinarily difficult.

Interactive RSA Key Generation and Encryption

The RSA Process: 1. Choose two distinct primes p and q. Compute n = pq and φ(n) = (p−1)(q−1).
2. Choose public exponent e such that gcd(e, φ(n)) = 1. Typically e = 65537 or 17.
3. Compute private exponent d such that ed ≡ 1 (mod φ(n)).
4. Encrypt message m: c = me mod n. Decrypt: m = cd mod n.
RSA Security: Breaking RSA requires factoring n into p and q.
Current recommendation: use 2048-bit or 4096-bit primes for security.
The computational gap: Multiplying two 1024-bit primes takes microseconds. Factoring the result would take longer than the age of the universe with classical computers—hence why RSA remains the bedrock of digital signatures and secure communication.

3. Elliptic Curve Cryptography

Elliptic curves provide an alternative to RSA that offers equivalent security with smaller key sizes. A cryptographic elliptic curve is defined by an equation of the form y² = x³ + ax + b, where we perform arithmetic operations on the points of this curve. The security comes from the elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem: given a point Q = kP (where k is a scalar and P is a base point), finding k is computationally infeasible.

Point addition on elliptic curves: To add two points P and Q, draw a line through them. This line intersects the curve at a third point R. The sum P + Q is defined as the reflection of R across the x-axis. This geometric operation has beautiful algebraic properties and forms a group structure.

ECDH: Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman

ECDH uses elliptic curve point multiplication instead of modular exponentiation. Two parties agree on a curve and base point G. Alice picks secret a and sends PA = aG. Bob picks secret b and sends PB = bG. Both compute the shared secret: S = aPB = bPA = abG.

Why elliptic curves? 256-bit ECC provides equivalent security to 2048-bit RSA. This means smaller keys, faster computation, and less bandwidth—critical for mobile and IoT devices. Modern protocols like TLS 1.3 favour elliptic curves.

4. The Computational Arms Race and Future Threats

Cryptographic security is not static. As computational power increases, key sizes must grow to maintain security. The history of cryptography is a race between code-makers and code-breakers.

Factoring Race: Brute Force vs Key Size

How key size affects security: Watch the animated timer below to see how brute-force factoring time scales with key size. Doubling the number of bits doesn't merely double the work — it increases it exponentially.
1977 RSA invented. Early implementations use 512-bit keys, considered secure.
1999 RSA-155 (512-bit) factored in 4 months using distributed computing. The field shifts to 1024-bit keys.
2009 RSA-768 (232-digit, ~768-bit) factored. Security agencies recommend 2048-bit minimum.
2020–Present 4096-bit and elliptic curve P-256 / P-384 become standard. Focus shifts to post-quantum cryptography.

The Quantum Threat: Shor's Algorithm

Peter Shor proved in 1994 that a sufficiently powerful quantum computer could factor large integers and solve discrete logarithms in polynomial time. This would render RSA and elliptic curve cryptography obsolete.

Shor's Algorithm Complexity:
Classical: O(exp(n1/3 log2/3 n)) — sub-exponential
Quantum: O(n³) — polynomial
Post-Quantum Cryptography: NIST and cryptographic communities are standardising lattice-based, hash-based, and multivariate polynomial schemes that resist quantum attacks. Organisations are beginning "quantum-safe migration" strategies now.
Current consensus: Quantum computers capable of breaking RSA-2048 do not yet exist, and estimates suggest 10–20 years before they might. However, "harvest now, decrypt later" attacks—where adversaries collect encrypted data today to decrypt once quantum computers exist—are a real concern. Post-quantum cryptography standardisation is a critical priority.

5. Comparing Cryptographic Systems

Diffie-Hellman

Security: Discrete logarithm problem
Typical key size: 2048–4096 bits
Use: Key exchange, forward secrecy
Speed: Moderate
Quantum-safe: No

RSA

Security: Integer factorisation
Typical key size: 2048–4096 bits
Use: Key exchange, digital signatures
Speed: Slower (especially decryption)
Quantum-safe: No

Elliptic Curves (ECC)

Security: Elliptic curve discrete log
Typical key size: 256–521 bits
Use: Key exchange, signatures, encryption
Speed: Fast
Quantum-safe: No

Post-Quantum (Lattice-based)

Security: Learning with errors, shortest vector problem
Typical key size: 1024–4096 bits
Use: Future-proof encryption
Speed: Very fast
Quantum-safe: Yes (conjectured)

The mathematics of encryption is a testament to the power of abstract algebra and number theory. What began as purely theoretical problems in the 1970s—can we do public-key cryptography? is the discrete logarithm hard?—became the foundation of digital commerce, secure communication, and trust on the internet. Every encrypted message you send, every HTTPS connection you make, every digital signature verifying software integrity relies on the hard mathematical problems explored in this interactive exploration.

As we approach the quantum era, cryptographers are already preparing the next generation of algorithms. The conversation between mathematicians, computer scientists, and engineers continues. The future of cryptography will be written not by breakthroughs in breaking existing systems, but by the discovery of new mathematical structures so hard that even quantum computers cannot overcome them.

Network Routing

How the internet moves information globally — and the mathematics that makes it work

When you send a message, it is not transmitted as one continuous stream to its destination. Instead, it is split into tiny packets that may take completely different routes across the world, travelling through different routers, links and continents—yet all arrive, reassemble perfectly, and deliver your intended meaning. This elegant idea, born in the 1960s, transformed communication forever. The mathematics underlying this magic comes from a surprisingly beautiful theory: flow networks and the algorithms that optimise them. Max-flow, min-cut, and protocol design all interweave to make the internet possible.

Circuit Switching vs Packet Switching

Before the internet, telephone networks operated on circuit switching. When you called someone, a dedicated electrical circuit was established from your phone to theirs. That circuit remained open for the duration of the call, whether anyone was speaking or not. If many people were calling at once, new calls would be rejected—the network could not share circuits. Bandwidth was wasted during silences, and capacity was fundamentally rigid.

In 1964, Paul Baran proposed a revolutionary alternative: packet switching. Rather than dedicating a path for the entire communication, data is divided into small packets. Each packet is independently routed through the network, potentially taking different paths. Links can be shared: when one packet finishes, another begins. This approach is far more efficient and resilient.

Flow Networks — Capacity and Flow

To understand routing and congestion mathematically, we model networks as directed graphs with capacity constraints. Each edge has a maximum amount of "flow" (data, packets, bandwidth) it can carry. A source node (s) generates flow, and a sink node (t) consumes it. At every other node, flow conservation must hold: what comes in must go out.

Flow conservation at node v ≠ s, t: Σ (flow into v) = Σ (flow out of v) At source s: Σ (flow out of s) = total flow At sink t: Σ (flow into t) = total flow

Max-Flow — The Ford-Fulkerson Algorithm

The max-flow problem asks: what is the maximum amount of flow we can push from s to t while respecting capacity constraints? The Ford-Fulkerson algorithm (1956) solves this elegantly by repeatedly finding augmenting paths: paths from s to t where every edge has spare capacity. We push as much flow as possible along each path, then repeat on the updated network (the residual graph), until no augmenting path exists.

Ford-Fulkerson Algorithm: 1. Start with zero flow on all edges 2. While there exists an augmenting path P from s to t: - Let δ = min capacity available on edges of P - Increase flow by δ along all edges of P 3. Return the total flow

The algorithm terminates when the residual graph has no s-t path. At this point, the flow is maximal. Remarkably, the max flow value equals the capacity of the minimum cut (a fact we explore next).

The Max-Flow Min-Cut Theorem

A cut in a flow network is a partition of nodes into two sets: S (containing source s) and T (containing sink t). The capacity of the cut is the sum of capacities of all edges leaving S and entering T (forward edges only).

Here is one of the most elegant theorems in computer science and mathematics:

Max-Flow Min-Cut Theorem (Ford & Fulkerson, 1956):

The maximum flow from s to t equals the minimum capacity of any cut separating s from t.

This theorem provides both a correctness proof and a bound: any cut's capacity is an upper bound on flow. The max-flow algorithm finds a flow that saturates a minimum cut, proving optimality.

Internet Routing Protocols

The theoretical max-flow framework guides real-world routing, but internet routing is more complex. Real routers must adapt to link failures, topology changes, and policy constraints. Two main paradigms exist:

OSPF (Open Shortest Path First)

OSPF is an intra-domain routing protocol used within an autonomous system (AS). Every router floods link-state information—the full network topology and link costs—to every other router. Each router then independently runs Dijkstra's algorithm to compute shortest paths. When a link fails, routers quickly detect it (via loss of "hello" packets), flood the new topology, and re-compute paths. The network converges within seconds to minutes.

BGP (Border Gateway Protocol)

BGP operates inter-domain, between autonomous systems (owned by different ISPs and organisations). BGP routers exchange reachability information: "I can reach prefix 192.0.2.0/24 via this path." Decisions are made not purely on path length but on policy: preferred AS paths, revenue agreements, and traffic engineering goals. BGP can take many minutes to converge after failures, leading to the famous phrase: "the internet is held together by BGP and goodwill."

Congestion Control and TCP

Once packets are routed, the rate at which sources send must be controlled to avoid overwhelming networks. TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) uses additive increase, multiplicative decrease (AIMD): senders increase their sending rate slowly (additively) when there is no loss, but halve it sharply (multiplicatively) when packets are lost or marked as congested.

This simple rule has a beautiful property: multiple flows competing for bandwidth converge to fair sharing, where each flow gets an equal fraction. The rate oscillates in a characteristic sawtooth pattern.

Why AIMD works: When congestion occurs, multiplicative decrease ensures all flows reduce rate proportionally. Additive increase then allows the network to explore higher rates. Together, they drive the system toward equilibrium where flows are fairly allocated and link is well utilised.

Synthesis: From Theory to Practice

Packet switching enables efficient use of bandwidth by multiplexing independent flows. Flow networksmax-flow/min-cut theorem provide the mathematical foundation: they tell us the maximum capacity and the bottleneck. Routing protocolsTCP congestion control ensures that sources behave fairly and adapt to network conditions.

These ideas, developed in the 1950s through 1980s, remain central to the modern internet. Every bit of data you send crosses routers running variants of these algorithms. The mathematics is not a luxury—it is the skeleton that holds the internet together.

Nuclear Physics

The mathematics of the atomic nucleus

The nucleus holds almost all the mass of an atom in a space ten thousand times smaller than the atom itself. Binding energy explains why some nuclei are stable and others are not. Radioactive decay follows a beautifully simple exponential law. Fission and fusion — splitting heavy nuclei and merging light ones — both release energy because they move towards the peak of the binding energy curve. These processes power stars and, for better or worse, have shaped modern civilisation.

Key insight: Iron-56 sits at the peak (~8.8 MeV per nucleon). Nuclei lighter than iron release energy by fusion; nuclei heavier than iron release energy by fission. This is why stars fuse hydrogen into helium, and why uranium can power reactors.

Each nucleus has an independent, random probability of decaying in each time step. The collective behaviour produces the smooth exponential curve N(t) = N₀ · 2−t/t½.

3. Nuclear Fission

Uranium-235 fission animation

Energy released: 0 MeV

A slow neutron is absorbed by ²³5U, forming an excited ²³6U compound nucleus that deforms and splits into two lighter fragments plus 2–3 neutrons. Each event releases roughly 200 MeV. The released neutrons can trigger further fissions — a chain reaction.

Temperature equivalent: T ≈ E / kB. At 10 keV, that is roughly 116 million kelvin — hotter than the core of the Sun.
Two light nuclei must overcome the Coulomb barrier (electrostatic repulsion) to fuse. At the energies in stellar cores, quantum tunnelling allows fusion even when classical physics says it should not happen. ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + 17.6 MeV.

5. What Have We Learnt?

Key concepts and equations

  • Binding energy per nucleon — the energy needed to disassemble a nucleus into free protons and neutrons, divided by A. Peaks near ⁵⁶Fe at ~8.8 MeV/nucleon.
  • Radioactive decay law — N(t) = N₀ · 2−t/t½. Equivalently, N(t) = N₀ · e−λt where λ = ln 2 / t½.
  • Fission — heavy nuclei (A > ~230) split into lighter fragments, releasing ~200 MeV per event. Neutron multiplication enables chain reactions.
  • Fusion — light nuclei (A < ~26) merge to form heavier ones, releasing energy. D–T fusion yields 17.6 MeV. The Coulomb barrier demands extreme temperatures or quantum tunnelling.
  • Stellar nucleosynthesis — stars fuse hydrogen to helium (main sequence), then progressively heavier elements up to iron. Elements heavier than iron are forged in supernovae and neutron star mergers.
B/A curve peak: ⁵⁶Fe ≈ 8.8 MeV/nucleon
Decay: N(t) = N₀ · 2−t/t½
Fission: ²³5U + n → fragments + 2–3n + ~200 MeV
Fusion: ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + 17.6 MeV
The binding energy curve is the Rosetta Stone of nuclear physics. Every process — decay, fission, fusion, stellar evolution — can be understood as nature seeking the most tightly bound configuration.

Cointegration & Error Correction Models

When non-stationary series share a long-run equilibrium — they wander together

2. Error Correction Mechanism

When the spread deviates from equilibrium, error correction forces pull the series back. Adjust the adjustment speed α to see fast vs slow correction. Arrows show the direction of adjustment.

0.15
The error correction model: Δy₁ = α(y₂ − βy₁) + ε. When the spread (y₂ − βy₁) is positive, y₁ increases to close the gap. The Granger Representation Theorem guarantees that cointegrated variables have an ECM representation.
The Engle-Granger two-step procedure
Step 1: Regress yt on xt by OLS. Even though both are I(1), if they are cointegrated, OLS is super-consistent (converges at rate T, not √T). Save the residuals ût = yt − β̂xt.
Step 2: Test the residuals ût for a unit root using ADF. If you reject, the variables are cointegrated. Then estimate the ECM: Δyt = αût−1 + lagged differences + εt.
Caveat: Critical values for the ADF on residuals differ from standard DF tables because the residuals are estimated, not observed. Use Engle-Granger critical values (more negative than standard ADF).
Where you will see this: Pairs trading in finance exploits cointegration: if two stock prices share a common stochastic trend, their spread is mean-reverting and tradable. In macroeconomics, consumption and income are classic cointegrated variables — they wander together in the long run, even if they diverge temporarily.

3. Spurious vs Cointegration

Side by side: two independent random walks (spurious) vs two cointegrated walks. Both regressions show high R², but only the cointegrated spread is stationary.

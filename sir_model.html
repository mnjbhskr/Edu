The SIR Epidemic Model

The mathematics of how diseases spread — and how we stop them

Chapter 15 · Biology & Life Sciences · Topic 1 · Difficulty 2

In 1927, Kermack and McKendrick proposed a stunningly simple idea: divide a population into three compartments — Susceptible, Infected, and Recovered — and track how people flow between them. This compartmental model, governed by just two parameters, captures the essential dynamics of an epidemic: exponential growth, a peak, and eventual decline. Nearly a century later, the same equations guided the global response to COVID-19.

dS/dt = −βSI/N
dI/dt = βSI/N − γI
dR/dt = γI
β = transmission rate  |  γ = recovery rate  |  R₀ = β/γ

The epidemic peaks exactly when the number of susceptible people drops below S = γN/β. At that moment, each infected person transmits the disease to fewer than one other — and the wave begins to recede.

The vertical dashed line at S = γN/β is the I-nullcline — it marks where infected numbers peak. Trajectories climb to the left of this line (epidemic growing) and fall to the right (epidemic declining). This is why herd immunity works: if S starts below the threshold, the trajectory never rises.

The herd immunity threshold is pc = 1 − 1/R₀. Vaccinate above this fraction and the disease cannot sustain an outbreak — even the unvaccinated are protected. You do not need to vaccinate everyone; you only need to break the chain of transmission.
Vaccination Mathematics
Effective R = R₀ × (1 − vaccination rate)
To achieve herd immunity: vaccination rate > 1 − 1/R₀
R₀ values for real diseases

Seasonal Flu R₀ ≈ 1.5 ~33% vaccination needed
COVID-19 R₀ ≈ 2–5 ~50–80% vaccination needed
Smallpox R₀ ≈ 5–7 ~80–86% vaccination needed
Measles R₀ ≈ 12–18 ~92–95% vaccination needed
Did you know? — Measles has an R₀ of 12–18, meaning each infected person infects 12 to 18 others in a fully susceptible population. This is why measles requires ~95% vaccination coverage for herd immunity — the highest of any common disease.

Why It Matters

The Kermack–McKendrick model (1927) is nearly a century old, yet it remains the foundation of modern mathematical epidemiology. Its power lies in its simplicity: just two parameters — β and γ — capture the essential dynamics of an epidemic.

R₀ determines everything. If R₀ < 1, each infected person transmits the disease to fewer than one other on average, and the outbreak dies out. If R₀ > 1, exponential growth takes hold and an epidemic unfolds. The single number R₀ = β/γ is the most important quantity in epidemiology.

This model and its descendants guided COVID-19 policy worldwide: lockdown timing, vaccination targets, hospital capacity planning, and social distancing measures were all informed by SIR-family models.

Extensions abound. The SEIR model adds an Exposed compartment (for the incubation period). The SIS model removes immunity (for diseases like the common cold). Age-structured models account for different contact patterns between age groups. Network models capture the topology of social connections.

The SEIR Extension — Many diseases have an incubation period where a person is infected but not yet infectious. The SEIR model captures this by adding an Exposed (E) compartment between Susceptible and Infected:

S → E → I → R

Exposed individuals carry the pathogen but cannot yet transmit it. A new parameter σ (the rate at which exposed individuals become infectious, i.e. 1/incubation period) governs the E → I transition. This extension was critical for modelling COVID-19, which has a significant incubation period of ~5 days during which carriers may be unaware they are infected.
